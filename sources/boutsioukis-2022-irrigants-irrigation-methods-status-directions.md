## Why Ingested
Commissioned status-and-directions review in the 2022 IEJ supplement that redefines the irrigation paradigm — instrumentation provides access, irrigants do the disinfection — and critically evaluates evidence for adjunct activation methods; directly complements [[endodontics/irrigation/khoury-2024-endodontic-irrigants-comprehensive-perspective-review]] and the intracanal medicaments companion [[endodontics/ordinola-zapata-2022-intracanal-medicaments-status-directions]].

## One-line Summary
Comprehensive narrative review critically appraising endodontic irrigants (NaOCl, EDTA, CHX) and delivery/activation methods (syringe, ultrasonic, sonic, negative-pressure, laser), concluding NaOCl + chelator remains the gold standard and no adjunct activation method has demonstrated improved long-term treatment outcomes.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Reframes the endodontic paradigm: instrumentation = access for irrigants; irrigants = primary cleaning/disinfection agents.
- Identifies biofilm EPS matrix, complex root canal anatomy (isthmuses, fins, tubules), and vapor lock as the main irrigation challenges.
- Concludes NaOCl (0.5–8.25%) remains the primary irrigant; no consensus on optimal concentration; EDTA 15–17% required for smear layer removal.
- States no clinical evidence that any adjunct activation method (PUI, sonic, negative-pressure, laser) improves long-term outcome of root canal treatment.
- NaOCl canal volume estimated ≤30 μL — frequent replenishment critical to maintain free available chlorine.
- Preheating NaOCl to 50–60°C drops to 37°C rapidly intracanally; limited in vivo benefit.
- Calls for redefined research priorities: clinically relevant comparisons, adequate sample sizes, long-term outcome measures.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: narrative review commissioned for the IEJ 2022 "Present Status and Future Directions" supplement.
- Scope: challenges to irrigation, ideal irrigant properties, currently used irrigants (NaOCl, EDTA, CHX, citric acid, MTAD, QMix), delivery systems (syringe/needle), activation methods (PUI, sonic, negative-pressure, laser), future directions.
- Limitations flagged in source studies: in vitro bias, non-standardised methods, insufficient sample sizes, lack of outcome-level endpoints.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- NaOCl proposed concentrations: 0.5–8.25%; laboratory data favour higher concentration for biofilm kill and tissue dissolution, but no significant clinical difference confirmed in RCTs to date (methodological bias cited).
- NaOCl pH ~11–12 (unbuffered); hypochlorite (OCl⁻) predominates; buffering to lower pH increases HOCl but reduces stability with negligible clinical benefit.
- Canal volume ≤30 μL (large canal approximated as conical frustum, apical size 60, 0.06 taper, 22 mm length) — rapid chlorine depletion mandates frequent exchange.
- EDTA 15–17% (neutral/slightly alkaline pH ~7–8): removes smear layer; weak antimicrobial; enhances NaOCl penetration into dentinal tubules.
- CHX 2%: broad-spectrum but no tissue-dissolving ability; substantivity is clinical advantage; cannot be combined with NaOCl (precipitate).
- No adjunct activation method (PUI, sonic, NPI, laser) shows improved long-term treatment outcome vs syringe + instrumentation in available RCT evidence.
- Biofilm persister cells and dormant sub-populations resist antimicrobials; EPS barrier further reduces effective concentration reaching cells.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Most evidence from in vitro/ex vivo models — clinical translation uncertain.
- Absence of standardised outcome metrics across activation studies prevents meta-analysis.
- Calls for multidisciplinary collaboration (chemistry, microbiology, fluid dynamics) to develop more effective antimicrobials and delivery systems.
- Future focus: irrigant penetration depth into biofilm, long-term radiographic/clinical outcomes, larger adequately powered RCTs.

## 7. Glossary
- PUI: passive ultrasonic irrigation. NPI: negative-pressure irrigation. EPS: extracellular polymeric substance.
- Vapor lock: air bubble entrapment at blind-ended canal terminus (result, not cause, of poor penetration).
- HOCl / OCl⁻: hypochlorous acid / hypochlorite — pH-dependent equilibrium species in NaOCl solutions.
- Smear layer: layer of organic + inorganic debris created by instrumentation; removed by chelators (EDTA/citric acid).
