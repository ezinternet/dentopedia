## One-line Summary
Concise narrative review (Quintessence Int 2016;47(4):275–278) defining dental erosion as
acid-related non-microbial tooth structure loss, classifying aetiology as extrinsic (dietary acids)
or intrinsic (endogenous acid), and identifying nutrition, saliva, systemic disease, and
mechanical stress (abrasion/attrition) as key severity-modifying parameters.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
(Narrative review — qualitative theses; no primary outcome data)

**Definition:**
Dental erosion = acid-related, non-microbial loss of tooth structure. Distinguishes from caries
(bacterial acid) and from abrasion/attrition (mechanical forces).

**Aetiology classification:**
- Extrinsic: acids from food (citric, phosphoric, carbonic, maleic, tartaric acids in beverages,
  fruits, sweets); occupational acid exposure (e.g., battery manufacturing, wine tasting);
  environmental (acid mist).
- Intrinsic: gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD); eating disorders (anorexia/bulimia nervosa)
  causing repeated vomiting; rumination.

**Severity-modifying parameters:**
1. Nutrition: frequency and duration of acid contact (dietary habits), acid type and concentration,
   titratable acidity, chelating capacity (citrate ion binds Ca²⁺).
2. Saliva: flow rate, buffering capacity (bicarbonate), calcium/phosphate concentration,
   pellicle formation speed — all protective; reduced by medications (anticholinergics,
   diuretics), Sjögren's syndrome, head-neck radiotherapy.
3. Systemic disease: GERD, eating disorders, hiatal hernia — increase intrinsic acid exposure.
4. Mechanical stress: abrasion (toothbrushing, diet) and attrition (bruxism) synergistically
   increase tissue loss by removing softened surface layer.

**Risk identification:**
- Thorough clinical examination + anamnesis (dietary history, medical/drug history, occupational).
- Pattern of erosion (palatal = intrinsic; labial/buccal = extrinsic) aids aetiological diagnosis.

## 7. Glossary
- Dental erosion: Acid-related, non-microbial irreversible loss of mineralised tooth structure.
- Extrinsic acids: Dietary, occupational, or environmental acids; most common = citric, phosphoric,
  carbonic acids in beverages.
- Intrinsic acids: Endogenous gastric HCl reaching the oral cavity (GERD, vomiting, rumination).
- Titratable acidity (TA): Total acid reservoir of a solution; determines overall demineralisation
  potential beyond instantaneous pH.
- Chelation: Citrate ion (from citric acid) binding Ca²⁺ from hydroxyapatite, amplifying
  demineralisation beyond simple H+ attack.
- Saliva buffering: Bicarbonate-mediated neutralisation of acids; impaired when flow rate is
  reduced (xerostomia, medications, radiation).
- Abrasion: Mechanical wear from external source (e.g., toothbrush); synergistic with erosion
  by removing softened enamel surface layer.
