## Why Ingested

The crown-prep geometry hub [[overviews/crown-preparation-geometry-convergence-resistance-retention]] is built largely on TOC/retention in-vitro studies; it needed the canonical narrative-review that distills the whole scientific basis into a numbered guideline set (TOC, O-C dimension, O-C:F-L ratio, auxiliary features, finish lines). This Goodacre 2001 review is the most-cited synthesis of complete-coverage prep principles and anchors the historic-to-evidence transition that [[prosthetic-materials/tiu-2015-clinical-tooth-preparations-measuring-sr]] later quantified.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (J Prosthet Dent 2001;85:363-376; 250 yr history, last 50 yr MEDLINE+manual): nine scientific principles for complete-crown preparation — 10-20° TOC, ≥4 mm occlusocervical for molars (3 mm others), occlusocervical-to-faciolingual ratio ≥0.4, preserved line angles, and auxiliary resistance (grooves/boxes) when dimensions are deficient.

## 1. Document Information

- **Type**: Narrative/critical literature review (J Prosthet Dent, Vol 85 Issue 4, April 2001, pp 363-376)
- **Authors**: Charles J. Goodacre (DDS, MSD), Wayne V. Campagni (DMD), Steven A. Aquilino (DDS, MS)
- **Scope**: 250 years of clinical practice reviewed, with emphasis on scientific data from the preceding 50 years; MEDLINE search plus extensive manual search, English-language articles
- **DOI**: 10.1067/mpr.2001.114685 (article ID S0022-3913(01)57751-3)

## 2. Key Contributions

- Distills complete-coverage tooth preparation into **nine scientific principles** ensuring mechanical, biologic, and esthetic success.
- Traces the historic evolution of crown preparations from Fauchard / porcelain-jacket-crown era through cast-gold to contemporary scientific guidelines.
- Reframes preparation as an "art form based on scientific principles" — judgment-driven, but grounded in measurable geometry.
- Establishes the now-standard numeric guidelines: **10-20° total occlusal convergence (TOC)**, **minimal occlusocervical dimension 4 mm (molars) / 3 mm (other teeth)**, **occlusocervical-to-faciolingual ratio ≥0.4**.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Literature spanning ~250 years located via MEDLINE plus an extensive manual search; emphasis on scientifically acquired data of the prior 50 years.
- English-language articles only.
- Narrative synthesis organized around mechanical resistance/retention, biologic preservation, and esthetics; not a systematic review (no PRISMA, no pooled quantitative analysis).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

The nine principles produce teeth that should exhibit:

1. **10 to 20 degrees of total occlusal convergence** (clinically realistic vs the textbook 6°).
2. **Minimal occlusocervical (axial) height** — 4 mm for molars, 3 mm for other teeth.
3. **Occlusocervical-to-faciolingual dimension ratio ≥0.4** (resistance form depends on the height relative to base width, not height alone).
4. **Preserved facioproximal and linguoproximal line angles** wherever possible (they contribute to resistance).
5. **Auxiliary resistance features (axial grooves or boxes)** — added preferentially on proximal surfaces when the above dimensional features are deficient (short or over-tapered teeth).
6. **Finish line selection** based on crown/retainer type, esthetics, ease of formation, and operator experience — claims of enhanced marginal fit with specific finish lines were **not validated** by then-current research.
7. **Finish line location** governed by esthetics and tooth conditions, with a **supragingival location more acceptable** (biologically favorable).
8. **Rounded line angles**.
9. **Reasonable surface smoothness**.

Key clinical takeaways: resistance form is a function of TOC, axial height, and base width together (the O-C:F-L ratio captures this); when geometry is inadequate, auxiliary features restore resistance rather than chasing an unachievable low taper.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative review — selection of cited evidence is not systematic; no pooled statistics; subject to author judgment.
- 2001 vintage: predates monolithic zirconia / fully digital CAD-CAM milling and intraoral-scanner workflows, so finish-line and marginal-fit conclusions reflect cast-metal and early all-ceramic eras.
- "Marginal fit unaffected by finish line type" reflects the materials and fabrication of its era; later CAD-CAM/ceramic data (e.g. finish-line configuration as a marginal-adaptation factor) refine this.

## 6. Related Work

- Tiu 2015 SR later quantifies the historic TOC drift this review describes (6° ideal → 10-22° clinical reality).
- Resistance-form in-vitro work (Roudsari 2011, Yang 2015) operationalizes principle #5 (auxiliary grooves/boxes for short molars).
- Contrepois 2013 SR addresses the marginal-adaptation question Goodacre left open for the ceramic era.

## 7. Glossary

- **TOC (Total Occlusal Convergence)**: total angle between two opposing prepared axial walls (sum of both wall tapers).
- **Occlusocervical dimension**: axial height of the preparation from finish line to occlusal/incisal surface.
- **Occlusocervical-to-faciolingual ratio**: prep height divided by its facio-lingual base width; a resistance-form descriptor (≥0.4 target).
- **Auxiliary resistance features**: grooves, boxes, or pinholes added to limit rotational displacement when basic geometry is insufficient.
- **Finish line (margin)**: the cervical termination of the preparation (chamfer, shoulder, knife-edge, etc.).
