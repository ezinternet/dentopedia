## Why Ingested

The "what modulates overeruption" table in [[wiki/overviews/unopposed-tooth-overeruption-overview]] needs a clean answer to the recurring clinical question: does *partial* occlusal contact (a tooth that still touches its antagonist on part of its surface) protect against overeruption the way full contact does? This paper directly tests that — and the answer (no) sharpens the overview's decision logic. It reinforces [[wiki/occlusion/livas-2016-fixed-retention-unopposed-molar-overeruption]] by showing that the absence of *functional* intercuspal loading, not the total loss of all contact, is what permits eruptive drift, so partial contact cannot be relied upon clinically to hold vertical position.

Note: only abstract-level detail available from the source PDF (publisher landing-page print). No full Results body, tables, or numeric effect sizes are present in the retrieved file.

## One-line Summary

Retrospective clinical study (n=91 patients with partially or completely unopposed posterior teeth): partial occlusal contact did NOT reduce the extent of overeruption versus complete lack of contact, and partially-opposed teeth tipped MORE — partial contact should not be relied on to maintain vertical tooth position.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Tests, in human patients, whether *partial* occlusal contact (as opposed to total absence of an antagonist) modulates the magnitude of posterior overeruption.
- Separates two distinct positional movements of an under-opposed tooth — vertical **overeruption** (supraeruption) and **tipping** — and analyses each against the presence/absence of partial contact.
- Provides the clinically counter-intuitive finding that partial contact does **not** protect against overeruption and is associated with **more** tipping, undermining the assumption that any remaining contact stabilises a tooth.
- Strengthens the case for active management/replacement of opposing teeth rather than relying on residual partial contact.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Sample**: 91 patients with posterior teeth that were either completely unopposed or partially opposed (some residual occlusal contact with an antagonist).
- **Groups**: unopposed (no occlusal contact) vs partially opposed (partial occlusal contact).
- **Measurements per tooth**: extent of overeruption; degree of tipping.
- **Analyses**:
  - Difference in extent of overeruption between the two groups.
  - Difference in degree of tipping between the two groups.
  - Correlation between presence of partial tooth contact and (a) extent of overeruption and (b) degree of tipping.
  - Correlation between extent of overeruption and degree of tipping.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **No significant difference** in the extent of overeruption between the unopposed and partially-opposed groups.
- Partially-opposed teeth displayed a **greater degree of tipping** than the unopposed group.
- **No significant correlation** between extent of overeruption and degree of tipping.
- **No significant correlation** between extent of overeruption and the presence of partial tooth contact.
- **Significant correlation** between degree of tooth tip and the presence of partial tooth contact.

**Authors' conclusions:**
1. Partial tooth contact does **not** appear to prevent or reduce overeruption.
2. Partially-opposed teeth show an **increased degree of tip** relative to teeth with complete lack of occlusal contact.
3. Clinically, **partial tooth contact should not be relied upon to maintain vertical tooth position.**

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Retrospective/observational design — associations, not causation; no longitudinal tracking of overeruption rate over time.
- "Partial contact" is a heterogeneous category (degree and location of residual contact vary) and was not finely quantified in the available text.
- Cross-sectional measurement cannot establish whether partial contact slows the *rate* even if final extent is similar.
- Numeric data, measurement method, and statistical detail are not available in the retrieved publisher landing-page text.

## 7. Glossary

- **Overeruption (supraeruption)**: continued vertical (occlusal-direction) eruptive movement of a tooth that has lost its antagonist, beyond the normal occlusal plane.
- **Partial occlusal contact**: a tooth that has lost full opposing contact but still occludes against an antagonist over part of its occlusal surface.
- **Tipping**: angular (rotational) displacement of a tooth about a horizontal axis, distinct from bodily vertical movement.
- **Unopposed tooth**: a posterior tooth with no occlusal antagonist.
- **Equilibrium theory (of tooth position)**: the concept that tooth position is maintained by a balance of forces (eruptive, occlusal, soft-tissue); loss of opposing occlusal force disturbs this equilibrium and permits eruptive drift.
