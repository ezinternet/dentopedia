## Why Ingested

Addresses a gap in the halitosis literature: most existing SRs focus on prevalence or etiology, but none had systematically examined the psychosocial/emotional impact specifically in adolescents and young adults. Complements [[wiki/halitosis/silva-2017-estimated-prevalence-halitosis-sr]] (prevalence focus) by adding the psychosocial dimension for younger populations.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (6 studies, adolescents/young adults aged 10–26) finding self-reported halitosis prevalence of 23.1–77.5% (mean 44.7%) and consistent associations with anxiety, depression, social isolation, and reduced self-esteem as measured by OHIP-14 and SCL-9-R instruments.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. First systematic review specifically targeting the psychosocial/emotional impact of halitosis in adolescents and young adults (age 10–26).
2. Documents self-reported halitosis prevalence range 23.1–77.5% (average 44.7%) across 6 observational studies — wide heterogeneity reflects varied measurement methods and populations.
3. Synthesizes evidence that young people with halitosis consistently report higher anxiety, depression, social isolation, and lower self-esteem using both standardized (OHIP-14, SCL-9-R) and non-standardized questionnaires.
4. Highlights the classification of halitosis (genuine / pseudo-halitosis / halitophobia) and how psychological burden persists even for pseudo-halitosis and halitophobia cases where no objective malodor is detected.
5. Calls for dual-track (medical + psychological) intervention for halitosis management in young populations.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Systematic review following PRISMA 2020 guidelines
- **Databases searched**: Scopus, Google Scholar, Web of Science, ProQuest Central (up to November 2022)
- **Search terms**: "Halitosis", "Humans", "Social rejection", "Quality of life", "Oral health", "Mouth diseases", "Socioeconomic factors", "Social behavior", "Social impact", "Social interaction" (Boolean AND/OR)
- **Inclusion criteria**:
  - Presence of halitosis (clinical exam or self-report)
  - Social/emotional impact measured (standardized or non-standardized)
  - Age 10–26 years
  - No underlying systemic disease as halitosis etiology
  - Observational study designs (cross-sectional, case-control, cohort)
- **Exclusion criteria**: Reviews, editorials, case reports; age >26; no social impact outcome
- **Screening**: Initial 593 records → 38 duplicates removed → abstract screening → 8 full-text assessed → 6 included
- **Quality assessment**: NHLBI Study Quality Assessment Tool for Observational Cohort and Cross-Sectional Studies (14-item; 0–4 low, 5–9 acceptable, 10–14 good)
- **Countries represented**: Brazil (2 studies), India (2 studies), Poland (1), USA (1) [inferred from context]

**Summary findings**:
- Self-reported halitosis prevalence: 23.1% to 77.5%, mean ~44.7%
- Significant heterogeneity across studies in measurement methodology (organoleptic test vs. questionnaire) and population
- Consistent association between halitosis (self-reported) and:
  - Increased anxiety and depressive symptoms (standardized SCL-9-R)
  - Lower oral health-related quality of life (OHIP-14)
  - Social isolation and avoidance of close-contact interactions
  - Reduced self-esteem and body image concerns
  - Social stigma and discrimination
- Halitophobia cases: psychological burden present even without objective malodor
- Incorrect tongue brushing identified as primary oral etiology; other factors include salivary flow, periodontal status, caries, orthodontic brackets, debris accumulation

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Only 6 studies met inclusion criteria — limited evidence base
- High heterogeneity in halitosis measurement (self-report vs. clinical) limits comparability
- Cross-sectional designs dominate — cannot establish causal direction between halitosis and psychosocial impact
- No meta-analysis performed due to heterogeneity
- All included studies in English; potential language bias
- Small and varied sample sizes across studies
- Limited geographic diversity (Brazil, India predominantly)
- Future needs: longitudinal studies; validated psychosocial instruments specifically designed for halitosis; interventional trials addressing both oral and mental health components

## 7. Glossary

- **Genuine halitosis**: Objectively measurable malodor (organoleptic test, portable sulfur meter)
- **Pseudo-halitosis**: Subjective complaint of bad breath not confirmed by objective measures; resolves with oral hygiene counseling
- **Halitophobia**: Persistent belief of having halitosis even after treatment; predominantly psychological etiology
- **OHIP-14**: Oral Health Impact Profile-14; validated 14-item OHRQoL questionnaire
- **SCL-9-R**: Symptom Checklist-9-Revised; standardized psychological symptom assessment (anxiety, depression subscales)
- **Organoleptic test**: Direct olfactory assessment of breath by a trained examiner; current clinical gold standard
- **PRISMA**: Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses
- **NHLBI**: National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute; publishes quality assessment tools for observational studies
