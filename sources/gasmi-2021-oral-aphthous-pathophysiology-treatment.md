## Why Ingested

Adds a pathophysiology-plus-treatment-ladder reference for aphthous ulcers, complementing [[oral-medicine/belenguer-guallar-2014-recurrent-aphthous-stomatitis-review]] and [[oral-medicine/manfredini-2021-recurrent-aphthous-stomatitis-treatment]].

## One-line Summary

Narrative review of oral aphthosis covering pathophysiology, three morphological types (minor/major/herpetiform), differential diagnosis (idiopathic vs systemic), and a treatment ladder with topical corticosteroids as first line.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames the three classic morphological types as a management-guiding classification.
- Stresses systematic history/exam to separate truly idiopathic RAS from systemic-disease-associated ulcers.
- Endorses topical corticosteroids as first-line, with systemic agents reserved for progression.
- Reviews microbial/microbiota contributions to aphthous pathogenesis.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Narrative review; PubMed/Google Scholar search on aphthous/canker-sore/microbiota/treatment keywords.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Etiology spans local trauma to systemic disease (Behçet, IBD, hematinic deficiency) — full workup advised when atypical.
- Topical corticosteroids = first-line for symptom control; systemic medication when disease progresses.
- Higher prevalence in developed countries; slight female predominance overall, female predominance in herpetiform type.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Pathophysiology remains incompletely understood; treatment recommendations are consensus/empirical rather than from pooled RCT data.

## 6. Related Work

- Historical aphthae descriptions (Hippocrates); RAS classification literature.

## 7. Glossary

- RAS: Recurrent Aphthous Stomatitis.
- Herpetiform ulcers: numerous small clustered aphthae (not HSV-caused).
