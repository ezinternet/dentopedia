## Why Ingested

Quantifies how much dental hard tissue (cementum/dentine) each professional instrumentation modality removes per stroke and per second, providing the numeric basis for selecting the least-invasive biofilm-management approach. Reinforces the narrative review [[wiki/periodontics/lodigkeit-2026-periodontal-instrumentation-enamel-cementum-review]] (same ingest batch) and supplies in-vitro material-loss evidence applicable to the instrumentation steps of [[wiki/periodontics/sanz-2020-treatment-stage-i-iii-periodontitis-efp]].

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (MEDLINE/EMBASE/Cochrane; human cementum/dentine, ≥6 specimens) finding hand curettes cause the greatest substance loss per stroke (mean 6.0 µm vs ultrasonic 4.89 µm) while air-polishing with low-abrasive powders causes the most loss per second; surface roughness results mixed and heterogeneity limits direct comparison.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Aggregates in-vitro evidence on dental hard-tissue effects (material loss + surface roughness) across the three main professional instrumentation modalities: hand instruments, ultrasonic scalers, and air-polishing devices.
- Reports comparable numeric ranges for root substance loss per stroke and per second, enabling a least-invasive ranking.
- Restricts air-polishing evidence to low-abrasive powders (glycine or erythritol) by excluding other powders and combined treatments — a clinically relevant scope for supportive periodontal/peri-implant care.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Databases searched: MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane Library.
- Inclusion: in-vitro studies on human cementum and dentine using ≥6 human tooth specimens.
- Exclusion: powders other than glycine or erythritol; combined treatments.
- Outcomes: material (substance) loss and surface roughness.
- (Number of included studies, risk-of-bias appraisal, and pooled statistics not available from abstract — full text not retrieved.)

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Root substance loss (quoted from abstract):
- Ultrasonic scalers: 0.28–14.73 µm per stroke (mean 4.89); 0.33–8.20 µm per second (mean 2.24).
- Hand instruments: 0.86–20.60 µm per stroke (mean 6.0).
- Air polishing (low-abrasive powders): 0.12–23.40 µm (mean 8.65).

Comparative findings:
- Most studies reported higher substance loss with hand curettes than ultrasonic scalers.
- Substance loss **per second** highest with air-polishing devices vs ultrasonic.
- Substance loss **per stroke** greatest with hand instruments vs ultrasonic scalers.
- Surface roughness: mixed — air polishing did not increase roughness in one study; others reported both increases and decreases.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- High heterogeneity across studies limits direct comparison between modalities (stated in conclusion).
- In-vitro design only; does not capture in-vivo biofilm removal efficacy, operator technique, or clinical endpoints.
- Abstract-only: included-study count, quality appraisal, and full methodology not retrievable here.

## 7. Glossary

- Substance loss (material loss): thickness of dental hard tissue (cementum/dentine) removed by an instrument, here reported in µm per stroke or per second.
- Surface roughness: micro-texture of the instrumented surface; relevant to plaque re-accumulation.
- Air polishing: powder-water-air spray for biofilm removal; low-abrasive powders are glycine or erythritol.
- Ultrasonic scaler: powered instrument using high-frequency tip oscillation for debridement.
- Hand instrument / curette: manual scaling/root-planing instrument.
