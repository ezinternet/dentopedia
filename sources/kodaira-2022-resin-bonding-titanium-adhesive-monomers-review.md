## Why Ingested

Extends the MDP chemical-bonding story from tooth/zirconia substrates to **titanium**. The sibling page [[wiki/resin-bonding/nagaoka-2017-mdp-zirconia-chemical-interaction-nmr]] establishes (by NMR) that 10-MDP forms a stable phosphate–zirconium chemical bond; this 2022 review surveys the parallel evidence that the same family of functional monomers (MDP, MEPS, 6-MHPA, 4-META) chemically bonds resin luting agents to titanium/titanium alloy — closing the substrate gap and reinforcing the "functional-monomer surface treatment" theme across the resin-bonding category. Timely because cast titanium restorations were newly added to Japan's national health insurance (NHI) system as palladium/gold prices rose, expanding clinical demand for reliable resin-to-titanium luting.

## One-line Summary

Narrative literature review of 11 studies concluding that adhesive functional monomers (MDP, MEPS, 6-MHPA, 4-META) in primers/luting agents bond resin to titanium and titanium alloy as effectively as silanization plus tribochemical silica coating, with MDP-based and 4-META/MMA-TBB systems showing the highest bonding durability across thermocycling aging tests.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Systematic narrative survey of 11 studies (selected by PubMed search + snowballing, August 2020 – March 2021) examining the role of the **adhesive (functional) monomer** in primers and luting agents for resin bonding to titanium and titanium alloys.
- Concludes that adhesive monomers are **effective for bonding resin to titanium**, achieving results **equal to silanization + tribochemical silica coating** — a simpler, equipment-free alternative.
- Identifies the four most effective monomer chemistries: **MDP** (phosphate), **MEPS** (thiophosphate), **6-MHPA** (phosphonoacetate), and **4-META** (carboxylate anhydride).
- Ranks primer and luting-agent performance by thermocycling durability across 11 study-comparisons summarized in four tables (primers on market, surface modification systems, luting agents on market, and effective primer/luting agent data).
- Clinical driver: inclusion of cast titanium restorations in the Japanese national health insurance (NHI) system due to rising palladium/gold prices, creating demand for proven resin-bonding protocols to titanium-based fixed prostheses.
- Demonstrates that **MDP-containing primers** (10 wt% preferred over lower concentrations) are more effective than 4-META-based primers at low concentrations, and that **4-META/MMA-TBB resin** (Super-Bond C&B) shows higher bonding durability than composite-based luting agents without adhesive monomers.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Study design**: Narrative literature review / scoping survey. Not a systematic review or meta-analysis; no pooled statistics, risk-of-bias appraisal, or GRADE evidence grading.
- **Search strategy**: PubMed search, August 2020 – March 2021. Keywords: *titanium AND (adhesive OR bond) AND (primer OR monomer) AND dental*. Additionally, "similar articles" in PubMed used for snowballing. First screening on title/abstract to exclude veneering and denture-repair material studies; second screening to retain articles focused on adhesive monomers → **11 studies selected**.
- **Outcome measure**: Shear or tensile bond strength after in vitro thermocycling aging (most commonly 10,000–20,000 cycles), used to rank primer and luting agent performance.
- **Substrates covered**: Commercially pure titanium (CP Ti, Grade II/III), Ti-6Al-7Nb alloy (T-Alloy Tough), Ti-Ni alloy, high-purity titanium, uncast Ti-6Al-7Nb.
- **Summary tables**:
  - Table 1: 22 commercial primers and their active monomers (MDP, MEPS, 4-META, 4-AET, MAC-10, VTD, MTU-6, BPDM, etc.)
  - Table 2: Surface modification systems (alumina airborne-particle abrasion 50–110 µm; Rocatec tribochemical silica coating)
  - Table 3: Commercial luting agents (RelyX Unicem [MDP-based], Panavia series [MDP], Super-Bond C&B [4-META], Bistite II DC [MAC-10], etc.)
  - Table 4: Study-by-study summary of effective primers and luting agents, sorted by thermocycling duration

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Primer performance (ranked by thermocycling durability)

- **MDP-containing primers** (Alloy Primer/Kuraray, Cesead II Opaque Primer, Estenia Opaque Primer) consistently showed significantly higher bond strength vs. unprimed controls, across CP Ti, Ti-6Al-7Nb, and CP Ti-Ni alloys (Taira [19,20,23], Matsumura [26], Tsuchimoto [31], Koizumi [33], Yanagida [49]).
- **10 wt% MDP-based primer** more effective than 1 wt% or 0.1 wt% MDP-based primer, and more effective than 4-META-based primers at all concentrations tested (Tsuchimoto [31]).
- **MEPS-containing primers** (Metal Primer, Metal Primer II/GC): effective for bonding durability (Taira [20]).
- **4-META** (Super-Bond Liquid): effective, especially with MMA-TBB resin luting agents.
- **VTD** (sulfur-containing monomer): NOT effective for titanium, unlike noble metals. Sulfur compounds effective for gold/noble alloys are not effective for titanium (Koizumi [37]).
- **MAC-10** and **4-AET** and **BPDM**: inferior to MDP/4-META/MEPS for both CP Ti and Ti-6Al-7Nb (Koizumi [43]).
- **MDP primer vs. tribochemical silica coating (Rocatec) + silane**: MDP-containing primer (Alloy Primer) showed **better or equivalent** bonding durability vs. Rocatec + silane (Yanagida [49]).

### Luting agent performance

- **4-META/MMA-TBB resin** (Super-Bond C&B, bulk-mix technique): highest bonding durability among luting agents, outperforming PMMA-based luting agents with MAC-10 and composite-based luting agents without adhesive monomers (Taira [19,23], Koizumi [33], Yanagida [49]).
- **4-META/MMA-TBB brush technique** < bulk-mix technique (Tsuchimoto [31]).
- **MDP-containing luting agents** (Panavia F 2.0, Panavia Fluoro Cement): significantly higher bonding durability than composite-based luting agents without adhesive monomers (Di Francescantonio [42]).
- **Luting agents without adhesive monomers** (RelyX ARC, plain composite): low bonding durability to titanium without priming.

### Surface pre-treatment findings

- **Alumina blasting (50–70 µm)**: widely used; promotes mechanical interlocking by roughening, increases surface area, removes contamination (saliva, oil). Excessive heat oxidation may reduce bonding — moderate oxidation layer is acceptable but thick layers are detrimental (Taira [21]).
- **Hydrochloric acid decontamination** (1 N): superior to 0.1 N HCl and 37 wt% H₃PO₄. Phosphoric acid inhibits adsorption of functional monomers onto titanium surface (Tsuchimoto [32]).
- **Tribochemical silica coating** (Rocatec): enables silanization, comparable to adhesive monomers — but requires specialized equipment (Rocatec system).

### Bonding mechanism

- Titanium's corrosion resistance is conferred by a passive oxide film (TiO₂). Resin bonding to titanium is therefore bonding to this TiO₂ film.
- Functional monomers with **carboxyl groups or phosphate groups** (4-META, MDP, MEPS, 6-MHPA) chemically interact with the TiO₂ film — likely via hydrogen bonding / ionic interactions (Kern and Thompson [55]). Precise molecular-level mechanism not yet confirmed by spectroscopic studies.
- Phosphate, thiophosphate, phosphonate, and aromatic carboxylate monomers are effective for base metals and metal oxides generally.
- **Bonding is tri-modal**: (1) mechanical interlocking from surface roughness; (2) chemical interaction via adhesive monomer; (3) combination of surface modification + adhesive monomer.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Narrative (non-systematic) design**: no formal study selection criteria beyond two-step screening; no risk-of-bias tool, evidence grading, or pooled analysis — limiting comparability across the 11 selected studies.
- All 11 studies are **in vitro** (laboratory); no in vivo clinical data on resin-to-titanium luting durability or prosthesis survival/failure rates.
- **Japan-centric** scope and clinical motivation (NHI, Japanese alloy grades); applicability to Ti-6Al-4V (the more commonly used dental implant and CAD/CAM alloy globally) or to materials systems outside Japan may differ.
- Bonding mechanism at the molecular/spectroscopic level remains unconfirmed — only intermolecular force (hydrogen bonding) is hypothesized.
- Clinical longevity, loading conditions, cementation gap, and in vivo contamination are not addressed.
- VTD ineffectiveness for titanium vs. effectiveness for noble metals suggests substrate-specificity that requires further mechanistic clarification.
- Short paper (4 pages, 4 tables) constrains depth; Table 4 data are qualitative rankings rather than quantitative comparisons with error bounds.

## 7. Glossary

- **MDP** — 10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate; phosphate functional monomer forming stable chemical bonds to metal oxides (TiO₂, ZrO₂).
- **MEPS** — methacryloyloxyalkyl thiophosphate derivative; thiophosphate functional monomer (commercial: Metal Primer/GC).
- **6-MHPA** — 6-methacryloxyhexyl phosphonoacetate; phosphonoacetate functional monomer.
- **4-META** — 4-methacryloyloxyethyl trimellitate anhydride; carboxylate (anhydride) functional monomer; most commonly used in 4-META/MMA-TBB resins.
- **MMA-TBB** — methyl methacrylate / tri-n-butyl borane; self-cure initiator system used in 4-META-based adhesive resins (e.g., Super-Bond C&B).
- **MAC-10** — 11-methacryloyloxyundecan-1,1-dicarboxylic acid; dicarboxylic acid monomer; effective for some metals but inferior to MDP/4-META for titanium.
- **VTD** — 6-(4-vinylbenzyl-n-propyl)amino-1,3,5-triazine-2,4-dithiol-dithione tautomer; sulfur-containing monomer effective for noble metals but NOT for titanium.
- **4-AET** — 4-acryloyloxyethyl trimellitate; carboxylate monomer; less effective than MDP/4-META for titanium.
- **BPDM** — adduct of 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate and 3,4,4',5'-biphenyl tetracarboxylic anhydride; less effective for titanium.
- **MTU-6** — 6-methacryloyloxyhexyl-2-thiouracil-5-carboxylate; sulfur-containing monomer.
- **Tribochemical silica coating** — air-abrasion with silica-modified alumina (Rocatec system) to deposit a silica layer enabling silane coupling on non-silica substrates such as titanium.
- **Silanization** — application of a silane coupling agent (γ-MPTS) to silica-coated titanium to bridge the silica surface and methacrylate resin.
- **NHI (Japanese national health insurance)** — public reimbursement system; inclusion of cast titanium restorations drove the clinical interest motivating this review.
- **CP Ti** — commercially pure titanium (Grades II/III used in dental casting).
- **Ti-6Al-7Nb** — titanium alloy with 6% aluminium, 7% niobium; used in dental casting (T-Alloy Tough/GC).
- **Passive oxide film** — TiO₂ surface layer responsible for titanium's corrosion resistance; the actual substrate to which adhesive monomers must bond.
