## Why Ingested

Extends our premolar C-shaped/morphology coverage with a pooled prevalence anchor. [[wiki/endodontics/anatomy/pertek-hatipoglu-2025-radicular-grooves-cshaped-taurodontism-premolars]] characterizes C-shaped/groove morphology and taurodontism in premolars at the descriptive level; this SR+MA supplies the missing population-level *how-common-is-it* number (pooled C-shaped canal prevalence in mandibular second premolars ≈1%) plus the geographic-variation signal, turning the morphology page into a prevalence-aware sibling.

## One-line Summary

SR+MA (17 studies, 7,047 participants, 12,523 teeth; random-effects) of C-shaped canal prevalence in adult mandibular second premolars via CBCT: pooled 1.31% participant-level (95% CI 0.68–2.53) and 0.96% tooth-level (95% CI 0.46–2.00), high heterogeneity (I²=90–93%); highest in South America (5.83%), lowest in Australia (0.42%).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides the first pooled, population-level prevalence estimate for C-shaped canal anatomy specifically in the **mandibular second premolar** (a tooth less classically associated with C-shaped canals than the mandibular second molar).
- Reports two estimands side-by-side — **participant-level (1.31%)** and **tooth-level (0.96%)** — clarifying denominator dependence and calling for standardised reporting across studies.
- Quantifies **geographic variation** across four continental subgroups, identifying South America as highest and Australia as lowest, supporting region-specific diagnostic vigilance.
- Demonstrates that **publication bias** (Egger's test P=0.0002 participant-level; P=0.0052 tooth-level) is present, urging caution in interpretation.
- Includes 17 CBCT-based studies (2012–2024) with CASP scores all ≥9.5/11, indicating low risk of bias within included studies.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Protocol**: PRISMA-guided SR+MA; PROSPERO CRD42024552310.
- **Databases** (5): PubMed, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, Scopus, Google Scholar. Search period: May 25–July 10, 2024.
- **Eligibility**: cross-sectional, prospective, or observational studies on adult mandibular second premolars using CBCT to diagnose C-shaped canal prevalence; English-language only. Case reports, letters, reviews, extracted-tooth studies (non-CBCT method), and studies combining data with other canal morphologies without distinction were excluded.
- **Study selection**: 156 records retrieved → 123 after deduplication → 109 full-texts assessed → **17 studies** included.
- **Data extracted**: author/year, country, continent, CBCT model, voxel size, FOV dimensions, sample size (people + teeth), C-shaped canal prevalence rate.
- **Risk of bias**: CASP checklist for cross-sectional studies (max 11 points); all included studies scored ≥9.5.
- **Statistical model**: random-effects model (RStudio) for pooled prevalence and 95% CIs.
- **Heterogeneity**: I²-statistic; sensitivity analysis (sequential leave-one-out).
- **Meta-regression**: publication year tested as covariate.
- **Subgroup analysis**: continent (Asia, Europe, South America, Australia).
- **Publication bias**: drapery plot + Egger's test.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Total sample**: 17 studies; 7,047 participants (range 100–1,000 per study; one study unreported); 12,523 teeth (range 100–2,000).

**Participant-level meta-analysis (16 studies)**:
- Pooled prevalence: **1.31%** (95% CI 0.68–2.53; I²=90%; P<0.01)
- Sensitivity: removing any single study did not collapse the heterogeneity; most influential study: Srivastava (2019), Saudi Arabia
- Meta-regression (year of publication): P=0.90 — not significant

**Tooth-level meta-analysis (17 studies)**:
- Pooled prevalence: **0.96%** (95% CI 0.46–2.00; I²=93%; P<0.01)
- Sensitivity: most influential study: Brea (2021), Venezuela
- Meta-regression (year of publication): P=0.92 — not significant

**Notable individual studies** (from Table 1):
- Chen et al., 2018 (Taiwan): 20 C-shaped in 317 participants / 580 teeth — comparatively high subgroup
- Brea et al., 2021 (Venezuela): 22 C-shaped in 292 participants / 132 teeth — most influential in tooth-level analysis
- Rae and Parashos, 2024 (Australia): 6 C-shaped in 880 participants / 1,424 teeth — lowest individual rate
- Most commonly used CBCT model: Morita Japan (5 studies); voxel sizes 20–300 µm; various FOV dimensions

**Publication bias**: Egger's test P=0.0002 (participant-level) and P=0.0052 (tooth-level) — significant asymmetry detected.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **High heterogeneity** (I²=90–95%) is a major constraint; pooled point estimates should be interpreted cautiously.
- **Publication bias** confirmed by Egger's test; likely driven by small studies with extreme positive results.
- **CBCT protocol variability**: different CBCT models, voxel sizes (20–300 µm), and FOV dimensions across studies affect resolution and detection rates. Larger voxels underestimate fine canal anatomy.
- **Ex vivo vs CBCT**: micro-CT (ex vivo) likely detects more C-shaped configurations than CBCT (in vivo), contributing to differences from older literature.
- **Gender and age effects**: existing data on age/gender and C-shaped prevalence in premolars remain ambiguous; future studies should address these.
- **South America subgroup**: CI of 0.57–40.04 is near-uninformative (few/small studies) — more South American data urgently needed.
- **English-language restriction**: may have excluded relevant non-English studies.
- Standardised reporting (participant vs tooth denominator, CBCT settings) is needed for future comparability.

## 7. Glossary

- **C-shaped canal**: root canal configuration where individual canals are connected by a fin/web, forming a C-shape in cross-section; presents 180°-arc orifice; complicates instrumentation, irrigation, and obturation.
- **CBCT (Cone-Beam Computed Tomography)**: non-destructive 3D imaging used in vivo; preferred diagnostic modality in this review; sensitivity influenced by voxel size and FOV.
- **Pooled prevalence**: meta-analytic combined estimate of how common a finding is across studies.
- **Random-effects model**: meta-analysis model assuming true effect varies across studies; appropriate under high heterogeneity (I²>50%).
- **I² (heterogeneity)**: percentage of total variability due to between-study differences; >75% is considered high.
- **Participant-level vs tooth-level**: prevalence denominator is either people examined or individual teeth examined; same dataset yields different rates (bilateral occurrence inflates tooth-level vs participant-level discrepancy).
- **Egger's test**: statistical test for publication-bias asymmetry in meta-analyses; P<0.05 indicates significant asymmetry.
- **CASP checklist**: Critical Appraisal Skills Programme checklist for assessing methodological quality of cross-sectional studies (11 questions, max 11 points).
- **PROSPERO**: International prospective register of systematic reviews; pre-registration reduces outcome reporting bias.
