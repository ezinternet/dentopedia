## Why Ingested

The wiki's oral frailty cluster (구강노쇠, Oral Frailty) documents oral manifestations of physical decline but lacked an anchor establishing why grip strength matters as a systemic frailty biomarker. This large UK Biobank prospective cohort (n=502,293) quantifies the dose–response relationship between 악력 (grip strength, GS) and cardiovascular, respiratory, cancer, and all-cause mortality — the general-medicine evidence base that underpins clinical use of grip strength as a physical frailty (신체노쇠, Physical Frailty) screening tool. Without this anchor, the [[wiki/geriatric-dentistry/li-2024-prevalence-oral-frailty-older-adults]] oral frailty prevalence study and the [[wiki/overviews/oral-frailty-geriatric-synthesis]] synthesis lack the mortality-outcome evidence to justify grip-strength measurement in dental settings. Also reinforces the sibling companion study [[wiki/geriatric-dentistry/leong-2015-prognostic-value-grip-strength-pure]] (PURE study, n=139,691) by extending its findings to a far larger, UK-based cohort with a broader outcome set including cancer sub-types.

## One-line Summary

Prospective cohort study (n=502,293 UK Biobank participants, mean 7.1 years follow-up) found that each 5 kg lower grip strength was independently associated with 16–20% higher all-cause mortality, 19–24% higher cardiovascular mortality, 24–31% higher respiratory mortality, and 10–17% higher cancer mortality, and adding grip strength to an office-based risk score improved cardiovascular mortality prediction (ΔC=0.012).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Largest prospective cohort (n=502,293) to date examining grip strength across cardiovascular, respiratory, and cancer outcomes simultaneously
- Each 5 kg lower grip strength independently predicted higher hazard for all-cause mortality, CVD, respiratory disease, COPD, and most cancer subtypes in both men and women
- Muscle weakness thresholds (≤26 kg men; ≤16 kg women) identified as clinically useful risk strata
- Grip strength addition to an established office-based risk score (age/sex/BMI/SBP/diabetes/smoking) meaningfully improved cardiovascular mortality prediction (ΔC=0.012) and all-cause mortality (ΔC=0.013)
- Hazard ratios were generally higher in younger age groups, suggesting grip strength reflects biological rather than purely chronological aging

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Prospective population-based cohort (UK Biobank)
- **Participants**: 502,293 (54% women), aged 40–69 years at recruitment (April 2007–December 2010), 22 UK assessment centres
- **Exposure**: Mean grip strength (right + left, kg) measured with a Jamar dynamometer; analyzed per 5 kg decrement (continuous) and as age/sex-specific quarters; muscle weakness defined as <26 kg (men) / <16 kg (women)
- **Outcomes (primary)**: All-cause mortality; incidence of and mortality from cardiovascular disease (CVD), all respiratory disease, COPD, all cancer, breast/prostate/colorectal/lung cancer
- **Follow-up**: Mean 7.1 years (range 5.3–9.9) for mortality; 6.1 years (4.4–9.0) for incidence
- **Analysis**: Cox proportional hazards models, 4 adjustment levels (sociodemographics → height → prevalent morbidity → lifestyle); landmark analysis excluding events within 2 years (reverse causality check); Harrell's C index for risk prediction; grip×age interaction terms; internal train/validation split (~50/50 by sex and age)
- **Confounders controlled**: Age, sex, ethnicity, Townsend deprivation index, height, BMI, smoking, physical activity (IPAQ-SF), sedentary time, dietary intake (Oxford WebQ), prevalent diabetes/hypertension/depression/CVD/respiratory/cancer/longstanding illness

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### All-cause mortality (per 5 kg lower grip strength, fully adjusted + landmark analysis)

| Outcome | Women HR (95% CI) | Men HR (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|
| All-cause mortality | 1.20 (1.17–1.23) | 1.16 (1.15–1.17) |
| CVD mortality | 1.19 (1.13–1.25) | 1.22 (1.18–1.26) |
| All respiratory mortality | 1.31 (1.22–1.40) | 1.24 (1.20–1.28) |
| COPD mortality | 1.24 (1.05–1.47) | 1.19 (1.09–1.30) |
| All cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.13–1.21) | 1.10 (1.07–1.13) |
| Colorectal cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.04–1.32) | 1.18 (1.09–1.27) |
| Lung cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.07–1.27) | 1.08 (1.03–1.13) |
| Breast cancer mortality | 1.24 (1.10–1.39) | — |
| Prostate cancer mortality | — | 1.05 (0.96–1.15) NS |

- Associations survived landmark analysis (events within 2 years excluded), ruling out major reverse causality
- Significant grip×age interactions: hazard ratios were larger in younger participants (≤55 years) for most outcomes
- Muscle weakness (≤26 kg / ≤16 kg) increased risk for nearly all outcomes except prostate cancer and some lung/colorectal cancers

### Risk prediction improvement (C-index change when grip strength added to office-based risk score)

| Outcome | ΔC-index (derivation) | ΔC-index (validation) |
|---|---|---|
| All-cause mortality | +0.013 | similar |
| CVD mortality | +0.012 | similar |
| Incident CVD | +0.009 | similar |

- 13,322 of 502,293 participants (2.7%) died during follow-up

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- UK Biobank response rate ~5.5% → healthy volunteer bias; generalizability outside UK is uncertain
- Single baseline grip strength measurement; longitudinal decline trajectories not captured
- Physical activity was self-reported (IPAQ-SF), subject to recall bias
- Ethnicity predominantly White (95%); limited minority data
- Dietary data available for ~211,064 subset only; not all participants had full dietary confounding data
- Incident cancer ascertained from cancer registry — possible under-registration
- Future work: randomized trials to test whether exercise-based grip improvement reduces mortality; grip strength integrated into primary care risk screening

## 7. Glossary

- **Grip strength (악력, GS)**: Maximum isometric contraction force of the hand/forearm muscles, measured in kg with a dynamometer; validated proxy for overall muscle function and physical frailty
- **Muscle weakness**: Clinically defined as grip strength <26 kg (men) or <16 kg (women) per FNIH Sarcopenia Project criteria
- **UK Biobank**: Population-based prospective resource of 500,000+ UK participants with biological samples, physical measures, and linked health records
- **HR (위험비, Hazard Ratio)**: Instantaneous risk ratio from Cox model; HR >1 = higher hazard in lower grip group
- **C index (Harrell's C-statistic)**: Concordance statistic for time-to-event models; higher = better discrimination
- **FNIH**: Foundation for the National Institutes of Health (Sarcopenia Project — established muscle weakness cut-offs)
- **COPD (만성폐쇄성폐질환, Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)**: Progressive obstructive airflow limitation, ICD-10 J44
- **ICD-10**: International Classification of Diseases, 10th revision; used to define outcomes from hospital records/death certificates
- **Reverse causality**: The risk that underlying disease causes low grip strength, not vice versa; addressed here by landmark analysis excluding early events
