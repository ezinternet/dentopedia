## Why Ingested

Clarifies which professional debridement modality is least abrasive to enamel and cementum — directly informs the least-abrasive biofilm-removal choice in supportive periodontal therapy (air polishing > ultrasonic > hand). Reinforces the in-vitro hard-tissue-loss evidence in [[wiki/periodontics/dvorska-2026-mechanical-instrumentation-hard-tissue-sr]] (same ingest batch) and adds biofilm/maintenance framing complementing [[wiki/periodontics/cyris-2024-guided-biofilm-therapy-versus-conventional]] and [[wiki/periodontics/manresa-2018-supportive-periodontal-therapy-cochrane-sr]].

## One-line Summary

Narrative review of 16 in vitro / in vitro-in vivo studies (last 15 yr): manual instrumentation causes the greatest surface irregularity and cementum removal, ultrasonic scaling produces more uniform surfaces with intermediate loss, and air polishing is the least abrasive with highest cementum preservation — but clinical relevance is uncertain (lab-based evidence).

## 2. Key Contributions

- First narrative synthesis focused specifically on enamel AND cementum surface alterations (roughness + hard-tissue loss) following periodontal instrumentation; 16 in vitro / in vitro-in vivo hybrid studies on human teeth, last 15 years.
- Establishes a relative abrasiveness ranking for cementum loss: **hand instrumentation > ultrasonic > air polishing** (air polishing most tissue-preserving).
- Surface-topography nuance: ultrasonic produces more *uniform* (lower Rz) surfaces but this can reflect flattening of natural peaks / cementum removal, not necessarily a "better" outcome; a lower roughness value ≠ better.
- Reframes root planing away from deliberate cementum removal toward cementum preservation (biologically active cementum supports reattachment/regeneration).
- Emphasizes operator-dependence (force, angulation, duration, tip wear, power setting) and equal calculus-removal efficacy between hand and ultrasonic despite differing surface effects.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Focused narrative review (not systematic; no formal risk-of-bias assessment, no meta-analysis due to heterogeneity).
- PubMed search, iterative Boolean combinations across three keyword groups (instrumentation method × substrate × surface alteration); English-only; 15-year window; final search January 2026.
- Inclusion: preclinical lab studies on enamel/cementum — in vitro on extracted human teeth + in vitro/in vivo hybrid (clinical instrumentation then lab analysis). Excluded animal teeth, artificial models, orthodontic/restorative/implant procedures.
- Outcomes grouped by substrate (enamel/cementum), instrumentation type (hand, ultrasonic, air polishing, rotary, polishing), and outcome (roughness, morphology, hard-tissue loss). Measurement methods: SEM, AFM, profilometry.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Cementum loss (Arefnia et al.):** air polishing ≤20 µm < ultrasonic ≥20 µm < hand instrumentation ≥40 µm. Enamel: air polishing no significant loss; ultrasonic and hand <3.0 µm.

**Cementum preservation in vivo (Bozbay et al. 2018):** coronal cementum retained — air polishing 94% > ultrasonic 84% > ultrasonic + air polishing 80% > hand instrumentation 65%.

**Enamel roughness (Arefnia, Rz vs untreated control Rz 3.0-6.0 µm):** air polishing 6.22 µm, manual 5.04 µm, ultrasonic 3.44 µm — none statistically significant vs control when used alone.

**Cementum roughness (Rz):** air polishing 6.64 µm ≈ untreated 7.48 µm; hand 3.82 µm; ultrasonic 2.36 µm (lower values here reflect tissue removal/peak flattening, not preservation).

- Enamel: stainless-steel ultrasonic tips = fine scratches/minimal roughness; titanium tips = deeper defects; titanium hand curettes = most damage. Ultrasonic morphology more destructive (prism-bundle destruction) than manual.
- Ultrasonic power: medium = lowest roughness; high power ≈ hand instrumentation.
- Piezoelectric vs magnetostrictive: both smoother than curettes; magnetostrictive least dentin exposure; piezoelectric slightly more cracks.
- Worn tips → more roughness, less calculus-removal efficiency. Rotary instruments most damaging.
- Air polishing alone cannot remove calculus → combine with ultrasonic for both preservation and calculus removal. Air polishing primarily for supportive/maintenance therapy, not standalone root planing.
- Hand vs ultrasonic: comparable calculus removal; ultrasonic needs less working time and less operator fatigue; hand offers better tactile sense.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Predominantly in vitro on extracted (caries-free) teeth; lab settings do not replicate the oral biological environment — clinical relevance uncertain.
- High heterogeneity in parameters, technique, measurement methods (SEM/AFM/profilometry at different scales) and roughness metrics (Ra ≠ Rz, not directly comparable).
- "Smooth surface" inconsistently defined; lower roughness may reflect cementum removal rather than preserved integrity.
- Narrative design, no risk-of-bias assessment; possible selection and language (English-only) bias.
- Future: standardized methodology and clinically oriented designs to assess long-term outcomes.

## 7. Glossary

- **Periodontal instrumentation**: mechanical removal of biofilm/calculus by hand scaling, ultrasonic debridement, or air-abrasive (air polishing).
- **Cementum**: thin mineralized tissue covering the root; softer than enamel, regulates fibroblast behaviour and connective-tissue reattachment.
- **Root planing**: traditionally deliberate cementum removal to eliminate endotoxin; modern view favours preservation of biologically active cementum.
- **Air polishing**: air-abrasive powder spray (e.g., glycine/erythritol/7 µm) for biofilm/stain removal; least abrasive, but cannot remove calculus.
- **Ra / Rz**: roughness parameters — Ra = mean deviation; Rz = average peak-to-valley depth (typically higher than Ra for the same surface).
- **SEM / AFM / profilometry**: surface characterization at differing spatial scales/resolutions.
