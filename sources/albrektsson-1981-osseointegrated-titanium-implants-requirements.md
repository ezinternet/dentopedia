## Why Ingested

This is the foundational paper that first formalized the **six factors governing osseointegration** (implant material, design, surface finish, status of the bone, surgical technique, loading conditions) — the conceptual bedrock under every modern stability, surface-modification, and loading-protocol page in the wiki. It anchors and gives historical provenance to [[implants/surface/abu-alfaraj-2023-osseointegration-strategies-implantology-review]] and [[implants/surface/smeets-2016-implant-surface-modifications-osseointegration]], which review how implant-surface technology has advanced from the empirical "somewhat rougher" finishes Albrektsson described to today's SLA/anodized/bioactive surfaces.

## One-line Summary

Landmark clinical/ultrastructural study (n=38 stable titanium screws removed from 18 patients; ~3000 implants placed since 1965, 400-patient base) that defined osseointegration as direct light- and electron-microscopic bone-to-implant contact and codified six prerequisites for achieving it: implant material, design, finish, bone status, surgical technique, and loading conditions.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Defines osseointegration**: "a direct contact between living bone and implant" — at the light-microscopic level (and now demonstrated at the electron-microscopic level), with no interposed connective/fibrous tissue layer.
2. **First TEM of an intact bone-implant interface in man** (group I implant removed after 30 months in situ, sectioned without separating bone from implant), revealing direct bone-to-titanium contact at the electron-microscopic level and suggesting possible direct chemical bonding between bone and titanium oxide.
3. **Codifies the six prerequisites for osseointegration** (the paper's enduring legacy — see §4).
4. **Establishes titanium's privileged status**: its instantaneously-formed stable oxide layer (~100 Å, a mix of TiO, TiO₂, Ti₂O₃, Ti₃O₄) means no bare metal contacts tissue, so titanium "may be regarded as a ceramic, not as a metal" — explaining its osseointegration capacity and corrosion resistance.
5. **Interface typology (Figure 1)**: (a) osseointegration — direct living bone contact, the only anchorage whose long-term fate is predictable and the only one tolerating heavy load; (b) interposed soft-tissue layer — leads to implant loss over time; (c) dead bone in partial contact — may function for years but limited load capacity and risk of resorption on revascularization; (d) bone-cement interface — always causes bone death at the cement border, replaced by connective tissue.
6. **Biological seal**: soft tissues adhere closely to the implant, preventing microorganism infiltration along the implant; transgingival/transcutaneous penetration caused no adverse tissue effects (loading times up to 90 months).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Clinical base**: ~3000 threaded cylindrical titanium implants placed at the Gothenburg clinic since 1965 (2895 in mandible/maxilla; 124 in tibial, temporal, or iliac bone for various bone-restorative procedures). Requirements section draws on experience from 400 patients followed up to 15 years.
- **Retrieved material (Table I)**: 38 stable, radiographically integrated, surgeon-judged-stable screws removed from 18 patients, in three groups:
  - **Group I** — 10 fixtures from edentulous jaws (3 patients; 30/42/90 months in situ; removed for psychiatric, implant-fracture, surgical reasons).
  - **Group II** — 22 tibial-metaphysis screws (11 patients, ~5–6 months, removed at predetermined interval after stabilizing a titanium mould for a preformed ossicular bone graft) + 2 iliac-crest screws.
  - **Group III** — temporal-bone screws (anchorage for bone-anchored external hearing aid); 2 removed (12–18 months) for problems not related to bone integration.
- **Surgical technique (Figure 3)**: graded series of drills → counter-sink → tap, all under generous saline irrigation; cover screw; defined unloaded healing period of several months; then abutment connection.
- **Retrieval**: implants cut out with a trephine under saline irrigation, leaving an intact bone cover that could not be removed without sharp instruments.
- **Interface analysis**: X-ray, SEM, TEM, and histology of the bone-implant interface zone; EDAX used to verify titanium deposits.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**The six prerequisites for osseointegration** (verbatim list: implant material; implant design; implant finish; status of the bone; surgical technique; implant loading conditions):

1. **Implant material** — tissue-tolerant, load-bearing, highly corrosion-resistant. Non-alloyed (unalloyed) titanium preferred; "the only metal shown to establish and maintain a direct bone-to-implant contact in man for periods of more than 10 years." Titanium had the least corrosive "Anodic Back e.m.f." (>5× vitallium, >7× stainless steel). Caveat: high titanium-on-titanium friction makes it poor for gliding joint surfaces (galling, wear).
2. **Implant design** — exact bone-implant fit matters; osseointegration more easily achieved with **cylindrical, threaded** implants placed for maximal bone contact. Screw design minimizes early micromovement and increases interaction surface area (a variant of the surface-porous concept).
3. **Implant finish** — surface effect hard to evaluate; very smooth surfaces avoided in favor of "somewhat rougher" finishes (empirical only). Future surface-bonding ideas mentioned: anodization, collagenization, bioglass coating.
4. **Status of the bone** — healthy bone essential; cortical vs. cancellous bed matters less because spongy bone tends to "corticalize" around metal implants. Severe resorption (edentulous jaw) may need a (preformed) bone graft.
5. **Surgical technique** — "minimal tissue violence" is vital: constant cooling, adequate drill geometry and speed, careful tapping — to limit thermal/surgical trauma.
6. **Implant loading conditions** — osseointegration occurs **only in perfectly stable situations**. A necrotic border zone inevitably forms from surgical trauma and must remodel before loading. Defined unloaded healing: ~6 weeks in rabbits, **3–4 months in man**. Immediate loading and rigid coupling to natural teeth (whose periodontal membrane permits micromovement) are major causes of the fibrous sheath that prevents osseointegration; minor movements inhibit osteogenesis.

**Outcome benchmarks cited** (from Adell et al. 1981, 400 patients / 2775 implants): 5-year survival of functioning jaw bridges ≈ 100% (lower jaw) and 95% (upper jaw); individual-fixture survival 91% (lower) and 82% (upper). Across the complete material, loss of anchorage over 5 years < 10%. No titanium wear products detected despite implantation times of 6–90 months; no microorganism infiltration; biological soft-tissue seal confirmed.

**Conclusions (paper, verbatim sense)**: (1) Osseointegration — direct bone-to-implant connection — can be permanently achieved in man. (2) The basic mechanism of bone-to-titanium bonding is still incompletely understood. (3) Direct titanium-bone contact now demonstrated at the electron-microscopic level. (4) To guarantee osseointegration, use threaded, unalloyed titanium implants of defined finish and geometry, inserted with delicate surgical technique and allowed to heal in situ without loading for at least 3–4 months.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Narrative / retrieval study, not a controlled trial**: the relative weight of each of the six factors "is difficult to evaluate with certainty"; the surface-finish preference is "only empirical."
- **Mechanism unresolved**: the true nature of the bone-titanium bond (suggested direct chemical bonding) was incompletely investigated; more knowledge needed.
- **Selection of retrieved implants**: the 38 analyzed screws were removed for incidental reasons (not failures), so the ultrastructural sample is of *successfully integrated* implants, not a failure analysis.
- **Forward-looking**: extension of osseointegration to joint reconstruction (metacarpophalangeal replacement already begun) flagged as needing long-term follow-up.

## 7. Glossary

- **Osseointegration**: direct structural and functional contact between living bone and the surface of a load-bearing implant, with no interposed fibrous/connective-tissue layer (defined at the light-microscopic level in this paper; confirmed at the electron-microscopic level).
- **Fixture**: the threaded titanium screw component placed in bone (precursor terminology for the implant body).
- **Biological seal**: close soft-tissue adhesion around the transmucosal/transcutaneous part of the implant that blocks microorganism infiltration.
- **Corticalization**: tendency of spongy (cancellous) bone to densify into cortical-like bone around a metal implant.
- **Necrotic border zone**: the inevitable layer of dead bone immediately adjacent to the implant produced by surgical trauma, which must remodel before loading.
- **Anodic Back e.m.f. (ABE)**: an electrochemical parameter (Clarke & Hickmann 1953) correlating with corrosion resistance; titanium scored highest (least corrosive).
- **Sharpey's fibres analogy**: the collagen-filament anchorage to the titanium surface appeared similar to Sharpey's-fibre attachment to bone (gluing by amorphous ground substance).
