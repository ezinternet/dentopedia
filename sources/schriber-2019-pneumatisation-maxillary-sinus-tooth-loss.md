## One-line Summary
Retrospective CBCT volumetric study (50 dentate vs 50 edentulous posterior maxillae): no significant difference in sinus volume, surface area, or maximum diameter between dentate and edentulous sides; males had significantly larger sinuses than females; intra- and inter-observer agreement strong; analysis time <4 min per case — concludes that vertical bone loss after posterior tooth loss is primarily **alveolar crest resorption**, not ongoing **sinus pneumatisation**.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Clinical Oral Investigations 2019;23(3):1349–1358 (online first 2018-07-17)
- **Pages**: 1349–1358 (10 pages)
- **Affiliation**: University of Bern, Switzerland (Department of Oral Surgery and Stomatology)
- **Study type**: Retrospective cross-sectional radiographic study using a custom volumetric software program

## 2. Key Contributions
- Directly tests the widely repeated clinical assumption that the **maxillary sinus pneumatises** ("expands downward") after posterior tooth loss.
- Shows that **sinus volume, surface, and diameter do not differ** between dentate and edentulous posterior maxillae — so the apparent loss of vertical bone in edentulous posterior maxillae must reflect **alveolar crest resorption**, not sinus expansion.
- Validates a **customised volumetric software program** with strong intra- and inter-observer agreement and a fast (<4 min) workflow on CBCT data.
- Provides **sex-stratified normative data**: males have significantly larger sinuses than females.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- 100 CBCT scans (50 dentate + 50 edentulous posterior maxillae) from the Bern collection.
- Two observers drew two planar curves per sinus in frontal, sagittal, and axial planes.
- Customised software computed sinus **volume (mm³), surface (mm²), and maximum diameter (mm)** from the planar curves.
- Primary outcome: effect of dentition state (dentate vs edentulous) on sinus dimensions.
- Secondary outcomes: effect of age, sex, side; analysis time per case; intra- and inter-observer agreement (Pearson correlation).
- Population: adults >30 years.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Dentate vs edentulous**: no significant difference in sinus volume, surface, or maximum diameter.
- **Sex**: males > females in volume, surface, and diameter (p < 0.05) significantly.
- **Side**: no significant left-vs-right difference.
- **Intra- and inter-observer Pearson correlations**: strong.
- **Time per analysis**: <4 minutes per CBCT for both observers.
- Clinical implication: in the posterior maxilla, **the alveolar crest moves apically**, not the sinus floor caudally.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Cross-sectional design; no longitudinal CBCT before and after extraction in the same patient.
- Selection by availability of CBCT — referral bias possible.
- Edentulous status was not stratified by **time since extraction** — long-term changes after extraction not isolated.
- Did not measure alveolar crest position directly; the conclusion about crestal resorption is inferential.
- Did not analyze partial edentulism or specific tooth loss patterns.

## 7. Glossary
- **Pneumatisation**: expansion of an air-filled cavity into adjacent bone; here, the proposed (but here refuted) expansion of the maxillary sinus into the alveolar process after tooth loss.
- **CBCT (Cone Beam Computed Tomography)**: 3D imaging modality used for sinus and alveolar volumetric analysis at lower radiation than medical CT.
- **Alveolar crest resorption**: vertical loss of the alveolar process from the crestal surface, the alternative explanation for reduced posterior vertical bone after extraction.
- **Volumetric software**: software that reconstructs 3D volume/surface measures from planar contours drawn on CBCT slices.
- **Pearson correlation (intra-/inter-observer)**: standard agreement metric for continuous radiographic measurements.
