## Why Ingested

This is the key histobacteriologic study that *empirically quantifies* the intraradicular-biofilm thesis Nair argued conceptually — providing the prevalence numbers (77% of canals harbor apical biofilm) that classify apical periodontitis as a biofilm-induced disease. It reinforces [[wiki/endodontics/diagnosis/nair-2006-causes-persistent-apical-periodontitis-review]] (intraradicular infection as the dominant cause) and adds histopathologic detail (cysts/abscesses/granulomas) to [[wiki/endodontics/diagnosis/karamifar-2020-endodontic-periapical-lesion-an-overview]].

## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional histobacteriologic study (Ricucci & Siqueira 2010, J Endod) of 106 roots (64 untreated, 42 treated) with apical periodontitis: intraradicular biofilms in 77% of canals (80% untreated, 74% treated), more frequent with larger lesions and significantly associated with epithelialized lesions (cysts 95%, abscesses 83%, granulomas 69.5%; p<0.001); extraradicular biofilms in only 6%.

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: Biofilms and Apical Periodontitis: Study of Prevalence and Association with Clinical and Histopathologic Findings
- **Authors**: Domenico Ricucci (private practice, Cetraro, Italy); José F. Siqueira Jr. (Estácio de Sá University, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil)
- **Journal**: Journal of Endodontics 2010;36(8):1277–1288
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.joen.2010.04.007
- **Type**: Cross-sectional histopathologic / histobacteriologic study

## 2. Key Contributions

- First large histobacteriologic prevalence study to directly quantify **intraradicular biofilms** across both untreated and treated canals with apical periodontitis.
- Demonstrates that biofilms are not an occasional finding but the **predominant mode of bacterial organization** in the apical canal (77% overall), satisfying accepted criteria to classify apical periodontitis as a **biofilm-induced disease**.
- Maps biofilm prevalence onto **lesion size** (dose-response: larger lesions → more biofilm) and onto **histopathologic type** (epithelialized lesions — cysts and epithelialized granulomas/abscesses — significantly more often biofilm-positive).
- Shows biofilms also colonize **ramifications and isthmuses**, not just the main canal — the anatomical refuge Nair predicted.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Material**: 106 roots (64 untreated, 42 treated) of teeth with apical periodontitis.
- **Specimens**: biopsy material obtained by **apical surgery or extraction**, processed for histopathologic and histobacteriologic techniques (serial sectioning, Brown & Brenn / Taylor-modified Gram staining).
- Biofilms scored in the apical segment of the canal, in ramifications/isthmuses, and extraradicularly; associations tested against clinical conditions (symptoms, sinus tract), radiographic lesion size, and histopathologic lesion type.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Bacteria found in all but one specimen.**
- **Intraradicular biofilm in 77% of canals overall** — untreated 80%, treated 74%.
- Biofilms also seen lining **ramifications and isthmuses**.
- **Lesion size gradient**: small lesions 62%, large lesions 82%, **very large lesions 100%** biofilm-positive.
- **Histopathologic association (p<0.001)**: biofilm prevalence by lesion type — **cysts 95%, abscesses 83%, granulomas 69.5%**; significantly associated with epithelialized lesions.
- **No correlation** between biofilm presence and clinical symptoms or sinus tract (p>0.05).
- **Extraradicular biofilms in only 6%** of cases — confirming infection is overwhelmingly intraradicular.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Cross-sectional design — associations, not causation; cannot establish whether biofilm drives lesion enlargement/epithelialization or vice versa.
- Surgical/extraction specimens introduce **selection bias** toward larger, longer-standing, or refractory lesions.
- No single morphologic biofilm pattern was identifiable ("varied from case to case"), so biofilm morphology cannot be used diagnostically.

## 7. Glossary

- **Intraradicular biofilm**: sessile bacterial community within the root canal lumen/walls/ramifications, the principal infection reservoir in apical periodontitis.
- **Extraradicular biofilm**: bacterial biofilm on the external apical root surface or within periapical tissue (here only 6%).
- **Epithelialized lesion**: periapical lesion containing epithelium (cyst, or granuloma/abscess with epithelial proliferation) — significantly more biofilm-associated.
- **Histobacteriology**: combined histologic + bacterial-staining examination of intact tissue sections to localize microbes in situ.
- **Isthmus**: a narrow ribbon-shaped communication between two canals within the same root, a common biofilm refuge.
