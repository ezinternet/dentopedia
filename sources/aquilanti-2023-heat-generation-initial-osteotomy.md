## One-line Summary
In vitro IR-thermography study (J Maxillofac Oral Surg 2023): piezo-surgical inserts generate dangerously higher temperatures during initial osteotomy than rotary drills (often >50°C vs always <40°C, p<0.001); lower saline temperature reduces heat, increased irrigation volume does not.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: Journal of Maxillofacial and Oral Surgery, 2023;22(2):313–320
- Type: In vitro experimental, infrared thermography
- Affiliation: Università Politecnica delle Marche, Ancona, Italy
- Published online: 27 October 2022
- 9 groups × 35 utilizations = 315 osteotomies

## 2. Key Contributions
- Quantitative head-to-head comparison: rotary drills vs piezo-surgical inserts at initial osteotomy.
- Identifies saline temperature (not volume) as the actionable cooling variable.
- SEM confirms rotary drills show negligible wear over 35 uses, while piezo inserts accumulate smear layer.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Artificial bone block samples.
- 9 groups defined by: drill/insert type, cooling volume (mL/min), cooling temperature.
- 315 total implant site preparations.
- Infrared thermal camera for temperature measurement.
- SEM wear assessment at baseline, 10, 35 utilizations.
- Constant 9.8 N (1 kg) load — note: above typical piezo recommendation of 100–400 g.
- Statistical analysis with comparison vs ΔT and maximum temperature.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Piezo-surgical insert groups: ΔT 53–65°C, max temperature 75–88°C in some groups (Groups G, H, I) — clearly above necrosis threshold.
- Rotary drill groups (Groups A–F): max temperature ≤22°C, never reached 40°C.
- Piezo > rotary, p < 0.001.
- Lower saline temperature → significant temperature decrease (p < 0.001).
- Increasing cooling volume → no significant temperature decrease.
- SEM: rotary drills show no wear at 35 uses; piezo inserts accumulate smear layer (organic residue from artificial bone), more pronounced over use.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- 9.8 N load exceeded piezo manufacturer recommendation (100–400 g) — may have biased piezo results.
- Sliding arm could not replicate the quarter-turn rotation recommended for piezo handling — known to reduce piezo heat.
- Artificial bone block left organic residue on piezo tip, possibly altering cutting efficacy.
- Single rotational speed; specific protocol details limit generalization.
- Future: piezo testing with manufacturer-recommended technique; in vivo validation.

## 6. Related Work
- Eriksson & Albrektsson — 47°C/1 min necrosis threshold.
- Prior comparisons of piezoelectric vs conventional drilling (Migliorati, dos Santos, Barrak, etc.).
- Implant survival not different between piezo and rotary (cited references).

## 7. Glossary
- **Piezo-surgical insert**: ultrasonic vibration-driven cutting tip used as alternative to rotary drilling; conventionally claimed to reduce thermal/mechanical trauma.
- **ΔT**: temperature variation from baseline.
- **Smear layer**: organic/inorganic residue deposited on a cutting surface during operation.
- **9.8 N**: 1 kg-force; load applied in this study, above the 100–400 g piezo recommendation.
