## Why Ingested

User requested a PubMed ingest on flapless implant placement. The wiki's flapless evidence is concentrated in the *immediate*-implant context ([[immediate-implant/pitman-2023-immediate-implant-flap-flapless-sr-ma]], [[immediate-implant/mansouri-2025-flapless-immediate-implant-bone-grafting-sr-ma]], [[immediate-implant/paknejad-2017-flapless-immediate-implant-buccal-gap-rct]]); this RCT addresses the more basic question of flapless vs flapped in *delayed/healed-ridge* single-tooth placement, where evidence on crestal bone preservation is thinner. It directly contradicts the no-difference crestal finding of the paired cohort [[implants/tarpara-2025-flapless-flapped-clinical-outcomes-cohort]], making the pair a useful illustration of how study design (RCT vs non-randomized cohort) and follow-up length drive opposite conclusions.

## One-line Summary

Prospective RCT (n=40, posterior mandible) finding flapless implant placement preserved significantly more crestal bone than the flapped technique at both 3 months (0.32 vs 0.56 mm) and 6 months (0.48 vs 0.82 mm, p<0.001), with 100% survival in both arms.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Isolates the flapless-vs-flapped question in **healed posterior-mandibular ridges** (not fresh sockets), where the immediate-implant flap literature does not directly apply.
- Reports a **statistically significant crestal-bone advantage for flapless** at both early time points (3 and 6 months).
- Mechanistic framing: periosteal blood-supply preservation and reduced surgical trauma as the basis for less marginal resorption.
- Confirms 100% short-term survival regardless of technique — the difference is in bone level, not failure.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Design: prospective randomized clinical trial; computer-generated allocation, 20 per arm.
- Population: 40 systemically healthy patients, 25–55 y, single posterior mandibular tooth replacement; CBCT-confirmed ≥6 mm width / ≥10 mm height; plaque index <20%; non-/light smokers.
- Exclusions: uncontrolled systemic disease, radiotherapy/chemotherapy, active periodontitis, need for simultaneous augmentation.
- Intervention: Group A flapless via soft-tissue punch; Group B full-thickness mucoperiosteal flap. Standardized implant size (4.0 × 10 mm) in both. Immediate healing abutments; sutures only in flap group.
- Outcome: crestal bone loss measured on standardized periapical radiographs (customized film holder) from implant-abutment junction to first bone-implant contact, mesial + distal, at baseline / 3 mo / 6 mo.
- Statistics: SPSS v26; paired t-test (intragroup), unpaired t-test (intergroup); α=0.05.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Crestal bone loss at 3 months: flapless 0.32±0.10 mm vs flapped 0.56±0.14 mm (p<0.001).
- Crestal bone loss at 6 months: flapless 0.48±0.16 mm vs flapped 0.82±0.21 mm (p<0.001).
- Significant intragroup progression 3→6 months in both arms (flapless p=0.012, flapped p=0.005).
- Implant survival 100% in both groups; no complications over 6 months.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Short follow-up (6 months) — durability of the crestal advantage unknown.
- 2D periapical radiography; authors recommend CBCT volumetric analysis for precision.
- Single center, single surgeon — limited external validity.
- No soft-tissue/esthetic or patient-reported outcomes reported.

## 6. Related Work

- Cites Jeong et al. (12-month flapless marginal-bone-loss reduction) and a Lin et al. systematic review (less discomfort, comparable success) as concordant evidence.
- Contrasts with cohorts (e.g., Gao et al. SR cited by [[implants/tarpara-2025-flapless-flapped-clinical-outcomes-cohort]]) reporting no crestal difference.

## 7. Glossary

- **Crestal bone loss (marginal bone loss):** vertical change in alveolar bone at the implant crest, measured from a fixed reference (implant-abutment junction) to first bone-implant contact.
- **Flapless technique:** implant osteotomy through a soft-tissue punch / direct transmucosal drilling, preserving the periosteum.
- **Mucoperiosteal flap:** full-thickness soft-tissue + periosteum elevation for direct bone visualization.
