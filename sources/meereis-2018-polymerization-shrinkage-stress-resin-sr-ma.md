## One-line Summary
SR+MA of 62 in vitro studies on composition strategies for reducing polymerization shrinkage stress in resin-based dental materials; resin matrix modification (including SDR-type chemistry and low-shrinkage monomers) is the most effective strategy, while alternative photoinitiators alone show no significant benefit.

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Systematic Review and Meta-analyses (multiple meta-analyses by composition strategy subgroup)
- **Published**: June 2018 in Journal of the Mechanical Behavior of Biomedical Materials, Volume 82, Pages 268–281
- **Search databases**: PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, SciELO, LILACS, IBECS, BBO (7 databases)
- **Search cutoff**: December 2016; no restriction on year of publication
- **Protocol**: PRISMA Statement; two independent reviewers

## 2. Key Contributions
- First SR+MA systematically evaluating composition strategies (not placement technique) for reducing polymerization shrinkage stress
- Three composition strategy subgroups analyzed: (1) filler phase modification, (2) coupling agent modification, (3) resin matrix modification
- Identified that **resin matrix modification** made the largest contribution to stress reduction
- Only subgroup NOT showing significant improvement over control: alternative photoinitiators (p = 0.29)
- SDR (Stress Decreasing Resin) chemistry validated as effective — optimizes both organic matrix and filler phase
- Demonstrated applicability to both low-shrinkage and bulk-fill formulation strategies

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Databases**: 7 databases (PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, SciELO, LILACS, IBECS, BBO)
- **Study type**: Laboratory studies evaluating polymerization shrinkage stress by DIRECT testing only
- **Exclusions**: Pilot studies, reviews, indirect measurement methods (microleakage, cuspal deflection, FEA), theoretical/mathematical models
- **Eligible**: 6,113 articles → 62 qualitative analysis → 58 for meta-analysis
- **Composition strategy subgroups**: Filler phase, coupling agent, resin matrix
- **Statistical analysis**: Random-effects models; α = 0.05; global comparison + subgroup analyses

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Resin matrix modification**: Significantly reduces shrinkage stress vs. control ✓ (largest effect)
- **Filler phase modification**: Significant reduction ✓
- **Coupling agent modification**: Significant reduction ✓
- **Alternative photoinitiators**: NO significant difference (p = 0.29) — only subgroup without benefit
- SDR (Stress Decreasing Resin) technology: promising application combining optimized matrix + filler
- Low-shrinkage materials (silorane, ormocer) and bulk-fill materials operate primarily through resin matrix modification mechanisms
- Mechanism of stress: van der Waals bonds → covalent bonds during polymerization → monomer approximation → volumetric contraction → stress at bonded interfaces
- Clinical consequences of uncontrolled shrinkage stress: restoration debonding, cuspal deflection, microleakage, post-operative hypersensitivity

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- All in vitro — direct clinical correlation not established
- "Direct testing" requirement excludes many studies using indirect measurement methods
- Search cutoff December 2016 — newer bulk-fill and low-shrinkage formulations not captured
- 58/62 studies included in meta-analyses (4 excluded from quantitative synthesis — reasons not specified in abstract)
- Standardization of testing methodology between laboratories remains a challenge

## 7. Glossary
- **Polymerization shrinkage stress**: Internal stress generated at the bonded tooth-restoration interface as the resin contracts during cure in a confined space
- **SDR (Stress Decreasing Resin)**: Dentsply Sirona's proprietary urethane dimethacrylate-based chemistry that modulates gel-point kinetics; used in SureFil SDR Flow and Filtek Bulk Fill base
- **Filler phase modification**: Changing type, size, or distribution of inorganic fillers to influence stress propagation
- **Coupling agent**: Silane molecule bonding inorganic filler to organic resin matrix; its modification affects stress transfer
- **Resin matrix modification**: Changing monomer type, molecular weight, functionality, or crosslink density to alter shrinkage and stress behavior
- **van der Waals forces**: Weak intermolecular forces between monomers that convert to covalent bonds during polymerization, causing the dimensional change underlying shrinkage
- **Direct testing of shrinkage stress**: Methods measuring actual force or stress (e.g., Bioman, tensometer, universal testing machine with bonded specimens) vs. indirect surrogates
