## Why Ingested

Adds a large-sample Israeli/Middle-Eastern data point to the C-shaped-canal prevalence cluster and contributes two clinically actionable findings beyond a raw prevalence number — asymmetry (only 55% bilateral) and configuration change along the root (63%) — which together argue against extrapolating one tooth/one level to its contralateral or apical counterpart. It reinforces the C-shaped morphology baseline of [[endodontics/anatomy/song-2008-cross-sectional-morphology-and-minimum-canal]] and pairs with sibling prevalence studies [[endodontics/anatomy/yu-2012-cbct-mandibular-premolars-root-canal-morphology]], [[endodontics/anatomy/shekarian-2023-cshaped-canals-three-rooted-mandibular-iranian]], and [[endodontics/anatomy/singh-2022-cshaped-canal-second-molars-indian-cbct]].

## One-line Summary

CBCT cross-sectional study (1020 Israeli patients; 1465 second + 1229 first mandibular molars): C-shaped canal prevalence 4.6% in second and 0.16% in first mandibular molars, with 55% unilateral occurrence and configuration changing along the root in 63% of cases.

## 1. Document Information

- **Journal**: Clinical Oral Investigations, 2017, 21(6):2039–2044 (published online 14 Nov 2016)
- **Study type**: Cross-sectional CBCT prevalence study
- **Population**: Israeli, 1020 patients' CBCT scans screened
- **Sample**: 1465 mandibular second molars + 1229 mandibular first molars evaluated
- **Outcome focus**: prevalence, symmetry, configuration change along root; proposed a new radicular-groove classification

## 2. Key Contributions

- Low C-shaped prevalence benchmark for an Israeli population: 4.6% (second molars), 0.16% (first molars).
- Quantified asymmetry: only 55% unilateral occurrence in second molars (i.e., bilateral presence cannot be assumed).
- Documented configuration instability: C-shaped cross-section changed along the root in 63% of cases — relevant to shaping/obturation strategy and danger-zone (perforation) awareness.
- No significant difference by gender or side of occurrence.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- 1020 patient CBCT scans screened; first and second mandibular molars with C-shaped canals identified.
- Prevalence, configuration along the root, and symmetry analyzed.
- Authors proposed a new radicular-groove classification scheme for C-shaped roots.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- C-shaped prevalence: mandibular **second molars 4.6%**, **first molars 0.16%**.
- No significant difference by gender or side.
- Unilateral (asymmetric) prevalence in second molars: **55%**.
- C-shaped configuration changed along the root in **63%** of cases.
- Conclusion: prevalence is low relative to East-Asian subpopulations; clinicians should anticipate asymmetry, along-root configuration change, and danger-zone location during treatment.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- The available PDF is a web-print/abstract version; fine numerical sub-tables (e.g., Fan-class breakdown) are limited.
- CBCT slice/voxel resolution limits detection of fine isthmuses.
- Single-population archival sample; referral bias possible.

## 6. Related Work

- Fan et al. 2004 — anatomical features of C-shaped canals in mandibular second molars (classification reference).
- Chai & Thong 2004 — cross-sectional morphology and minimum canal-wall widths (danger-zone context).
- Zheng et al. 2011; Walker 1988 — C-shaped prevalence in Chinese populations (high-prevalence comparators).

## 7. Glossary

- **C-shaped canal**: Canal system with a continuous or interrupted C-/ribbon-shaped cross-section linking canals by a fin; most common in mandibular second molars.
- **Unilateral prevalence**: Proportion of affected patients in whom only one side (not both) shows the variant — a measure of asymmetry.
- **Radicular groove**: External longitudinal depression on the root surface associated with C-shaped morphology; the thin wall here is a perforation danger zone.
