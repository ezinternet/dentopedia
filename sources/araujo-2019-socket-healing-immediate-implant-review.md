## One-line Summary
Narrative/scoping review (Periodontology 2000) of Latin-American clinical and animal studies on socket healing with vs. without immediate implant placement: immediate implant installation yields predictable osseointegration and high survival but does not prevent bone modeling or alveolar ridge dimensional reduction; hard- and soft-tissue grafting at the time of placement partially compensates for the ridge loss.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Periodontology 2000, Vol. 79, Issue 1, pp. 168–177
- **First published**: 20 March 2019
- **DOI**: 10.1111/prd.12252
- **Article type**: Review article (narrative review limited to Latin-American–originated literature)
- **Corresponding author institution**: State University of Maringá (UEM), Maringá-PR, Brazil

## 2. Key Contributions
- Synthesises clinical and experimental (canine/animal) socket-healing evidence produced by Latin-American researchers, anchored by the Araújo–Lindhe dog model line.
- Reaffirms that **immediate implant placement does NOT prevent post-extraction ridge resorption** — the buccal bundle bone resorbs regardless of implant presence.
- Argues that biologic ridge modelling/remodelling is independent of implant placement and is driven by the loss of the bundle bone–periodontal ligament unit after extraction.
- Reviews adjunctive measures (connective tissue graft, buccal gap grafting with xenograft, soft-tissue seal) shown to mitigate buccal contour collapse at immediate sites.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Literature search across MEDLINE (PubMed), Scopus, Scielo, Lilacs, and Embase.
- **Inclusion criteria**: (i) clinical and/or histological data on socket healing with or without immediate implant; (ii) approval by a Latin-American ethics committee; (iii) at least one Latin-American author or institution.
- Study designs included: case reports, case series, prospective and retrospective clinical studies, animal (dog) histology studies.
- Narrative synthesis without quantitative meta-analysis.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Immediate implant survival in Latin-American case series: consistently >95% at 1–3 years.
- Animal model (dog premolar/molar): buccal bone wall resorbs 1.5–2 mm vertically by 12 weeks whether or not an implant is placed.
- Buccal bundle bone is the dominant determinant of vertical buccal ridge loss.
- Grafting the implant–socket gap with deproteinized bovine bone mineral (DBBM) reduces but does not abolish horizontal buccal collapse.
- Adding a connective tissue graft (CTG) at the time of immediate implant placement improves mid-facial mucosal level and ridge contour stability.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Geographic restriction (Latin-American studies only) limits generalisability.
- Narrative methodology — no formal risk-of-bias appraisal or quantitative pooling.
- Heterogeneous protocols (flap vs flapless, grafted vs ungrafted, immediate vs delayed loading) confound between-study comparison.
- Long-term (>5 y) esthetic outcome data scarce in the included corpus.

## 7. Glossary
- **Immediate implant placement (Type 1)**: implant insertion into a fresh extraction socket on the same day as extraction.
- **Bundle bone**: portion of the buccal alveolar wall continuous with the periodontal ligament; resorbs after extraction.
- **Ridge modelling**: surface bone resorption/apposition that reshapes the alveolar process after tooth loss.
- **DBBM (Deproteinized Bovine Bone Mineral)**: low-substitution xenograft scaffold used to stabilise the buccal gap.
- **CTG (Connective Tissue Graft)**: subepithelial graft used to thicken peri-implant mucosa at immediate placement.
