## One-line Summary
Systematic review (Eur J Dent 2024;18:65–72) of 11 in vitro studies concluding that the literature is inconclusive on whether drill material affects heat generation during osteotomy, but drill wear is consistently associated with increased temperatures regardless of material.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: European Journal of Dentistry, 2024;18:65–72
- Type: Systematic review (PICO-based)
- Online publication: 19 June 2023
- Affiliations: BPP University (UK); University of Sharjah (UAE)
- Open access (CC-BY 4.0)

## 2. Key Contributions
- First systematic appraisal of drill material as an independent variable in osteotomy heat generation.
- Identifies methodological heterogeneity and bias as key reasons literature cannot resolve material vs. heat relationship.
- Highlights drill wear as a more consistent driver of heat than the base material.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Databases searched: Medline/PubMed Central, Science Direct, Google Scholar, plus manual bibliography search.
- PICO framework with predefined inclusion/exclusion criteria.
- 11 in vitro studies included.
- Critical appraisal of study aims, outcome measures, method clarity, sample selection, randomization, allocation concealment, attrition, bias, data analysis, and external validity.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Both ceramic and metal drills show increased heat generation; relationship between material and heat is inconsistent.
- Heat generation proportional to drill wear in all included studies.
- Non-coated drills demonstrated higher wear resistance in some studies.
- Worn drills → more friction contact, reduced cutting efficiency, increased heat.
- Confounders identified: drill geometry, speed, load, depth, diameter, number of uses, irrigation protocol, study specimen type, heat measurement device.
- Operator/assessor bias and sponsorship bias common.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- All included studies in vitro — no in vivo validation.
- Lack of standardization across studies (specimens, devices, irrigation).
- Considerable methodological heterogeneity prevents meta-analysis.
- Sponsorship bias in several studies.
- Future: standardized protocols and head-to-head material comparisons under uniform conditions.

## 6. Related Work
- Branemark osseointegration literature on 47°C/1 min thermal necrosis threshold.
- Eriksson & Albrektsson (1983) thermal damage thresholds.
- Studies on drill wear (e.g., Allsobrook, Sarker, Bernabeu-Mira).

## 7. Glossary
- **Osteotomy**: surgical cutting of bone, here the implant site preparation.
- **Thermal necrosis**: irreversible bone damage from sustained heat exposure (>47°C for 1 min commonly cited).
- **PICO**: Patient/Intervention/Comparison/Outcome — systematic review framework.
- **Sponsorship bias**: distortion of results favoring the funding manufacturer.
