## Why Ingested

Foundational evidence for the qualifier that **not every unopposed tooth overerupts** — directly tests and refutes the long-held "every tooth without an antagonist overerupts" belief, supplying the "~18% show no overeruption" non-eruptor figure used in [[wiki/overviews/unopposed-tooth-overeruption-overview]]. Reinforces [[wiki/occlusion/livas-2016-fixed-retention-unopposed-molar-overeruption]] by grounding the overview's variability/non-eruptor branch in a large long-term human sample.

## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional human study (53 individuals, 84 molars unopposed ≥10 years): only 24% showed moderate-to-severe overeruption (≥2 mm) while 18% showed none — concluding not all unopposed molars overerupt, even long-term.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Directly **tests and refutes** the widely held clinical belief that every permanent tooth without an antagonist overerupts and eventually causes clinical problems.
- Quantifies the distribution of vertical response in a large, long-term human sample: a substantial fraction of unopposed molars show **no or only slight** overeruption.
- Identifies **age at antagonist loss** (adult-age loss → lower overeruption risk) and **presence of adjacent teeth** as modifiers of unopposed-molar position.
- Distinguishes **arch-specific malposition patterns**: rotation more frequent in the maxilla, tipping more common in the mandible.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Sample**: 53 individuals examined clinically; dental casts taken to evaluate unopposed-molar position.
- **Teeth**: 84 molars (61 maxillary, 23 mandibular) each documented as unopposed for ≥10 years. Of these, 25 molars had neither an antagonist nor a mesially adjacent tooth.
- **Vertical grading (qualitative, 3 categories)**:
  1. No sign of overeruption
  2. Slight overeruption (< 2 mm)
  3. Moderate to severe overeruption (≥ 2 mm)
- Position in the mesiodistal and buccolingual directions, and rotation/tipping, were also assessed.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Of the 84 unopposed molars:

| Vertical category | Teeth | % |
|---|---|---|
| No sign of overeruption | 15 | 18% |
| Slight overeruption (< 2 mm) | 49 | 58% |
| Moderate to severe overeruption (≥ 2 mm) | 20 | 24% |

- Individuals who lost antagonists **in adult age** had a **lower risk** of overeruption than the other subjects.
- **Presence of adjacent teeth** was important for the unopposed molar's mesiodistal and buccolingual position.
- **Rotation** was more frequent in the maxilla; **tipping** was more common in the mandible.
- **Conclusion**: not all molars without antagonists overerupt, not even in a long-term perspective.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Abstract-only ingest** — full-text methodology (selection, blinding, examiner reliability, statistics) not retrieved; figures limited to those reported in the abstract.
- Cross-sectional, qualitative vertical grading (3-category, mm thresholds) rather than continuous measurement.
- Sample skewed toward maxillary molars (61 vs 23 mandibular); subgroup of teeth lacking both antagonist and adjacent tooth is small (n = 25).
- No longitudinal tracking of the same teeth; "≥10 years unopposed" inferred from documentation rather than serial casts.

## 7. Glossary

- **Unopposed (molar)**: a tooth that has lost its occlusal antagonist in the opposing arch.
- **Overeruption (supraeruption)**: continued vertical (occlusal-direction) movement of a tooth beyond the occlusal plane, here graded as none / slight (<2 mm) / moderate-severe (≥2 mm).
- **Tipping**: inclination of a tooth's crown about its root, here more common in the mandible.
- **Rotation**: rotation of the tooth about its long axis, here more frequent in the maxilla.
- **Antagonist**: the opposing-arch tooth that occludes with a given tooth.
