## Why Ingested

The wiki currently lacks any dedicated pericoronitis page, despite pericoronitis being one of the highest-frequency oral-surgery diagnoses driving inappropriate antibiotic use in dentistry. This paper fills that gap with a combined evidence-based therapy review (Part A) and systematic review of prescribing behaviour (Part B), directly reinforcing the stewardship principles already documented in [[drug/antibiotics/mendez-romero-2025-antibiotic-stewardship-dentistry-sr]] and providing the clinical context that is absent from [[oral-surgery/damico-2025-third-molar-surgical-indications-guidelines]] regarding pericoronitis-specific management.

## One-line Summary

Dual-part review (narrative + SR, 11 studies) showing ~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics for pericoronitis despite evidence that local irrigation/debridement and operculectomy are the appropriate first-line treatments; amoxicillin and metronidazole are most commonly prescribed, and systemic antibiotics should be reserved for infection spread or systemic involvement.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Provides a comprehensive evidence-based clinical framework for pericoronitis: classification (transient/non-transient; acute/chronic), etiopathogenesis, diagnosis, complications, and stepwise management.
2. Establishes that local therapy — irrigation and debridement of the pericoronal space — is the primary treatment, with antibiotics reserved only for severe cases with infection spread or systemic response.
3. Performs the first dedicated systematic review of antibiotic prescribing for pericoronitis, revealing a striking gap between evidence and practice (~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics; pericoronitis ranks in top 4 of all diagnoses for antibiotic use).
4. Identifies amoxicillin and metronidazole as the most commonly prescribed antibiotics, consistent with appropriate empirical choice for anaerobic oral infections.
5. Highlights antimicrobial photodynamic therapy (aPDT) as a promising adjunctive non-antibiotic antibacterial option.
6. Documents that dentists prescribe antibiotics for pericoronitis due to diagnostic uncertainty, fear of litigation, time pressure, and patient expectations — not clinical need.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

### Part A — Narrative Review

- PICO framework applied; summarized recommendations from professional societies, university guidelines, government health agencies (NHS/NICE), and peer-reviewed literature.
- Covered: classification, etiopathogenesis, clinical manifestation, complications, infection management, pain management (NSAIDs, local/topical anesthesia), and prevention (extraction indications, operculectomy, laser/cautery, oral hygiene).

### Part B — Systematic Review

- **PICO**: Problem = pericoronitis treatment; Intervention = antibiotic prescribing; Comparison = non-antibiotic treatment; Outcome = frequency of antibiotic prescribing.
- **Databases**: Web of Science + MEDLINE (2000–May 2021), no language restriction.
- **Search terms**: "Pericoronitis AND Antibiotics" (MeSH).
- **Initial records**: 65 (+ 5 additional) → 11 included after duplicate removal and eligibility screening.
- **Inclusion**: Original studies reporting antibiotic use for pericoronitis, ≥15 participants.
- **Exclusion**: studies not distinguishing antibiotic vs. non-antibiotic treatment, in vitro studies, reviews, case reports, conference summaries.
- **Data extraction**: Three authors independently, consensus for disagreements.
- **Sub-analyses**: Questionnaire studies (n=6) vs. patient cohort studies (n=5) evaluated separately.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Pericoronitis Epidemiology (Part A)
- Incidence: ~4.92%; 95% of cases involve the lower third molar.
- Peak age: 20–29 years (vicenarians); rare in children and adults >40 years.
- Sex distribution: slight female predominance but not statistically significant.
- Systemic/local risk factors: upper respiratory tract infection (33–37.9% of cases preceded by URI), stress (17–66%), menstruation (4–12% concurrent), poor oral hygiene, smoking, competitive sports.

### Evidence-Based Treatment (Part A)
- **First-line**: Local irrigation and debridement of the pericoronal space; resolves most cases without antibiotics.
- **Antibiotics**: Indicated ONLY for spread of infection or systemic involvement (fever, trismus, dysphagia, lymphadenopathy, Ludwig's angina risk).
- **Preferred antibiotics**: Metronidazole and amoxicillin (most effective against oral anaerobes); may be combined in severe cases.
- **Penicillin allergy**: Erythromycin as alternative.
- **Surgical options for prevention**: Operculectomy (±gingivoplasty) preferred over extraction for first episode; laser/cautery over scalpel for less bleeding, pain, complications.
- **Extraction**: Indicated for second or subsequent pericoronitis episodes per NICE guidelines; should not be delayed once decided.
- **aPDT**: Promising adjunct — reduces TNF-α in gingival fluid and periodontal pathogens; no evidence of resistance induction; insufficient studies for firm recommendations.
- **NSAIDs**: Analgesic of choice for pericoronitis pain.

### Antibiotic Prescribing Reality (Part B)
- **Questionnaire studies (6 studies)**: ~75% of dentists prescribed antibiotics for pericoronitis; pericoronitis ranked in the top 4 of all diagnoses/situations for antibiotic use.
- **Patient cohort studies (5 studies)**: Antibiotics prescribed to >50% of pericoronitis patients; pericoronitis ranked 1st or 2nd in frequency of antibiotic use across diagnoses.
- **Most prescribed antibiotics**: Amoxicillin and metronidazole (consistent with appropriate choice).
- **Exception**: UK military dental study (Combes) and German study (Mahmoody) showed relatively lower antibiotic use — attributed to better adherence to prescribing guidelines.
- **Reasons for overprescribing**: diagnostic uncertainty (47.3% in Palmer study), time pressure (30%), treatment delay (72.5%), patient expectations, patient social background.
- **Dentist awareness**: 91% acknowledged antibiotic resistance as a concern; 47.7% felt inadequately trained; 93% wanted practical guideline updates.
- **Context**: Dentistry accounts for 10% of antibiotic prescriptions in the USA, 11.3% in Canada, 8% in France; unlike other specialties, dental antibiotic prescribing is increasing, not decreasing.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Part B: Only 11 studies included (2000–2021); heterogeneity in study designs (questionnaires vs. patient cohorts) and geographic settings limits pooled estimates.
- No meta-analysis performed; frequency data not combined statistically.
- Questionnaire studies subject to social-desirability bias (self-reported prescribing behaviour may underestimate actual prescribing).
- Patient cohort studies vary in the diagnoses/situations included, making cross-study pericoronitis ranking comparisons imprecise.
- aPDT evidence base for pericoronitis is still early-stage (insufficient for firm recommendations).
- The review calls for: standardized international prescribing guidelines for pericoronitis, mandatory continuing dental education on antibiotic stewardship, and updated practical guidelines in dentist-friendly formats.

## 7. Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Pericoronitis | Inflammation of soft tissues around the crown of an incompletely erupted tooth |
| Operculum | Soft tissue cap overlying the crown of a partially erupted tooth |
| Operculectomy | Surgical removal of the pericoronal flap to eliminate the bacterial stagnation space |
| Gingivoplasty | Reshaping of gingival tissue; performed alongside operculectomy to eliminate deep pockets |
| aPDT | Antimicrobial photodynamic therapy — photosensitizer + laser light generates cytotoxic free radicals to eliminate bacteria |
| Ludwig's angina | Life-threatening phlegmonous infection of submandibular, sublingual, and submental spaces; can complicate untreated pericoronitis |
| Quinsy (peritonsillar abscess) | Pus collection behind the tonsil; a complication of pericoronitis spread |
| Trismus | Restricted mouth opening; a warning sign of pericoronitis with adjacent space involvement |
| Dentitio difficilis | Hypernym for any complication of tooth eruption (pericoronitis is a subtype) |
| MIC | Minimum inhibitory concentration; antibiotics must be dosed to achieve MIC at the infection site |
| NSAIDs | Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs; first-line analgesic for pericoronitis pain |
