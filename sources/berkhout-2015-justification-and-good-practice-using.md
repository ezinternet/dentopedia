Handheld portable intraoral X-ray units are increasingly marketed but raise distinct occupational-exposure, collimation, and uncontrolled-environment risks that no existing radiology page covered — this EADMFR consensus fills that handheld/portable gap and frames justification + ALARA from the operator's standpoint.

## One-line Summary

EADMFR consensus/position paper: handheld portable intraoral X-ray units introduce new operator/third-party exposure and collimation risks; they should be used only exceptionally, after documented justification, with strict ALARA safeguards.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides the first European Academy of DentoMaxilloFacial Radiology (EADMFR) formal position on **handheld portable intraoral X-ray equipment**, framing it as an exceptional-use modality rather than a routine replacement for wall-mounted units.
- Enumerates the **new/revised risks** unique to handheld operation requiring fresh risk assessment before use: difficulty applying rectangular collimation with beam-aiming devices, more complex receptor–exposure matching (often longer exposure times), unit weight causing operator movement, operator and third-party protection, and use in uncontrolled environments.
- Anchors the argument in the **ALARA principle**, warning that handheld units can increase (re)exposures relative to conventional intraoral devices, violating ALARA.
- Establishes a **justification threshold**: handheld use is acceptable only after careful, documented evaluation (ideally with medical-physics support), only when handheld operation demonstrably benefits over traditional modalities, and only when no new operator/third-party risk is introduced.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Expert consensus / position paper format produced by the EADMFR; not an experimental study. Synthesizes radiation-protection principles (ALARA, justification, optimization) and applies them to the specific technology of battery-powered handheld intraoral X-ray generators.
- Recommendations organized around the technical points of failure: beam-aiming devices, rectangular collimation, X-ray receptor selection, focus–skin distance, and backscatter shielding.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

(abstract-only — full text not retrieved; the following are the paper's stated conclusions/recommendations)

- The expectation stated by EADMFR is that handheld portable X-ray use will be **"very exceptional, and for justified situations only."**
- Special attention must be paid to: **beam-aiming devices, rectangular collimation, X-ray receptor section/selection, focus–skin distance, and backscatter shielding.**
- The unit must deliver **reproducible dose across the full range of environmental conditions** (e.g. battery charge status and temperature) — an explicit handheld-specific quality concern absent from fixed installations.

## 7. Glossary

- **ALARA** (As Low As Reasonably Achievable): radiation-protection principle requiring dose to be minimized consistent with achieving the diagnostic task.
- **Rectangular collimation**: restricting the X-ray beam to a rectangle matching the receptor, reducing exposed tissue area and scatter; technically harder with handheld beam-aiming devices.
- **Backscatter shielding**: a protective shield (often a lead-equivalent ring/cone) on the handheld unit's tube end that intercepts radiation scattered back toward the operator.
- **Focus–skin distance (FSD)**: distance from the X-ray focal spot to the patient's skin; minimum FSD requirements limit entrance skin dose.
- **EADMFR**: European Academy of DentoMaxilloFacial Radiology, the issuing professional body.
- **Justification**: the radiation-protection requirement that any exposure produce sufficient benefit to outweigh its detriment.
