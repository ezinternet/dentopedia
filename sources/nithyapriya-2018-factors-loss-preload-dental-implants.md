## Why Ingested

This is the foundational reference page for the *complete inventory of factors that cause loss of preload* in implant abutment screws — the upstream mechanism behind screw loosening and implant failure. It anchors the more specific connection/torque papers such as [[prosthetic-materials/velez-2020-implant-connection-abutment-design-screw]], giving each one a shared map of which variable (material, connection type, torque method/sequence, lubrication, settling effect, collar length) it is probing.

## One-line Summary

Narrative/literature-search review (MEDLINE-PubMed, 10-year window; 84 articles screened → 36 analyzed, no quality appraisal) cataloguing the factors that cause loss of preload in implant abutment screws — material (gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti), connection type (internal > external hex), calibrated torque, retorque after 10 min, lubrication, settling effect, and abutment collar length.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides a single consolidated map of the **factors that cause loss of preload** — the axial clamping force in the abutment screw neck that holds the abutment to the implant — and links preload loss to the most common implant complication, screw loosening (and subsequent fracture/failure).
- Introduces and frames the **settling effect (embedment relaxation)** as the central mechanism: microroughness on the two contacting surfaces collapses under initial torquing, so the joint loses clamping force and the screw loosens.
- Synthesizes per-factor findings across 36 studies into clinical rules: lubricated gold screws with an internal connection, tightened with a calibrated device and retorqued after 10 min, give maximum preload.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Literature search following PRISMA *search/flow* conventions, but explicitly without quality assessment (see Limitations).
- **Database/search**: MEDLINE-PubMed, searched September 2016 back over the prior 10 years; supplemented by hand-searching of journals and cross-references.
- **Search terms**: MeSH terms "abutment screw," "preload," "dental implants" combined with Boolean AND/OR — e.g. `([dental implants] AND abutment screw) AND preload`.
- **Focused question (PICO-style)**: "What are the factors causing loss of preload which eventually leads to dental implant failure?"
- **Inclusion**: loss of preload / screw loosening / screw fracture / screw geometry; implant-abutment connection type (external hex platform, Morse taper); material properties (stiffness, resilience) and materials (gold, Ti, Ti-alloy); surface texture of abutment screw; condition of mating surfaces; lubrication; torque value; rate of tightening (10, 20, 35 N, and retorque after 10 min); integrity of joint. English-language, human or animal studies, plus systematic reviews, cohort studies, and RCTs.
- **Exclusion**: functional habits (bruxism); clinical syndromes (epilepsy, psychological disorders, osteoporosis); implant fracture; non-English; off-topic; full text unavailable; duplicates; letters/commentaries. Study designs other than RCT, SR, and cohort were eliminated "to reduce bias."
- **Yield**: 84 articles → after de-duplication and criteria → **36 studies analyzed**.
- **Data extracted**: reference, study design, number of implants, group specification, initial torque, preload, and loss of preload.
- **Quality assessment**: NONE — "The quality of the various studies was not considered in the final analysis; therefore, no quality assessment has been done."

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Per-factor findings (with the sub-pools of studies the authors grouped):

- **Type of material** (6 articles, 102 implants): preload values rank **gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti**; gold exhibits the highest preload.
- **Torque method** (2 articles, one an SR): manual vs digital torque meter — **calibrated torquing devices are mandatory**; the abutment must be neither over- nor under-tightened to avoid implant-abutment misfit.
- **Torque sequence** (7 articles): **retorquing after 10 min** of the initial torque is efficient for maintaining preload value.
- **Abutment connection type** (subset of 7 articles): 2 studies (56 implants) found joint *design* not significant for preload; other studies (51 implants) found **internal hexagon exhibits greater preload than external hexagon**.
- **Influence of lubrication** (3 studies, 85 implants): dry-lubricant coatings — 60–80 nm Ti nanoparticles, Vaseline, human saliva — **decrease friction and help maintain preload by regulating the settling effect**.
- **Abutment collar length** (1 article, 15 implants): **increasing abutment collar height significantly increases torque loss** of the abutment-implant screw after cyclic loading.

Summary rules stated by the authors:
1. Gold fixation screws give higher preload than Ti and Ti-alloy screws.
2. A calibrated torquing device is mandatory for adequate preload.
3. Retorque abutment screws 10 min after initial torque during connection.
4. Abutments with more extensive contact areas with the implant have lower incidence of torque loss.
5. Internal connection type has higher preload than external hexagon.
6. Lubricants reduce friction and help maintain preload by regulating the settling effect.

**Bottom line (conclusion)**: Ideally, lubricated gold screws with an internal connection, placed with a calibrated torquing device and retorqued after 10 min, give maximum preload. Because screw loosening (from embedment relaxation) is a major cause of implant failure, clinicians must understand the determinants of preload loss.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **No quality/risk-of-bias appraisal** and **no quantitative (meta-analytic) synthesis** — despite the PRISMA framing, this is a qualitative factors catalogue, so it is labeled `narrative-review` here, not `sr`.
- Authors flag the **small sample sizes** (few implants) of the included studies as the chief limitation; several per-factor pools rest on 1–3 articles (collar length on a single 15-implant study).
- Authors call for further studies of higher methodological quality.

## 7. Glossary

- **Preload**: the axial (tensile) clamping force generated in the neck of the abutment screw — between the first mating thread and the screw head — that clamps the abutment to the implant.
- **Loss of preload**: reduction of that clamping force over time/loading; the principal upstream cause of screw loosening.
- **Settling effect (embedment relaxation)**: collapse of surface microroughness on the two contacting surfaces under initial torque, which reduces clamping force and loosens the joint.
- **Retorque**: re-application of the target torque a short interval (here 10 min) after the initial tightening, to recover preload lost to settling.
- **Implant-abutment connection type**: geometry of the joint — external hexagon, internal hexagon, or Morse taper.
- **Abutment collar length**: vertical height of the abutment collar; greater height was associated with greater torque loss after cyclic loading.
