## Why Ingested

This is **general-healthcare (acute-care) teamwork evidence, not dental-specific** — ingested deliberately to supply the quantitative backbone that the dental workforce / team-delegation cluster lacks. The dental literature argues for skill-mix and team-based care largely on workforce-economics and access grounds, but rarely quantifies the *performance* payoff of teamwork itself. This SR+MA gives the field a hard number (r=0.28 ≈ OR 2.8) that anchors the team-delegation/communication axis. Extrapolation to dentistry is by analogy (acute-care teams ≠ dental practice teams), and that framing is stated explicitly on the wiki page. Reinforces [[overviews/dental-workforce-delegation-skill-mix-overview]].

## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 31 acute-care studies (1390 teams): teamwork process correlates with clinical performance at r=0.28 (≈OR 2.8, medium effect), robust across team composition, size, task type, and patient realism (moderators non-significant).

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: How effective is teamwork really? The relationship between teamwork and performance in healthcare teams: a systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Authors**: Jan B Schmutz, Laurenz L Meier, Tanja Manser
- **Journal**: BMJ Open 2019;9(9):e028280
- **DOI**: 10.1136/bmjopen-2018-028280 (PMID 31515415, PMCID PMC6747874)
- **Design**: Systematic review + random-effects meta-analysis (PRISMA)
- **Source**: PMC full text retrieved (full_text: true). Per PubMed attribution policy.

## 2. Key Contributions

- **First quantitative (meta-analytical) test** of the teamwork→clinical-performance link in healthcare; prior reviews were qualitative or focused on *team training* rather than teamwork itself.
- Pooled effect: sample-size weighted mean **r = 0.28 (95% CI 0.20–0.35), OR ≈ 2.8** — teams engaging in teamwork are ~2.8× more likely to achieve high performance.
- Establishes that the effect is **context-robust**: none of six pre-specified moderators (professional composition, team familiarity, team size, task type, patient realism, performance-measure type) significantly altered the average effect.
- Practical message: teamwork matters in **routine** situations, not only emergencies/crisis — so training should not be limited to crisis resource management (CRM) scenarios.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Search**: PubMed (no date limit; last search 19 Jun 2018) + manual backward search of 8 teamwork/non-technical-skills reviews + backward/forward (Web of Science) search of included studies + author-solicited unpublished work.
- **Inclusion**: studies linking ≥1 teamwork *process* (coordination, communication, leadership, non-technical skills) to a *performance* measure in an **acute care** setting; team-level effect sizes only; long-term care excluded.
- **Construct frame**: Input–Process–Output (IPO) model; teamwork = team process distinct from taskwork.
- **Effect-size handling**: heterogeneous metrics (OR, p-values, etc.) converted to correlation r; multiple within-sample correlations averaged via Fisher's z to preserve independence; sample-size weighted; *not* corrected for measurement reliability.
- **Model**: random-effects (REML estimator). Moderators tested via mixed-effects omnibus test. Publication bias assessed via random-effects Egger test (z=1.79, p=0.074 → no asymmetry).
- One outlier (Carlson 2009, r=0.83/0.89) excluded via studentized deleted residuals.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Overall**: N=1390 teams, k=31 studies, **r=0.28** (95% CI 0.20–0.35), 80% credibility interval 0.09–0.45; heterogeneous (Q=53.7, I²=46.0%).
- **Moderator omnibus test NOT significant** (QM=0.18, df=6, p>0.20). Subgroups (all r≈0.25–0.30):
  - Interprofessional r=0.28 (k=27) vs uniprofessional r=0.28 (k=4).
  - Real teams r=0.29 (k=21) vs experimental teams r=0.25 (k=10).
  - Routine task r=0.27 (k=14) vs non-routine r=0.29 (k=16).
  - Real patient r=0.28 (k=16) vs simulated r=0.28 (k=15) → simulation generalises to real settings.
  - **Performance-measure type** was the one notable gradient: process performance r=0.30 (k=27) vs outcome performance r=0.13 (k=4) — but still significant for hard outcomes (mortality, complications, bloodstream infection) despite only 4 studies.
- Settings: predominantly surgery, anaesthesia, emergency/resuscitation, obstetrics, neonatal/intensive care — i.e. high-task-interdependence acute episodes.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Only **31 studies / single primary database (PubMed)**; most primary studies had small samples.
- Outcome-performance subgroup very small (k=4) → low power to detect process-vs-outcome difference.
- "Team familiarity" coded coarsely (experimental vs real) — does not capture actual prior interaction; team climate not captured.
- **Generalisability bound to acute care** — authors explicitly note results may not extend to long-term care or mental health (and, by extension here, not directly to dental outpatient practice — this is the wiki's extrapolation caveat).
- Calls for standardised teamwork/performance measures and interdisciplinary (medicine + psychology + health-professions-education) research teams.

## 6. Related Work

- Builds on prior **team-training** meta-analyses (Hughes et al.) showing training effectiveness irrespective of composition/fidelity/acuity — consistent with this study's moderator-null result.
- IPO and advanced team-process frameworks (Marks/Mathieu/Zaccaro) underpin the construct definition.
- Within this wiki: foundational pairing with team-communication / patient-safety reviews in `practice-management`; extrapolated anchor for [[overviews/dental-workforce-delegation-skill-mix-overview]].

## 7. Glossary

- **Teamwork process**: interactions among members combining collective resources to meet task demands (coordination, communication, leadership, monitoring); distinct from taskwork (member's interaction with tools/task).
- **Non-technical skills (NTS)**: cognitive/social skills (situation awareness, decision-making, communication, leadership) that complement clinical technical skills.
- **IPO model**: Input–Process–Output framework — team processes mediate inputs (member experience, task complexity) into outputs (quality, errors, outcomes).
- **Process vs outcome performance**: process = how well the treatment was executed (guideline adherence, expert checklist rating); outcome = downstream patient result (mortality, morbidity, infection rate).
- **Credibility interval (CR)**: range of true effects across studies (vs CI, which reflects precision of the point estimate); wide CR signals possible moderators.
- **CRM (crisis resource management)**: team-training approach for crisis/emergency situations.
