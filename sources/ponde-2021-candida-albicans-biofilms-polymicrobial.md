## Why Ingested
Provides the fungal biofilm perspective that complements bacterial matrix reviews; C. albicans cross-kingdom interactions with S. mutans and oral bacteria are directly relevant to [[oral-microbiology/karygianni-2020-biofilm-matrixome-extracellular-components]] and [[oral-microbiology/koo-2013-exopolysaccharides-matrix-biofilm-virulence]].

## One-line Summary
Narrative review of C. albicans biofilm formation, transcriptional regulation, ECM composition, antifungal resistance mechanisms, and polymicrobial interactions with oral bacteria, establishing biofilm-associated candidaemia (>400,000 cases/year; ~50% mortality) as a major clinical challenge.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Defines the four-stage C. albicans biofilm lifecycle: adhesion → microcolony/ECM production → maturation → dispersal.
- Characterises ECM composition: proteins 55%, carbohydrates 25%, lipids 15%, nucleic acids 5%; predominant polysaccharide is mannan associated with β-1,6 glucans.
- Identifies the core transcriptional network controlling biofilm formation (Bcr1p, Tec1p, Efg1p, Ndt80p, Rob1p, Brg1p); ablation of BCR1, TEC1, EFG1, NDT80, or ROB1 prevents biofilm formation in vivo.
- Demonstrates eDNA's structural and protective roles in ECM; DNase treatment combined with chitinase inhibitor acetazolamide enhances amphotericin B efficacy in vitro.
- Documents dispersed cells as "pre-conditioned" for maximum virulence — increased filamentation, biofilm formation, antifungal resistance, and endothelial damage capacity compared to planktonic cells.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: narrative (non-systematic) review.
- Scope: biofilm formation mechanics, ECM composition, transcriptional regulation, in vitro/in vivo model comparisons, dispersal biology, antifungal resistance, and polymicrobial interactions with bacteria (S. mutans, Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Rothia dentocariosa).
- Draws on C. albicans mutant studies, rat catheter/denture models, and multispecies in vitro biofilm data.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Recurrent candidaemia affects >400,000 people/year globally; mortality rate ~50%.
- Biofilm maturation on PVC catheter discs: 24–48 h; on PMMA strips: 38–72 h.
- In vivo biofilm maturation phase shorter than in vitro (rat central venous catheter model).
- ECM: proteins 55%, carbohydrates 25%, lipids 15%, nucleic acids 5%.
- Core network of 6 transcription factors (Bcr1p, Tec1p, Efg1p, Ndt80p, Rob1p, Brg1p) controls ~1,000 genes involved in biofilm development.
- 51 transcription factors identified as contributing to biofilm formation/development.
- Zap1p negatively regulates β-1,3 glucan (via GCA1, GCA2); Rlm1p positively regulates ECM production.
- Rothia dentocariosa inhibits C. albicans cell dispersal from biofilms in vitro.
- S. mutans–C. albicans cross-kingdom biofilm interaction: Gtf-mediated glucan production on fungal surface enhances adhesion (referenced via Koo et al.).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Definition of clinical C. albicans biofilm vs. altered microbiota remains debated, particularly for vaginal candidiasis.
- Most biofilm studies are in vitro; in vivo polymicrobial interaction data are sparse.
- Tissue/anatomical location and surrounding microbiota significantly influence biofilm development — underexplored.
- Authors note dispersed cell biology as an emerging and clinically important research frontier.

## 6. Related Work
- Directly extends Koo et al. glucan/Gtf data to cross-kingdom C. albicans context.
- Complements Karygianni et al. (Trends Microbiol 2020) matrixome framework with fungal ECM data.
- S. mutans–C. albicans polymicrobial interaction is central to the cariogenic biofilm virulence model in rodent studies.

## 7. Glossary
- Als (agglutinin-like sequence) adhesins: GPI-linked cell-wall proteins (Als1p–Als7p, Als9p) mediating C. albicans adhesion during biofilm formation.
- Hwp1p (hyphal wall protein 1): molecular mimic of transglutaminase substrate enabling covalent cross-linking to host cells; critical for hyphal adhesion.
- Bcr1p: master biofilm transcriptional regulator; controls Als3p, Hwp1p, Hyr1p, and ECE1 (candidalysin).
- Zap1p: zinc regulator; negatively controls β-1,3 glucan production in ECM.
- Candidalysin: peptide toxin encoded by ECE1; upregulated in high biofilm-forming isolates.
- Dispersed cells: cells released from mature biofilm, phenotypically distinct with enhanced virulence and antifungal resistance.
