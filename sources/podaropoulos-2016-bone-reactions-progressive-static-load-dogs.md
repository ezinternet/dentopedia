## Why Ingested

교합 과부하가 임플란트 주위골에 미치는 영향을 다루는 [[wiki/overviews/implant-occlusion-loading-biomechanics-overview]] 종합 페이지에 동물모델 실험 근거가 부족했다. 본 연구는 개 상악에서 점진적 정적 교합력(100→200→300 g)이 BIC 및 골밀도에 미치는 영향을 조직학·조직형태계측학적으로 직접 측정한 유일한 점진적 프로토콜 동물실험으로, 진행성 부하가 BIC를 유의하게 높인다는 생역학적 근거를 제공하여 위 종합 페이지를 보강한다.

## One-line Summary

Animal (dog) histomorphometric RCT-analog: progressive static orthodontic loading (100→200→300 g, 9 wks) on 12 Biomet 3i implants in beagle maxillae significantly increased BIC vs unloaded controls (P = 0.018), with no difference in peri-implant bone density or crestal bone resorption.

## 한줄요약

비글 개 상악에서 점진적 정적 교합력(100→200→300 g, 9주) 적용 임플란트(12개)가 무부하 대조군 대비 골-임플란트 접촉률(BIC)을 유의하게 높였고(P = 0.018), 골밀도 및 치조정 골흡수는 두 군 간 차이 없었다.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides the first animal-model evidence specifically for **progressive** (stepwise-increasing) static orthodontic loading on osseointegrated implants, distinct from constant static load studies (Gotfredsen series).
- Demonstrates that progressive loading increases BIC significantly in low-density (type IV equivalent) dog maxillary bone without provoking crestal bone resorption.
- Supports Misch's clinical concept of progressive bone loading (PBL) at the histologic level.
- Uses Ni-Ti orthodontic coil springs as a controlled, measurable force-delivery mechanism — a methodological advance over bite-force loading models.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

**Animal model**
- 3 beagle dogs; bilateral edentulous ridges created posterior to maxillary canines; 8 weeks of ridge healing before implant placement.
- 24 Biomet 3i implants (surface type not specified in abstract); inserted into prepared sites.

**Groups**
- *Progressive loading group* (n = 12): healed submerged 8 weeks, then Ni-Ti orthodontic coil springs connected pairs of abutments; force escalated in three 3-week periods: 100 g → 200 g → 300 g (total loading: 9 weeks).
- *Unloaded control group* (n = 12): healed undisturbed for the same duration.

**Histology**
- Donath & Breuner undecalcified ground-section technique (standard in peri-implant histomorphometry).
- Sections evaluated under light microscopy.

**Outcome measures**
- Bone-to-Implant Contact (BIC) — percentage
- Bone density 1 mm from implant threads
- Bone density 2 mm from implant threads
- Crestal bone resorption (linear measurement)

**Statistics**
- Median regression models (robust to non-normality; appropriate for small n per group); Parente & Santos Silva (2013) quantile regression approach.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

| Outcome | Progressive loading | Unloaded control | P-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| BIC (%) | Higher (significantly) | Lower | **0.018** |
| Bone density @1 mm | — | — | 0.734 (NS) |
| Bone density @2 mm | — | — | 0.961 (NS) |
| Crestal bone resorption | — | — | 0.813 (NS) |

- Exact median BIC percentages not recoverable from available text; statistical significance confirmed (P = 0.018).
- Bone density at 1 mm and 2 mm from implant threads: no significant difference between groups — progressive loading improved BIC without diffuse bone densification beyond the implant surface.
- No crestal bone resorption difference — progressive loading within the 100–300 g range did not provoke marginal bone loss.

**Conclusion (verbatim from abstract)**: "The application of progressive loading by controlled orthodontic force on osseointegrated implants provoked significant increase in the percentage of bone-to-implant contact of the low-density bone of the dog maxilla."

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Small sample**: Only 3 dogs; 24 implants total (12 per group); statistical power is limited.
- **Single bone type**: Maxillary dog bone is inherently low-density (type III–IV equivalent); generalizability to mandibular or denser human bone is uncertain.
- **Loading direction**: Ni-Ti springs apply horizontal/tensile static forces between paired abutments — this is orthodontic-type load, not occlusal axial load; clinical relevance to occlusal overload is indirect.
- **No functional loading control**: Comparison is between progressive static load and zero load; no conventional immediate- or conventional-loading arm.
- **Short duration**: 9 weeks of progressive loading; long-term stability of BIC gains unknown.
- **PDF body inaccessible**: Exact BIC percentages, histologic images, and full statistical tables are in the paywalled body; only abstract-level data are available in this source page.
- **Future work cited by authors**: The companion study by the same group (Podaropoulos 2020) extended these findings to evaluate implant withstand capacity under overloading forces.

## 6. Related Work

- **Gotfredsen, Berglundh & Lindhe (2001a, b, c)** — Three-paper dog series on static load of different magnitudes, surface types, and durations; foundational comparators for this study.
- **Melsen & Lang (2001)** — Biological reactions of alveolar bone to orthodontic loading of oral implants; conceptual backbone for the orthodontic force model used here.
- **Isidor (1996, 1997)** — Loss of osseointegration from occlusal overload in monkeys; provides the failure-side reference.
- **Heitz-Mayfield et al. (2004)** — Excessive occlusal load and osseointegration in dogs; no adverse effect found, consistent with the present absence of crestal resorption.
- **Misch (1990, 1993)** — Progressive bone loading concept that this study tests histologically.
- **Podaropoulos 2020** — Companion study on implant withstand capacity under overloading after progressive loading.

## 7. Glossary

- **BIC (Bone-to-Implant Contact)**: Percentage of implant thread surface in direct contact with mineralized bone on histologic section; primary osseointegration metric.
- **Progressive loading (PBL)**: Misch's clinical concept of gradually increasing occlusal force on implants to allow adaptive bone remodeling before full functional load.
- **Ni-Ti orthodontic coil spring**: Nickel-titanium spring used in orthodontics for force delivery; provides predictable, measurable static tension between abutments.
- **Mechanostat (Frost)**: Theoretical framework predicting bone remodeling based on strain thresholds: below minimum effective strain → resorption; within physiologic window → maintenance/formation; above pathologic strain → overload damage.
- **Median regression / quantile regression**: Statistical method modeling the median (50th percentile) of the outcome rather than the mean; robust with small, potentially skewed histomorphometric datasets.
- **Undecalcified ground section (Donath technique)**: Histologic preparation where mineralized bone is ground to transparency without decalcification, preserving the bone-metal interface for direct BIC measurement.
