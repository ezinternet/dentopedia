## One-line Summary

Narrative review of implant stability measurement methods (clinical perception, percussion, reverse torque, cutting torque resistance, Periotest, RFA/ISQ), concluding no single critical cutoff value reliably predicts implant success and RFA/ISQ is the most useful noninvasive option.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Distinguishes **primary implant stability** (mechanical phenomenon at placement, depends on local bone quality/quantity, implant type, placement technique) from **secondary implant stability** (biological increase attributable to bone formation/remodeling at the implant/tissue interface).
- Frames stability measurement as a clinical decision aid for loading protocol selection (immediate, early 6–8 weeks, or classical 3–6 month delayed loading).
- Catalogs six clinical measurement methods with their mechanisms and limitations: clinical perception, percussion test, reverse torque test, cutting torque resistance analysis, Periotest, and resonance frequency analysis (RFA).
- Provides interpretation thresholds: Periotest reading ranges and RFA/ISQ loading guidelines.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

This is a non-systematic narrative review (practitioner section). It surveys published methods of implant stability assessment without meta-analysis or pooled data. It notes histologic/microscopic analysis is the historical "gold standard" for assessing osseointegration but is too invasive and ethically constrained for clinical use, motivating the noninvasive alternatives reviewed.

The methods reviewed:
- **Clinical perception** — mobility detected by blunt-ended instruments / cutting resistance feel during insertion; tapered root-form implants can give a false perception of high stability via an abrupt seating stop.
- **Percussion test** — tapping a mirror handle against the implant carrier to elicit a ringing sound; yields at best poor qualitative information.
- **Reverse (unscrewing) torque test** — applied at abutment connection; rotating implants are deemed failures and removed. Fallen into disrepute because the osseointegrating surface may fracture under the torque, and mobilized implants can re-integrate (animal data).
- **Cutting torque resistance analysis** — energy a current-fed electric motor uses to cut a unit volume of bone during surgery; correlates with bone density. Limited because no lower-limit failure threshold is established, it can only be used intra-surgically, and it cannot assess secondary stability.
- **Periotest** — electrically driven tapping head percusses the implant 16 times (~4 s total); an accelerometer-coupled rod impacts the abutment at constant velocity. Readings range −8 to +50.
- **RFA** — noninvasive; transducer attached to implant/abutment, measures resonance frequency converted to ISQ. Two device types: original electrical (wired L-shaped cantilever transducer with piezoelectric crystals) and newer magnetic (metallic peg/SmartPeg excited by a wireless magnetic pulse, ~1 ms). Magnetic device yields higher ISQ values for nonsubmerged implants.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Periotest interpretation** (Table 1):
  - −8 to 0 → good osseointegration, implant can be loaded
  - +1 to +9 → clinical examination required; loading usually not possible
  - +10 to +50 → osseointegration insufficient, implant cannot be loaded
- Periotest limitations: in-vitro studies showed no statistically significant operator-to-operator difference and high repeatability between units, but no absolute acceptable value exists; measurements must be taken mid-buccal, perpendicular to implant axis; molars are difficult due to buccal mucosa; cannot diagnose borderline/in-process cases; does not reflect peri-implant bone level and cannot replace radiography.
- **RFA/ISQ**: ISQ classically ranges 40–80; higher ISQ = higher stability. Reported loading thresholds:
  - Stability can be determined for implants with ISQ of 47.
  - All implants with ISQ > 49 osseointegrated when left to heal 3 months.
  - All implants with ISQ > 54 osseointegrated when immediately loaded.
- Low-ISQ implants: a decrease should prompt tighter follow-up and unloading until stability is regained. High-ISQ implants: a stability reduction during the first 12 weeks of healing is a common event not requiring altered follow-up.
- ISQ factors: effective implant length (length of exposed threads + abutment height, inversely proportional to resonance frequency), bone quality/quantity, implant length, diameter, shape.
- RFA drawbacks: transducer limited to ~60 measurements (expensive); requires fixing a transducer to the implant, so cemented restorations cannot be monitored.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- The review's own conclusion: the number of variables affecting results makes it difficult to derive a single critical value that determines success, failure, or long-term prognosis; more research is needed for an accurate stability-gauging instrument.
- As a narrative practitioner review it has no systematic search, no quality appraisal, and no pooled estimates; thresholds are quoted from individual primary sources (chiefly Nedir et al. 2004) rather than synthesized evidence.

## 7. Glossary

- **Primary stability**: mechanical anchorage of an implant at placement, from bone quality/quantity, implant type, and placement technique.
- **Secondary stability**: biological stability gain from bone formation and remodeling at the implant/tissue interface after placement.
- **RFA (Resonance Frequency Analysis)**: noninvasive method measuring the resonance frequency of the implant-bone complex, expressed as ISQ.
- **ISQ (Implant Stability Quotient)**: scaled RFA output, classically 40–80; higher = more stable.
- **Periotest**: electronic percussion device giving a damping value (−8 to +50) reflecting interface stiffness.
- **Reverse torque test**: applying unscrewing torque at abutment connection; rotation indicates failure.
- **Cutting torque resistance analysis**: bone-density–correlated energy measured by the surgical motor during osteotomy.
