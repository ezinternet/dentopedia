## Why Ingested

Provides the human longitudinal magnitude of risk for unopposed-molar overeruption that anchors the modifier table in [[wiki/overviews/unopposed-tooth-overeruption-overview]]: a **4.9× higher risk of ≥2 mm overeruption** (95% CI 1.5–15.3) over 12 years, plus the **bone-support modifier** (overeruption increases as alveolar bone support decreases). It reinforces the implant-CBCT evidence in [[wiki/occlusion/wang-2022-unopposed-molar-overeruption-implant-cbct]] with a population-based radiographic cohort, converting a cross-sectional CBCT snapshot into a 12-year incidence/risk estimate in real patients.

## One-line Summary

Prospective 12-year panoramic study of 292 women: unopposed molars carried a 4.9× higher risk of ≥2 mm overeruption (95% CI 1.5–15.3) than opposed molars, averaging ~4.5% (≈0.9 mm), with overeruption increasing as alveolar bone support decreased; mesial tipping was small (mean 0.8°) and not predicted by edentulous space, but overeruption and tipping interacted significantly.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Quantifies, in a **prospective human population** over a long (12-year) interval, the risk of overeruption for unopposed molars vs opposed molars — a 4.9× relative risk for ≥2 mm overeruption (95% CI 1.5–15.3).
- Establishes a **dose-response with bone support**: degree of overeruption increased with decreased alveolar bone support.
- Reports the **average overeruption magnitude** (4.5%, s.d. 7.6 ≈ 0.9 mm) — small on average but with a meaningful tail crossing the ≥2 mm threshold.
- Shows **mesial tipping into a bounded space is generally minor** (mean 0.8°, s.d. 5.6) and is **not** significantly predicted by the presence of an edentulous space or by bone level/bone-level change.
- Identifies a **significant interaction between overeruption and tipping** — the two movements are not independent.
- Documents jaw-site asymmetry: unopposed molars more common in the **upper jaw**; molars with a mesial edentulous space more prevalent in the **lower jaw**.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Sample**: 292 subjects drawn from a prospective population study of women (the "Study of Women in Gothenburg," Bengtsson et al.), with panoramic radiographs taken at a **12-year interval**.
- **Measurements**: panoramic radiographs were scanned and analysed for changes in **overeruption, mesial tipping, and alveolar bone height** of molars and of control (opposed/non-edentulous) teeth.
- **Outcome modelling**: relative risk of ≥2 mm overeruption (unopposed vs opposed) reported with 95% CI; effects of edentulous space and alveolar bone level/bone-level change on tipping tested; interaction between overeruption and tipping assessed (reference list includes multilevel-analysis methodology — Snijders & Bosker — consistent with a multilevel/mixed model for clustered tooth-within-subject data).
- **Reproducibility / error references** cited (Larheim & Svanaes; Stramotas et al.; Tronje et al.; Dahlberg) indicate attention to panoramic linear/angular measurement error and image distortion.
- *Methods detail beyond the above is abstract-level only in this artifact.*

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Overeruption risk (unopposed vs opposed)**: 4.9× higher risk of ≥2 mm overeruption; 95% CI 1.5–15.3, over 12 years.
- **Average overeruption (unopposed molars)**: 4.5% (s.d. 7.6), corresponding to approximately 0.9 mm.
- **Bone-support modifier**: degree of overeruption **increased with decreased bone support**.
- **Mesial tipping**: average 0.8° (s.d. 5.6); molars facing a mesial edentulous space showed a **low risk** of mesial tipping; neither the edentulous space nor alveolar bone level/bone-level change had a significant effect on tipping.
- **Interaction**: a statistically significant interaction between overeruption and tipping was identified.
- **Site distribution**: unopposed molars more common in the upper jaw; mesial-edentulous-space molars more prevalent in the lower jaw.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **All-female cohort** — generalizability to men is untested.
- **Panoramic radiography** carries linear/angular measurement error and distortion for inclined objects; the study cites reproducibility references but 2-D panoramic measurement is less precise than 3-D CBCT (cf. Christou & Kiliaridis 2007; Wang 2022).
- Average overeruption is small (≈0.9 mm), so the **clinically meaningful signal lives in the tail** (the ≥2 mm subgroup), not the mean.
- Mechanistic drivers of why some unopposed molars over-erupt and others do not are not resolved here; the bone-support association is observational.
- **Artifact limitation**: this ingest is from the publisher landing-page print; full Methods/Results tables were not available for extraction.

## 7. Glossary

- **Unopposed molar**: a molar that has lost its antagonist (opposing) tooth, leaving the occlusal surface without functional contact.
- **Overeruption (supraeruption)**: continued vertical eruption of a tooth past its normal occlusal plane, typically when the opposing tooth is absent.
- **Mesial tipping**: angular drift of a tooth toward a mesial edentulous space.
- **Bounded edentulous space**: a gap with teeth on both sides (mesial and distal).
- **Alveolar bone support / bone level**: height of supporting bone around a tooth; reduced support is associated here with greater overeruption.
- **Panoramic radiograph**: 2-D extra-oral rotational radiograph used here for longitudinal linear/angular tooth-position measurement.
- **Interaction (statistical)**: the effect of one factor (overeruption) depends on the level of another (tipping) — the two movements co-vary non-additively.
