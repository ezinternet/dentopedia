## One-line Summary

Updated narrative review (15 observational studies of healthy elderly ≥65y vs. younger controls, ROBINS-I risk-of-bias assessed) concluding presbygeusia (age-related taste alteration without pathology) is a real, relatively frequent phenomenon — driven by reduced fungiform papillae density, taste bud homeostasis decline, altered saliva composition/mucin, central nervous system changes, and oral microbiome shifts — but its clinical impact on dietary choices in the elderly is likely modest, since cultural/psychological/social factors dominate food-preference determination.

## 1. Document Information

- Journal: Aging Clinical and Experimental Research, 2024;36(1):84
- Publication date: 2024-04-01
- Article type: Narrative Review (updated)
- 15 observational studies included after screening 837 records (762 excluded on title/abstract)

## 2. Key Contributions

- Directly addresses whether "presbygeusia" is a real, distinct phenomenon (vs. always a proxy for undiagnosed pathology) by restricting eligibility to studies explicitly assessing healthy elderly without comorbidities/drug effects — a stricter inclusion bar than most prior taste-aging literature.
- Compiles a comprehensive mechanistic table (Table 1) spanning peripheral (fungiform papillae density/vascularization, taste bud homeostasis, ENaC/TRPV1 gene polymorphisms, salivary flow/mucin composition), central (EEG/fMRI brain-activation changes), mechanical (mastication, dentures), olfactory (retro-nasal stimulation loss), and microbiome-mediated pathways to age-related taste alteration.
- Concludes 12/15 included studies support the existence of presbygeusia (against 3 controversial), with sour and bitter taste most affected and sweet perception relatively preserved.
- Explicitly separates the *existence* of presbygeusia from its *clinical significance* — finding the former well-supported but the latter (impact on food choice/nutrition) weakly supported, since genetic taste-receptor variation explains only ~20% of food-preference variance.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: updated narrative review with a systematic-style search and ROBINS-I risk-of-bias appraisal for included observational studies (not a full systematic review/meta-analysis).
- **Eligibility**: studies of adults ≥65y without evident underlying pathological causes vs. healthy younger controls; reviews, case reports/series, conference proceedings, non-English and animal studies excluded from the core evidence table (but manually screened for background/discussion material).
- **Search**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, EMBASE, CINAHL to May 30, 2023; ('taste alteration*' OR 'taste dysfunction*' OR 'taste problem*') AND (aging OR age-related OR elderly).
- **Selection**: 837 records → 762 excluded on title/abstract → full-text assessment by 2 independent reviewers (disagreements resolved by a third) → 15 observational studies included.
- **Quality appraisal**: ROBINS-I tool; overall moderate risk of bias across all 15 studies, serious risk of bias in 3 (selection bias, uncontrolled confounders).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- General population TA prevalence: 5–20%; complete ageusia rare (<3%); men more frequently affected than women.
- Elderly-specific TA prevalence: 10–30%, higher than general population.
- Sour and bitter tastes most compromised with age; sweet perception relatively maintained across most (though not all) cited studies.
- 12/15 included studies support presbygeusia's existence; 3 present controversial/negative findings (e.g., Drewnowski 1996 found no age-related salt-taste decline in chicken broth ratings; Wayler 1990 found no significant recognition-threshold difference for salt/sucrose between age groups, though suprathreshold salt function was impaired).
- Electrogustometry threshold increases begin at age 60 (chorda tympani/glossopharyngeal nerve areas) and age 70 (greater petrosal nerve area).
- Amino acid taste thresholds ~2.5× higher in elderly (75–87y) vs. young (17–27y) subjects (Schiffman 1979).
- Genetic predisposition explains only ~20% of food-preference variance; environmental/cultural/social factors dominate the remainder — supporting the conclusion that TAs have limited practical dietary impact despite being mechanistically real.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Author-acknowledged: measuring tastant intensity in water has limited ecological validity for real-world complex-food perception; few studies used repeated threshold measurements (which better capture individual variability); many included studies had small samples and methodological heterogeneity (varying assessment tools, threshold definitions, food systems).
- Older adults are relatively inaccurate at self-reporting taste dysfunction, limiting self-report-based prevalence estimates.
- Possible publication bias (null findings less likely published) may inflate apparent TA prevalence in the elderly.
- Authors note the causal chain from TA → food preference → food choice → nutritional status remains unproven and is likely weaker than assumed; future studies should better isolate taste-specific effects from confounding cultural/psychological/social determinants of food choice.

## 6. Related Work

- Cites foundational taste-threshold aging studies (Schiffman 1979/1981/1994; Mojet 2001/2003; Nakazato 2002; Pavlidis 2013; Pingel 2010; Fukunaga 2005; Yamauchi 2002; Sato 2022; Wang 2020; Wayler 1990; Drewnowski 1996; Fark 2013).
- Cites structural/mechanistic studies on fungiform papillae density and vascularization (Fischer 2013; Piochi 2018/2019; Walliczek-Dworschak 2017/2019), taste bud homeostasis (Feng 2014), salivary mucin (Pushpass 2019), central taste processing (Iannilli 2017; Green 2013 fMRI study), and oral microbiome (Belibasakis 2018; Schamarek 2023).

## 7. Glossary

- **Presbygeusia**: age-related impairment of taste perception, analogous to presbyopia (vision) and presbycusis (hearing).
- **Hypogeusia / Ageusia / Hypergeusia**: diminished / absent / increased taste sensitivity (quantitative abnormalities).
- **Dysgeusia / Phantogeusia / Aliageusia**: altered taste in response to a stimulus / taste sensation without a stimulus / pleasant food tasting unpleasant (qualitative abnormalities).
- **Fungiform papillae (FP)**: anterior tongue papillae housing taste buds, commonly used as a proxy measure for taste bud density in aging research.
- **ROBINS-I**: Risk Of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Interventions, a quality-appraisal tool for observational studies.
