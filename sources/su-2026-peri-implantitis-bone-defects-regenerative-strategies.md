## One-line Summary

Narrative review (2026) systematically summarizing the pathological mechanisms, diagnostic modalities (CBCT, MRI, radionuclide imaging), and regenerative surgical strategies (GBR, xenografts, 3D-printed Ti mesh, biodegradable Mg membranes) for peri-implantitis-associated bone defects, highlighting remaining gaps in predictability and bacterial eradication.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Synthesizes the multifactorial pathogenesis of peri-implantitis bone loss: microbial biofilm (distinct from periodontitis biofilm due to Ti surface), host immune dysregulation, titanium corrosion releasing metal ions, and biomechanical overload.
2. Reviews diagnostic advances — CBCT as gold standard for 3D defect characterization, with adjunctive roles for MRI and radionuclide imaging; digital bioinformatics for CBCT-based bone regeneration monitoring.
3. Summarizes evidence that GBR with xenogenic bone substitutes outperforms autogenous bone grafts or non-GBR treatment in inflammation reduction and radiographic bone gain.
4. Highlights emerging biomaterials: biodegradable magnesium membranes (mechanical stability + gradual resorption) and customized 3D-printed titanium meshes (precision ridge augmentation).
5. Discusses machine learning models for predicting implant failure/peri-implantitis risk as a route to personalized maintenance.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Study type**: Narrative review
- **Scope**: Pathological mechanisms → morphological/defect classification → diagnostic imaging → non-surgical and surgical therapies → GBR techniques → novel biomaterials → emerging trends
- **Literature basis**: Not a systematic review; author-selected representative studies and meta-analyses cited throughout
- **Key sources**: Meta-analyses on GBR outcomes, studies on xenogenic vs autogenous grafts, 3D-printing case series, ML-based risk prediction models

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **GBR outcomes**: Recent meta-analyses report low implant failure rates and favorable probing depth reduction + bone level gain post-GBR surgery.
- **Xenogenic bone substitutes**: Superior reduction in inflammation and better radiographic bone levels vs autogenous bone or non-GBR protocols.
- **3D-printed Ti mesh**: Enhanced precision for alveolar ridge augmentation; mesh exposure remains a significant complication.
- **Biodegradable Mg membranes**: Mechanical stability, biocompatibility, gradual resorption aligned with bone healing; promising alternative to collagen or non-resorbable membranes.
- **Risk factors confirmed**: Systemic conditions (diabetes, smoking), history of periodontitis are significant risk indicators for peri-implant bone loss and implant failure.
- **Postoperative pain**: Short-term increases associated with GBR using resorbable membranes; requires patient counseling.
- **CBCT**: Superior sensitivity/specificity for detecting dehiscence and infrabony lesions vs conventional intraoral radiography.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (not systematic) review — subject to selection bias; conclusions lack the rigor of SR/MA.
- Remaining gaps: predictability of regenerative outcomes, complete bacterial eradication from complex implant topographies (especially roughened/threaded surfaces).
- Personalized therapy (ML-based risk models, novel biomaterials) requires prospective RCT validation before routine clinical adoption.
- Long-term data on biodegradable Mg membranes and 3D-printed Ti meshes still limited.

## 7. Glossary

- **GBR (Guided Bone Regeneration, 골유도재생술)**: Surgical technique using barrier membranes ± bone graft to exclude soft tissue and direct bone healing into a defect.
- **Peri-implantitis (임플란트 주위염)**: Inflammatory condition involving progressive loss of bone around a dental implant.
- **Xenogenic graft (이종골 이식)**: Bone substitute derived from non-human species (e.g., bovine DBBM).
- **CBCT (Cone Beam Computed Tomography, 콘빔 CT)**: 3D radiographic imaging used to characterize bone defect morphology.
- **Mg membrane (생분해성 마그네슘 막)**: Biodegradable barrier membrane made of magnesium alloy that gradually resorbs while maintaining mechanical stability during bone healing.
- **Ti mesh (티타늄 메쉬)**: Non-resorbable or 3D-printed titanium scaffold used for space maintenance during GBR in complex ridge augmentation.
