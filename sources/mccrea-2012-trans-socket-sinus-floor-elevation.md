## One-line Summary
Case series (n=10) reporting a trans-socket osteotome approach: maxillary extraction socket — even with apical radiolucency or infection — used as the conduit to penetrate or fracture the sinus floor for immediate implant placement with bicortical fixation, without bone substitute, achieving high primary stability when residual bone depth >5 mm.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Clinical Advances in Periodontics, Volume 2, Issue 2, pp. 80–87
- **First published online**: 18 December 2017 (originally 2012)

## 2. Key Contributions
- First clinical and radiologic report (per author) describing **trans-socket transcrestal sinus floor elevation through an infected/radiolucent maxillary socket** at the time of extraction.
- Demonstrates that **periapical infection is not an absolute contraindication** to immediate implant + transcrestal sinus floor manipulation, provided the socket is thoroughly debrided and ≥5 mm bone remains apical to the sinus floor.
- Achieves bicortical fixation by intentionally penetrating/fracturing the sinus cortical floor with the implant apex.
- Uses **no bone substitute** — relies on blood clot and elevated Schneiderian membrane for healing.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Consecutive case series, 10 patients.
- Inclusion: planned extraction of a maxillary tooth with periapical radiolucency/infection in close proximity to sinus floor; ≥5 mm bone depth below sinus floor at planned implant site.
- Surgical sequence:
  1. Atraumatic extraction and thorough socket curettage/debridement.
  2. Osteotome osteotomy through the socket, 1–2 mm below sinus floor.
  3. Intentional penetration/fracture of sinus floor with osteotome to elevate Schneiderian membrane.
  4. Immediate placement of implant with apex engaging the elevated sinus cortex (bicortical fixation).
  5. No graft material introduced.
- Follow-up: clinical and radiographic, with outcome reported per case.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- All 10 implants achieved high primary stability sufficient for immediate placement.
- Bicortical engagement (crestal + sinus floor) recorded in all cases.
- No reported acute sinusitis or implant failures in the included case series follow-up.
- Healing occurred without sinus floor grafting — radiographic bone gain around implant apex observed in serial radiographs (qualitative).
- Demonstrates that prior periapical infection, after debridement, did not preclude osseointegration in this small series.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Small, single-operator, uncontrolled case series — no comparator.
- No standardised long-term survival or peri-implant marginal bone loss data quantitatively analysed.
- "Infected" socket criteria not microbiologically standardised; selection bias toward chronic, contained periapical lesions.
- Bicortical engagement carries theoretical risk of Schneiderian membrane perforation, implant displacement, or maxillary sinusitis if technique mismanaged.
- Generalisability limited without subsequent RCT or prospective cohort confirmation.

## 7. Glossary
- **Trans-socket sinus elevation**: transcrestal sinus floor lifting performed through the existing extraction socket rather than a fresh prepared osteotomy.
- **Bicortical fixation**: implant primary stability achieved by engagement of two cortical layers — crestal cortex and sinus floor cortex.
- **Schneiderian membrane**: ciliated pseudostratified epithelial lining of the maxillary sinus; preserved during sinus floor elevation.
- **Osteotome technique**: malleted, tapered instruments used to fracture and elevate the sinus floor without rotary instrumentation.
- **Periapical radiolucency**: radiographic finding indicating periapical bone resorption associated with pulpal/periapical pathology.
