## One-line Summary
Big-data retrospective (n=650k patients, 58 clinics, 2014–2021): amalgam annual failure rate 3.5% vs composite 4.5%; composite HR 1.29 overall, significantly higher in multi-surface restorations; no difference for single-surface.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Bioengineering 2024, 11, 579
- **DOI**: 10.3390/bioengineering11060579
- **Study type**: Retrospective cohort, real-world database
- **Setting**: 58 dental clinics, 440 dental units, Maccabi Healthcare Fund (Israel), 2014–2021

## 2. Key Contributions
- Largest real-world dataset comparing amalgam vs composite survival (>650,000 patients, ~260,905 treated patients)
- Annual failure rate: amalgam 3.5%, composite 4.5% (composite HR 1.29, p<0.001)
- **Single-surface restorations**: no significant difference between materials
- **Multi-surface restorations**: composite significantly higher failure rate
- Provides real-world evidence in era of mercury restriction (Minamata Convention context)
- Failure defined as: restoration replacement, root canal treatment, extraction within follow-up

## 3. Methodology
- **Design**: Retrospective cohort using electronic health records
- **n**: >650,000 patients; 260,905 with restorations during 2014–2021
- **Outcome**: Restoration failure (replacement, RCT, or extraction)
- **Analysis**: Kaplan-Meier survival + Cox proportional hazards regression
- **Confounders adjusted**: age, sex, number of surfaces, tooth type, operator

## 4. Key Results
| Material | Annual Failure Rate | HR vs Amalgam |
|---|---|---|
| Amalgam | 3.5% | reference |
| Composite | 4.5% | 1.29 (p<0.001) |
| Single-surface | NS between materials | — |
| Multi-surface | Composite significantly higher | p<0.001 |

## 5. Limitations
- Administrative database; no clinical exam data (probing depth, caries risk)
- Failure definition dependent on billing codes, not clinical criteria
- Operator variability across 440 units uncontrolled
- Material selection not randomized (selection bias possible)
