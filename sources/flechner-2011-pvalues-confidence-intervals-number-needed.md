## One-line Summary
Symposium tutorial (Indian J Urol 2011;27:532) using a single clinical chemoprevention scenario to teach the meaning and proper interpretation of p-values, 95% CIs, and NNT.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: Indian Journal of Urology 2011 Oct-Dec; Vol 27, Issue 4, p.532
- Symposium article in an EBM teaching series
- Authors: UCSF and UT Health Science Center San Antonio

## 2. Key Contributions
- Frames p-value, CI, and NNT as three complementary lenses on the same data — not interchangeable.
- Uses a 60-year-old patient post-negative biopsy asking about chemoprevention (5α-reductase inhibitor) to anchor the abstract concepts in a decision the urologist actually faces.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Single-scenario didactic walkthrough.
- p-value: probability of observed result (or more extreme) under null hypothesis; threshold convention (0.05) is convention, not truth.
- 95% CI: range of plausible true effect; if it excludes the null value, result is "significant" at α=0.05.
- NNT: 1/ARR — translates relative risk reduction into the practical question "how many patients must I treat to prevent one event".

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- No original empirical results.
- Reinforces: statistical significance ≠ clinical significance; a tight CI excluding null is more informative than a bare p<0.05; NNT lets a clinician weigh benefit against harms and cost.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Single-disease (prostate cancer chemoprevention) scenario; readers must transpose to their own field.
- Does not cover Bayesian alternatives or false discovery rates.

## 6. Related Work
- Pairs naturally with Monaghan 2021 (which expands OR/RR/AR/NNT into a multi-measure tutorial) and Barraclough 2011 (hazard ratios for time-to-event).

## 7. Glossary
- P-value: probability of observing the data, or more extreme, under H₀.
- 95% CI: interval that would contain the true parameter in 95% of replicated samples.
- NNT: Number Needed to Treat = 1 / Absolute Risk Reduction.
