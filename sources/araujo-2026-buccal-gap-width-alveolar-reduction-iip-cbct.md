## Why Ingested

[[immediate-implant/esthetic-soft-tissue/hamilton-2023-type1a-immediate-implant-immediate-loading-esthetic-sr-ma]] identified a buccal gap > 2 mm as a favorable modifier for *implant survival* (+3.1 PP) but did not quantify the dimensional/ridge-preservation payoff of that threshold. This Araújo 2026 retrospective CBCT study (n=28 maxillary central incisors, contralateral-tooth control) is the first to isolate gap width as a determinant of alveolar-process preservation, giving the > 2 mm rule a hard-tissue mechanism. It also directly contrasts [[immediate-implant/esthetic-soft-tissue/yang-2019-labial-bone-thickness-esthetics-iipp]] — finding that baseline buccal bone *thickness* (not gap) was non-significant for resorption in central-incisor sites with thin walls.

## One-line Summary

Retrospective CBCT cohort (n=28 maxillary central incisors, contralateral-tooth control, mean 6 y): DBBM-grafted buccal gap > 2 mm preserved > 90% of alveolar-process cross-sectional area (8.5% reduction) vs ~40% reduction with a narrow (≤ 2 mm) grafted gap (p<0.001).

## 한줄요약

후향적 CBCT 코호트(상악 중절치 28부위, 반대측 자연치 대조, 평균 6년): DBBM 이식한 협측 gap이 2 mm 초과면 치조제 단면적의 90% 이상 보존(8.5% 감소)했으나, 2 mm 이하 좁은 gap은 약 40% 흡수로 보존 실패(p<0.001).

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **First study to isolate the grafted buccal-gap width** (> 2 mm vs ≤ 2 mm) as a determinant of alveolar-process *dimensional preservation* after immediate implant placement (IIP) + DBBM, rather than survival or recession.
2. **Clean anatomical control**: restricted to maxillary central incisors with an *intact* buccal wall, using the contralateral pristine tooth as the within-subject baseline — avoiding the premolar dilution that confounds most IIP literature.
3. **Mechanistic reframe**: the healed buccal wall in the wide-gap group (median 2.1 mm) is *entirely newly formed bone within the grafted gap* — the original thin (~0.7 mm) bundle bone resorbs regardless; gap volume, not baseline wall thickness, drives preservation.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Sample**: 28 patients, one failing maxillary central incisor each, treated since 2003 by a single experienced periodontist (R.A.L.). 68% women, mean age 57 (SD 16).
- **Groups**: Wide gap > 2 mm (n=14) vs Narrow gap ≤ 2 mm (n=14), gap measured intraoperatively with UNC-15 probe + confirmed by photographs.
- **Protocol (Levine et al.)**: minimally invasive extraction, guided implant placement (Straumann/Nobel) 3 mm apical to future restoration margin, conical healing abutment, buccal gap packed firmly with DBBM (Geistlich Bio-Oss) to resistance; CTG (palatal) if gingival thickness < 1 mm (wide 64.3%, narrow 57.1%). Provisional at 3 mo, ceramic crown 2-3 mo later, SPT.
- **Imaging**: CBCT (CS 9300, FOV 8×8 cm, voxel 0.09 mm) at mean 6 ± 4 y (range 2-14); single blinded calibrated examiner (ICC ≥ 0.9). 3D implant template aligned to compensate for metallic artifact.
- **Primary outcome**: total reduction in cross-sectional area of the alveolar process (%) — implant ridge vs contralateral tooth. Secondary: basal bone area, linear width/height of AP, buccal bone thickness, basal bone width.
- **Stats**: parametric (cross-sectional, normal) + non-parametric (linear); simple + multiple linear regression; interaction terms GAP × function, GAP × CTG.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Primary — total cross-sectional AP reduction**: narrow **41.1% ± 20.2%** vs wide **8.5% ± 11%**, p<0.001. Implant-site area: wide 75.7 vs narrow 47.2 mm² (p=0.004), from near-identical tooth-site baselines (~80 mm²).
- **Reduction gradient (coronal→apical)**: coronal wide -28.4% vs narrow -66.8%; middle wide -0.5% vs narrow -37.1%; apical wide +6.9% vs narrow -17.6% (all p<0.001).
- **AP width reduction**: at 3 mm below crest wide -7.4% vs narrow -52.9% (p=0.001); at 5 mm wide -1.2% vs narrow -42.8% (p<0.001); at 1 mm wide -48.4% vs narrow -71.2% (p=0.052).
- **Buccal wall height reduction**: wide -1.1% ± 9.5% vs narrow -52.3% ± 41.5% (p<0.001).
- **Healed buccal bone thickness (implant sites)**: median wide 2.1 mm [1.7;2.4] vs narrow 0.5 mm [0;1]; significant at all levels (1/3/5 mm, p≤0.001).
- **Regression**: gap (>2 vs ≤2 mm) the only significant predictor — simple beta 32.6 (95% CI 19.9-45.2, p<0.001); adjusted beta 31.2 (95% CI 16.55-45.8, p<0.001). Age, gender, implant diameter, design, time in function, CTG, baseline AP width and buccal bone thickness all NS. GAP × function (p=0.513) and GAP × CTG (p=0.092) NS.
- **Basal bone** largely stable (NS area change); apex width more diminished in narrow group (-13.5% vs +7%, p=0.008), attributed to root/implant position rather than gap.
- Post hoc power 99.9% for the primary contrast.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Retrospective convenience sample; single operator (high skill — limits generalizability).
- Wide follow-up range (2-14 y); evolving implant designs/surfaces over the period (no significant effect detected, but residual confounding possible).
- CBCT artifact obscures extremely thin buccal walls despite template alignment.
- Contralateral-tooth design substitutes for, but cannot fully replace, baseline 3D data at the implant site; only intact-buccal-wall sockets included.
- Central-incisor-only — does not extend to premolar/posterior or to non-intact (dehisced) sockets.

## 6. Related Work

- Hamilton et al. 2023 (ITI Consensus SR+MA) — gap > 2 mm → higher implant survival (this paper supplies the dimensional mechanism).
- Yang et al. 2019 (prospective) — buccal bone thickness < 0.5 mm worsens resorption (contrasted: thickness NS here).
- Seyssens et al. (SR+MA) — IIP + socket grafting = 54% less horizontal buccal resorption vs IIP alone.
- Clementini et al. (RCT) — DBBM socket grafting reduces horizontal change with/without IIP.
- Araújo et al. (preclinical) — wide vs narrow gap healing; original thin buccal wall resorbs, new bone fills graft.

## 7. Glossary

- **IIP**: Immediate implant placement (Type 1) — implant into a fresh extraction socket.
- **Buccal gap**: horizontal space between the implant surface and the inner buccal socket wall; grafted to preserve ridge.
- **DBBM**: Deproteinized bovine bone mineral (Bio-Oss) — slow-resorbing xenograft used to fill the gap.
- **Alveolar process (AP) reduction**: % loss of bone cross-sectional area at the implant site vs the contralateral pristine tooth.
- **CTG**: Connective tissue graft — soft-tissue augmentation; here it did not affect hard-tissue preservation.
- **Bundle bone**: the inner socket wall (tooth-dependent); resorbs after extraction regardless of grafting.
