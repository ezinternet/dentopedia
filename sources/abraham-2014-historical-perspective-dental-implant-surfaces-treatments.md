## One-line Summary
Narrative review tracing dental implants from ~2500 BC (Egyptian ligature wire) through Brånemark's titanium osseointegration to modern surface treatments (machined, sand-blasted, etched, HA-coated, fluoride/statin).

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Narrative review / historical perspective
- **Journal**: The Open Dentistry Journal, 2014, 8 (Suppl 1-M2), pp. 50–55
- **DOI**: 10.2174/1874210601408010050
- **Affiliation**: Texas A&M University Health Science Center, Baylor College of Dentistry, Department of Periodontics, Dallas, TX
- **Primary Question**: What is the historical and developmental arc of dental implant materials and surface treatments?

## 2. Key Contributions
- Chronological timeline from ~2500 BC (Egyptian gold ligature wire) → ~500 BC (Etruscan soldered gold bands, ox bone replacements) → ~600 AD (Mayan shell implants in mandible; bone formation visible on 1970s radiographs) → ~800 AD (Honduran stone implant) → 1600s European tooth stabilization → 1700s Hunter's tooth-in-rooster-comb implantation → 1809 Maggiolo's gold tube → 1913 Greenfield's iridio-platinum hollow lattice → 1930s Strock brothers' Vitallium screws → 1940s Formiggini spiral implant → modern Brånemark titanium osseointegration.
- Categorizes contemporary surface modifications: machined, sand-blasted, acid-etched, sand-blasted + acid-etched (SLA), hydroxyapatite (HA) coatings, fluoride-impregnated, statin-loaded.
- Frames "modified surfaces" as the response to the need for *faster* osseointegration in the post-Brånemark era.
- Notes laminin coating as "the center of focus in present day implant endeavors" (as of 2014).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
This is a narrative review — no systematic search, no formal inclusion criteria, no quality appraisal. The structure is biographical/chronological, following named dentists and their contributions (Hunter, Maggiolo, Greenfield, Strock, Formiggini, Andres, Linkow, Brånemark) rather than thematic.

It serves as a teaching review for dental trainees and a historical anchor for clinical implant texts; the surface-treatment section is encyclopedic-style coverage rather than evidence synthesis.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
Not applicable in the typical sense — no quantitative outcomes. Useful claims for trainees:
- Vitallium = chromium-cobalt alloy used by the Strock brothers from 1930s.
- The Strock brothers first credited with successful endosteal (in-bone) implants.
- Formiggini termed "Father of Modern Implantology."
- Surface modifications enumerated include HA, composites, carbon, glass, ceramic, titanium oxide; treatments include sand-blasting, oxidation, fluoridation, acid-etching, and pharmaceutical medication (e.g., statins).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Not a systematic review; should not be cited as evidence for the efficacy of any specific surface.
- 2014 publication — predates many widely-used contemporary surfaces (e.g., UV photofunctionalization, INICELL/CA, nanoscale topography studies) and current osseointegration time benchmarks.
- No quantitative data on osseointegration kinetics or comparative survival of surface treatments.
- Statin and fluoride coatings discussed without clinical-outcomes evidence — should be read as historical signaling, not contemporary practice.
- Useful as a starting point for trainees and as a reference for the historical timeline section of a lecture, but not for clinical decision-making.

## 7. Glossary
- **Endosteal implant**: Implant placed *within* the bone (vs subperiosteal which sits on bone).
- **Vitallium**: Chromium-cobalt alloy used as early biocompatible orthopedic and dental metal.
- **Hydroxyapatite (HA) coating**: Calcium phosphate ceramic coating sprayed onto implant surface to enhance osteoconduction.
- **SLA (Sand-blasted, Large-grit, Acid-etched)**: Industry-standard rough titanium surface (Straumann SLA, etc.).
- **Osseointegration**: Direct, functional connection between living bone and a load-bearing artificial implant (Brånemark/Albrektsson definition).
- **Laminin**: Extracellular matrix protein; trialed as a biomimetic coating for implant surfaces.
