## One-line Summary
Chinese expert consensus from Beijing TongRen Hospital and national dental/ENT centers providing standardized classification, diagnostic criteria, and multidisciplinary treatment algorithms for odontogenic maxillary sinusitis (OMS), published in International Journal of Oral Science 2024.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: International Journal of Oral Science 2024;16:11
- **DOI**: 10.1038/s41368-024-00278-z
- **Type**: Expert consensus (OPEN ACCESS)
- **Lead institutions**: Department of Stomatology, Beijing TongRen Hospital; Department of Otolaryngology, Head and Neck Surgery, Beijing TongRen Hospital; Capital Medical University
- **Received**: 14 November 2023; Revised: 25 December 2023; Accepted: 2 January 2024

## 2. Key Contributions
- Provides the most structured, multi-institutional Chinese expert consensus for OMS diagnosis and treatment
- Establishes standardized classification and diagnostic criteria to address the lack of unique OMS clinical features
- Emphasizes that OMS's distinct infectious oral pathogeny makes it resistant to conventional rhinosinusitis therapy
- Proposes multidisciplinary treatment decisions integrating otolaryngologists and dentists
- Addresses treatment complications and their management
- Fills the gap identified by low publication volume and quality in OMS literature

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Basis**: Systematically reviewed literature + practical experience of expert members from major Chinese medical universities
- **Expert composition**: Otolaryngologists, oral surgeons, periodontists, implantologists, endodontists, oral pathologists from 15+ institutions across China
- **Content**: OMS characteristics, symptoms, classification, diagnosis, multidisciplinary treatment decisions, complications management
- **Format**: Open-access consensus statement in a peer-reviewed journal

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Epidemiology (from reviewed literature)
- Population-based incidence: approximately 15% of acute rhinosinusitis may be odontogenic (Wuokko-Landén retrospective cohort, n=385)
- Approximately one-quarter of chronic maxillary sinusitis attributable to dental pathologies
- CT-based studies: OMS accounts for 45–72% of cases with unilateral maxillary sinus opacification (studies of 130–190 patients)
- Meta-analysis (31 studies): aggregated CT prevalence 51% per maxillary sinus and 50% per patient
- Affects males and females almost equally; predominantly unilateral
- Most OMS cases are chronic but can present acutely and spread to extrasinus locations (orbital, intracranial, parapharyngeal)
- Can present with concomitant fungal ball

### Classification and Diagnosis
- OMS is distinct from other rhinosinusitis subtypes due to oral infectious pathogeny
- Diagnosis requires identifying dental/iatrogenic causative lesion in adjacent maxillary teeth
- CBCT is superior to conventional CT for dental-sinus relationship assessment

### Treatment Framework
- Combined dental treatment (elimination of oral cause) + nasal treatment (sinus drainage/clearance)
- Multi-disciplinary cooperation between ENT and dentistry is "absolutely urgent"
- Standardized treatment regimen proposed to replace ad hoc management
- Addresses common complications: OAF, recurrence, fungal ball co-existence

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Consensus statement (not SR or RCT); evidence level inherently limited
- Chinese institutional experience may not fully generalize to other healthcare systems
- OMS has "actually received little attention and has been underrepresented by relatively low publication volume and quality" — acknowledged by authors
- More high-quality RCTs needed for head-to-head comparison of treatment modalities

## 6. Related Work
- Psillas 2020 (J Dent Sci): comprehensive narrative review from Greece; similar scope, complementary perspective
- Wuokko-Landén (cited): retrospective cohort establishing 15% OMS rate in acute rhinosinusitis
- General OMS literature: multiple single-center CT studies (130–190 patients each) cited for 45–72% prevalence in unilateral opacification

## 7. Glossary
- **OMS (odontogenic maxillary sinusitis)**: Inflammation of the maxillary sinus secondary to adjacent infectious dental lesion or iatrogenic dental damage
- **Multidisciplinary treatment**: Coordinated care involving ENT (sinus surgery, drainage), oral surgeon (tooth/implant management), periodontist, implantologist as appropriate
- **Fungal ball**: Co-existing mycetoma in the maxillary sinus; may develop alongside OMS, particularly with displaced endodontic filling materials (zinc oxide–containing sealers can promote fungal growth)
- **Iatrogenic OMS**: OMS caused by dental procedures (sinus floor perforation during implant placement, endodontic material overfill, extraction with OAF)
- **Extrasinus spread**: Extension of infection beyond the sinus into orbital, intracranial, or parapharyngeal spaces — a serious complication requiring urgent management
- **CT prevalence**: Based on imaging evidence of unilateral maxillary opacification with identifiable dental cause
