## One-line Summary
SR+NMA of 12 RCTs evaluating Class II restorations at 6, 12, and 24 months — no statistically significant differences between bulk-fill subtypes and incremental layering for marginal adaptation, fractures, or retention; ormocer-based and overlay bulk-fill ranked highest (non-significantly) at 12–24 months.

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis
- **Published**: First published November 25, 2025; Journal of Esthetic and Restorative Dentistry, Volume 38, Issue 4, pp. 764–779
- **PROSPERO Registration**: CRD420251170292
- **Protocol**: PRISMA 2020 guidelines; RoB2 for risk of bias; CINeMA for confidence in evidence
- **Data availability**: Zenodo (https://zenodo.org/records/17401097)

## 2. Key Contributions
- First NMA (network meta-analysis) approach for this clinical question — allows simultaneous comparison and ranking of multiple bulk-fill subtypes against each other and incremental layering
- P-score ranking across 6, 12, and 24-month timepoints — minimal separation between techniques overall
- Ormocer-based and overlay bulk-fill composites showed highest (non-statistically superior) ranking probabilities at 12 and 24 months
- CINeMA evaluation showed predominantly low concerns for indirectness and risk of bias — robust transitivity of the network
- Confirmed equivalence regardless of bulk-fill viscosity or curing mode

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Databases**: 5 databases searched following PRISMA 2020
- **Inclusion**: RCTs on Class II posterior restorations comparing bulk-fill vs. incremental layering
- **Studies included**: 12 RCTs
- **Risk of bias**: RoB2 (Cochrane)
- **Evidence quality**: CINeMA framework
- **Statistical models**: Random-effects NMA at 6, 12, and 24 months follow-up
- **Outcomes**: Marginal adaptation, fractures, retention (relative risks)
- **Ranking**: P-scores for treatment ranking

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- No statistically significant differences between bulk-fill and incremental techniques at any timepoint (6, 12, 24 months)
- Risk ratios clustered around unity for all primary outcomes (marginal adaptation, fractures, retention)
- P-scores: minimal ranking variability overall
- **Ormocer-based** bulk-fill: highest ranking probability at 12 and 24 months (not statistically superior)
- **Overlay bulk-fill**: also high ranking at 12 and 24 months (not statistically superior)
- Most evidence rated low risk of bias; acceptable indirectness
- Viscosity and curing mode of bulk-fill do not significantly affect comparative outcomes

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Network geometry limited by 12 RCTs — indirect comparisons between some subtypes less reliable
- Follow-up limited to 24 months; long-term data on Class II restorations lacking
- Subtype classification (ormocer, overlay, flowable, sculptable) may not be consistently applied across trials
- Absence of standardized outcome reporting (CONSORT extensions) limits pooling precision

## 7. Glossary
- **NMA (Network Meta-Analysis)**: Meta-analytic method allowing simultaneous comparison of multiple treatments using both direct and indirect evidence
- **P-score**: Probability-based ranking metric in NMA; higher score = better ranking, but not indicative of statistical superiority
- **CINeMA**: Confidence in Network Meta-Analysis — framework for assessing quality of NMA evidence
- **Ormocer**: Organically modified ceramic — alternative composite matrix with reduced shrinkage properties
- **Overlay bulk-fill**: Bulk-fill composite designed for the full restoration thickness (base + occlusal surface)
- **Transitivity assumption**: NMA assumption that study populations and interventions are sufficiently similar to allow indirect comparisons
