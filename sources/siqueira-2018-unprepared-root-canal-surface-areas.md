## Why Ingested

This critical review quantifies how much of the canal wall instruments leave untouched (10–50% round canals, up to 80% oval canals) and lays out the rationale for supplementary disinfection — the mechanistic bridge between canal anatomy and persistent infection. It extends shaping work on instrument geometry/transportation in [[wiki/endodontics/shaping/kim-2021-protaper-waveone-trunatomy-canal-transportation]] and connects directly to irrigation-activation strategies in [[wiki/endodontics/irrigation/boutsioukis-2022-irrigants-irrigation-methods-status-directions]].

## One-line Summary

Critical review (Siqueira et al. 2018, Braz Oral Res) explaining why instruments leave substantial canal surface unprepared (~10–50% in round/small canals, up to 80% in oval/flattened canals; even higher apically), the resulting biofilm-persistence risk for post-treatment apical periodontitis, and strategies to compensate (adequate apical size, copious/renewed NaOCl, supplementary activation, anatomy-adapted techniques).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames apical periodontitis as a disease of infectious etiology whose successful treatment depends on infection control, and identifies **untouched (unprepared) canal surface area** as a central, under-appreciated cause of persistent infection.
- Compiles micro-CT evidence that **instruments leave 10–50% of the full canal surface unprepared in round/small canals**, and **10–80% in oval/flattened canals**, with even higher untouched fractions when only the **apical** surface is evaluated.
- Explains *why* areas stay untouched: final instrument smaller than initial canal diameter, **curvature** concentrating cutting on some walls, and irregular **oval / C-shaped / flattened** morphologies incompatible with round rotary preparations.
- Argues residual **biofilm in untouched recesses/isthmuses/ramifications** survives mechanical + chemical procedures and drives post-treatment apical periodontitis, and lays out **supplementary disinfection** strategies.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Critical narrative review integrating micro-CT surface-area studies, histology/histobacteriology of instrumented extracted teeth, and antimicrobial-efficacy (culture and molecular) studies.
- Structured around: antimicrobial effects of preparation → why bacteria escape instruments/irrigants → correlative methods to evaluate preparation → therapeutic strategies to reach untouched areas.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- After chemomechanical preparation, **bacteria still detectable in ~30–60%** of apical-periodontitis canals (paper-point samples, mostly main-canal lumen).
- **2.5% NaOCl** reduces bacterial levels 10²–10⁵-fold (~95–99% overall reduction); best effect with **large volume and frequent exchange**. **CHX** gives similar antimicrobial results.
- **Unprepared surface area**: round/small canals **~10–50%**; oval/flattened canals **~10–80%**; apical third worse than overall.
- Histology shows untouched walls retaining **predentin and pulp-tissue remnants** (e.g., oval mandibular incisors instrumented on only one side) — direct evidence instruments missed those walls.
- Therapeutic levers: adequate **apical preparation size**, preparation to full **working length**, **copious/renewed irrigant** with sufficient needle depth, **supplementary irrigant activation** and anatomy-adapted (e.g., reciprocating/adaptive, supplementary) approaches for oval/C-shaped canals.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative critical review, not a systematic review/meta-analysis; no pooled effect estimates.
- Most surface-area data are ex vivo micro-CT; the clinical link from untouched area → treatment failure is inferential.
- Optimal supplementary-disinfection protocol (activation method, apical size targets) is not resolved — flagged as needing further evidence.

## 7. Glossary

- **Unprepared (untouched) surface area**: portion of canal wall not contacted by instruments after preparation; refuge for residual biofilm.
- **Chemomechanical preparation**: combined mechanical instrumentation + chemical irrigation/medication to clean, disinfect, and shape the canal.
- **Oval / flattened / C-shaped canal**: non-round canal morphologies whose recesses resist round rotary preparation, leaving large untouched areas.
- **Isthmus / ramification / recess**: anatomical sub-spaces communicating with or branching off the main canal that instruments rarely reach.
- **Predentin**: unmineralized dentin layer on the canal wall; its persistence on a section proves the wall was not instrumented.
- **Supplementary disinfection**: adjunct measures (activated irrigation, increased apical size, interappointment medicament) to reach areas instruments miss.
