## One-line Summary
Systematic review analyzing 124 cases of late mandibular fracture following third molar removal, identifying risk factors, timing, and management strategies.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: International Journal of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery 2017;46(1):46-53
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.ijom.2016.09.018
- **Type**: Systematic Review
- **Year**: 2017

## 2. Key Contributions
- Comprehensive analysis of late-onset (>24h postoperative) mandibular angle fractures after third molar extraction
- Identifies key patient- and procedure-related risk factors predisposing to late fracture
- Characterizes typical time window for fracture occurrence
- Describes most common fracture patterns and management approaches
- Differentiates late fracture from intraoperative mandibular fracture

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Systematic review with case series analysis
- Literature search in PubMed, Medline, Cochrane, and other databases
- Included: case reports and case series describing late mandibular fracture (>24h postoperatively) following third molar extraction
- Total: 124 cases compiled and analyzed
- Data extracted: patient demographics, third molar classification, depth of impaction, time to fracture, precipitating event, treatment, outcome
- No meta-analysis due to nature of data (case-level reporting)

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Timing**: Most fractures occurred within 4 weeks postoperatively; peak risk at 1–3 weeks
- **Gender**: Male predominance (males at higher risk due to greater masticatory force and higher prevalence of deeply impacted third molars)
- **Tooth depth/classification**: Deeply impacted (horizontal and mesioangular) third molars associated with highest risk; Pell-Gregory Class III/C most common
- **Risk factors**: Deep impaction, osteoporosis/osteopenia, prior radiation therapy, large follicular cysts, aggressive bone removal, thin lingual plate, immediate loading/trauma after extraction
- **Precipitating event**: Many fractures triggered by normal chewing or minimal trauma
- **Management**: Most treated with open reduction internal fixation (ORIF); outcomes generally favorable
- **Fracture location**: Mandibular angle almost exclusively

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Data derived from case reports; likely significant underreporting
- Heterogeneous reporting of surgical details limits risk factor analysis
- No standardized definition of "late fracture" across included studies
- Need for prospective registries to better quantify true incidence

## 6. Related Work
- Related to literature on mandibular third molar surgical technique (ostectomy, tooth sectioning)
- Connects to biomechanical studies on mandibular strength post-extraction
- Informs surgical risk counseling and postoperative instruction protocols

## 7. Glossary
- **Late Mandibular Fracture**: Mandibular fracture occurring >24 hours after third molar extraction, typically at the angle region
- **Mandibular Angle**: Region posterior to second molar and inferior to third molar; structurally weakened by impacted tooth removal
- **Pell-Gregory Classification**: Classifies mandibular third molar impaction depth (A/B/C) and relationship to ramus (I/II/III)
- **Lingual Plate**: Thin cortical bone on the lingual aspect of mandibular third molar; at risk during extraction
- **ORIF**: Open Reduction Internal Fixation; surgical fracture repair with plates and screws
- **Follicular Cyst (Dentigerous Cyst)**: Cyst associated with crown of impacted tooth; large cysts weaken mandible significantly
