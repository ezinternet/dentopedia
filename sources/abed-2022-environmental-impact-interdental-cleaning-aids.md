## Why Ingested

The interdental-cleaning category is built almost entirely on clinical efficacy and adherence evidence (e.g. [[interdental-cleaning/yilmaz-2025-interdental-cleaning-tool-sustainability-rct]] addresses *behavioral/adherence* sustainability, and [[interdental-cleaning/mancinelli-lyle-2024-water-flosser-vs-interdental-brush-rct]] compares device efficacy), but it carries no *environmental* (planetary-sustainability) dimension. This first-of-kind comparative life-cycle assessment (LCA) of 8 interdental aids adds the green-dentistry axis — single-use floss picks are the worst environmental choice, bamboo/replaceable-head interdental brushes and reusable floss the best — so product recommendations in [[overviews/interdental-cleaning-devices-synthesis]] can weigh environmental impact alongside clinical need and cost.

## One-line Summary

Comparative life-cycle assessment (LCA, not a clinical trial) of 8 interdental cleaning aids — one person using each daily for 5 years across 16 environmental impact categories — finding single-use floss picks have the largest footprint (worst in 13/16 categories) while bamboo interdental brushes have the lowest in 5 categories including carbon (climate change).

## 한줄요약

치간 청소 도구 8종을 1인이 5년간 매일 사용하는 시나리오로 16개 환경영향 범주에 걸쳐 비교한 전과정평가(Life Cycle Assessment, LCA; 임상시험 아님). 일회용 플로스픽(floss pick)이 16개 중 13개 범주에서 가장 큰 환경발자국을 남겼고, 대나무 치간칫솔(bamboo IDB)이 탄소(기후변화) 포함 5개 범주에서 가장 작은 발자국을 기록.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **First quantification** of the environmental footprint of different types of interdental cleaning aids (floss and interdental brushes, IDBs). Prior LCA work in dentistry covered toothbrushes; interdental aids had never been measured.
2. Demonstrates that **carbon footprinting alone is insufficient** — a full 16-category LCA reveals no single "best" product, and rankings shift across impact categories.
3. Identifies **single-use floss picks as the worst overall** (highest impact in 13/16 categories), driven mainly by the polypropylene plastic handle (49% of the floss pick's carbon footprint).
4. Provides a **clinical translation**: where floss is indicated, regular/sponge/bamboo floss beat floss picks; where IDBs are indicated, weekly reusable brushes (bamboo handle or reusable plastic handle + replaceable heads) beat single-use IDB picks.
5. Adds the **green-dentistry / planetary-health axis** to interdental product selection alongside clinical need and cost.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Attributional comparative LCA using physical allocation by mass; software OpenLCA v1.8 with the Ecoinvent v3.7 reference database; methodology following ISO standards and EU Product Environmental Footprint (PEF) guidance.
- **Functional unit (baseline scenario)**: one individual using an interdental cleaning aid **every day for 5 years** to prevent/manage periodontal disease. The 5-year unit was chosen to allow comparison with a prior toothbrush LCA (Lyne et al.).
- **8 products compared** (4 floss + 4 IDB), best-selling exemplars from Amazon UK (brands anonymized):
  1. **Regular floss** — nylon floss roll in plastic dispenser
  2. **Sponge floss** — pre-cut expanded floss for appliances/prosthetics
  3. **Floss picks** — nylon floss fixed to a plastic handle (single-use)
  4. **Bamboo floss** — bamboo floss roll in a glass jar
  5. **Regular IDB** — plastic-handle IDB, changed weekly
  6. **IDB picks** — rubber brush head on plastic handle, single-use
  7. **Replaceable-head IDB** — reusable handle + replaceable heads, changed weekly
  8. **Bamboo IDB** — bamboo-handle IDB, changed weekly
- **System boundaries (cradle-to-grave)**: raw materials (dismantled and weighed to 0.01 g; quantity needed over 5 yr calculated), manufacture (manufacturer-supplied energy data), transport (6/8 made in Europe, 2 in China; modeled by weight × distance × mode), retail (excluded — assumed equal), consumer use (UK user, unheated tap water for cleaning weekly IDBs), disposal (UK recycling where possible).
- **Primary outcome**: life-cycle impact assessment (LCIA) across **16 impact categories** spanning ecosystem quality (acidification, freshwater/marine/terrestrial eutrophication, freshwater ecotoxicity), human health (carcinogenic/non-carcinogenic effects, ionizing radiation, respiratory inorganics, photochemical ozone, ozone depletion, climate change), and planetary resource use (land, fossil fuel, minerals/metals, water).
- **Secondary outcomes**: normalized data, disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), and contribution analysis.
- **Key limitation in method**: LCA does not yield p-values or confidence intervals — results are interpreted by descriptive statistics only.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Worst overall**: **floss picks** — largest footprint in **13 of 16** impact categories. IDB picks were second worst.
- **Lowest footprint by category** (no single product won everything):
  - **Bamboo IDB** — lowest in **5** categories: climate change, freshwater eutrophication, ionizing radiation, fossil use, mineral/metal use.
  - **Replaceable-head IDB** — lowest in **4**: acidification, marine eutrophication, terrestrial eutrophication, photochemical ozone creation.
  - **Regular floss** — lowest in **3**: non-carcinogenic effects, respiratory inorganics, land use.
  - **Sponge floss** — lowest in **3**: freshwater ecotoxicity, carcinogenic effects, ozone layer depletion.
  - **Bamboo floss** — lowest in **1**: water use.
- **Carbon (climate change, kg CO₂e over 5 yr)**: floss picks **11.42** vs bamboo IDB **1.31** — bamboo IDB produced ~48% less CO₂ than a regular IDB. (For reference, a plastic toothbrush over 5 yr = 25.6 kg CO₂e, more than any interdental aid here.)
- **DALYs** (scaled to entire UK adult population, ~52.9M, over 5 yr): floss pick **753 DALYs** (highest), IDB picks **412**, bamboo floss **125** (lowest). Regular floss had 75% lower DALY impact than floss picks; regular IDBs 57% lower than IDB picks. DALYs were driven almost entirely by climate change (44–99%) and carcinogenic effects (0–64%).
- **Contribution analysis**: for floss picks, the polypropylene plastic handle alone = 49% of the carbon footprint; for the bamboo IDB, the bamboo handle = just 5%.
- **Context**: even the worst aid's environmental burden is far smaller than the burden of established periodontal disease (e.g. smoking-attributable periodontitis ≈ 251,160 DALYs), so the planetary benefit of preventing disease likely outweighs the aids' environmental cost.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Manufacturer-dependent inventory**: model relied on manufacturer-supplied data; where unavailable, reasonable assumptions were made (listed in the appendix), which may shift results. No legal obligation exists for manufacturers to report footprints, even for "eco-friendly"/"sustainable"-labelled products.
- **8 sample products only** (best-sellers on Amazon UK) — may not represent the full UK/international market; all assumed equally clinically effective.
- **No electronic interdental aids** (water flossers / air flossers) included — assumed worse than manual based on a prior toothbrush LCA, but not directly measured.
- **UK-based transport assumptions** — footprints may differ in other countries (transport was only 1.3–10.2% of carbon footprint here).
- **LCA interpretability**: no p-values/CIs; three toxicity categories (freshwater ecotoxicity, carcinogenic, non-carcinogenic effects) are under PEF methodological review and need cautious interpretation.
- **Bamboo is not unambiguously green** — requires water/fertilizer, reduces biodiversity, and rising demand may worsen its footprint; recycled plastic may eventually outperform bamboo (as found for toothbrushes).
- **Future work**: quantify the DALY "gain" from preventing periodontal disease vs the DALY "loss" from the aids' environmental impact.

## 6. Related Work

- Lyne et al. — prior LCA of manual vs electric toothbrushes (functional-unit and method template for this study; plastic toothbrush = 25.6 kg CO₂e/5 yr).
- Duane et al. — environmental footprint of toothbrush types (bamboo/replaceable-head better than traditional/electric; recycled plastic best).
- Worthington et al. — systematic review: interdental aids reduce gingivitis vs toothbrushing alone; weak preference for IDBs over floss.
- Sanz et al. (EFP) / Chapple et al. — European periodontal guidelines favoring IDBs; clinical basis for recommending interdental cleaning.
- Schwendicke et al. — global smoking-attributable periodontitis DALY burden (~251,160), used as the disease-burden comparator.

## 7. Glossary

- **LCA (Life Cycle Assessment / analysis)**: cradle-to-grave environmental accounting of a product across raw materials, manufacture, transport, use, and disposal — more comprehensive than carbon footprinting alone.
- **LCIA (Life Cycle Impact Assessment)**: the impact-category results of an LCA (here, 16 categories).
- **Functional unit**: the standardized usage scenario enabling fair comparison (here, one person using an aid daily for 5 years).
- **Impact category**: a discrete measure of environmental sustainability (e.g. climate change, ozone depletion, water use).
- **kg CO₂e (carbon dioxide equivalent)**: unit of greenhouse-gas / climate-change potential.
- **DALY (Disability-Adjusted Life Year)**: a year of healthy life lost; aggregates human-health impact categories into one figure.
- **PEF (Product Environmental Footprint)**: EU-recommended LCA methodology guidance followed here.
- **IDB (inter-dental brush)**: small brush for cleaning between teeth.
- **Floss pick / IDB pick**: single-use floss or rubber brush head fixed to a plastic handle.
- **Attributional LCA**: LCA that allocates environmental burdens to a product based on its physical share (here, by mass).
