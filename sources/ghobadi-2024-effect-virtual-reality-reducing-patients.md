## Why Ingested

Adds the first **adult, intraoperative VR distraction RCT** to a dental-anxiety folder that is otherwise pediatric-heavy, and reinforces [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/pandey-2025-monitoring-patient-anxiety-pain-physiological-rct]] by independently confirming — with the same psychophysiological-validation approach (EMG/EKG/SCR alongside self-report) — that a non-pharmacological behavioral intervention measurably lowers both subjective and objective anxiety/pain markers during dental procedures. Pairs with a pediatric VR systematic review being ingested concurrently.

## One-line Summary

Randomized controlled crossover trial (n=73 adults, each patient own control across two implant-surgery sessions) showing VR distraction with high-resolution headset and natural-scene content significantly reduced experienced pain (−1.71 NRS), state anxiety (STAI-S −3.45), and dental anxiety (MDAS −4.55) versus no VR, with physiological confirmation (lower EMG, heart rate, GSR) and 90.4% wanting to reuse VR.

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: The effect of virtual reality on reducing patients' anxiety and pain during dental implant surgery
- **Authors**: Alireza Ghobadi, Hedaiat Moradpoor, Hamid Sharini, Habibolah Khazaie, Pooya Moradpoor (Kermanshah University of Medical Sciences, Iran)
- **Journal**: BMC Oral Health, 2024;24(1):186
- **DOI**: 10.1186/s12903-024-03904-8 (PMID 38317209, PMCID PMC10845398)
- **Study type**: Randomized controlled crossover clinical trial (RCT)
- **Registration**: IRCT20230612058461N1; ethics IR.KUMS.REC.1402.076

## 2. Key Contributions

1. First RCT to test VR distraction specifically in **adult dental implant surgery** — a procedure named by the authors as among the most anxiety-provoking in dentistry.
2. **Within-subject crossover design** (each patient receives VR on one first-molar implant and no-VR on the contralateral first molar), eliminating between-subject pain-threshold/anxiety confounding.
3. **Dual outcome validation**: psychometric self-report (STAI, MDAS, NRS) corroborated by objective psychophysiology (EMG, EKG/HRV/heart rate, SCR/GSR) via a 10-channel biofeedback system.
4. Tested two cognitive hypotheses beyond pain/anxiety: VR reduces **memory vividness** (Elaborated Intrusions theory) and **time perception** — both confirmed.
5. Documents a clinically important **trade-off**: surgeon distress was *higher* with VR (bulky headset impeding access), though it declined with experience (dissatisfaction 9.2 → 5.4 across patients).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Participants**: 215 screened → 90 eligible → 17 dropouts → 73 analyzed (36 M / 37 F; mean age 44.29 ± 12.98 y). 10% treatment discount for participation.
- **Intervention**: Oculus Quest 2 (Meta), 3664×1920 px, 100° FOV, 90 Hz, external speakers so patient could hear surgeon; natural-scene VR content. Control = identical surgery without headset.
- **Standardization**: every session fixed at 25 min actual operative time (surgeon padded short cases to a clock); identical implants (WhiteSky, Bredent) at sites 36/46; inferior alveolar nerve block; identical antibiotic/analgesic protocol; patients attended alone in a silent waiting room.
- **Outcomes**: expected & experienced pain (NRS 0–10); STAI-S/STAI-T; MDAS; satisfaction (NRS); Treatment Distress Rating (surgeon); memory vividness (phone 1 wk later, 5 items); time-perception ratio; EMG (SCM), EKG (HR, HRV), SCR/GSR.
- **Sample size**: powered (α=0.05, 90%) from Tanja-Dijkstra et al. → minimum 70 (PASS).
- **Analysis**: Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) modeling intervention type, gender, age; intention-to-treat; SPSS v18; α=0.05.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

| Outcome | VR vs no-VR effect | p |
|---|---|---|
| Experienced pain (NRS) | −1.712 units | <0.001 |
| Expected pain (NRS) | no significant effect | 0.526 |
| State anxiety (STAI-S) | −3.452 units | <0.001 |
| Trait anxiety (STAI-T) | no significant effect | 0.382 |
| Dental anxiety (MDAS) | −4.548 units | <0.001 |
| Patient satisfaction | +1.137 units | <0.001 |
| Memory vividness | −0.689 units | <0.001 |
| Time perception ratio | −0.128 units | <0.001 |
| **Surgeon distress (TDR)** | **+3.452 units (worse)** | <0.001 |
| EMG RMS amplitude | −14.158 | <0.001 |
| EMG median frequency | +12.766 | <0.001 |
| EMG power >100 Hz | −18.648 | <0.001 |
| Heart rate | −10.300 | <0.001 |
| HRV (ms) | −3.752 (did not support hypothesis) | <0.001 |
| GSR RMS | −5.389 | <0.001 |

- **90.4% of patients requested to reuse the headset** at future visits.
- Gender had no significant effect on any anxiety/pain outcome; age affected only satisfaction (−0.037/year — younger patients more satisfied).
- Higher-baseline-anxiety patients showed the **largest reductions in memory vividness**.
- VR helped most when anxiety root was a discrete trigger (injection, room, the word "surgery"); less helpful for fear of being vulnerable/unaware during surgery.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Headset bulkiness** impeded surgical access, isolation, and visibility — the dominant practical barrier; surgeon distress *increased* with VR (improving only with habituation).
- HRV moved opposite to the predicted direction (decreased), partially discordant with other physiological markers.
- Single operator, single center, Iranian sample; satisfaction confounded by a participation discount.
- Some patients (notably elderly, unfamiliar with technology) declined future VR despite high satisfaction; a few felt uneasy not seeing surroundings.
- Authors call for higher-quality, smaller, lighter headsets in future trials.

## 6. Related Work

- Tanja-Dijkstra et al. — VR in routine dental treatment; found pain reduction only in nature (coastal) environment and no memory-vividness effect (low-pain context), motivating this high-pain implant test.
- Hoffman et al. — fMRI evidence VR reduces pain-related brain activity; equipment quality matters.
- Kipping et al. — VR reduced analgesic use vs standard distraction.

## 7. Glossary

- **STAI**: State-Trait Anxiety Inventory; STAI-S = state (situational), STAI-T = trait (dispositional), each 20–80.
- **MDAS**: Modified Dental Anxiety Scale; 5 items, total 5–25.
- **NRS**: Numerical Rating Scale (0–10) for pain/satisfaction.
- **GEE**: Generalized Estimating Equations — regression for correlated/repeated-measures data (suits crossover design).
- **EMG / EKG / SCR (GSR)**: electromyography (muscle tension), electrocardiography (heart rate/HRV), skin conductance response (sympathetic arousal).
- **EI theory (Elaborated Intrusions)**: cognitive model predicting that competing for attention reduces the vividness and intrusiveness of distressing memories.
- **Crossover trial**: each participant serves as their own control across sequential conditions.
