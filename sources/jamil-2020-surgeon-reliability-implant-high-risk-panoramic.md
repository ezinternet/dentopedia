## One-line Summary
Prospective study (73 patients, 148 posterior implants: 68 maxilla, 80 mandible) comparing the surgeon's panoramic-radiograph–based implant-length choice against post-operative CBCT-measured available bone height; 93.2% of decisions underestimated available bone, and in 73.9% of those, remaining bone after placement was <2 mm — concluding panoramic radiographs are "reliable" only because surgeons systematically choose short implants, with 5 cases of mandibular transient paresthesia among overestimated insertions.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Consecutive 73 patients (predominantly female) receiving 148 implants in posterior premolar/molar sites adjacent to maxillary sinus (MS) or mandibular canal (MC).
- Surgeon selected implant length **using preoperative panoramic radiograph only**.
- Postoperatively, **CBCT** measured the true available bone height (ABH) from apical end of implant to MS floor or roof of MC.
- Discrepancy classified as **underestimation** (chose shorter implant than ABH allowed) or **overestimation** (implant violated structure).
- Complications recorded: sinus perforation, IAN paresthesia.
- χ² and Fisher's tests for posterior maxilla vs mandible comparison.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Quantifies surgeon panoramic-only decision accuracy against CBCT ground truth in the **posterior region** — the highest-risk implant zone.
- Shows **systematic conservative bias**: surgeons pick implants substantially shorter than the bone actually permits.
- Identifies **posterior mandible** as the site of higher overestimation risk vs posterior maxilla.
- Reports a real-world rate of **transient IAN paresthesia** (5/80 mandibular = 6.25%) tied to length overestimation.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **n implants**: 148 (68 maxilla / 80 mandible).
- **Underestimation rate**: **93.2%** (138/148).
- **Remaining bone after placement <2 mm** in underestimated cases: **73.9%**.
- **Remaining bone ≥2 mm** only 26.1% (significant, p<0.01).
- Overestimation in posterior mandible significantly higher than posterior maxilla.
- **Transient paresthesia**: 5 cases, all in overestimated mandibular implants.
- Conclusion of authors: panoramic-based selection is "reliable" but only because of conservative bias.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- "Reliability" here equates to *not causing complications* — does not assess optimality of implant length for biomechanics / crown-to-implant ratio.
- Underestimation may sacrifice long-term success (short implants in poor-density posterior maxilla).
- Single-center, single-surgeon-style decision-making.
- No comparison arm where the surgeon had CBCT preoperatively.
- Sample skewed female; learning-curve effect not analyzed.

## 7. Glossary
- **ABH (Available Bone Height)**: vertical bone from alveolar crest to the next anatomical limit (sinus floor, mandibular canal roof).
- **Panoramic radiograph**: 2D rotational tomogram; magnification ~25%, lacks third dimension.
- **CBCT (Cone-Beam Computed Tomography)**: 3D dental imaging; magnification ≈ 1:1, isotropic voxels.
- **IAN (Inferior Alveolar Nerve)**: sensory nerve traversing the mandibular canal; injury → paresthesia/dysesthesia of lower lip/chin.
- **Underestimation / Overestimation**: implant length shorter / longer than ABH would permit safely.
- **Transient paresthesia**: temporary sensory deficit, typically resolves within weeks to months.
