## One-line Summary
Comprehensive narrative review by Breschi's group tracking the evolution from 3-step etch-and-rinse to universal adhesives, with emphasis on hybrid layer degradation mechanisms and biomodification strategies to extend bond longevity.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Dental Materials 41 (2025) 141–158
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.dental.2024.11.011
- **Lead institution**: University of Bologna (DIBINEM)
- **Type**: Narrative review (PubMed/Scopus/Web of Science)

## 2. Key Contributions
- Authoritative synthesis from one of the most cited groups in adhesive dentistry (Breschi, Mazzoni)
- Classifies adhesives by smear-layer interaction (EAR vs SE) — rejects the obsolete "generation" terminology as marketing-driven
- Tracks hybrid layer (HL) degradation mechanisms: hydrolytic degradation, endogenous MMP/cysteine cathepsin activity, water sorption, unbound monomers
- Reviews 10+ HL-stabilization strategies: MMP inhibitors (chlorhexidine, EDC), ethanol-wet bonding, biomodification (proanthocyanidin, riboflavin/UV), hydrophobic coating, dimethyl sulfoxide, bioactive monomers
- Discusses "universal" adhesive limitations — not truly universal across all clinical scenarios; operator knowledge of protocol still critical
- Emphasizes Bis-GMA replacement trend with bio-safer monomers

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Design**: Narrative review (not systematic)
- **Databases**: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science
- **Keywords**: dental substrate structure, hybrid layer degradation mechanisms, contrast strategies
- **Structure**: (1) Adhesive systems overview → (2) EAR vs SE → (3) Universal adhesives → (4) HL degradation mechanisms → (5) Strategies to stabilize HL

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
This is a review without quantitative meta-analysis. Major qualitative findings:
- EAR with 32–37% phosphoric acid remains gold standard for enamel bonding
- Universal adhesives can be applied in either EAR or SE mode; enamel selective-etch is widely recommended
- 3-step EAR adhesives retain superior long-term performance over simplified systems
- MMP inhibition (chlorhexidine 2% rinse, EDC primer) clinically extends HL stability
- Ethanol-wet bonding improves resin infiltration but technique-sensitive
- Polymer cross-linking agents (proanthocyanidin, riboflavin/UV) enhance dentin collagen resistance to degradation

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Narrative format — no quantitative synthesis
- Many biomodification strategies remain in vitro; bench-to-chairside translation incomplete
- Long-term (>10y) clinical data still scarce for universal adhesives
- "Universal" terminology criticized as misleading; standardized clinical protocols still lacking

## 6. Related Work
- Updates and synthesizes work from Pashley, Tay, Nakabayashi (classical hybrid layer theory)
- Aligns with Hong 2021 (clinical EAR vs SE meta-analysis), Hardan 2021 (universal adhesive bond strength meta-analysis)
- Companion to Mazzoni-led MMP inhibition body of work

## 7. Glossary
- **Hybrid Layer (HL)**: Interdiffusion zone of resin monomers and demineralized dentin collagen; primary mechanism of micromechanical retention
- **MMP (Matrix Metalloproteinase, 매트릭스 메탈로프로테이나제)**: Endogenous dentin enzyme that degrades collagen; activated during acid etching
- **EAR (Etch-and-Rinse, 산부식-세척)**: 2- or 3-step adhesive requiring separate phosphoric acid etch
- **SE (Self-Etch, 자가산부식)**: Acidic primer that simultaneously demineralizes and infiltrates dentin without rinse step
- **Universal adhesive**: Single-bottle adhesive usable in EAR, selective-etch, or SE modes; contains both hydrophilic/hydrophobic monomers + 10-MDP
- **Bis-GMA**: Bisphenol-A glycidyl methacrylate; Bowen's resin; primary matrix monomer in dental composites
- **10-MDP**: 10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate; functional monomer with strong chemical bond to hydroxyapatite and zirconia
