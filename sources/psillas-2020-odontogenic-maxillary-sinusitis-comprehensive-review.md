## One-line Summary
Comprehensive narrative review covering anatomy, epidemiology, etiology, bacteriology, diagnosis, and treatment of odontogenic maxillary sinusitis (OMS); surgical treatment combining dental surgery with endoscopic sinus surgery (ESS) is the standard; multidisciplinary ENT + dental team is essential.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Journal of Dental Sciences 2021;16:474–481 (available online 22 August 2020)
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.jds.2020.08.001
- **Institution**: 1st Academic ENT Department, Aristotle University of Thessaloniki, AHEPA Hospital, Greece; School of Dentistry, Aristotle University of Thessaloniki
- **Study type**: Narrative review
- **Search**: PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, ScienceDirect; articles after 2000; search period January 2018 – January 2019

## 2. Key Contributions
- Synthesized current evidence on OMS from a dual ENT and dental perspective
- Quantified epidemiologic burden: approximately 30% of unilateral maxillary sinusitis cases may have dental pathology; incidence up to 51.8% by Maillet et al.
- Characterized the anatomical basis: maxillary second molar roots are in closest proximity to the sinus floor, followed by first molar, third molar, second premolar, first premolar
- Identified the most common etiologies: dentoalveolar surgery, odontogenic infection, oroantral fistula (OAF), dental implants, foreign body (root canal filling material)
- Provided evidence for the multidisciplinary treatment mandate: ENT for sinus disease + dental for causative tooth/implant

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Bibliographic search: PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, ScienceDirect
- Inclusion: articles after 2000, discussing anatomy/epidemiology/etiology/bacteriology/diagnosis/treatment of OMS
- Free-term search: "odontogenic sinusitis"
- Narrative (not systematic) methodology; findings presented thematically

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Anatomy
- Maxillary sinus reaches full development at 12–14 years (permanent tooth eruption); average volume 15–20 mL
- Second molar roots in closest proximity to sinus floor; followed by first molar, third molar, second premolar, first premolar
- Alveolar bone thins with age → mucoperiosteum (Schneiderian membrane) may be the only barrier
- Sinus pneumatization can cause premolars, canines, and third molars to protrude into sinus

### Epidemiology
- Before 1970s: ~10–12% of OMS cases attributed to odontogenic infections
- Maillet et al.: incidence up to 51.8%
- Patel and Ferguson: approximately 30% of unilateral maxillary sinusitis has dental etiology
- Incidence has increased with widespread use of FESS (functional endoscopic sinus surgery)

### Etiology
- **Most common**: Dentoalveolar surgery / odontogenic infection → Schneiderian membrane perforation
- **Troeltzsch et al.**: Dentoalveolar surgical intervention with subsequent oroantral fistula (OAF) formation
- Other causes: dental implants (displacement into sinus, peri-implantitis), root canal treatment (overfilling with material entering sinus), apical periodontitis, periodontal disease
- Periapical infections of the maxillary posterior teeth can migrate through alveolar bone → sinus

### Bacteriology
- OMS flora differs from rhinogenic sinusitis: mixed aerobic-anaerobic; includes streptococci, anaerobes, gram-negative rods
- Unlike non-odontogenic sinusitis, OMS is frequently polymicrobial and includes anaerobic species from the oral cavity

### Diagnosis
- History of dental treatment in posterior maxilla
- Unilateral nasal symptoms (congestion, discharge, foul odor)
- CT: unilateral maxillary opacification, presence of periapical pathology, displaced dental material
- CBCT: superior spatial resolution for dental relationship to sinus floor
- Nasal endoscopy: mucosal thickening, pus in middle meatus

### Treatment
- **Principle**: Eliminate the dental cause + restore sinus drainage
- **Dental surgery**: Extraction, endodontic treatment, implant removal, repair of OAF
- **ESS (functional endoscopic sinus surgery)**: Middle meatus antrostomy for drainage; removal of infected/necrotic tissue
- Combined dental + ESS preferred; dental surgery alone may be insufficient if mucosal changes established
- Multidisciplinary team (ENT + oral surgeon/dentist) mandated for optimal outcomes
- **Antibiotic therapy**: Adjunctive; not definitive treatment

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Narrative (not systematic) review; no pooled quantitative analysis
- Heterogeneous primary literature with variable diagnostic criteria
- Limited comparative data on dental-only vs. combined dental + ESS outcomes
- No standardized classification system for OMS severity used across included studies

## 6. Related Work
- Lin 2024 (Int J Oral Sci): expert consensus on multidisciplinary OMS treatment; more recent and structured guidelines
- General OMS literature: Maillet, Patel-Ferguson, Troeltzsch (cited within the review)

## 7. Glossary
- **OMS (odontogenic maxillary sinusitis)**: Sinusitis of the maxillary sinus secondary to dental or dentoalveolar pathology
- **Schneiderian membrane**: The mucoperiosteum lining the paranasal sinuses; acts as the barrier between maxillary sinus and oral cavity
- **OAF (oroantral fistula)**: Abnormal communication between the oral cavity and the maxillary sinus, typically from extraction or implant complications
- **ESS (functional endoscopic sinus surgery)**: Endoscopic surgical procedure to restore maxillary sinus drainage via middle meatus antrostomy
- **Pneumatization**: Expansion of the maxillary sinus into surrounding bone with increasing age
- **FESS**: Functional endoscopic sinus surgery — standard ENT approach for chronic sinusitis
