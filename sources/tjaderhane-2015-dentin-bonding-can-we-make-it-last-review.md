## Why Ingested

Durability review anchoring the dentin-adhesion hybrid-layer batch. It frames the mechanistic problem — endogenous proteases (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) plus the hydrophilicity of contemporary adhesives — that explains hybrid-layer degradation, and catalogs the countermeasures (enzyme inhibitors, collagen cross-linking, water elimination). Reinforces [[wiki/resin-bonding/liu-2011-limitations-bonding-dentin-bond-degradation-review]], which lays out the same degradation limitations of dentin bonding.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Buonocore Lecture) arguing that dentin bond durability is undermined by endogenous collagenolytic enzymes (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) and adhesive hydrophilicity, and systematically cataloging countermeasures including chlorhexidine, alternative MMP inhibitors, collagen cross-linkers, ethanol-wet bonding, and biomimetic remineralization.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides a comprehensive framework for why hybrid-layer bonds fail: (1) **endogenous collagenolytic enzymes** (MMPs: MMP-2, -3, -8, -9, -20; cysteine cathepsins B and K) and (2) **adhesive hydrophilicity** (insufficient resin impregnation, phase separation, low polymerization rate).
- Characterizes dentin substrate heterogeneity: mineral ≈50 vol%, collagen + noncollagenous proteins ≈30 vol%, water ≈20 vol%; tubule density and permeability vary by depth and caries status, directly affecting bond quality and durability.
- Identifies that both ER and SE adhesive acidity activates latent dentinal MMPs, accelerating collagen degradation.
- Demonstrates quantitatively (Figure 3 meta-analysis of 25 studies, 102 groups) that chlorhexidine (CHX) significantly reduces the rate of bond strength loss vs controls.
- Catalogs five countermeasure families: (A) enzyme inhibitors (CHX, BAC, tetracyclines/analogs, bisphosphonates, QAMs, DMSO, zinc); (B) 10-MDP chemical bonding; (C) collagen cross-linkers (glutaraldehyde, carbodiimide, proanthocyanidins, riboflavin, genipin); (D) ethanol-wet bonding; (E) biomimetic remineralization.
- Establishes the clinical principle: CHX can and should be used until alternatives are proven equally safe and effective.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative review synthesizing the principles, current status, and future directions of techniques designed to prevent loss of the hybrid layer and long-term bond strength. Covers literature on dentin substrate biology, mechanisms of bond degradation, and five categories of countermeasures. Includes a pooled graphical analysis (Figure 3) of 25 CHX studies to illustrate magnitude of protection but does not perform formal meta-analysis with risk-of-bias assessment. 15 pages, 150 references.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Substrate factors affecting durability
- Deep dentin bond strength is 30–50% lower than superficial dentin immediately; even lower with caries-affected (20–50% reduction) and caries-infected dentin.
- Collagen loss in the hybrid layer was first identified ~15 years before publication, confirmed by many morphological and bond-strength studies.
- MMP-2, -9, -8, -3, -20 and cysteine cathepsins B and K are all present in intact human dentin; cathepsin K/B and MMP-2/-9 dramatically increase in caries-affected dentin.

### Enzyme inhibitors
- **Chlorhexidine (CHX):** Inhibits collagenases and gelatinases. A figure-3 pooled analysis of ≥0.2% CHX studies (≥6 months, in vitro and in vivo) shows significantly lower bond strength reduction rates in CHX-treated groups vs controls (p<0.001 for two-step ER; p<0.05 for SE). Application: 15–60 sec of 0.2–2.0% CHX on acid-etched dentin before primer. CHX incorporated in SE primer inhibits MMP and prevents bond strength loss in vitro. CHX in acid conditioner reduces nanoleakage and bond strength loss for up to 2 years. One commercialized product (Peak Universal Bond, 0.2% CHX) launched but long-term clinical evidence pending. CHX leaches from hybrid layer over time, limiting long-term effect.
- **Benzalkonium chloride (BAC):** Inhibits dentinal MMPs; BAC-containing etchants maintain immediate bond strength and may improve durability when incorporated in etchant or adhesive.
- **Tetracyclines/analogs (incl. minocycline):** Inhibit MMPs by cationic chelating. Minocycline shows potential in adhesive dentistry.
- **Bisphosphonates (galardin, batimastat, zoledronate):** Galardin 0.2 mM reduces bond strength loss as effectively as CHX; lower concentrations (1:20) failed — dentin requires higher concentrations than purified enzymes.
- **MDPB (QAM):** Copolymerizes with adhesive; nearly completely inhibits MMPs. In vitro and in vivo data indicate MDPB-containing adhesives may be superior to other SE adhesives in preventing bond strength loss.
- **DMSO:** MMP-inhibiting solvent; fully miscible in all adhesive solvents, breaks down self-associative water, improves collagen wetting and resin penetration. Improves immediate and long-term bond strength; promising but needs more study.
- **Zinc-containing adhesives:** High zinc reduces MMP-mediated collagen degradation; zinc binds collagen, protecting MMP cleavage sites. May improve hybrid layer durability and promote dentin remineralization, though excess zinc may compromise MDP-containing SE adhesives.

### Chemical bonding (10-MDP)
- 10-MDP and similar functional monomers in mild SE adhesives leave residual hydroxyapatite around collagen fibrils, believed to keep resident enzymes inactive. However, bond strength still shows time-dependent reduction both in vitro and in vivo; the deepest part of the hybrid layer remains the weakest link, and additional enzyme inhibition is still needed.

### Collagen cross-linkers
- **Glutaraldehyde:** Effective cross-linker but cytotoxicity limits clinical use.
- **Carbodiimide (EDC):** Low cytotoxicity; eliminates collagen degradation and preserves bond strength in vitro; can be added to adhesive primers; treatment time may be too long for clinical use.
- **Proanthocyanidins:** Effective; short treatment times achievable; improve immediate and long-term bond strength.
- **Riboflavin:** Effective with UV light / separate curing; poor clinical acceptability due to need for UV.
- Mechanism: cross-links increase collagen matrix stiffness; may also cause allosteric silencing of collagenolytic enzymes by altering their binding site conformation.

### Ethanol-wet bonding
- Uses ethanol to dehydrate demineralized dentin matrices and assist infiltration of hydrophobic monomers, reducing water sorption/solubility and enzymatic hydrolysis of collagen. Currently prohibitive in clinical settings due to technique sensitivity and long treatment times.

### Biomimetic remineralization
- Polyanions (polyacrylic acid, polyaspartic acid) bind collagen and act as analogs of dentin phosphoproteins; combined with amorphous calcium phosphate-containing composite, apatite crystallites replace matrix water. In vitro: preserves mechanical properties of hybrid layer and bond strength with time. Still at proof-of-concept stage; clinically applicable materials not yet developed.

### HEMA-free adhesives
- Less hydrophilic but phase separation with water blisters from acetone/ethanol evaporation reduces immediate bond strength; conflicting durability results; generally bond strength loss still occurs.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review with no formal risk-of-bias appraisal or quantitative meta-analysis; Figure 3 is a pooled descriptive display, not a SR+MA.
- Long-term clinical performance studies for CHX and other inhibitors remain lacking at time of publication.
- Ethanol-wet bonding and biomimetic remineralization are not yet clinically applicable.
- Combining cross-linkers with DMSO or ethanol-wet bonding to enable hydrophobic monomer infiltration is proposed but not validated.
- Deep vs shallow cavity bond durability comparison absent from literature.

## 7. Glossary

- **Hybrid layer** — biocomposite of demineralized dentin collagen infiltrated by polymerized resin adhesive (5–10 µm thick in ER); structural basis of dentin bonding.
- **MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases)** — endogenous zinc-dependent collagenolytic enzymes in dentin (MMP-2/-3/-8/-9/-20); activated by acid etching and SE adhesive acidity; degrade exposed collagen over time.
- **Cysteine cathepsins (B, K)** — lysosomal cysteine proteinases present in dentin; co-degrade collagen alongside MMPs; elevated in carious dentin; also inhibited by CHX.
- **10-MDP** — 10-methacryloyloxydecyldihydrogen phosphate; functional monomer that chemically bonds to hydroxyapatite calcium ions; used in mild SE adhesives.
- **HEMA** — 2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate; most common hydrophilic adhesive monomer (present in 77% of 62 reviewed adhesives); absorbs water and may inhibit polymerization.
- **Phase separation** — segregation of hydrophilic/hydrophobic adhesive components creating water blisters and voids that weaken the hybrid layer.
- **Ethanol-wet bonding** — technique using ethanol to dehydrate demineralized dentin matrix before hydrophobic resin infiltration, reducing hydrolytic vulnerability.
- **Biomimetic remineralization** — replacing matrix water with apatite crystallites using polyanion analogs and amorphous calcium phosphate; mimics natural progressive dehydration during mineralization.
- **Carbodiimide (EDC)** — low-cytotoxicity synthetic cross-linker that inactivates matrix-bound MMPs by cross-linking collagen; can be added to adhesive primers.
- **Proanthocyanidins** — plant-derived natural collagen cross-linkers (e.g., grape-seed extract); improve immediate and long-term bond strength.
- **MDPB** — 12-methacryloyloxydodecylpyridinium bromide; polymerizable QAM incorporated in Clearfil Protect Bond; inhibits MMPs nearly completely.
- **DMSO** — dimethyl sulfoxide; polyfunctional penetration enhancer and potential MMP inhibitor; breaks self-associative water structure, enhances collagen wetting.
- **Substantivity** — property of CHX to bind stably to both mineralized and demineralized dentin (high affinity); limits leach-out but molecule size still allows gradual loss.
