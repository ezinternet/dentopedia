## Why Ingested

Durability review anchoring the dentin-adhesion hybrid-layer batch. It frames the mechanistic problem — endogenous proteases (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) plus the hydrophilicity of contemporary adhesives — that explains hybrid-layer degradation, and catalogs the countermeasures (enzyme inhibitors, collagen cross-linking, water elimination). Reinforces [[wiki/resin-bonding/liu-2011-limitations-bonding-dentin-bond-degradation-review]], which lays out the same degradation limitations of dentin bonding.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (abstract-only) arguing that long-term dentin bond loss is driven by endogenous collagenolytic enzymes (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) and adhesive hydrophilicity, and that preserving collagen-matrix integrity is the key to durable bonding.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames the **hybrid layer** as a biocomposite of dentin collagen + polymerized resin adhesive that achieves good immediate bond strength but degrades over time both in vitro and in vivo.
- Identifies **endogenous collagenolytic enzymes** — matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and cysteine cathepsins — as responsible for the time-dependent loss of hybrid-layer collagen.
- Identifies **adhesive hydrophilicity** as a parallel failure axis: insufficient resin impregnation, phase separation, and low polymerization rate yield suboptimal hybrid layers.
- States the central principle: **preservation of collagen-matrix integrity via inhibition of endogenous dentin proteases is key** to durable dentin bonding.
- Catalogs three strategy families to improve longevity (see Results).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative review synthesizing the principles, current status, and future of techniques aimed at preventing loss of the hybrid layer and bond strength. No quantitative pooling (not a meta-analysis). Abstract-only — methodological detail beyond the abstract was not retrieved.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

(Abstract-only — qualitative; no numeric benchmarks reported in the abstract.)

- Two mechanistic causes of bond degradation: (1) endogenous collagenolytic enzymes (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) degrading hybrid-layer collagen over time; (2) adhesive hydrophilicity → insufficient resin impregnation, phase separation, low polymerization rate.
- Three countermeasure families to retain hybrid-layer integrity and long-term bond strength:
  1. **Enzyme inhibitors** — used separately or incorporated into the adhesive resins.
  2. **Increased collagen resistance** to enzymatic degradation (e.g., cross-linking).
  3. **Elimination of water** from the interface to slow or eliminate hydrolytic loss of hybrid-layer components.

## 7. Glossary

- **Hybrid layer** — biocomposite of demineralized dentin collagen infiltrated by polymerized resin adhesive; the structural basis of dentin bonding.
- **MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases)** — endogenous, host-derived collagenolytic enzymes within dentin that degrade exposed collagen and the hybrid layer over time.
- **Cysteine cathepsins** — endogenous proteases that, alongside MMPs, contribute to time-dependent hybrid-layer collagen breakdown.
- **Phase separation** — segregation of hydrophilic and hydrophobic adhesive components that compromises hybrid-layer quality.
- **Hydrolytic degradation** — water-mediated breakdown of hybrid-layer resin and collagen components.
