## Why Ingested

소아에서 치성 피부누공(Cutaneous Sinus Tract, CST)이 피부과·외과 병변으로 오진되는 흔한 함정을 다룬 후향 증례군으로, 치성 감염의 기원을 무시한 반복적 피부 수술이 어떻게 6.5개월의 진단 지연으로 이어지는지를 보여준다. 치근단 감염(periapical infection)의 origin tooth를 치료(근관치료/발치)하면 병변이 자연 치유된다는 점에서 치성 감염의 외과적 발현을 다루는 [[oral-surgery/gargava-2022-deep-neck-space-infection-150-cases]]와 같은 odontogenic infection 페이지를 보완한다.

## One-line Summary

Retrospective case series (n=28 children, mean age 10.25y): cutaneous sinus tract (CST) of dental origin most often arises from a carious mandibular first molar presenting on the mandibular–submandibular skin; mean 6.5-month diagnostic delay; resolves rapidly after root canal therapy or extraction, with scar revision needed in 29% of (longer-standing) cases.

## 한줄요약

후향적 증례군(소아 28명, 평균 10.25세): 치성 피부누공(CST)은 주로 우식이 있는 하악 제1대구치에서 기원해 하악-하악하부 피부에 나타나며, 평균 6.5개월의 진단 지연 후 근관치료 또는 발치로 빠르게 치유되고, 병변 기간이 긴 29% 환자에서 흉터 교정술이 필요했다.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Reports one of the larger pediatric series (n=28) of cutaneous sinus tract (CST) of dental origin, a condition for which "little is known" about pediatric characteristics.
- Characterizes the pediatric demographic and lesion pattern: mean age 10.25 years (range 4–16), male:female ratio 1:1.74 (female predominance).
- Identifies the most common skin site as the mandibular–submandibular area, and the most common offending tooth as the mandibular first molar, followed by the mandibular incisor.
- Establishes caries as the most common etiology and documents a substantial diagnostic delay (mean 6.5 months, range 0.3–12 months).
- Highlights that early dental treatment can produce spontaneous healing of the cutaneous lesion without a scar, whereas longer-standing lesions more often require surgical scar revision.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Retrospective review of 28 children diagnosed with CST of dental origin.
- Recorded variables: age, sex, site of the skin lesion, duration until diagnosis, teeth involved, etiology, treatment, and outcome.
- Last dental care visit was recorded as a proxy for preventive dental care.
- Treatment was either root canal therapy (endodontic) or extraction of the offending tooth; surgical scar revision performed when indicated for cosmetic reasons.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Mean age**: 10.25 years (range 4–16).
- **Sex ratio**: male:female = 1:1.74 (female predominance).
- **Most common skin site**: mandibular–submandibular area.
- **Most involved tooth**: mandibular first molar, then mandibular incisor.
- **Etiology**: caries most common.
- **Diagnostic delay**: mean 6.5 months (range 0.3–12 months) until correct diagnosis.
- **Treatment**: root canal therapy or extraction; CSTs resolved rapidly after appropriate dental treatment.
- **Scar revision**: indicated in 8/28 patients (29%) for better cosmetic results; these patients had longer-duration lesions.
- **Preventive care**: poor, as indicated by last dental care visit.
- **Pediatric vs adult contrast**: CST in children differs from adults in skin-lesion site, lesion duration, and involved tooth, but is similar in etiology and treatment modality. Early treatment may heal the cutaneous lesion spontaneously without scarring.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Retrospective design with a single-institution case series; no control group and limited level of evidence.
- Sample size (n=28), while relatively large for this rare entity, limits subgroup statistical inference.
- Outcome (scar revision) is associated with longer lesion duration but causal/temporal thresholds are not quantified beyond the mean-duration comparison.
- Detailed per-case Methods/Results tables were not recoverable from the captured landing-page text; some figures are summarized from the abstract.
- Authors emphasize the public-health gap in preventive pediatric dental care as an underlying driver.

## 7. Glossary

- **Cutaneous Sinus Tract (CST)**: An abnormal epithelialized or granulation-lined channel draining a chronic odontogenic (periapical) infection to the skin surface, often on the face or neck.
- **Odontogenic / Dental origin**: Originating from a tooth, here most often from pulpal necrosis secondary to caries.
- **Periapical infection**: Infection at the apex of a tooth root, the typical source of the draining tract.
- **Root canal therapy (RCT)**: Endodontic removal of necrotic pulp and obturation of the canal system to eliminate the infection source.
- **Scar revision**: Secondary surgical correction of the residual skin scar for cosmetic improvement.
