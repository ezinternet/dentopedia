## One-line Summary

Narrative review of dental luting materials covering 7 cement families (zinc oxide eugenol, zinc phosphate, zinc polycarboxylate, GIC, RMGIC, hybrid CaAl/GI, resin cements), classifying them by chemistry/bonding/indication and detailing properties, clinical indications, and recent modifications.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Consolidates the full landscape of luting cements into one reference, classified three ways: chemical composition (water- vs resin-based), bonding mechanism (non-adhesive / chemically adhesive / micromechanically adhesive), and clinical indication (temporary vs permanent).
- States the "ideal luting cement" requirement set and the central thesis that **no single material satisfies all of them** — selection is always a trade-off.
- Maps each material to concrete clinical indications (e.g., zinc phosphate for cast metal post-core; CaAl/GI for high-strength ceramics; light-cured resin for veneers; non-eugenol temp before composite).
- Reviews emerging modifications: nano-hydroxyapatite / bioactive-glass GIC, antimicrobial & self-healing resin cements, restorative composite as luting agent, and novel castor-oil polyurethane cement.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative literature review (no systematic search protocol, no quality appraisal, no quantitative synthesis). Organized by material class, then by property/indication, then by recent developments. Evidence cited ranges from in-vitro bond-strength studies to multi-year prospective clinical studies, but is not pooled.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Selected material-specific points:
- **Zinc phosphate** — "gold standard," non-adhesive; very low initial setting pH (<2.0) → sensitivity; water-soluble → secondary caries risk.
- **Zinc polycarboxylate** — compressive strength only ½–⅔ of zinc phosphate; weak chemical bond; short working time (2.5 min); now mainly for long-term provisionals.
- **GIC** — fluoride release (24-h burst then slow) → anticariogenic; brittle (low flexural strength, high modulus).
- **RMGIC** — better bond strength/fracture toughness than GIC but more cytotoxic to pulp.
- **Hybrid CaAl/GI** — self-adhesive, bioactive (Ca/PO₄ release, remineralization), stronger antibacterial than self-adhesive resin & GIC; long-term clinical data lacking.
- **Resin cements** — highest compressive/tensile/bond strength and aesthetics; moisture-control sensitive. Dual-cure degree of conversion without light can be as low as 10.82%; extend light cure to 120 s; for ceramic <2.0 mm light/dual > self-cure.
- **Self-adhesive resin** — easier, less postoperative sensitivity than GIC/RMGIC, but lower bond strength; not for resin-bonded bridges, short crowns, or veneers.
- **Castor-oil polyurethane** — flexural strength ~25% of adhesive resin cement, comparable to zinc phosphate/GIC.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review → selection bias, no quality grading, no pooled effect estimates.
- CaAl/GI and several novel materials lack long-term clinical evidence.
- Restorative composite as a luting agent cannot reliably reach the ISO 4049 <50 μm film thickness after preheating/ultrasonic vibration.
- Future work: fillers to strengthen castor-oil polyurethane; optimal antimicrobial concentrations in resin without harming mechanics; long-term clinical trials of bioactive cements.

## 6. Related Work

Builds on classic cement chemistry literature (zinc phosphate, Smith's polycarboxylate 1968, Wilson & Kent GIC, Hench 45S5 Bioglass 1969) and ISO 4049:2019 specification for polymer-based luting/restorative materials.

## 7. Glossary

- **Luting cement** — material filling voids between an indirect restoration and prepared tooth to retain it.
- **GIC / RMGIC** — glass ionomer cement / resin-modified glass ionomer cement.
- **CaAl/GI** — hybrid calcium aluminate / glass ionomer (bioactive) cement.
- **10-MDP** — 10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate, functional acidic adhesion monomer.
- **Total-etch vs self-etch** — etch-and-rinse vs self-etching adhesive strategy.
- **ISO 4049** — standard specifying film thickness (<50 μm) and cure classes (1 self, 2 light, 3 dual).
