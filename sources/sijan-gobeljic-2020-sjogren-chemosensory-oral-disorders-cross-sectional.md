## Why Ingested

미각상실·침분비저하 관련 논문을 COVID와 무관한 범주로 확장하던 중, 자가면역질환(Sjögren 증후군)을 병인으로 하는 미각·후각 기능이상을 직접 정량 측정한 대조군 연구를 발견. 기존 [[implants/almeida-2017-dental-implants-sjogren-syndrome-sr]]·[[oral-medicine/poudel-2026-xerostomia-dental-treatment-outcomes-sr]]가 Sjögren의 치료 결과(임플란트·보철)를 다뤘다면, 본 cross-sectional study는 Sjögren 환자의 미각상실·미각이상(dysgeusia) 자체를 정량 측정하고 침분비저하와의 관계(약한 상관관계에 그침)를 직접 검증한 원자료를 제공한다.

## One-line Summary

Comparative cross-sectional study (58 primary Sjögren's syndrome patients vs. 55 age/gender-matched healthy controls) finding significantly higher rates of gustatory dysfunction (ageusia/hypogeusia for sweet 34%, bitter 19%, salty 10%, sour 11%), olfactory dysfunction (anosmia OR 5.2), dysgeusia (52.6% vs. 9.4%), and burning tongue sensation (45.6% vs. 0%), all associated with significantly worse oral health-related quality of life (OHIP-14), while halitosis rates did not differ between groups.

## 한줄요약

대조군 비교 횡단연구(원발성 쇼그렌증후군 환자 58명 vs 연령·성별 매칭 건강대조군 55명) — 미각기능이상(단맛 34%·쓴맛 19%·짠맛 10%·신맛 11% 무미각/저미각), 후각기능이상(무후각 교차비 5.2), 미각이상 dysgeusia(52.6% vs 9.4%), 설작열감(45.6% vs 0%)이 유의하게 높고 모두 구강건강관련 삶의 질(OHIP-14) 저하와 연관; 구취는 두 군간 차이 없음.

## 1. Document Information

- Journal: BMC Oral Health, 2020;20(1):187
- Publication date: 2020-07-03
- Setting: Outpatient Clinic, Institute of Rheumatology, University of Belgrade, Serbia
- Ethics: approved by Local Ethics Committee, informed consent per Declaration of Helsinki

## 2. Key Contributions

- Directly quantifies both olfactory (Sniffin' Sticks identification test) and gustatory (taste-strip, 4 basic qualities) dysfunction in primary Sjögren's syndrome (pSS) with objective test batteries, not just self-report.
- Quantifies odds ratio for anosmia in pSS (OR 5.2, 95% CI 1.9–14.3) vs. matched healthy controls.
- Demonstrates dysgeusia and burning tongue sensation are markedly elevated in pSS (52.6% and 45.6%) while halitosis is not — dissociating the oral symptom cluster.
- Links impaired chemosensory function to significantly worse OHRQoL (OHIP-14) across all four sub-domains (functional, physical, psychological, social).
- Cites evidence that salivary flow rate correlates only weakly with chemosensory dysfunction severity, suggesting the taste/smell impairment in pSS is not purely a downstream consequence of hyposalivation but may involve a separate inflammatory mechanism (interferon-pathway-mediated taste-bud/taste-transduction dysfunction).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: comparative cross-sectional study, single center (2017–2019 recruitment).
- **Population**: 58 pSS patients (American-European Consensus classification criteria; age 25–77y) vs. 55 age/gender-matched healthy controls; exclusion criteria applied to both groups (other rheumatic/metabolic/malignant disease, active infection).
- **Clinical/lab workup**: Schirmer's I test, Rose Bengal score, salivary scintigraphy (Tc99m), labial salivary gland biopsy (Chisholm-Mason grading), ANA/RF/anti-SS-A/anti-SS-B serology.
- **Chemosensory testing**: Sniffin' Sticks 12-odor identification test (anosmic 0–5, hyposmic 6–9, normosmic 10–12); taste strips for sweet/sour/salty/bitter (0=loss, 1=reduced, 2=normal per quality, max 8); self-reported VAS 0–10 for both smell and taste.
- **PROs**: structured questionnaire for dysgeusia/BST/halitosis; OHIP-14 (Serbian-validated) for OHRQoL; ESSDAI for disease activity; ESSPRI for patient-reported symptom burden.
- **Statistics**: independent-samples t-test (continuous), chi-square (dichotomous), odds ratios with 95% CI; SPSS v16.0.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Olfactory**: self-reported VAS smell 8.57±2.21 (pSS) vs. 9.56±0.72 (controls), p=0.016; anosmia 3.8% vs. 0.0%, hyposmia 36.5% vs. 13.2% (χ²=9.9, p=0.002); OR for anosmia 5.2 (95% CI 1.9–14.3, p<0.001).
- **Gustatory**: self-reported VAS taste 8.48±2.10 vs. 9.54±0.67, p=0.014; objective taste-strip score 4.11±1.82 vs. 6.11±1.93, p<0.0001; impaired taste by quality — sweet 34.0% vs. 7.5% (p<0.0001), sour 10.6% vs. 0.0% (p=0.054), salty 10.0% vs. 5.7% (p=0.018), bitter 19.1% vs. 1.9% (p=0.001).
- **Dysgeusia**: 52.6% (pSS) vs. 9.4% (controls), χ²=23.6, p<0.0001; most common descriptor was distorted bitter taste (36.7%).
- **Burning tongue sensation (BST)**: 45.6% (pSS) vs. 0.0% (controls), χ²=31.6, p<0.0001.
- **Halitosis**: 31.6% vs. 28.3%, not significant (χ²=0.40, p=0.434) — the one oral symptom that did NOT differ between groups.
- **OHRQoL**: OHIP-14 6.79±7.03 (pSS, worse) vs. 2.27±8.46 (controls), p<0.001, elevated across all 4 domains; VAS-EQ5D 6.67±2.02 vs. 8.28±1.02, p<0.0001 (lower = worse in pSS).
- Only 4% of pSS patients used artificial saliva despite the high symptom burden (vs. 81% using artificial tears) — a notable undertreatment gap for the salivary/gustatory domain relative to the ocular domain.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Author-acknowledged: no non-Sjögren's "sicca" comparator group (dry mouth from other causes — diabetes, menopause, hypothyroidism, radiotherapy, age) — cannot fully separate autoimmune-specific mechanism from generic dry-mouth damage.
- No chemosensory threshold testing for smell (identification-only test); authors note UPSIT (University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test) as a potential gold-standard alternative for future work.
- Single-center design; sample size (58 vs. 55) limits subgroup power.
- Correlation between salivary flow rate and chemosensory dysfunction severity was only weak in cited prior literature — the causal mechanism linking pSS to taste/smell impairment (hyposalivation vs. independent inflammatory/interferon-pathway effect on taste buds) remains unresolved and flagged for future research.
- Authors call for future studies on oral hygiene habits and dietary intake in pSS patients to better inform management.

## 6. Related Work

- Cites the American-European Consensus (AEC) classification criteria for Sjögren's syndrome diagnosis (compare [[legoff-2017-sjogren-classification-criteria-comparison]], [[franceschini-2017-sjogren-2016-acr-eular-criteria-commentary]], [[shiboski-2016-acr-eular-sjogren-classification-criteria]] for classification-criteria context already in this wiki).
- Cites a meta-analysis (Al-Ezzi et al., 5 studies, 378 pSS patients) confirming pSS negatively affects smell, taste, sexual function, and quality of life in female patients.
- Cites a comparator study (non-SS sicca patients having similar/worse oral complaints than pSS patients) motivating the authors' proposed future non-SS sicca comparison arm.
- Cites Rusthen et al. for a differing anosmia prevalence estimate (12.9% vs. this study's 3.8%), attributed to differing olfactory test methodology.

## 7. Glossary

- **Primary Sjögren's syndrome (pSS)**: autoimmune exocrinopathy (salivary/lacrimal glands) not secondary to another connective tissue disease.
- **BST (burning sensation in the tongue)**: burning/painful tongue sensation, a form of burning mouth symptom.
- **Ageusia/hypogeusia**: complete/partial loss of ability to taste a given quality.
- **Anosmia/hyposmia**: complete/partial loss of smell.
- **OHIP-14**: 14-item short-form Oral Health Impact Profile, patient-reported OHRQoL instrument (0–56, higher = worse).
- **ESSDAI / ESSPRI**: EULAR Sjögren's Syndrome Disease Activity Index (clinician-rated) / Patient Reported Index (patient-rated symptom burden).
- **M3R (muscarinic acetylcholine receptor type 3)**: salivary acinar cell receptor targeted by autoantibodies in Sjögren's syndrome, functionally inhibiting salivary secretion.
