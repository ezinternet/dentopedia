## Why Ingested

기존 위키의 처방 행태 survey([[drug/antibiotics/sbricoli-2024-antibiotic-prescribing-habits-dentistry-survey]], [[drug/antibiotics/sutej-2024-antibiotic-prescribing-trends-dentistry-croatia]])와 stewardship SR([[drug/antibiotics/mendez-romero-2025-antibiotic-stewardship-dentistry-sr]], [[drug/antibiotics/sandulescu-2024-antibiotic-prescribing-dental-best-practices-review]])가 모두 서구/유럽 데이터에 치우쳐 아시아 권역 근거가 부족했다. 본 Singapore cross-sectional survey (Low 2026, n=280)는 아시아 첫 종합 처방·AMR 지식 데이터로, 부적절 처방의 modifiable driver(public vs private, 경력, 국내/해외 수련)와 third-molar/implant 예방적 항생제 과처방 패턴을 보강한다.

## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional survey of 280 Singapore dentists (2024): appropriate antibiotic prescribing ranged 6.5-97.7% across scenarios (lowest for periodontal 30.4% and oral surgery 34.0%), 71.2% over-prescribed prophylaxis after third-molar surgery and 73.5% before implant placement, and AMR-transmission knowledge was poor (59.3% unaware resistant bacteria spread easily).

## 한줄요약

싱가포르 치과의사 280명 단면조사(2024): 적절한 항생제 처방률이 임상상황별 6.5~97.7%로 편차가 컸고(치주 30.4%·구강외과 34.0%가 최저), 사랑니 발치 후 예방적 처방 71.2%·임플란트 식립 전 73.5%로 과처방이 만연했으며, 공공부문·국내수련·저경력 치과의사가 더 적절히 처방했고 AMR 전파에 대한 지식은 부족했다(59.3%가 내성균 전파 가능성 모름).

## 2. Key Contributions

- **First study in Singapore** to assess dental antibiotic prescribing practices, knowledge, and attitudes; the only prior local study (a decade earlier) covered only infective-endocarditis prophylaxis. A 2024 multinational SR found no Singapore data.
- Quantifies **wide variability** in appropriate prescribing (6.5-97.7% across clinical scenarios) and maps it onto clinical categories (restorative / oral surgery / periodontal & implant / medically complex).
- **Uniquely assesses dentists' AMR-mechanism knowledge** (not done in prior surveys), revealing consistently poor knowledge regardless of experience or sector.
- Identifies modifiable predictors of appropriate prescribing (practice sector, experience, training location) and dentists' preferred intervention modalities (local guidelines, mobile apps).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Cross-sectional online survey (Qualtrics) distributed in **2024** via mailing lists of Singapore Dental Association, College of General Dental Practitioners, and specialist associations; convenience sampling, anonymous, voluntary.
- 5-section, ~50-item questionnaire: demographics (6), prescribing preferences (4), clinical/nonclinical prescribing scenarios (29), AMR knowledge & attitudes (7), knowledge sources & training needs (4).
- Appropriateness scored against an earlier study, local guidelines, and scenario-specific references; per-respondent appropriate-prescribing % computed per clinical category (excluding "do not do procedure").
- **t-tests / one-way ANOVA (Bonferroni)** for group comparisons; **multivariable logistic regression** for per-scenario predictors. SPSS v27, p < .05.
- Target sample 337 (95% CI, 5% margin, population 2690 dentists); achieved **n = 280** after excluding 72 incomplete surveys (10.4% of practising dentists; ~5.5% margin with finite population correction).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Respondents (n = 280):** 79.6% under 50 yo; 60.4% female; 75% locally trained; 34.6% public sector; 32.9% specialists; 18.2% with 0-5 years experience.

**Prescribing preferences:**
- 5-day duration most common (86.8%).
- Amoxicillin and Augmentin most commonly prescribed (97.1%).
- 14.9% always/often prescribe multidrug regimens; most common combination amoxicillin + metronidazole (89.0%).

**Appropriate prescribing by category (mean ± SD):**
- Restorative 58.3% ± 28.6% (highest)
- Medically complex patients 56.6% ± 18.0%
- Oral surgery 34.0% ± 29.0%
- Periodontal 30.4% ± 37.5% (lowest)
- Overall range across scenarios: 6.5% to 97.7%

**Over-prescription hotspots:**
- 71.2% always/often prescribe prophylaxis after surgical removal of impacted third molars in healthy patients (unnecessary; local intervention study cut use 84%→21% with no infection increase).
- 73.5% routinely prescribe prophylaxis before implant placement (absolute risk reduction only 2.9%; no benefit in healthy patients).
- Inappropriate prescribing for pulp necrosis with acute apical abscess 40.1%; localised periodontal abscess 24.7%.

**Medical-indication prophylaxis (4 commonly misunderstood conditions, all needing NO prophylaxis):** only 25.4% correctly identified that none required prophylaxis; mitral valve prolapse misidentified by 52.9%, unrepaired septal defects by 62.5% (outdated AHA understanding).

**Nonclinical pressures:** only 21.2-37.5% consistently adhered to appropriate practice under patient requests / time pressure / anaesthesia difficulty / diagnostic uncertainty; 6.8% prescribed defensively for medico-legal reasons; ~half would prescribe at least occasionally-to-rarely.

**Predictors (multivariable logistic regression):**
- Public-sector dentists generally more appropriate (except implant placement OR 0.16, p<.001; head/neck radiotherapy extraction OR 0.34, p=.015).
- Senior dentists (≥21 yrs) more often inappropriate, esp. prophylaxis (total joint replacement OR 0.30, p=.001); but less likely to prescribe without a diagnosis (OR 3.87, p=.004) yet more defensive (OR 0.38, p=.034).
- 11-20 yrs experience avoided unnecessary post-extraction (OR 2.61, p=.039) and CABG-patient (OR 2.67, p<.001) antibiotics.
- Overseas-trained: better for antiresorptive agents (OR 9.79, p=.030), healthy post-extraction (OR 2.57, p=.012), alveolar osteitis (OR 2.69, p=.007); worse for head/neck radiotherapy extractions (OR 0.32, p=.014) and mitral valve prolapse (OR 0.49, p=.025).
- Male dentists more inappropriate for pulp necrosis with chronic apical abscess (OR 0.47, p=.017).

**AMR knowledge & attitudes:** 80% positive attitude toward AMR, but 59.3% unaware resistant bacteria spread easily; AMR-mechanism knowledge consistently poor across all groups.

**Training:** only 25.7% had prior antibiotic training; 88.2% want further initiatives; preferred: local guidelines (83.6%), mobile prescribing apps (70.4%).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Convenience sampling; total recipients unknown so response rate uncomputable; possible nonresponse bias (stewardship-engaged dentists overrepresented → may over-state appropriateness).
- n = 280 < target 337 (precision modestly lower; ~5.5% margin).
- Cannot compare general dentists vs specialists or among individual specialties (specialists few; aggregated specialist group showed higher inappropriate rates, possibly reflecting case complexity not knowledge deficit).
- Self-reported behaviour (not actual prescribing audit). Single high-income city-state context limits generalisability.
- Future: COM-B / implementation-science-guided, audit-based, personalised-feedback stewardship interventions; local-guideline dissemination; embed prescribing decision support in mobile apps; AMR curriculum at undergraduate level.

## 6. Related Work

- Singapore third-molar intervention study (84%→21% reduction, no infection increase) — cited as local evidence against routine third-molar prophylaxis.
- 2024 multinational SR on drivers of inappropriate dental prescribing — found no Singapore data (this study fills that gap).
- AHA infective-endocarditis prophylaxis guidelines — basis for medical-indication appropriateness scoring.
- COM-B behaviour-change model and implementation-science stewardship frameworks.

## 7. Glossary

- **AMR (Antimicrobial Resistance)**: bacteria/microbes evolving to withstand antimicrobials, making infections harder to treat.
- **Antibiotic stewardship**: coordinated effort to optimise antibiotic use to improve outcomes and reduce resistance.
- **Prophylactic antibiotics**: antibiotics given before a procedure to prevent infection (vs therapeutic, for active infection).
- **Augmentin**: amoxicillin/clavulanic acid (AMX/CLV) combination.
- **COM-B**: Capability–Opportunity–Motivation–Behaviour model for behaviour change.
- **Alveolar osteitis**: "dry socket", post-extraction complication.
- **Convenience sampling**: non-probability sampling of readily available participants (limits generalisability).
