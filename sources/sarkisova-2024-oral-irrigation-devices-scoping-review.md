## Why Ingested

The interdental-cleaning category holds several head-to-head water-flosser RCTs/SRs (e.g. [[interdental-cleaning/mohapatra-2024-water-flosser-vs-floss-plaque-sr]], [[interdental-cleaning/mancinelli-lyle-2024-water-flosser-vs-interdental-brush-rct]]) but no map of the *whole* oral-irrigator evidence base. This is the first broad scoping review (275 sources, 1962–2023) that frames device types, mechanism, safety, and the evidence gaps those narrower trials sit inside — an anchor/overview backbone for the category.

## One-line Summary

Scoping review (JBI / PRISMA-ScR; 275 sources spanning 1962–2023) mapping the entire oral-irrigation-device evidence base: oral irrigators consistently reduce periodontal/peri-implant inflammation (likely via microbiota modulation, not full biofilm removal), are safe and well-accepted when used correctly, but their plaque-removal effect is mixed and caries/PROM/pediatric/cost/environmental evidence is sparse.

## 1. Document Information

- **Type**: Scoping review (Joanna Briggs Institute methodology; reported per PRISMA-ScR).
- **Title**: Oral Irrigation Devices: A Scoping Review.
- **Authors**: Farzana Sarkisova, Zac Morse, Kevin Lee, Nagihan Bostanci.
- **Journal**: Clinical and Experimental Dental Research 2024;10(3) (Wiley). DOI 10.1002/cre2.912. PMID 38881230, PMCID PMC11180943.
- **Research question** (JBI population–concept–context): concept = "oral irrigation devices"; population/context deliberately unrestricted. Primary question: "What is known about oral irrigation devices?" with subquestions on methodologies used, existing research gaps, and aspects needing further investigation.
- **Conflicts of interest**: none declared (though 69 of the 275 included sources declared industry funding/affiliation).

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **First broad scoping review of oral irrigators (OIs).** No prior broad SR existed; earlier reviews either bundled multiple hygiene aids or answered narrow efficacy/population questions with restricted searches. This maps 275 sources accumulated since OIs launched in 1962.
2. **Bottom line — inflammation yes, plaque unclear.** Oral irrigation consistently reduces periodontal and peri-implant inflammation across populations and irrigant solutions; plaque-reduction findings are mixed.
3. **Proposed mechanism — microbiota modulation, not biofilm elimination.** Anti-inflammatory benefit likely comes from shifting the oral microbiota away from dysbiosis (fewer late colonizers/anaerobic pathogens, more early colonizers/aerobes) rather than fully removing biofilm.
4. **Safety envelope quantified.** Generally safe and well-accepted; 70–90 psi safe for non-ulcerated attached gingiva, 50–70 psi for ulcerated/non-keratinized tissue; transient bacteremia risk warrants caution in infective-endocarditis-risk patients.
5. **Gap map for future research:** caries prevention, validated patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs), pediatric/special-needs populations, peri-implantitis, cost and environmental impact, and irrigator-vs-air-flosser comparisons.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Framework**: JBI scoping-review guidance; reporting via PRISMA-ScR. Data extraction used a refined publicly-available protocol; one extractor, verified by a second; disagreements by discussion. No risk-of-bias / quality assessment (by scoping-review design).
- **Databases (4)**: CINAHL, Dentistry & Oral Sciences Source, MEDLINE, Scopus. **Gray-literature sources (8)**: Cochrane Library, Cochrane Oral Health Trials Register, Google, Google Scholar, ISRCTN Registry, OpenGray, ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global, WHO ICTRP. Plus Scopus citation searching.
- **Search dates**: main search 12 Jan 2022 (no date limiter), follow-up 23–24 Dec 2023.
- **Eligibility**: English-language primary + secondary research, any geography/setting. Excluded letters, narratives, opinion/commentary, guidelines, registered trials without results, historical reviews (with two deliberate exceptions: one letter that was a case report; one systematically-searched narrative decision-tree review). **Air flossers were explicitly excluded** — they use a pressurized air stream with water droplets, a technological difference from conventional pressurized-water OIs.
- **Screening flow (PRISMA)**: 1769 records screened on title/abstract (pilot-tested to ≥75% reviewer agreement) → 418 full-text → 174 excluded → 244 from databases + 31 from gray literature/citation searching = **275 included**.
- **Synthesis**: studies grouped by methodology, then sub-grouped by themes, irrigant solutions, and populations.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Corpus characteristics (n=275)**: 259 from scientific journals; 227 academic settings. By region: North America 148, Western Europe 49, Asia 47, Middle East 22, South America 4, Africa 2, Oceania 1, unreported 2. By design: 185 clinical trials, 30 in vitro, 15 systematic reviews, 13 animal models, 12 observational, 9 descriptive, 11 non-oral-hygiene applications. Mostly adults; only 6 children and 26 adolescent studies (15 of the adolescent trials in fixed orthodontics). 69 studies declared industry funding/affiliation.

**Clinical trials (n=185)**: sample sizes 2–240 (mean 44.1, SD 36.2); durations a single episode to 56 weeks. Conditions: periodontitis 57, gingivitis 44, gingivitis+early periodontitis 15, healthy 13, peri-implant mucositis 5, peri-implantitis 1, status unreported 39. Populations (of 170 oral-hygiene-effect trials): general 128, fixed ortho 21, implants 9, special needs 6, intermaxillary fixation 3, diabetes 2, pregnancy 1. Commonest irrigant: water (101); also non-ozone antimicrobials 31, ozonated water 13, herbal extracts 10, antibiotics 9. OIs were adjunctive to brushing in 69 water trials; only 13 trials had any sole-OI group.

**Systematic reviews (n=15)**: only 3 focused primarily on adjunctive OI vs other aids; the rest compared multiple aids including OIs. 3 covered implant home-care, 2 fixed ortho.

**Outcome map (Table 2 highlights, n of sources per outcome)**:
- Device types/brands (275): pulsating > non-pulsating; faucet-attached can exert higher pressure but is harder to attach; **Waterpik most-studied brand (~139–156 sources)**.
- Pressure & safety (19): 70–90 psi safe for attached gingiva, 50–70 psi for ulcerated/non-keratinized tissue; particulate penetration possible.
- Bacteremia (10): risk present; caution in infective-endocarditis-risk patients (case reports of endocarditis).
- Effects on dental materials (6): generally safe; some risk to microhybrid composites at 100 psi, bulk-fill at 63 psi, inlays with poor margins.
- Subgingival penetration (6): supragingival tips reach 44–71% of pocket depth; subgingival tips up to 64–100% (potentially the pocket base — OIs are the only home aid that can reach deep-pocket base).
- Periodontal/peri-implant inflammation (138): consistent reductions in bleeding, edema, erythema, probing depth, CAL.
- Dental biofilm (164): in vitro disruption consistent; clinical/review plaque reduction **mixed** (23 trials no reduction vs 31 showing improvement); consistent benefit in special-needs groups.
- Oral microbiome (58): potential shift away from dysbiosis.
- Staining (12): chlorhexidine increases staining; lower concentration + subgingival delivery mitigates.
- Patient acceptance (21): generally high but validated PROM tools rarely used.
- Plus: calculus mixed (12), inflammation markers/GCF reductions (12/6), endodontic in-vitro promise (4), endocarditis-era non-hygiene uses (wound/colonoscopy/xerostomia).

**SR plaque verdict**: most SRs found no plaque benefit or could not pool (heterogeneity); only 2 SRs (Allen; AlMoharib) showed plaque-score improvement.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Methodological limits**: English-only sources; no quality/risk-of-bias assessment (inherent to scoping reviews); no brand/model comparative analysis, so findings may not generalize across irrigator models; OI effect hard to isolate because most trials used it adjunctively to brushing.
- **Evidence gaps flagged for future research**: caries-prevention potential (none identified — possibly needs solutions like electrolyzed water that reduce biofilm or raise plaque pH); validated PROMs; pediatric and special-needs feasibility trials; peri-implantitis (only 1 of 9 implant trials was peri-implantitis); mechanism clarification with modern molecular techniques (few periodontitis microbiome studies); cost and environmental-impact comparisons vs other aids; irrigator-vs-air-flosser head-to-heads; sufficiently powered long-duration trials.

## 6. Related Work

- EFP S3 guidelines (Stage I–III periodontitis; peri-implant diseases) — frame OIs as an adjunct option but note insufficient high-quality evidence; this review maps that gap.
- Cochrane interdental-cleaning review (Worthington 2019) — could find no caries studies; this review confirms the caries gap persists.
- Narrower OI SRs (Slot/Valkenburg/Van der Weijden; Husseini; Gennai; Sälzer; Zhao) — mostly null on plaque, supportive on inflammation; this scoping review situates them in the full corpus.

## 7. Glossary

- **OI (Oral Irrigator)**: electric device producing a pulsating or continuous pressurized water stream to remove interproximal debris and deliver solutions sub-/supragingivally; launched 1962.
- **Scoping review**: evidence-mapping method (JBI / PRISMA-ScR) that charts the breadth of a literature and identifies gaps, without risk-of-bias appraisal — distinct from a systematic review.
- **Air flosser**: device using a pressurized *air* stream with water droplets (e.g. Sonicare AirFloss); technologically distinct from water-stream OIs, and explicitly excluded here.
- **psi**: pounds per square inch — the pressure unit used to define OI safety thresholds (70–90 psi attached gingiva; 50–70 psi ulcerated tissue).
- **PROM (Patient-Reported Outcome Measure)**: validated patient-perspective metric (satisfaction, ease of use, QoL); rarely standardized in OI trials.
- **GCF (Gingival Crevicular Fluid)**: inflammatory exudate from the gingival sulcus; volume/cytokine markers used as outcomes.
- **Dysbiosis**: pathogenic shift in microbial community composition; OIs may modulate the microbiota away from it.
