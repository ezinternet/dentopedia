## One-line Summary
In-vitro study: Ti biological aging continues progressively up to 6 months (not just 4 weeks); cell attachment/spread/ALP all decrease time-dependently; degradation driven by carbon contamination rather than hydrophilicity per se.

## 1. Document Information
Materials 2016;9:102. doi:10.3390/ma9020102. UCLA LBIS + Hokkaido University. Published 2016-02-06.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Extends known biological aging timeline from 4 weeks to 6 months
- Separates hydrophilicity from carbon contamination as aging drivers
- Regulatory implication: current implant shelf life standards inadequate
- Cell number negatively correlated with hydrophilicity; other parameters NOT correlated with hydrophilicity

## 3. Methodology
Acid-etched cpTi disks: new (0), 1-month, 3-month, 6-month. Rat bone marrow osteoblasts. Parameters: contact angle, XPS (surface carbon), cell attachment, spread, density, ALP activity.

## 4. Key Results
- New surface: contact angle ≈0° (superhydrophilic); 6-month: ~90° (hydrophobic)
- Cell attachment, spread, density, ALP: highest at new, decreased progressively to 6 months
- Degradation statistically significant for most parameters at all timepoints including 6 months
- Hydrophilicity correlation: negative for cell number, NOT significant for spread/density/ALP → carbon contamination as primary driver

## 5. Limitations
In vitro; acid-etched surface only; 6-month endpoint (beyond 6 months unknown); rat osteoblast model.

## 6. Related Work
Hayashi 2014 (hydrocarbon effect on osteoblast), Ogawa 2025 (D3 aging theory), Iwasa 2011 (nano-hybrid anti-aging).

## 7. Glossary
XPS: X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy — measures surface elemental composition; ALP: alkaline phosphatase — osteoblast differentiation marker.
