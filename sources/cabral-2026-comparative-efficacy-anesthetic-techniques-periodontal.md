## Why Ingested

바늘없는/컴퓨터제어 압력 침윤마취기(Wand-type computerized delivery system) 관련 질의 대응 목적으로 인제스트. 기존 [[local-anesthesia/wambier-2017-intrapocket-topical-versus-injected-anesthetic-srp]] (SR+MA)는 SRP에서 injected anesthetic이 topical gel보다 통증강도·rescue 필요성에서 우위라고 결론지었는데, 본 RCT(Cabral 2026)는 통증 강도 자체는 두 기법이 동등하다고 보고해 부분적으로 상충하며, 대신 "보충마취 필요성"이라는 secondary outcome에서 컴퓨터제어 침습기법의 우위(24% vs 100%, p<0.001)를 명확히 정량화해 기존 근거를 정제(refine)한다.

## One-line Summary
Parallel, blinded RCT (n=76) comparing computerized-controlled invasive local anesthesia vs noninvasive lidocaine/prilocaine gel for nonsurgical periodontal instrumentation: pain intensity (NRS-11) was similar between groups, but the noninvasive gel group required supplementary anesthesia in 100% of patients vs only 24% in the computerized group (P<0.001); hemodynamics were stable in both.

## 한줄요약
평행·맹검 RCT(n=76), 비외과적 치주기구조작 시 컴퓨터제어 침습마취 vs 비침습 리도카인/프릴로카인 겔 비교: 통증강도(NRS-11)는 두 군 유사했으나, 비침습 겔군은 100%가 보충마취 필요 vs 컴퓨터제어군 24%만 필요(p<0.001); 혈역학은 양 군 모두 안정.

- **Registration**: Brazilian Registry of Clinical Trials (REBEC RBR-9d8cxh), ethics approval CAAE 12203319.9.0000.0105
- **Study type**: Parallel-group, examiner-blinded (single-blind), randomized controlled trial

## 2. Key Contributions
- First direct RCT comparison of a **computer-controlled anesthetic delivery device** (Morpheus, MeibachTech) against **Oraqix** (25 mg/g lidocaine/prilocaine thermosensitive gel), the dental-specific noninvasive topical anesthetic, during nonsurgical periodontal (subgingival) instrumentation.
- Separates two distinct outcome axes that prior literature often conflates: (1) achieved pain intensity during instrumentation, and (2) need for anesthetic *supplementation/rescue* — showing these can diverge (similar pain, very different rescue rates).
- Adds hemodynamic monitoring (BP, SpO2) pre/post as an objective corroborating measure, largely absent from prior gel-vs-injection periodontal literature.
- Frames the invasive-vs-noninvasive trade-off in terms of clinical workflow efficiency (fewer interruptions for re-anesthesia) rather than just patient comfort.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Design**: Parallel, blinded (pain assessor only — participants and treating clinicians could not be blinded due to technique nature), randomized, single-center (university dental clinic), Brazil.
- **Sample**: 76 volunteers (38/group), 1:1 simple randomization via web-based system with sealed sequentially numbered envelopes. Sample size via G*Power (two-tailed, power 80%, effect size 0.7, alpha 0.05), +5/group for attrition.
- **Inclusion**: age >18, periodontal attachment loss 1-2mm interproximal or <15% coronal radiographic bone loss, probing depth <=4mm, informed consent.
- **Exclusion**: amide-anesthetic allergy, recent analgesic/anesthetic use (<12h), alcoholism history, pregnancy/lactation, uncontrolled systemic disease, psychiatric interference, local pathology (ulcers, abscess, acute infection, need for extraction in sextant, dentin hypersensitivity, endodontic treatment need).
- **Interventions**:
  - *Invasive/computerized*: Morpheus computer-controlled device, 30G needle, vestibular injection 5mm from gingival margin, initial 0.10mL 2% lidocaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine deposited before full insertion, needle advanced to bone contact then repositioned, additional 0.6mL delivered; repeated palatally; conventional carpule supplementation if pain reported.
  - *Noninvasive*: Oraqix gel (25mg/g lidocaine/prilocaine, thermosensitive, neutral pH) applied intrapocket via proprietary blunt-tip dispenser after cotton-roll isolation; onset 30s–2min; up to 2 gel applications before supplementary injectable anesthesia offered.
- **Instrumentation**: Manual subgingival instrumentation with Gracey and McCall curettes.
- **Outcomes**: Primary = pain perception (NRS-11, assessed post-instrumentation by blinded second examiner). Secondary = need/number of supplementary anesthesia applications; hemodynamics (systolic/diastolic BP, SpO2) pre- and post-instrumentation.
- **Baseline instruments**: MDAS (Modified Dental Anxiety Scale, 5–25) for anxiety; 2018 World Workshop classification for periodontal disease staging; periodontal probe measures (recession, PD, CAL, BOP, suppuration, mobility, hyperplasia).
- **Calibration**: intra/inter-examiner kappa on 14% of sample — recession 0.84, PD 0.93, CAL 0.87 (substantial agreement).
- **Statistics**: Shapiro-Wilk normality; t-test/Mann-Whitney for continuous; Chi-square for categorical; alpha=0.05 (JASP 0.19.3).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Demographics: 45F/31M, mean age 44.2±12.9y, balanced between groups (age P=0.556, sex P=0.815).
- Baseline imbalance: computerized group had more severe periodontal disease (Stage III/IV 63% vs 39%, P=0.011) — a limitation to note when interpreting comparability.
- Anxiety (MDAS): noninvasive 9.6±3.7 vs invasive 8.3±2.9, P=0.087 (NS) — both low-anxiety populations.
- **Pain intensity (NRS-11)**: noninvasive median 0 (IQR 0–1) vs invasive median 1 (IQR 0–3), both "mild," P>0.05 (no significant difference). No-pain rate: 37% noninvasive vs 53% invasive. Moderate-severe pain: 18% noninvasive vs 13% invasive.
- **Supplementary anesthesia (key finding)**: 100% (38/38) of noninvasive-gel patients required at least one additional application (median 1, IQR 1–1) vs 24% (9/38) of computerized-invasive patients (median 0, IQR 0–0) — **P<0.001**.
- Hemodynamics: BP and SpO2 stable and statistically similar between groups both before and after treatment; only within-group finding was a significant systolic BP drop pre-to-post in the computerized group (P=0.024), attributed to pre-treatment anxiety resolving during the procedure.
- Age/sex did not influence pain experience.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Parallel (not crossover) design increases inter-subject variability; authors note crossover would reduce interpersonal bias but risks carry-over effects — a deliberate trade-off, not an oversight.
- No participant/clinician blinding possible given technique differences (needle vs gel); only the pain-outcome assessor was blinded.
- Baseline periodontal severity imbalance between groups (more Stage III/IV in the computerized arm) is a potential confounder for pain-intensity comparability, though it did not appear to disadvantage that group.
- Single-center, Brazilian university clinic population — generalizability to other settings/operators untested.
- Electronic/computerized devices carry higher acquisition and maintenance cost, a practical adoption barrier not quantified here.
- No long-term follow-up (e.g., patient technique preference for future visits) reported beyond the single-session comparison.

## 6. Related Work
- Cites prior comparative literature on computer-controlled anesthetic delivery vs intrapocket lidocaine/prilocaine gel, generally reporting invasive/computerized techniques achieve superior pain control and patient preference — this study nuances that by showing pain-intensity parity but a large rescue-anesthesia gap.
- Aligns conceptually with [[local-anesthesia/wambier-2017-intrapocket-topical-versus-injected-anesthetic-srp]] (SR+MA, 6 RCTs), which found injected anesthetic superior to intrapocket topical gel on pain intensity (P=.03) and rescue-anesthetic need (P<.0001) during SRP — Cabral 2026's rescue-need finding is directionally consistent, though its pain-intensity finding (parity) is not, likely reflecting the specific computerized-delivery protocol and gel product (Oraqix) used.

## 7. Glossary
- **Computer-controlled local anesthetic delivery system**: Electronic device that regulates injection flow rate/pressure (e.g., Wand-type, or the Morpheus device used here) to reduce injection discomfort.
- **Intrapocket topical anesthesia**: Gel-based, needle-free anesthetic applied directly into the periodontal pocket rather than injected submucosally.
- **NRS-11**: 11-point Numerical Rating Scale (0=no pain, 10=worst pain) for patient-reported pain intensity.
- **MDAS**: Modified Dental Anxiety Scale, a 5-item, 5–25 point validated instrument for dental anxiety.
- **Rescue/supplementary anesthesia**: Additional anesthetic administered when the initial technique fails to achieve adequate analgesia during the procedure.
- **Subgingival instrumentation**: Manual, sonic, or ultrasonic removal of subgingival plaque/calculus (scaling/root planing) as nonsurgical periodontal therapy.
