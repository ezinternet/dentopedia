## One-line Summary

Finite element analysis showing that lower trabecular bone density (D4) and reduced cortical thickness increase peri-implant stress, and that thread design and abutment angulation modulate stress distribution under a 30° 200 N oblique load.

## 한줄요약

유한요소해석(FEA) — trabecular 골밀도 저하(D4)와 cortical 두께 감소가 임플란트 주위 응력을 증가시키며, 나사 설계·지대주 각도가 30° 200N 경사하중에서 응력분포를 좌우.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Quantifies how bone density (D1–D4) and cortical thickness drive peri-implant stress.
- Isolates thread design and abutment angulation as stress-distribution modifiers.
- Provides a biomechanical rationale for design choices in low-density bone.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- 3D finite element analysis (in silico).
- Two implant designs (different thread geometry; same length/brand); four bone types; two abutment angulations.
- Loading: 30° oblique load of 200 N on abutments.
- Outcomes: maximum/minimum principal stress (bone), Von Mises stress (implant).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Load concentrated at the coronal portion of bone and implant.
- D4 (low-density) bone: highest stress for the implant design tested.
- Decreased trabecular density and decreased cortical thickness → increased stress.
- Thread design and angled abutments significantly affect stress on bone and implant.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- FEA is a simplified in silico model (homogeneous/isotropic assumptions; static load).
- No biological healing or fatigue/cyclic loading.
- Two designs only; results device-specific.

## 6. Related Work
- Thread-geometry/osseointegration reviews; cortical-thickness–stability literature.

## 7. Glossary
- FEA: finite element analysis. D1–D4: Misch bone-density classes.
- Von Mises stress: scalar stress measure for yield prediction.
