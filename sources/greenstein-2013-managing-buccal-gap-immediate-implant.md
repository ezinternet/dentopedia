## Why Ingested

즉시식립 (Immediate Implant Placement) 시 협측 간격(buccal gap) 관리 결정은 임상에서 자주 직면하는 문제이지만, 골이식 시행 여부의 임계값과 임플란트 위치 선정 기준이 불분명하다. 본 CE 리뷰(Greenstein & Cavallaro 2013)는 동물실험·인체임상 데이터를 종합해 14가지 실용 가이드라인을 도출하며, [[immediate-implant/chen-2007-immediate-implant-nonsubmerged-outcomes]]과 함께 협측 갭 의사결정 트리의 핵심 참고문헌으로 기능한다.

## One-line Summary

CE narrative review synthesizing animal and human data on buccal gap management in immediate implant placement, providing 14 clinical guidelines on grafting thresholds, implant positioning, buccal plate thickness, and flap approaches.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Practical 14-point guideline set** for managing the buccal gap and buccal plate during and after immediate implant placement
2. Critical synthesis of animal model vs. human clinical data, highlighting where animal data may not directly translate
3. Decision framework for grafting vs. no grafting based on gap size (< 2 mm vs. ≥ 2 mm threshold)
4. Emphasis on implant positioning (palatal/lingual, 2 mm from buccal plate, 1 mm subcrestal) as the primary buccal bone preservation strategy
5. Comprehensive table of treatment options with and without flap elevation

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Literature review of experimental animal studies (primarily dog models) and human clinical trials
- Comparison of data from created (artificial) defects vs. natural extraction gap healing
- Covers: gap fill biology, biomaterial options (DBBM, allografts, barriers), flap vs. flapless approaches, soft-tissue biotype effects, implant surface effects, buccal plate thickness effects

**Key limitation of animal data**: Dog bone has higher mineral density and remodels faster than humans; artificially created defects heal more completely than natural gaps — results must be interpreted cautiously.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Gap Fill Thresholds
- **< 2 mm gaps**: Usually heal spontaneously without biomaterial in both submerged and non-submerged protocols
- **≥ 2 mm gaps**: Spontaneous bone regeneration less predictable; grafting recommended
- **> 3–4 mm gaps**: Bone fill has been reported without grafts (Botticelli et al, Tarnow & Chu), but incomplete fill risk increases with gap size
- Natural gaps heal less completely than artificially created defects (important limitation when reading animal studies)

### Buccal Plate Thickness
- **> 1 mm**: Less bone resorption in dog models (Ferrus et al)
- **> 2 mm**: Does not usually resorb vertically in humans after extraction (Spray et al)
- **< 1–2 mm**: Hard-tissue augmentation recommended to maintain crestal bone
- Approximately 33% of patients show buccal gingival recession (~1 mm) after immediate implant placement

### Implant Positioning
- Place **2 mm from buccal plate** to avoid encroachment and additional resorption
- Position **lingually/palatally** to socket center — buccally placed implants show 3× more recession than lingually placed (1.8 vs. 0.6 mm)
- Place **1 mm subcrestal** to account for crestal bone resorption
- Wider implants near the buccal plate cause greater bone loss than narrower implants

### Biomaterials
- DBBM (deproteinized bovine bone mineral) is most studied and widely used
- DBBM reduces horizontal bone resorption by ~25% (Chen et al); results in more coronal BIC and thicker buccal plate vs. no graft
- No single graft material demonstrably superior to others
- Augmentation procedures reduce horizontal bone resorption but NOT crestal bone loss

### Flap Approach
- No clear consensus on flap vs. flapless for vertical bone loss
- Flapless approach preserves horizontal bone width better (more recent evidence)
- Authors recommend: no flap elevation in the esthetic zone; raise lingual flap only if visualization needed
- Flap elevation alone (without extraction) causes ~0.5 mm bone loss

### Survival Rates
- Immediate implants in fresh extraction sockets and healed ridges: similar survival (97.3–99%)
- Similar survival at infected sites and sites with periapical lesions

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Critical gap size for unaided healing remains undefined due to multiple confounding variables
- Exact relationship between bone modeling and recession is unclear
- Most histological evidence from animal models; human biopsies are limited
- Only histological studies can confirm true osseointegration when gap fills with bone
- Long-term data on comparative biomaterial efficacy is limited

## 6. Related Work

- Araújo et al (2006, 2010): Dog model series showing buccal plate resorption after immediate implant placement; DBBM reduces resorption
- Botticelli et al (2003, 2004, 2006): Jumping distance concept; human gap healing without bone grafts in defects ≥ 3 mm
- Caneva et al (series): Lingual implant positioning reduces buccal plate resorption; DBBM + collagen barrier preserves horizontal dimension
- Chen ST et al (2007): Prospective clinical study of non-submerged immediate implants — key source for Chen's data cited throughout
- Tarnow & Chu: Histological evidence of bone fill in a 4 mm gap healed by secondary intention (no flap, no graft)
- Spray et al: Buccal plate > 2 mm does not resorb vertically post-extraction

## 7. Glossary

- **Buccal gap / jumping distance**: Space between the immediate implant surface and surrounding alveolar bone after extraction; has horizontal width and vertical depth dimensions
- **DBBM (Deproteinized Bovine Bone Mineral)**: Xenograft material (e.g., Bio-Oss); most commonly used gap filler in reviewed studies
- **BIC (Bone-to-Implant Contact)**: Histological measure of osseointegration quality; distance from bone crest to first BIC indicates quality of coronal integration
- **Platform switching**: Placing a smaller-diameter abutment on a wider-diameter implant; reduces crestal bone resorption
- **Flapless implant insertion**: Implant placed without reflecting a mucoperiosteal flap; reduces periosteal blood supply disruption
- **Biotype (periodontal)**: Tissue thickness/architecture; thick biotype more resistant to recession than thin biotype
- **Secondary intention healing**: Wound allowed to fill and contract without primary closure of soft tissue
