## Why Ingested

Part of an autoimmune-dentistry batch building out the oral-medicine view of systemic autoimmune disease. Where [[wiki/implants/esimekara-2022-dental-implants-autoimmune-diseases-sr]] covers whether implants survive in autoimmune patients (SLE among them), this SR maps the *oral mucosal/soft-tissue burden* of SLE itself — oral ulcers (the most prevalent, and a classification criterion), hyposalivation, mucosal lesions, caries and periodontal disease — which is the diagnostic and risk context a clinician needs before considering any implant or restorative work in these patients. It also reinforces the oral-mucosal differential-diagnosis theme shared with [[wiki/oral-medicine/opmd/kaur-2022-oral-lichen-planus-malignant-disorder-appraisal]] (SLE oral lesions mimic lichen planus, pemphigus, syphilis).

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (15 STROBE-appraised observational studies, PRISMA/PROSPERO CRD42021291356) finding oral ulcers the most prevalent oral manifestation of SLE (11 of 14 prevalence studies), most commonly on the hard palate, with hyposalivation, pigmentation, glossodynia, cheilitis and secondary Sjögren also frequent.

## 한줄요약

전신홍반루푸스(SLE) 환자의 구강 증상을 다룬 15편 관찰연구 체계적 고찰 — 구강궤양(oral ulcer)이 가장 흔한 증상(14편 중 11편)이며 경구개(hard palate)에 호발, 타액감소·색소침착·설통·구순염·이차 쇼그렌도 빈번. 구강궤양은 청소년기 발병 SLE에서 특히 흔하다.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Consolidates 15 observational studies (2012–2022) on the oral manifestations of SLE into a single qualitative synthesis with explicit STROBE risk-of-bias appraisal.
2. Establishes **oral ulcers as the dominant oral manifestation** of SLE (11 of 14 prevalence-reporting studies), reinforcing their status as a formal SLE classification criterion (EULAR/ACR 2019) and an early-diagnosis flag.
3. Identifies the **hard palate as the most frequent location** (all 6 studies reporting location agreed), with soft palate and lower-lip vermilion next.
4. Surfaces a **juvenile- vs adult/late-onset gradient**: oral ulcers significantly more common in juvenile-onset SLE; hyposalivation and secondary Sjögren more characteristic of adult/late-onset.
5. Links specific oral findings to disease activity and mechanism (CD34 polymorphism, VEGF-pathway dysregulation, antiphospholipid antibody, immunosuppressant-driven periodontal/candidal infection).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Question (PICO/PCO)**: in patients with SLE (P), vs healthy controls (C), which oral manifestations are present (O)?
- **Databases**: PubMed/Medline, Web of Science, Scopus. Searched 19 Nov 2021, updated 15 Feb 2022. MeSH terms for "systemic lupus erythematosus" (incl. Libman–Sacks disease) AND "oral manifestations", Boolean-combined.
- **Eligibility**: articles Jan 2012–Feb 2022 identifying/analyzing oral manifestations in SLE patients; English and Spanish only. Excluded: systemic-manifestation-only reports.
- **Selection**: deduplication in Mendeley → title/abstract screen → full-text eligibility confirmation.
- **Data extraction** (single reviewer, PGR): authorship/year, study type, most frequent manifestations, most frequent oral locations, treatment, differential diagnosis, associated conditions; association significance via p-value.
- **Quality**: STROBE 22-item scale by author consensus — 16–22 = low risk of bias, 8–15 = moderate, ≤7 = high.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Screening flow (PRISMA)**: 180 records identified (PubMed 70, WoS 25, Scopus 85) → 65 duplicates removed → 115 title/abstract screened → 94 excluded → 21 full-text → 6 excluded → **15 studies included**.
- **Risk of bias**: 11/15 low risk, 4/15 moderate risk, 0 high risk.
- **Most prevalent manifestation**: **oral ulcers** in 11 of 14 prevalence-reporting studies.
- **Most frequent location**: **hard palate** (all 6 location-reporting studies), then soft palate and lower-lip vermilion.
- **Zakeri et al.**: oral involvement in **61.4%**; commonest = aphthous ulcers, erosion, hyperkeratosis, pigmentation; mainly hard palate.
- **Hyposalivation**: Leite et al. — **>75%** of SLE patients had hyposalivation; worsens with age and SLE activity; drives xerostomia.
- **Other manifestations**: hyperpigmentation (antimalarial-related), oral mucosal lesions, dental caries, periodontal disease, candidiasis, glossodynia, fissured (cleft) tongue, angular cheilitis, dysphagia, dysgeusia.
- **Associations / mechanisms**: CD34 polymorphism (Ali et al.) and VEGF-pathway dysregulation (Aterido et al.) linked to oral ulceration; antiphospholipid (APL) antibody associated with *lower* ulcer prevalence; periodontal disease more common when SLE duration >8 years (Hammoudeh et al.); Crincoli et al. — hyposalivation significantly associated with SLE, plus oral ulcers, glossodynia, fissured tongue, cheilitis significantly SLE-associated.
- **Juvenile vs adult onset**: Fonseca et al. — oral ulceration **45.5%** (juvenile) vs **17.5%** (adult); Artim-Esen et al. — **23.1%** (juvenile) vs **15.4%** (adult). Secondary Sjögren significantly more prevalent in late-onset SLE.
- **Bibliometrics**: most studies from Brazil (3) then China (2); 5 articles published in 2020; SAGE journals carried 4, Journal of Rheumatology 3.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Small number of usable articles (15) providing oral- (non-systemic) specific data; many retrieved records were repetitive.
- Heterogeneous observational designs; **qualitative synthesis only (no meta-analysis)** — no pooled prevalence estimate.
- Single-reviewer data extraction (PGR), increasing extraction-bias risk.
- English/Spanish-only restriction risks language bias.
- Oral lesions of SLE overlap clinically with lichen planus, pemphigus, syphilis and COVID-19 — differential diagnosis required; authors call for more dedicated studies.

## 7. Glossary

- **SLE (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus)**: chronic multi-system autoimmune disease driven by antibodies against nuclear/cytoplasmic antigens; relapsing-remitting course.
- **Oral ulcer**: mucosal break; commonest oral manifestation of SLE and an EULAR/ACR classification criterion.
- **Hyposalivation**: objectively reduced salivary flow (vs xerostomia = subjective dry-mouth sensation).
- **Glossodynia**: burning/painful tongue.
- **Secondary Sjögren syndrome**: sicca complex (dry eyes/mouth) accompanying another autoimmune disease such as SLE.
- **STROBE**: 22-item reporting/quality scale for observational epidemiology studies, used here for risk-of-bias grading.
- **EULAR/ACR 2019 criteria**: updated joint classification criteria for SLE.
- **VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor) pathway**: angiogenesis regulator; dysregulation linked to oral ulceration in SLE.
