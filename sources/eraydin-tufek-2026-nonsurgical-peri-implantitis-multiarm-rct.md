## Why Ingested

This assessor-blinded RCT (80 implants / 26 patients, 5 arms, 6 mo) reinforces the in-vitro biofilm-removal finding [[wiki/implants/peri-implantitis/pujarern-2024-biofilm-removal-implant-airflow-erythritol]] with clinical short-term outcomes for air-powder abrasion, and complements the adjunctive-therapy synthesis in [[wiki/implants/peri-implantitis/mauriello-2026-peri-implant-mucositis-adjunctive-narrative-review]]. It directly tests whether common nonsurgical adjuncts (CHX, ozone, glycine powder air abrasion) add benefit over mechanical instrumentation alone in early peri-implantitis.

## One-line Summary

Assessor-blinded multi-arm RCT (80 implants, 26 patients, 5 nonsurgical arms, 6 mo): all modalities improved PPD/BOP/plaque, with glycine powder air abrasion + mechanical instrumentation giving the greatest numerical reductions, but NO statistically significant between-group differences.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Head-to-head comparison of five nonsurgical decontamination strategies for early peri-implantitis within a single randomized, assessor-blinded design.
- Tests three common adjuncts (chlorhexidine irrigation, ozone, glycine powder air abrasion) against mechanical instrumentation alone, plus glycine powder air abrasion as monotherapy.
- Provides 6-month clinical (PPD/BOP/plaque) outcomes addressing whether adjuncts confer measurable advantage over baseline mechanical debridement.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Randomized, assessor-blinded, multi-arm controlled clinical trial.
- **Sample**: 80 implants from 26 patients.
- **Groups (5)**:
  1. Mechanical instrumentation alone
  2. Mechanical instrumentation + chlorhexidine irrigation
  3. Mechanical instrumentation + ozone
  4. Mechanical instrumentation + glycine powder air abrasion
  5. Glycine powder air abrasion monotherapy
- **Outcomes**: Probing pocket depth (PPD), bleeding on probing (BOP), modified plaque index.
- **Timepoints**: Baseline, 3 months, 6 months.
- **Analysis**: Linear mixed-effects models with patient-level clustering (accounts for multiple implants per patient).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

(abstract-only — full text not retrieved; no point estimates beyond the abstract)

- All five modalities produced significant clinical improvements over 6 months.
- Greatest numerical reductions in PPD/BOP/plaque occurred with mechanical instrumentation + glycine powder air abrasion.
- No statistically significant differences among the five groups.
- **Conclusion**: Nonsurgical mechanical instrumentation ± adjuncts yields meaningful short-term improvement in early peri-implantitis; adjunctive glycine powder air abrasion may add benefit without clear overall superiority.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Abstract-only ingest: exact effect sizes, confidence intervals, and per-group means not available.
- Short follow-up (6 months); long-term peri-implant stability and re-treatment rates unknown.
- Modest sample (26 patients across 5 arms) limits power to detect between-group differences — a null between-group result may reflect underpowering rather than true equivalence.
- "Early peri-implantitis" framing limits generalizability to advanced defects.

## 7. Glossary

- **PPD (Probing Pocket Depth)**: Depth from gingival/mucosal margin to base of pocket on probing.
- **BOP (Bleeding on Probing)**: Bleeding response to probing; marker of peri-implant mucosal inflammation.
- **Glycine powder air abrasion**: Low-abrasive air-polishing decontamination using glycine powder to disrupt biofilm on implant surfaces.
- **Mechanical instrumentation**: Debridement with curettes/ultrasonic devices to remove biofilm/calculus.
- **Linear mixed-effects model**: Regression accounting for clustered/repeated observations (here, multiple implants per patient).
