## Why Ingested

This is the contemporary synthesis tying together the perio-restorative batch: it reviews margin location, biologic width / supracrestal tissue attachment, contour and finish-line design as they affect the periodontium, drawing on long-term cohort evidence such as [[wiki/periodontics/schatzle-2001-restoration-margins-periodontal-tissues-26year]] and reframing the biologic-width concept covered in [[wiki/periodontics/hamasni-2017-biologic-width-restorative-dentistry]] and [[wiki/periodontics/alam-2024-supracrestal-tissue-attachment-biologic-width]]. It provides the modern "intracrevicular margins do not cause periodontitis, but large overhangs do" nuance that updates older blanket warnings.

## One-line Summary

Narrative (systematically-searched) review concluding that contemporary tooth-supported restorations/FDPs are compatible with periodontal health when oral hygiene is maintained — subgingival/intracrevicular margins per se do not cause periodontitis, but large overhangs and connective-tissue-attachment invasion drive inflammation and bone loss.

- **Authors**: Carlo Ercoli, Dennis Tarnow, Carlo E. Poggio, Alexandra Tsigarida, Marco Ferrari
- **Journal**: Journal of Prosthodontics 2021;30(4):305–317 (first published 19 Nov 2020)
- **DOI**: 10.1111/jopr.13292
- **Study type**: Narrative review (systematic literature search, narrative synthesis)

## 2. Key Contributions

- Synthesizes periodontal outcomes of direct restorations, indirect restorations, biologic width / supracrestal tissue attachment, and tooth preparation / finish-line design into one framework.
- Clarifies that intracrevicular margins do not *cause* periodontal diseases; inflammation and bone loss occur with large overhangs (both direct and indirect restorations).
- Notes that different restorative materials elicit different clinical responses when placed within the sulcus or attachment, and that finish-line design does not produce clinically different periodontal responses.
- Establishes that gingival displacement during impression can cause recession, and that emergence profile spans a range of values not inherently associated with periodontal disease.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- PubMed searched via a previously reported systematic methodology, expanded to a larger database.
- Filters: case reports, clinical trial, review, guideline, RCT, meta-analysis, systematic reviews, English.
- Narrative synthesis organized into four sections: direct restorations, indirect restorations, biologic width / supracrestal tissue attachment, tooth preparation / finish-line design.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Increased gingival index, BOP, probing depth and clinical attachment loss are associated with subgingival restorations, BUT intracrevicular margins alone do not cause periodontal disease.
- Inflammation and bone loss occur (direct and indirect) only with large overhangs.
- Removing the connective tissue attachment causes histological changes, apical shift, then re-establishment of the attachment.
- Finish-line design: clinically not different periodontal response across designs.
- Conclusion: contemporary materials/procedures are compatible with periodontal health given adequate self-performed oral hygiene; periodontal diagnostic criteria should be reviewed before fixed restorative treatment.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (not meta-analytic) synthesis — no pooled effect estimates; heterogeneous evidence base spanning decades.
- Reliance on the 2017 World Workshop classification framing; conclusions depend on assumption of maintained oral hygiene.

## 6. Related Work

## 7. Glossary

- **Intracrevicular margin**: restoration margin placed within the gingival sulcus but not invading the attachment (distinguished from frank subgingival overhang).
- **Supracrestal tissue attachment (biologic width)**: junctional epithelium + connective tissue attachment between sulcus base and alveolar crest.
- **Emergence profile**: contour of the restoration as it emerges from the gingival sulcus.
