## Why Ingested

The first Bayesian network meta-analysis (NMA) directly ranking all at-home bleaching concentrations (CP 5%–37% and HP 2%–16%) for efficacy — fills the gap left by pairwise RCTs in [[tooth-whitening/terra-2025-at-home-bleaching-concentration-tooth-sensitivity]], which compared individual concentrations but could not produce a ranked network. The same research group (Loguercio/Reis) enabling direct comparison with their earlier work.

## One-line Summary

SR + Bayesian NMA of 81 eligible RCTs (53 in NMA) found most at-home bleaching concentrations equally efficacious except extreme concentrations (CP 5% and HP 2–4% inferior to CP 15–17% and HP 14–16%), with low quality of evidence.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First Bayesian NMA to simultaneously compare all available at-home bleaching agents (CP and HP across concentration ranges) in a single ranked network
- Confirmed that intermediate concentrations are clinically equivalent — only extreme concentrations (lowest vs highest) show significant efficacy differences
- Applied GRADE to quantify certainty: low quality of evidence, driven by high risk of bias in most included RCTs
- Covered 7 databases including grey literature (LILACS/BBO, EMBASE) — most comprehensive search
- Identified the practical insight: clinical concentration choice within the 10–22% CP / 3–8% HP range is not supported to affect efficacy

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Study type eligible**: RCTs comparing any two at-home bleaching systems
- **Databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Central, LILACS/BBO, SCOPUS, Web of Science, EMBASE, grey literature
- **Network type**: Bayesian NMA (indirect treatment comparisons + direct evidence)
- **Primary outcomes**: ΔE (spectrophotometric), ΔSGU (shade guide units)
- **Subgroups**: CP 5%, CP 10%, CP 15–17%, CP 20–22%, CP 35–37%, HP 2–4%, HP 5–7%, HP 10%, HP 14–16%
- **RoB**: Cochrane tool 2.0
- **QoE**: GRADE approach
- **n eligible / n in NMA**: 81 / 53

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**For ΔE (spectrophotometric color change):**
| Comparison | Mean Difference (95% CrI) | Significant? |
|---|---|---|
| CP 5% vs CP 15–17% | −3.36 (−6.53 to −0.22) | Yes |
| HP 2–4% vs CP 15–17% | −1.85 (−3.63 to −0.08) | Yes |
| CP 5% vs CP 35–37% | −4.23 (−8.21 to −0.22) | Yes |
| All other comparisons | Not significant | No |

**For ΔSGU (shade guide units):**
| Comparison | Mean Difference (95% CrI) | Significant? |
|---|---|---|
| HP 14–16% vs CP 5% | +4.66 (0.27 to 9.01) | Yes |
| HP 14–16% vs HP 2–4% | +4.06 (0.74 to 7.46) | Yes |
| All other comparisons | Not significant | No |

- High risk of bias in most included RCTs
- Quality of evidence: low (GRADE)
- **Clinical bottom line**: Within the 10–22% CP and 3–8% HP range used in standard at-home products, concentrations are clinically interchangeable for efficacy

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Low QoE (high RoB in most RCTs) limits clinical translation
- Many studies failed to blind appropriately for agent type vs concentration
- No sensitivity data in NMA (focus was efficacy only)
- NMA indirect comparisons introduce additional uncertainty vs direct RCTs
- Need for well-designed, low-bias RCTs with standardized endpoints

## 7. Glossary

- **NMA (Network Meta-Analysis)**: Simultaneous comparison of multiple treatments using both direct and indirect evidence in a statistical network
- **Bayesian NMA**: Uses prior distributions + likelihood to generate posterior credible intervals (CrI) instead of frequentist CI
- **CrI (Credible Interval)**: Bayesian equivalent of confidence interval; 95% CrI means 95% posterior probability the true value lies in the range
- **GRADE**: Framework for rating quality of evidence: high/moderate/low/very low
- **CP / HP**: Carbamide peroxide / hydrogen peroxide; see das-2025 for mechanism comparison
