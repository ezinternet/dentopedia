## Why Ingested

Plaque disclosing agents are the patient-education backbone of any mechanical plaque-control regimen, yet the standard agent (erythrosine) was FDA-banned in ingested products in Jan 2025, leaving an unanswered substitution question. This RCT directly tests a regulatory-safe alternative (D&C Red No. 33) and complements the mechanical plaque-removal evidence in [[wiki/periodontics/luo-2026-electric-toothbrush-technologies-plaque-gingivitis-nma]] by addressing how plaque is *visualized* for instruction rather than how it is removed.

## One-line Summary

Partially-blinded randomized crossover trial (n=11 healthy adults) comparing 3% erythrosine vs 3% D&C Red No. 33 vs a mixed two-tone agent for plaque disclosure: erythrosine gave the highest plaque visibility but percentage plaque-index reduction was equivalent across all three, while D&C Red No. 33 had the least residual staining, best taste, and easiest removal — supporting it as a patient-friendly, regulatory-safe alternative.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First head-to-head RCT positioning **D&C Red No. 33** as a substitute for erythrosine after the U.S. FDA banned erythrosine (FD&C Red No. 3) in food and orally ingested drugs effective **15 January 2025** (carcinogenicity in male rats at high exposure).
- Shows that despite markedly different baseline staining intensity and visibility, the three agents produce **statistically equivalent percentage plaque-index reduction** — i.e., disclosing-agent choice does not compromise the instructional/plaque-removal endpoint.
- Demonstrates D&C Red No. 33's practical advantages: least residual staining, best taste acceptability, and easiest stain removal — and notes it is already FDA-approved in oral-care products (mouthwash, dentifrice).
- Tests and rejects a **mixed D&C Red No. 33 + green two-tone formulation** intended to differentiate old vs new plaque; it failed to differentiate plaque maturity and gave no added clinical benefit.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Randomized crossover, partially blinded. Each participant received all three formulations in random order, one per visit, with a 1-week washout.
- **Participants**: 11 healthy adults (7F/4M, mean age 22.5 ± 1.3 y, range 20–25), convenience sample at Chulalongkorn University. Sample size 9 + 20% dropout → 11 (G*Power, α 5%, power 90%, primary outcome TQHPI).
- **Interventions**: A = 3% erythrosine; B = 3% D&C Red No. 33; C = mixed 3% D&C Red No. 33 + green powder (two-tone). 3% concentration chosen (6% D&C Red No. 33 had lower solubility; pilot found no 3% vs 6% erythrosine difference).
- **Protocol**: Standardized modified Bass brushing + once-daily night flossing; 7-day familiarization. On testing days: brush in morning only, chew assigned tablet ~1 min, expectorate without rinsing.
- **Primary outcome**: Plaque index by Turesky modification of the Quigley–Hein Plaque Index (TQHPI) on labial surfaces of anterior teeth, scored by a calibrated examiner before and after brushing/flossing.
- **Secondary outcomes (participant-rated)**: plaque visibility (VAS 0–10); residual staining (teeth/lips/tongue/oral mucosa/sink, 3-point scale, immediate and 1 h post-brush); ease of stain removal (3-point); taste perception (3-point).
- **Statistics**: repeated-measures ANOVA, paired t-tests, Friedman + Wilcoxon signed-rank; α = 0.05.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Plaque visibility (VAS)**: highest for erythrosine, then D&C Red No. 33, lowest for the mixed agent; erythrosine significantly higher than both alternatives.
- **Baseline plaque index (TQHPI)**: highest with erythrosine, lowest with mixed; erythrosine significantly differed from both alternatives.
- **After brushing/flossing**: all three groups showed statistically significant plaque-index reduction. Expressed as **percentage reduction**, **NO significant difference** among the three agents — effectiveness in facilitating plaque removal was comparable.
- **Residual staining**: erythrosine highest overall. On lips, erythrosine significantly greater than both others at 1 h; on oral mucosa, erythrosine significantly greater than D&C Red No. 33 at both times. D&C Red No. 33 produced the least residual staining; no significant between-agent difference on the wash basin.
- **Ease of stain removal**: erythrosine rated most difficult to brush off (significantly worse than D&C Red No. 33); mixed intermediate.
- **Taste satisfaction**: D&C Red No. 33 highest, mixed intermediate, erythrosine lowest (all significantly different).
- **Two-tone failure**: the mixed D&C Red No. 33/green formulation failed to differentiate plaque maturity (stained plaque appeared purplish-brown); participants maintained daily hygiene, so little mature plaque was present.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Small sample (n=11), convenience sampling of healthy young adults (limited generalizability).
- Incomplete blinding (mixed formulation visibly distinct).
- Assessment limited to labial anterior surfaces (posterior efficacy not captured).
- Possible carryover despite 1-week washout.
- Uncontrolled chewing behavior causing uneven dye distribution.

## 7. Glossary

- **Erythrosine (FD&C Red No. 3)**: Xanthene red dye, the long-standard plaque disclosing agent; FDA-banned in food and orally ingested drugs as of 15 Jan 2025.
- **D&C Red No. 33**: Azo red dye approved by the FDA for use in drugs and cosmetics, including oral-care products (mouthwash, dentifrice).
- **TQHPI (Turesky-modified Quigley–Hein Plaque Index)**: Plaque-index scoring system rating disclosed plaque extent on tooth surfaces (0–5 per surface).
- **Disclosing agent**: Dye that selectively stains dental biofilm to make it visible for hygiene instruction and plaque assessment.
- **Two-tone disclosing agent**: Multi-dye formulation intended to color new vs mature plaque differently.
- **VAS (Visual Analog Scale)**: Continuous self-rating scale (here 0–10) for perceived plaque visibility.
