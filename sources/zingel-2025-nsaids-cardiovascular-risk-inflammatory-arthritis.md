## 2. Key Contributions
- General population: NSAIDs associated with increased CV risk (established evidence)
- Inflammatory arthritis (RA, AS): NSAID use NOT associated with increased CV risk in this context
- Proposed mechanism: NSAIDs' analgesic/anti-inflammatory properties may confer cardioprotective effect by reducing systemic inflammation burden
- RA/AS patients with elevated systemic inflammation benefit on CV events with NSAID use (observational registry data)
- Oslo RA registry: coordinated treatment (MTX + TNFi) reduced mortality; NSAID prescriptions declined post-TNFi but remain higher than general population in RA/AS
- Pain in RA not strongly linked to synovial inflammation — many patients need long-term high-dose NSAIDs

## 3. Methodology
Narrative review. No systematic search protocol. Reviews data from Oslo RA registry and other observational registries. Discusses mechanisms by which NSAIDs may modulate CV risk differently in systemic inflammatory disease.

## 5. Limitations
- Narrative review — no systematic search, selection bias possible
- Evidence primarily from observational registries; causality cannot be established
- Industry affiliation of first author (MSD Sharp & Dohme) — potential conflict of interest
- Does not address GI or renal NSAID toxicity in this population

## 6. Dental Relevance
Patients with RA or AS are commonly seen in dental practice. This review supports that NSAID prescriptions (for postoperative pain) in these patients do not carry the additional CV risk often assumed. However, long-term NSAID use in this group is typically already managed by their rheumatologist; dental NSAIDs are short-term and lower-risk.
