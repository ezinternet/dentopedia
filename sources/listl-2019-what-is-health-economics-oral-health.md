## 2. Key Contributions

(abstract-only — full text not retrieved; the following are stated in the abstract)

- Frames the core principles of economics and how they apply to the promotion, protection, and restoration of oral health in populations, and to the planning, management, and delivery of oral health care.
- Identifies the **economic determinants of oral health** as the entry point for analysis.
- Centers the **demand for oral health care** on **asymmetric information** between patient and provider — the patient cannot fully judge the need for or quality of care, so the provider's advice shapes demand.
- Explains **market failure** in (oral) health care and its consequences for **efficiency and equity**.
- Positions **economic evaluation** as the tool for allocating constrained resources to maximise oral health gains.
- Highlights the **behavioural** dimension of both patients' demand and dental professionals' provision of services (i.e., supplier-induced demand).
- Outlines **dental workforce planning** methods aligned with system goals.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Not applicable — conceptual primer, no quantitative results. (abstract-only — full text not retrieved.) Key conceptual takeaways:

- Demand for dental care is distorted by **asymmetric information**, giving providers an agency role and creating scope for **supplier-induced demand**.
- **Market failure** in oral health care undermines both efficiency (resources not allocated to highest-value care) and equity (access/utilisation skewed by ability to pay rather than need).
- **Economic evaluation** is the mechanism for choosing among interventions when resources are constrained.
- **Workforce planning** should be derived from system goals (need/demand) rather than supply inertia.

## 7. Glossary

- **Asymmetric information** — situation where the provider knows more about the patient's need and the quality of care than the patient does; basis for the agency relationship in health care.
- **Market failure** — market outcome that is not efficient (e.g., due to asymmetric information, externalities, public-good features), justifying intervention.
- **Supplier-induced demand (SID)** — demand created by the provider acting as the patient's agent beyond what a fully informed patient would choose.
- **Economic evaluation** — comparison of costs and consequences of interventions (e.g., cost-effectiveness analysis) to guide allocation under constraints.
- **Equity / efficiency** — distributional fairness vs. maximising total health gain from given resources; often in tension in health-care policy.
- **Workforce planning** — methods for determining the number/mix of dental personnel needed to meet system goals.
