## Why Ingested

Foundational, frequently-cited AHRQ evidence base establishing that low health literacy is associated with poorer health outcomes — the empirical justification for patient communication, education, and plain-language counseling in clinical practice. Underpins the dentist-patient communication pages: it supplies the "why it matters" evidence behind the communication-quality synthesis in [[behavioral-dentistry/communication-relationship/ho-2025-overview-dentist-patient-communication-quality]]. Note the explicit dentistry-relevant gap: the review found NO evidence on oral health literacy and outcomes.

## One-line Summary

Updated AHRQ systematic review (96 good/fair-quality studies, 111 articles) finding low health literacy consistently associated with more hospitalizations, greater emergency-care use, lower preventive-screening uptake, poorer medication and label comprehension, and (in the elderly) worse health status and higher mortality; no evidence found on oral health literacy.

## 한줄요약

AHRQ 업데이트 체계적 문헌고찰(양호/중간 질 96편, 논문 111편): 낮은 건강 문해력 (Health Literacy)은 입원 증가, 응급실 이용 증가, 예방검진(유방촬영·독감백신) 수진율 저하, 약 복용·라벨·건강정보 해석 능력 저하, 노인의 건강상태 악화·사망률 증가와 일관되게 연관됨. 단, 구강 건강 문해력 (Oral Health Literacy)과 결과에 대한 근거는 발견되지 않음.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Updates the 2004 systematic review on health literacy, extending the search through February 2011 and adding numeracy as a distinct construct.
- Synthesizes 96 good/fair-quality studies (111 articles): 98 on health literacy, 22 on numeracy, 9 on both.
- Establishes a consistent association between low health literacy and a defined set of poorer process and outcome measures — the empirical anchor for plain-language and patient-education interventions.
- Identifies that poor health literacy partially explains racial disparities in some outcomes.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Databases searched: MEDLINE, CINAHL, PsycINFO, ERIC, Cochrane Library.
- Date ranges: health literacy 2003 to 22 Feb 2011; numeracy 1966 to 22 Feb 2011.
- Inclusion: studies comparing outcomes by **directly measured** health literacy or numeracy (not proxy measures such as education).
- Two reviewers abstracted data and rated study quality; only good/fair-quality studies retained.
- Population scope: all age groups.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Low health literacy was **consistently** associated with:

- More hospitalizations
- Greater use of emergency care
- Lower receipt of mammography screening
- Lower receipt of influenza vaccine
- Poorer ability to take medications appropriately
- Poorer ability to interpret labels and health messages
- Among elderly persons: poorer overall health status and higher mortality

Additional findings:

- Poor health literacy partially explains racial disparities in some outcomes.
- Numeracy conclusions were limited (few and inconsistent studies).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- English-language studies only.
- No MeSH heading exists for these studies, complicating the literature search and risking missed evidence.
- **No evidence was found on oral health literacy and outcomes** — a direct gap for dentistry; the dental analogue of this body of evidence was not established as of 2011.
- Abstract-only ingestion here; full-text effect sizes and study-level detail not extracted.

## 6. Related Work

- Updates Berkman et al.'s 2004 AHRQ evidence report on health literacy.
- Foundational citation for patient-education and health-literacy work in dentistry.
- Provides the outcome rationale referenced in communication-quality synthesis [[behavioral-dentistry/communication-relationship/ho-2025-overview-dentist-patient-communication-quality]].

## 7. Glossary

- **Health literacy**: the degree to which individuals can obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions.
- **Numeracy**: the ability to understand and use numerical health information (risks, doses, probabilities).
- **AHRQ**: Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (US), which commissioned this evidence review.
- **Oral health literacy**: the dental-specific subset of health literacy; identified by this review as having no outcome evidence as of 2011.
