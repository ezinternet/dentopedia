## Why Ingested

This is the foundational longitudinal evidence behind the modern guidance synthesized in [[wiki/periodontics/ercoli-2021-fixed-dental-prostheses-restorations-periodontium]] and the biologic-width papers [[wiki/periodontics/hamasni-2017-biologic-width-restorative-dentistry]] / [[wiki/periodontics/alam-2024-supracrestal-tissue-attachment-biologic-width]]: it provides the 26-year prospective demonstration that subgingival restoration margins are detrimental to periodontal tissues, anchoring the perio-restorative batch with hard long-term cohort data rather than histology/case material.

## One-line Summary

26-year prospective longitudinal cohort (160 Norwegian males; 615 control vs 98 test mesial sites) showing subgingival filling margins cause higher plaque, gingival inflammation, and ~more attachment loss than supragingival/sound surfaces, with damage detectable 1–3 years after placement followed by a "burn-out" effect.

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: The influence of margins of restorations on the periodontal tissues over 26 years
- **Authors**: Marc Schätzle, Niklaus P. Lang, Åge Ånerud, Hans Boysen, Walter Bürgin, Harald Löe (University of Berne School of Dental Medicine, Switzerland; data from Oslo, Norway)
- **Journal**: Journal of Clinical Periodontology 2001;28(1):57–64
- **DOI**: 10.1111/j.1600-051X.2001.280109.x
- **Study type**: Prospective longitudinal cohort (26-year natural-history database)

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides one of the longest (26-year, 7 examinations 1969–1995) prospective demonstrations that restoration margins placed subgingivally are detrimental to gingival and periodontal health.
- Quantifies the divergence between subgingivally restored (test) sites and sound/supragingival (control) sites for plaque index (PlI), gingival index (GI), and loss of attachment (LA).
- Introduces the clinical observation that increased attachment loss at subgingival-margin teeth begins slowly and becomes detectable 1–3 years after restoration placement, then displays a subsequent "burn-out" (plateau) effect.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Population**: Norwegian longitudinal study of natural history of periodontal disease (Löe et al.); 565 males aged 17–30 enrolled 1969; 160 selected for this analysis on mesial surfaces of premolars/molars.
- **Examinations**: 1969, 1971, 1973, 1975, 1981, 1988, 1995 (intervals 2y for first 4 surveys, then 6y and 8y); same examiner, pointed probe.
- **Groups**: Test cohort = 98 mesial sites that were sound or >1 mm from gingival margin at baseline (1969) but had a subgingival filling margin (>1 mm subgingival) by 1971. Control = 615 sites sound or with margins >1 mm from gingival margin at all 7 surveys.
- **Indices**: Gingival Index (Löe & Silness), Plaque Index (Silness & Löe), Calculus Index, Gingival Caries Index, Gingival Restoration Index (Löe 1967 retention-index system), Loss of Attachment to nearest mm, recession from CEJ (from 1975).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Test (subgingival-margin) sites showed significantly higher plaque index and gingival index than control sites at nearly every survey (GI p<0.001 at all 7 surveys; e.g. survey 1 GI 1.19 test vs 0.76 control).
- Subgingival restorations were confirmed detrimental to gingival and periodontal health (plaque-retention mechanism, consistent with Waerhaug's histology).
- Increased loss of attachment at subgingival-margin teeth started slowly and was clinically detectable 1–3 years after restoration placement, followed by a suggested "burn-out" effect (the rate of additional damage tapered over time).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Observational cohort of a single demographic (Scandinavian middle-class males, good–moderate hygiene, regular care) — limited generalizability.
- Substantial dropout over 26 years (565 → 223 at 26y; 54 of 160 attended all 7 surveys).
- Mesial/buccal surfaces with a pointed probe; pre-modern restorative materials/techniques; cannot isolate margin location from overhang quality in every site.

## 6. Related Work

- Waerhaug (subgingival margin = plaque-retaining factor histology); Newcomb 1974 (subgingival crown margin depth and inflammation); Gargiulo (biologic width histology); Ercoli & Caton 2018 (tooth-related factors consensus); Padbury/Kosyfaki/Schmidt reviews of crown margin and periodontium.

## 7. Glossary

- **Gingival Restoration Index (GRI)**: Löe 1967 retention-index scoring of restoration-margin position relative to gingival margin (0=none/>1mm, 1=<1mm subgingival, 2=>1mm subgingival, 3=grossly insufficient fit).
- **Loss of Attachment (LA)**: distance from CEJ to base of pocket, surrogate for periodontal destruction.
- **Burn-out effect**: observed tapering of additional attachment loss after an initial period of accelerated damage.
