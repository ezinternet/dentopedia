## Why Ingested

The `behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety` folder is currently pediatric-heavy (behavior/anxiety in pediatric patients, physiological monitoring, contemporary assessment). This SR+MA adds **adult** non-pharmacological intervention evidence — quantifying hypnosis/hypnotherapy vs CBT, relaxation, audiotape self-hypnosis, and sedation for anxiety reduction. It extends [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/pediatric-2026-dental-anxiety-contemporary-assessment-management]] by supplying the adult-intervention side of the management evidence base and flagging that CBT (not hypnosis) carries the strongest efficacy signal.

## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 19 adult clinical trials (1979–2021; meta-analysis of only 5 STAI studies): hypnosis shows promising but highly heterogeneous, sometimes null/slightly-negative effects on dental anxiety reduction, while CBT carried the most consistent efficacy evidence.

- **Date range searched**: 1 Jan 1979 – 31 Dec 2021

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Quantitative + narrative synthesis of hypnosis for adult dental anxiety** — pools 19 trials and attempts a meta-analysis, one of the few to do so for this intervention.
2. **CBT > hypnosis on evidence strength** — CBT delivered in varied formats (including one-session treatment) showed the most evidence for anxiety reduction; hypnosis effects were promising but inconsistent.
3. **Methodological heterogeneity is the central finding** — 15 different anxiety-measurement methods across studies forced the meta-analysis down to only 5 STAI-using studies, with effect sizes spanning −4.30 to 6.20 (high heterogeneity).
4. **Audiotape self-hypnosis = most practical** — easiest to standardize/reproduce and deployable by general dentists without a trained hypnotist, but live individualized hypnosis is more effective.
5. **Neurophysiological plausibility** — cites fMRI evidence (Halsband & Wolf) that hypnosis reduces activation in amygdala, ACC, insula, and hippocampus in dental-phobic subjects.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: PRISMA-guided SR+MA; protocol registered on PROSPERO (CRD42020172052, 14 Jul 2020).
- **Eligibility**: RCTs, cross-sectional, comparative, validation, and evaluation studies on hypnosis for dental anxiety/phobia, any age; English-language, 1979–2021.
- **Search**: Five electronic databases + grey literature; MeSH/keyword combination (dental anxiety/fear/phobia × hypnosis/hypnotic/hypnotherapy/sedation).
- **Selection flow**: 257 identified → 223 after de-duplication → 35 full-text assessed → 25 → 19 included (after removing case reports, questionnaire-only, and studies lacking a comparable anxiety scale).
- **Outcome scales recorded**: DAS (Dental Anxiety Scale, most used — 12 studies), VAS (9 studies), STAI (7 studies), plus mDAS, Spielberger STAI, Harvard Hypnotic Susceptibility scale.
- **Meta-analysis**: ProMeta 3 software; OR and mean difference effect sizes; random-effects model, 95% CI; restricted to the 5 studies using STAI because of scale heterogeneity.
- **Risk of bias**: NHLBI/RTI quality-assessment tools; rated good/fair/poor by three reviewers (G.C., T.G.W., S.S.).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Included studies quality**: 15 good, 4 fair, 0 poor (n = 19).
- **Hypnosis techniques reviewed**: audiotape self-hypnosis (most common — 10 studies used audio recording; 7 showed significant anxiety reduction, 1 marginal), progressive muscle relaxation, CBT, Chiasson's standardized optical technique, hypnotherapy, nitrous oxide sedation, benzodiazepine premedication.
- **Meta-analysis (5 STAI studies)**: high heterogeneity; effect size range −4.30 to 6.20; **no reliable pooled effect could be established** due to heterogeneity.
- **CBT**: most consistent evidence for efficacy, including one-session treatment formats — positioned by one included study as treatment of choice for dental phobia.
- **Physiological signal**: one study showed reduced mean diastolic BP and heart rate during surgery with hypnotherapy (audio pillow + relaxation music) vs increases in non-hypnosis controls.
- **Neuroimaging**: hypnosis reduced activation in left amygdala, bilateral ACC, insula, hippocampus in phobic subjects (Halsband & Wolf fMRI).
- **Bottom line**: SR narrative is promising (hypnosis can reduce anxiety as an adjunct), but the MA is inconclusive; some studies show null or slightly negative effects.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Severe **measurement heterogeneity** (15 methods) — only 5 STAI studies were poolable; DAS-using studies often had incomplete data.
- **No double-blinding possible**; outcomes are subjective self-report, and suggestibility/prior hypnosis experience confound the effect.
- No anxiety questionnaire specifically designed for hypnosis research.
- Two pediatric studies were included despite the adult focus; child self-report (often parent-assisted) is unreliable.
- Some included designs used same-patient test/control (e.g., contralateral wisdom-tooth extraction with vs without hypnosis), risking carryover effects.
- **Future work**: standardized RCTs with homogeneous groups, consistent validated anxiety measures, and defined measurement timepoints.

## 7. Glossary

- **Hypnosis**: APA Division 30 — a state of consciousness involving focused attention and reduced peripheral awareness with enhanced response to suggestion.
- **Dental anxiety vs dental phobia**: anxiety is generalized apprehension; phobia is a specific, severe phobic disorder with marked physiological/psychological symptoms and avoidance.
- **CBT (Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy)**: structured psychological intervention; in this review, the technique with the strongest efficacy evidence.
- **DAS / mDAS (Dental Anxiety Scale / modified)**: most-used dental anxiety self-report scale.
- **STAI (State-Trait Anxiety Inventory)**: Spielberger anxiety scale; the only common scale across the 5 meta-analyzed studies.
- **VAS (Visual Analogue Scale)**: 0–100 mm subjective symptom-severity line.
- **Audiotape self-hypnosis**: standardized recorded hypnotic induction, deployable without a trained hypnotist.
- **PROSPERO**: international prospective register of systematic reviews (this protocol: CRD42020172052).
