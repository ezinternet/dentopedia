## Why Ingested

Anchors the 10-MDP functional-monomer batch by synthesizing the three interface features that make 10-MDP the benchmark monomer — self-assembled nanolayering, the acid-base-resistant zone (ABRZ), and stable low-solubility MDP-Ca salts — and ties them to bond durability over time. Reinforces the mechanistic, microscopy-level finding in the sibling page [[wiki/resin-bonding/yoshihara-2011-nanolayering-mdp-enamel-dentin]] (nanolayering on enamel/dentin), aggregating it with 71 other studies into a clinical recommendation (scrubbing application + infiltration dwell time to form stable MDP-Ca).

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA-P; 72 of 1383 references; PubMed/Cochrane/Web of Science/Embase) finding that 10-MDP–containing self-etch and universal adhesives produce a nano-layered, acid-base-resistant interface with low-solubility MDP-Ca salts that yields the most hydrolytically stable, durable resin-dentin bond among functional monomers — provided a scrubbing application technique and adequate infiltration time are used.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Aggregates 72 studies on what physically distinguishes the 10-MDP (10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate) adhesive interface from interfaces produced by other functional monomers (4-META, phenyl-P, HEMA), across three axes: molecular structure/nanolayering, acid-base-resistant zone (ABRZ) formation, and adhesive stability over time.
2. Grounds 10-MDP's superiority in the "adhesion-decalcification" concept: the phosphate functional group ionically bonds calcium in hydroxyapatite, and because the resulting MDP-Ca salt has lower water solubility than salts of competing monomers, the bond remains stable rather than re-decalcifying.
3. Identifies self-assembled nano-layered structures (a signature of 10-MDP) at enamel and dentin interfaces as contributors to a water-stable interface — while flagging the caveat that nanolayering is not seen across all commercial 10-MDP adhesives, so it cannot be the sole driver of durability.
4. Translates the mechanism into clinical application rules: scrubbing (active) application, selective enamel etching for higher-pH one-bottle systems, and sufficient dwell time for the solution to infiltrate, hybridize and form MDP-Ca that protects collagen fibrils.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Protocol**: PRISMA-P.
- **PICO**: Problem = resin-bonded restorations; Intervention = dental adhesives containing 10-MDP; Comparison = other adhesive systems / functional monomers; Outcome = adhesive interface characteristics, bond strength, and adhesive stability.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, Embase. Languages: English, Spanish, Portuguese. Cutoff: January 2019.
- **Screening**: titles/abstracts by one reviewer (M.C.); full texts independently by two reviewers (M.C., E.C.); disagreements adjudicated by a third (A.S.C.).
- **Included material**: both commercially available adhesive systems and experimental formulations (varying component concentrations) so each component's mechanism could be isolated.
- **Yield**: 1383 references (274 PubMed, 9 Cochrane, 711 Web of Science, 389 Embase) → 212 after title/abstract → **72 included** after full-text. No meta-analysis (methodological disparity).
- **Outcome grouping**: 7 articles on molecular structure / hydrophilicity / nanolayering; 6 on acid-base-resistant zone; 22 on adhesive stability.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Nanolayering**: Self-assembled nano-layered structures, typical of 10-MDP, are found at commercial self-etch and universal adhesive interfaces on both enamel and dentin; they produce a more water-stable interface favorable to adhesion and help explain 10-MDP's higher stability — but are NOT identified across all 10-MDP adhesives, so they enhance immediate performance and biodegradation resistance rather than being the sole durability mechanism.
- **Acid-base-resistant zone (ABRZ)**: 10-MDP creates an ABRZ adjacent to the hybrid layer, evidenced across commercial and experimental primer/bond systems, resisting acid-base challenge better than other common functional monomers.
- **MDP-Ca salts**: 10-MDP forms water-insoluble MDP-Ca salts (lower solubility than salts of other functional monomers) that protect collagen fibrils; the MDP-Ca–rich interface remains stable after one year of water storage.
- **Stability vs other monomers (22 studies)**: 10-MDP's long hydrophobic spacer chain resists hydrolytic degradation; competing monomers (4-META, phenyl-P) bond at substantially lower potential or produce hydrolytically unstable bonds.
- **Concentration/purity matters**: some universal adhesives gave poor interfaces because they were less 10-MDP–concentrated → an optimal concentration and purity of 10-MDP likely exists.
- **Component interactions**: MDP-Ca formation depends more on the adhesive's other constituents than on MDP/water concentration; ethanol limits dissociation of MDP phosphate groups; MDP-HEMA aggregates compromise the MDP-collagen interaction (leaving collagen unprotected); zinc ions compete with 10-MDP for calcium; calcium hydroxide raises degree of conversion without harming bond strength or nanoleakage; HEMA-free formulations are preferred (hydrophilicity concern).
- **Application technique**: active scrubbing application improves solvent evaporation, monomer impregnation, water-chasing and smear-layer dissolution (especially mild self-etch); an extra hydrophobic layer over one-step systems improves durability; selective enamel etching recommended for higher-pH one-bottle systems.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- No meta-analysis — methodological heterogeneity precluded quantitative synthesis; conclusions are qualitative.
- Nanolayering's true causal role in durability remains uncertain (not universal across 10-MDP adhesives).
- Cytotoxicity poorly characterized: minimally toxic 10-MDP concentrations promoted inflammatory response and suppressed odontoblastic differentiation of pulp cells; clinical conditions are hard to mimic, especially in deep cavities where adhesive may reach pulp.
- MDP hydrolysis during storage acidifies the adhesive solution, altering its chemical properties over shelf life.

## 7. Glossary

- **10-MDP**: 10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate; functional monomer with a polymerizable methacrylate group, a long hydrophobic C10 spacer chain, and a phosphate functional group.
- **Functional monomer**: acidic monomer that wets/demineralizes substrate and can chemically bond calcium in hydroxyapatite (adhesion-decalcification concept).
- **MDP-Ca salt**: water-insoluble calcium salt formed when MDP phosphate bonds hydroxyapatite calcium; low solubility = durable interface.
- **Nanolayering / nano-layered structure**: self-assembled lamellar layers of MDP-Ca at the interface, signature of 10-MDP.
- **ABRZ (acid-base-resistant zone)**: zone adjacent to the hybrid layer resistant to acid-base challenge, indicating durable chemical interaction.
- **Hybrid layer**: 3D collagen-resin biopolymer linking adhesive to dentin.
- **Nanoleakage**: nanoscale defects at the resin-dentin interface from hydrolytic degradation, serving as degradation pathways.
- **Scrubbing technique**: active rubbing application of adhesive to enhance solvent evaporation and monomer infiltration.
