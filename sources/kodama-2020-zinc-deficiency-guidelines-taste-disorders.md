## One-line Summary

Narrative review summarizing Japan's 2018 Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency (diagnostic criteria: serum zinc <60 μg/dL = deficiency, 60–80 μg/dL = marginal; symptom-based + exclusion + zinc-responsiveness criteria) with a focused section on zinc-deficiency taste disorders — affecting ~240,000 Japanese patients (1.7× increase since 1990), improved by oral zinc (Polaprezinc 150mg/day) in 50–82% of cases but requiring ≥3 months of therapy for full effect (13.6% response at week 4 rising to 58.8% at week 24).

## 1. Document Information

- Journal: International Journal of Molecular Sciences, 2020;21(8):2941
- Publication date: 2020-04-22
- Article type: Review (condensed summary of Japan's Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency 2018, issued by the Japanese Society of Clinical Nutrition, plus original data on IBD and liver cirrhosis mechanisms)

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides the only cited practical clinical guideline (besides BMJ Best Practice) with explicit, actionable serum-zinc cutoffs for diagnosing zinc deficiency (<60 μg/dL) vs. marginal deficiency (60–80 μg/dL) vs. normal (>80 μg/dL), plus a 4-criterion diagnostic algorithm (symptom/low-ALP + exclusion of other disease + low serum zinc + symptom response to zinc).
- Quantifies zinc-deficiency taste disorder epidemiology in Japan: ~240,000 patients (1.7× increase since 1990), more common in women (M:F ratio 2:3), rising with the aging population.
- Documents specific zinc-replacement dosing regimens and their response rates (Yoshida 82% at 67.5mg zinc gluconate; Sakai 76% at 87mg zinc picolinate; Heckmann 50% at 20mg zinc gluconate; Sakagami 80% at 150mg Polaprezinc) and the critical treatment-duration finding that efficacy climbs from 13.6% (week 4) to 47.7% (week 12) to 58.8% (week 24) — establishing a minimum 3-month treatment trial as standard of care.
- Extends beyond taste disorders to mechanistically link zinc deficiency to IBD (via IRF/IL-23-mediated M1 macrophage activation and suppressed GATA-3/IL-4-mediated M2 macrophages) and liver cirrhosis (via reduced ornithine transcarbamylase activity impairing hepatic ammonia detoxification), demonstrating zinc deficiency as a multi-system rather than isolated oral condition.
- Provides rat model evidence for the taste-disorder mechanism: zinc-deficient diet causes taste cell microvilli rupture/vacuolation, impaired basal-cell differentiation, prolonged cell turnover, and reduced bitter-taste-receptor (TAS2R40/TAS2R107) expression.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: narrative review condensing an official clinical practice guideline (Japan's Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency 2018) plus the authors' own prior cohort data (IBD case-control/registry studies; 299-patient cirrhosis cohort) and murine mechanistic experiments (MT knockout mice, TPEN-induced zinc deficiency/TNBS colitis models).
- No formal systematic search methodology reported (guideline-summary format); original experimental sections describe standard in vivo mouse colitis models and human cohort/case-control data separately.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Zinc intake insufficient (below RDA) in 60–70% of Japanese adults ≥20y; inadequate zinc intake prevalence rose from 14.7% (1990/1995) to 15.5% (2000).
- Diagnostic cutoffs: serum zinc <60 μg/dL = deficiency; 60–80 μg/dL = marginal deficiency; >80 μg/dL = normal — though the review notes serum zinc has limited sensitivity for marginal deficiency (cited studies found symptomatic, zinc-treatment-responsive patients with serum zinc up to 78±6 μg/dL and 75.0±12.7 μg/dL).
- Zinc-deficiency taste disorder affects ~240,000 patients in Japan (1.7× increase since 1990), M:F ratio 2:3, rising with population aging.
- Zinc replacement response rates: 50–82% improvement across different zinc salts/doses; standard Japanese first-line agent Polaprezinc (150mg/day) achieves 80% subjective improvement, but with a delayed onset — 13.6% efficacy at 4 weeks, 47.7% at 12 weeks, 58.8% at 24 weeks — establishing that ≥3 months of therapy is required before efficacy can be judged.
- Recommended zinc intake in Japan: 10mg/day (men), 8mg/day (women); actual mean intake 8.9mg/day (men), 7.3mg/day (women) — roughly half the population at risk of zinc-deficiency taste disorder.
- Adult treatment dosing: 50–100 mg/day zinc administration when dietary correction is insufficient; adverse effects include nausea/vomiting (mitigated by post-meal dosing) and copper deficiency (anemia, leukopenia) requiring serum zinc/copper monitoring every 3–4 months.
- Mechanistic (IBD): serum zinc <80 μg/dL in >75% of Crohn's disease patients; zinc negatively correlated with Harvey-Bradshaw Index clinical activity (r=−0.75, p<0.05); zinc deficiency (TPEN model) shifts macrophages toward M1 (IL-23p19-producing) and away from M2, driving Th17-mediated colonic inflammation.
- Mechanistic (cirrhosis): serum zinc significantly lower in Child-Pugh class B/C vs. A; positively correlated with albumin and BCAA/tyrosine ratio, negatively correlated with ammonia; zinc supplementation (150mg/day zinc acetate, 3 months, RCT) significantly increased serum zinc and decreased serum ammonia vs. placebo.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Author-acknowledged: serum zinc level, the primary diagnostic criterion, does not reliably reflect true zinc status especially in marginal deficiency — more sensitive/accurate biomarkers are needed (zinc clearance test proposed as more accurate but less practical for routine use).
- The precise pathophysiological mechanism by which zinc administration improves idiopathic, drug-induced, and systemic taste disorders (as opposed to classic zinc-deficiency taste disorder) requires further study.
- IBD mechanistic data derive primarily from murine models (MT knockout, TPEN-chelation); the authors note the detailed mechanism of zinc-deficiency-enhanced inflammation in humans remains incompletely understood and call for future studies (including potential anti-IL-23 antibody trials informed by the zinc-IL-23p19 pathway).
- Zinc supplementation benefit needs verification separately for each specific disease/condition rather than assumed to generalize.

## 6. Related Work

- Cites the Japanese Society of Clinical Nutrition's Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency (2018) as the primary reference document being summarized, alongside BMJ Best Practice's Zinc Deficiency guidance as the only other comparable published practical guideline.
- Cites Henkin et al. 1970 (first report of oral zinc treating drug-induced [D-penicillamine] taste disorder) as the foundational zinc-taste-therapy reference.
- Cites Yoshida 1991, Sakai, Heckmann, and Sakagami for comparative zinc-salt dosing/response-rate data.
- Cites Reding et al. and Marchesini et al. for zinc supplementation's effect on hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis.
- Cites Schmidt et al. on IL-23p19 upregulation specific to Crohn's disease (not ulcerative colitis) mucosa, and ustekinumab's efficacy as an IL-23-pathway-blocking therapy.

## 7. Glossary

- **Serum zinc clearance test**: a more sensitive (but less routinely used) alternative to static serum zinc measurement for diagnosing marginal zinc deficiency.
- **Polaprezinc**: a zinc-L-carnosine chelate compound, originally approved in Japan for gastric ulcers, off-label-approved for taste disorders in 2011.
- **Zinc acetate hydrate**: originally approved for Wilson's disease, approved for hypozincemia treatment in Japan since 2017.
- **Ornithine transcarbamylase**: a zinc-dependent urea-cycle enzyme; reduced activity under zinc deficiency impairs hepatic ammonia detoxification.
- **Metallothioneins (MTs)**: low-molecular-weight cysteine-rich proteins that bind zinc/heavy metals and exert anti-inflammatory functions in macrophages.
- **TAS2R40 / TAS2R107**: bitter-taste receptor genes whose expression is reduced under zinc deficiency in rat taste bud studies.
- **Th17 / M1 / M2 macrophages**: T-helper-17 cells and pro-inflammatory (M1) vs. anti-inflammatory (M2) macrophage phenotypes implicated in zinc-deficiency-driven intestinal inflammation.
