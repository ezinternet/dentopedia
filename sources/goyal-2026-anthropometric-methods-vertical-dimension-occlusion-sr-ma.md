## Why Ingested

Provides the missing quantitative layer under the OVD-determination narratives: the two review pages [[complete-denture/alhajj-2017-occlusal-vertical-dimension-complete-denture-review]] and [[complete-denture/fayad-2025-vertical-dimension-determination-prosthodontics-review]] *list* anthropometric/craniofacial-landmark methods but do not pool their accuracy. This SR+MA ranks 11 anthropometric methods by their pooled correlation with the standard OVD recording method, identifying which facial/finger measurement is the strongest predictor.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review + meta-analysis (13 cross-sectional studies, 3275 participants, 11 anthropometric methods; J Prosthet Dent 2026) finding anthropometric (facial/finger) measurements correlate highly overall with the standard OVD recording method — thumb length strongest overall (r ≈ 0.63 common / 0.57 random), distal outer-canthus–rima-oris strongest in men (≈0.57–0.58), little-finger length strongest in women (≈0.46) — but with very high heterogeneity (I² up to 99%) and high risk of bias in patient selection/index test.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First meta-analysis to **rank anthropometric (facial/finger) measurements by their correlation with the standard OVD recording method**, answering "which anthropometric proxy best predicts OVD?"
- Provides **sex-stratified** best predictors (subgroup analysis by sex).
- Concludes anthropometric methods are usable OVD predictors but flags **very high heterogeneity** and **high risk of bias**.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **PICO:** Population — edentulous or dentulous individuals (28 fully erupted, periodontally sound teeth) with Angle Class I occlusion; Intervention — anthropometric methods of recording OVD; Comparison — standard OVD recording method; Outcome — correlation between anthropometric-derived and standard OVD; Study type — cross-sectional observational.
- **Synthesis:** correlations pooled under both common-effects and random-effects models; heterogeneity by I²; subgroup analysis by sex.
- **Risk of bias:** QUADAS-2.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Included:** 13 cross-sectional observational studies; **3,275 participants** (1,618 men, 1,657 women); **11 different anthropometric methods**.
- **Strongest overall:** **thumb length** — common effects **r = 0.63**, random effects **0.57**, heterogeneity **I² = 99%**.
- **Strongest in men:** distance between **distal outer canthus and rima oris** — common 0.57, random 0.58, I² = 86%.
- **Strongest in women:** **little-finger length** — common 0.46, random 0.46, I² = 94%.
- **Risk of bias:** high in patient-selection and index-test domains in most studies.
- **Conclusion:** anthropometric methods correlate highly with the standard method and can be used to record OVD; **thumb length is potentially the most accurate predictor**, but overall heterogeneity was very high.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Extreme statistical heterogeneity (I² up to 99%) — pooled correlations must be read cautiously; population/ethnicity differences drive divergence.
- High risk of bias in patient selection and index test (QUADAS-2).
- Only cross-sectional studies; no edentulous-specific validation of the "best" proxies.
- Implies need for standardized, low-bias diagnostic-accuracy studies before clinical adoption of a single anthropometric proxy.

## 7. Glossary

- **Anthropometric method:** estimating OVD from external body/facial measurements (e.g. finger/thumb length, eye-canthus to mouth-corner distance) assumed to correlate with facial vertical height.
- **Standard (conventional) method:** the reference OVD recording technique against which anthropometric proxies are correlated.
- **Distal outer canthus to rima oris:** distance from the outer corner of the eye to the corner of the mouth — a craniofacial-landmark proxy for OVD.
- **Common vs random effects:** fixed-effect vs random-effects meta-analytic pooling models.
- **I² (heterogeneity):** proportion of total variation across studies due to heterogeneity rather than chance; here as high as 99%.
- **QUADAS-2:** quality-assessment tool for diagnostic-accuracy studies.
