Provides mechanistic detail on streptococcal virulence factors and immunomodulatory roles that underpin the polymicrobial ecology model reviewed in [[oral-microbiology/sedghi-2021-oral-microbiome-key-organisms-networks]] and the matrix assembly processes in [[oral-microbiology/jakubovics-2021-dental-plaque-biofilm-matrix]].

## One-line Summary
Narrative review of the taxonomic diversity, virulence factors, immunomodulatory properties, and systemic disease associations of oral streptococci — positioning them as dual-role pioneer colonisers that maintain oral health and, under specific conditions, drive caries, periodontal disease, and extra-oral infections including infective endocarditis.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Classifies oral streptococci into eight phylogenetic groups (mitis, sanguinis, anginosus, salivarius, downei, mutans, pyogenic, bovis) based on 16S rRNA sequencing; mitis group is largest with 20 oral members.
- Documents immunomodulatory roles: 30% of streptococcal isolates from supra-/subgingival plaque downregulate IL-8 in epithelial cells; S. gordonii suppresses IL-6 and IL-8; S. mitis induces hBD-2 while concurrently dampening neutrophil activity via IL-10, PD-L1, PGE2.
- Characterises streptococcal adhesins (AgI/II family, LPxTG-anchored) and their roles in salivary pellicle binding, sucrose-independent biofilm formation, and platelet aggregation.
- Establishes the dual role of oral streptococci as both homeostasis maintainers and "accessory pathogens" enabling P. gingivalis and A. actinomycetemcomitans pathogenicity.
- Reviews streptococcal contribution to systemic infections: infective endocarditis (S. mitis, S. oralis, S. gordonii), bacteraemia, and brain haemorrhage.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: narrative (non-systematic) review; published 22 February 2024.
- Scope: taxonomy; ecology in distinct oral niches; immunomodulation; adhesion and biofilm; caries, pulpitis, periodontitis, and systemic disease; virulence factors.
- Literature synthesis from in vitro, animal (germ-free mouse), and clinical studies.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Streptococcus is the dominant genus in supra- and subgingival plaque and soft tissues.
- Mitis group streptococci (S. mitis, S. oralis) classified as core oral microbiota members.
- 30% of supra-/subgingival streptococcal isolates show anti-inflammatory properties (IL-8 downregulation in epithelial cells).
- S. gordonii interaction with P. gingivalis and A. actinomycetemcomitans substantially elevates pathogenicity of these species.
- In pathogen-free mice inoculated with P. gingivalis, streptococcal cell numbers increase massively; P. gingivalis alone cannot cause periodontal disease in germ-free mice without the commensal core microbiome.
- AgI/II adhesins bind fibronectin, salivary glycoproteins, proline-rich proteins, collagen, laminin, fibrinogen, platelets, and α5β1 integrin.
- S. mutans CovR repressor regulates complement resistance, blood survival, and cariogenic biofilm formation.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Mechanistic in vivo data on streptococcal systemic dissemination pathways remain limited.
- The dual commensal/pathogen nature of mitis group streptococci makes clinical risk stratification difficult.
- Further investigation needed on how specific streptococcal surface glycan modifications influence host immune priming.

## 6. Related Work
- Complements Hajishengallis & Lamont keystone pathogen and polymicrobial synergy models (2012, 2014, 2015).

## 7. Glossary
- AgI/II: Antigen I/II family of cell-wall-anchored adhesins present in most oral streptococci; mediates sucrose-independent biofilm formation and host-tissue binding.
- hBD-2: human beta-defensin 2; antimicrobial peptide induced by S. mitis that kills competing pathogens without harming S. mitis.
- LPxTG motif: cell-wall anchoring sequence for streptococcal surface proteins, catalysed by sortase enzymes.
- CovR: orphan response regulator in S. mutans that represses virulence factor expression including complement resistance.
- Mitis group streptococci (MGS): largest phylogenetic group of oral streptococci (20 members), associated with oral health but increasingly linked to infective endocarditis.
- Infective endocarditis (IE): bacterial infection of heart valves, a major extra-oral disease caused by disseminated oral streptococci.
