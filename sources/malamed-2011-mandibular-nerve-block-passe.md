## One-line Summary

JADA supplement introduction: reviews why the standard inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB) has a high failure rate (e.g., 81% in lateral incisors) — cortical bone thickness, soft-tissue-thickness-driven needle deflection, difficulty locating the nerve, and accessory innervation — framing the case for alternative mandibular anesthesia techniques.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames IANB failure as **multifactorial**: thick cortical bone plate in adults, thick soft tissue at the injection site (→ needle deflection), difficulty precisely locating the inferior alveolar nerve, and accessory innervation — not accessory innervation alone.
- Cites a striking failure-rate benchmark: **81% failure in lateral incisors** for the traditional IANB.
- Sets up the supplement's broader argument (continued in companion papers, e.g. Haas 2011 on Gow-Gates/Akinosi-Vazirani) that alternative injection techniques/devices are needed given IANB's unreliability.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Narrative review / introductory supplement article — synthesizes prior literature on IANB failure causes rather than presenting new primary data.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- IANB failure rate cited at **81% for lateral incisors** as an example of how unreliable the technique can be for certain teeth.
- Four named contributing factors to failure: (1) cortical bone thickness, (2) soft-tissue thickness → needle deflection, (3) difficulty locating the nerve, (4) accessory innervation.
- Despite unreliability, IANB remains the most commonly used technique for mandibular anesthesia.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative/introductory piece, not a systematic or quantitative synthesis — factors are listed qualitatively without a pooled failure-rate breakdown by cause.
- Abstract-only ingest — the full supplement content (detailed technique comparisons) is covered by companion papers, not this introduction itself.

## 7. Glossary

- **IANB (inferior alveolar nerve block)**: the standard mandibular local anesthesia technique targeting the inferior alveolar nerve near the mandibular foramen.
- **Needle deflection**: lateral displacement of the needle tip from its intended path, often due to bevel design and tissue thickness, leading to anesthetic deposition away from the target nerve.
