## Why Ingested

Informed consent (IC) is the legal backbone of shared decision-making — it operationalizes patient autonomy and converts "informing the patient" into a documented care process. This paper extends [[behavioral-dentistry/communication-relationship/elwyn-2025-shared-decision-making-primer-clinicians]] from the communication/process angle into the medico-legal angle: it argues IC is a *care process* of which the signature is the last link, not the ICD form itself, and quantifies the legal stakes (more convictions for defective IC than for malpractice; ~90% of claims relate to information defects). It also bridges to the medico-legal/complaint literature ([[complaint-management/kwon-2017-analysis-of-the-legal-effect]]) by grounding consent failures as the dominant origin of practitioner liability.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review of Spanish IC law/jurisprudence + INFLESZ readability analysis of official dental ICDs: IC is a care PROCESS (not the ICD form), defective IC has produced more convictions than malpractice, and the analysed ICDs (INFLESZ 41.24–52.18) were all below the 55-point readability threshold for the general Spanish public.

- **Material**: National + regional IC statutes and jurisprudence; a sample of official ICDs from Spanish dental societies (orthodontics, implant, filling, extraction, endodontics, periapical surgery, immature-tooth pulp treatment, periodontics, oral surgery) scored with INFLESZ readability software

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **IC ≠ ICD.** The central thesis: it is a mistake to confound *informed consent* (IC, a care process) with the *informed consent document* (ICD, a form). The signature is the **last link in a chain** dominated by the informative process and the patient's deliberation; verbal information cannot be replaced by any written document.
2. **Legal stakes quantified.** To date there have been **more convictions for defective IC than for malpractice**; ~90% of legal claims against doctors/dentists relate to defects in information transmission and IC.
3. **Readability deficit.** All analysed dental ICDs scored INFLESZ **41.24–52.18** — "somewhat difficult," all below the **55-point** cut-off for accessibility to the general Spanish public, despite conforming to international-law content requirements.
4. **Satisfactive (voluntary) medicine.** Dentistry is largely configured by courts as voluntary/satisfactory medicine (e.g., whitening, some orthodontics); the **less necessary** the intervention, the **greater** the information that must be provided.
5. **Adherence link.** Treatment adherence and outcomes improve when patients feel involved and autonomous in shared decision-making.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Detailed analysis of **Law 41/2002** (Spanish national basic regulation of patient autonomy, information, and clinical documentation), regional autonomous-community laws, and relevant jurisprudence; plus historical international instruments.
- PubMed index search (terms: informed consent AND patient information AND informed consent legal AND health literacy), articles from 2002 onward.
- Readability of a sample of official dental-society ICDs analysed with **INFLESZ** online software (score 0–100: 0–40 very difficult, 40–55 quite difficult, 55–65 normal, 65–80 quite easy, 80–100 very easy).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **IC types**: express (verbal/written/unequivocal signs) — required in writing for surgical/invasive procedures; tacit consent acceptable for routine acts but not where law requires express will.
- **Law 41/2002 core**: right to all information (risks, benefits, alternatives, possibility of technique failure); right NOT to be informed (must be in writing); consent revocable anytime; emergency/public-health/therapeutic-need exceptions; consent by representation for incapacity / minors <16 not emancipated.
- **Regional variation**: e.g., Valencia Law 10/2014 mandates information ≥24 h before the procedure and forbids informing a patient who is sedated/asleep or already in the procedure room — clarifications absent from other regions; national law only requires "sufficiently in advance."
- **Jurisprudence**: "double standard" — without physical damage, not all risks need disclosure; with physical damage and no malpractice, exhaustive information is demanded. Damages for lack of IC are compensated even when only one treatment exists and even when the patient would have proceeded anyway. Compensation includes moral damage → fuels defensive medicine.
- **ICD requirements**: procedure-specific (generic forms invalid); must carry clinician signature, patient signature, date, revocation section; patient given a copy.
- **Readability**: 9 dental ICDs, INFLESZ **41.24–52.18**, all <55 → not accessible to the general public; signing ≠ reading/understanding.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative review (not systematic); Spain-specific legal framework — directly transferable conclusions are limited to other jurisdictions only at the level of principles.
- ICD readability sample is convenience-based (dental-society documents), no patient-comprehension testing.
- Recommendation rather than trial evidence: improve ICD readability via shorter sentences/words, fewer technicalities, subsections, graphics/drawings; emphasize clinician communicative skill and empathy in the IC process.

## 7. Glossary

- **IC (Informed Consent, 사전동의)** — the care process by which an informed patient autonomously authorizes an intervention; the signature is its last link.
- **ICD (Informed Consent Document, 동의서)** — the written, procedure-specific form recording the IC; legally a record, not the consent itself.
- **INFLESZ** — Spanish-language text readability index (0–100; ≥55 = accessible to general public).
- **Satisfactive / voluntary medicine (만족적·임의 의료)** — interventions to "improve" rather than treat disease (e.g., whitening, esthetic orthodontics); courts demand higher disclosure.
- **Defensive medicine (방어 의료)** — practice patterns driven by fear of litigation rather than clinical need.
- **Consent by representation (대리동의)** — consent given by a linked person when the patient lacks capacity.
