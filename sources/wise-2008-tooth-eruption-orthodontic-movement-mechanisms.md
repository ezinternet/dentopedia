## 2. Key Contributions
- Compares molecular mechanisms of tooth eruption and orthodontic tooth movement (OTM)
- **Tooth eruption**: dental follicle is essential — surgical removal prevents eruption; inert object substitution with follicle intact → eruption proceeds
- Eruption vs OTM: both rely on osteoclastogenesis and osteogenesis, but differ fundamentally
  - Eruption: primarily physiological, developmental; force secondary
  - OTM: combines pathological (minor injury) and physiological (mechanoadaptation) responses
- Key molecular pathway: RANKL (drives osteoclastogenesis), OPG (decoy receptor, inhibits RANKL), CSF-1 (colony-stimulating factor, promotes osteoclast precursor proliferation)
- CSF-1 accelerates rat molar eruption (not incisor) — functional distinction between limited vs continuous eruption teeth
- OTM three clinical phases: (1) immediate displacement; (2) delay/no visible movement; (3) linear movement
- Soft tissue requirement: eruption requires dental follicle; OTM requires PDL — both absolutely dependent on intervening biologically active soft tissue

## 3. Methodology
- Narrative review of molecular and cellular events
- Focus on teeth of limited eruption (human dentition, rodent molars) — not continuous eruption (rodent incisors)
- Reviews prior surgical, transgenic, and pharmacological studies
- Emphasis on translation from basic science to clinical dental treatments

## 4. Key Results
| Feature | Tooth Eruption | Orthodontic Tooth Movement |
|---|---|---|
| Essential soft tissue | Dental follicle | Periodontal ligament (PDL) |
| Force role | Secondary | Primary driver |
| Process type | Physiological/developmental | Pathological + physiological |
| RANKL/OPG | Both involved | Both involved |
| CSF-1 | Critical for molar eruption | Less central |
| Inflammatory response | Not required | Sterile inflammation required |

- Finite element models used to characterize compressive/tensile strains in PDL during OTM
- Dexamethasone: accelerates incisor eruption but not molar — confirms biological heterogeneity of eruption types

## 5. Limitations
- 2008 publication; molecular tools (transgenic models, ChIP-seq, single-cell) significantly advanced since
- Limited to specific tooth types (rodent molars as model for human limited eruption)
- Some mechanisms inferred from surgical experiments rather than molecular knockouts
