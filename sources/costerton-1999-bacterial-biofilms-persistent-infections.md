## Why Ingested

This is the seminal Science review that reframed biofilms — not planktonic cells — as the dominant mode of bacterial life and the root cause of persistent, antibiotic-tolerant chronic infections; ingested as the foundational citation underpinning every plaque-as-biofilm argument in the wiki. It establishes the structure-and-tolerance principles that the oral biofilm matrix work of [[oral-microbiology/jakubovics-2021-dental-plaque-biofilm-matrix]] specializes to dental plaque, and grounds the community-behavior premise of the polymicrobial models in [[oral-microbiology/lamont-2014-polymicrobial-synergy-dysbiosis-inflammatory]].

## One-line Summary

Seminal Science review (Costerton, Stewart, Greenberg 1999) establishing that surface-attached bacteria embedded in a self-produced polymeric matrix form structured biofilm communities whose inherent tolerance to antibiotics and host defenses underlies most persistent and device-related chronic infections — including dental caries and periodontitis.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Articulated the now-standard **definition of a biofilm**: "a structured community of bacterial cells enclosed in a self-produced polymeric matrix and adherent to an inert or living surface."
- Reframed bacterial life: **sessile biofilm bacteria, not planktonic cells, constitute a major fraction of the bacterial biomass** in most environments and in disease.
- Established that **more than half of human bacterial infections** in mildly compromised hosts involve biofilms, and provided a now-classic table of biofilm-associated infections (including **dental caries** and **periodontitis**).
- Defined the clinical hallmarks of biofilm infection: slow onset, recurring symptoms after antibiotic cycles, ineffective antibody-mediated killing, and resolution often requiring **physical/surgical removal** of the sessile population.
- Synthesized the leading hypotheses for **inherent antibiotic tolerance** of biofilms (matrix penetration barrier, slow-growth/nutrient-limited subpopulations, a distinct surface-induced biofilm phenotype).
- Pointed to **molecular-genetic and quorum-sensing targets** (via the *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* CF-lung model) as a new therapeutic frontier.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

This is a narrative review, not a primary study. Its conceptual architecture:

- **Biofilm structure**: microcolonies on a surface, separated by water channels for nutrient circulation, with spatially differentiated gene expression and metabolism — the authors draw an analogy to tissues of higher organisms.
- **Life cycle**: planktonic cells → surface attachment → microcolony formation → matrix-encased mature biofilm → programmed detachment releasing planktonic cells ("nidus" of acute exacerbation).
- **Model system**: *P. aeruginosa* chronic lung biofilms in cystic fibrosis as the exemplar of modern molecular biofilm research, including O'Toole & Kolter's *sad* (surface-attachment-defective) mutants implicating flagellar motility and Type IV pili (twitching) in attachment/microcolony formation, and *algC/algD/algU* induction showing attachment itself switches on matrix (alginate) synthesis.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **>50%** of infectious diseases affecting mildly compromised individuals involve biofilm-forming commensal/environmental species (e.g., *Staphylococcus epidermidis*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*).
- **Antibiotic tolerance is inherent**, not (only) genetic resistance — three proposed mechanisms:
  1. **Penetration/reaction-diffusion barrier**: the matrix retards antibiotic diffusion; reactive oxidants (hypochlorite, H₂O₂ — also phagocyte oxidative-burst products) are deactivated in outer layers faster than they penetrate, partly explaining why phagocytes fail against biofilms.
  2. **Slow-growth / nutrient-limited subpopulations**: starved or non-growing cells are intrinsically refractory to most antibiotics; biofilms are physiologically heterogeneous, so some cells survive any metabolically directed attack.
  3. **Distinct biofilm phenotype**: a biologically programmed response to surface growth, independent of nutrient limitation.
- **Clinical behavior**: antibiotics clear shed planktonic cells (symptom relief) but spare the biofilm → **recurrent infection cycles** until the biofilm is removed; antibodies are raised but cannot kill biofilm-resident bacteria and may cause immune-complex tissue damage.
- **Representative biofilm infections** (Table 1) include dental caries, periodontitis, otitis media, native-valve endocarditis, cystic-fibrosis pneumonia (*P. aeruginosa*), osteomyelitis, and essentially all indwelling-device/catheter/prosthesis infections.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- A 1999 review: many resistance mechanisms (persister cells, efflux upregulation, eDNA/matrix shielding specifics) were proposed but not yet molecularly resolved.
- Heavily *P. aeruginosa*-centric for molecular detail; oral polymicrobial biofilms are mentioned but not dissected.
- Set the agenda for the field's next two decades: anti-biofilm therapeutics, quorum-sensing interference, matrix-targeting agents, and dispersal-induction strategies.

## 6. Related Work

- van Leeuwenhoek's observation of dental-plaque "animalculi" — invoked as the first biofilm ever observed.
- O'Toole GA & Kolter R — *P. aeruginosa sad* mutant screen (attachment genetics).
- Quorum-sensing literature (the authors' own work on cell-to-cell signaling in *P. aeruginosa* biofilm maturation).
- In dentistry, this review is the conceptual parent of all "dental plaque is a biofilm" frameworks and matrix-biology work.

## 7. Glossary

- **Biofilm**: a structured community of bacterial cells enclosed in a self-produced polymeric matrix and adherent to an inert or living surface.
- **Planktonic vs sessile**: free-floating individual cells (planktonic) vs surface-attached, matrix-embedded cells (sessile); sessile cells dominate biofilm biomass and are far more tolerant.
- **Extracellular polymeric matrix (EPS)**: the self-synthesized hydrated polymer (e.g., alginate in *P. aeruginosa*) that encases biofilm cells and retards antimicrobial diffusion.
- **Microcolony**: a small surface-attached cluster of biofilm bacteria; mature biofilms are microcolonies separated by water channels.
- **Inherent (phenotypic) antibiotic tolerance**: reduced killing arising from biofilm physiology (penetration barrier, slow growth, biofilm phenotype) rather than acquired genetic resistance.
- **Programmed detachment**: regulated release of planktonic cells from a biofilm, which can seed acute infection elsewhere ("nidus").
- **Quorum sensing**: cell-density-dependent chemical signaling that coordinates biofilm gene expression and maturation.
- **Type IV pili / twitching motility**: surface appendages and surface-associated motility required for *P. aeruginosa* microcolony aggregation during early biofilm formation.
