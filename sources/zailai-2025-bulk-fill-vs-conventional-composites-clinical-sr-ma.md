## One-line Summary
SR+MA of 9 RCTs (632 restorations) finding no significant difference in failure rates between bulk-fill and incremental resin composites in posterior permanent teeth (RR = 0.82; 95% CI: 0.33–2.01; p = 0.67).

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis
- **Published**: December 21, 2025 in Cureus (open access, CC-BY 4.0)
- **PROSPERO Registration**: CRD420251173044
- **Search cutoff**: November 2025
- **Protocol**: PRISMA guidelines; Cochrane RoB 2 risk-of-bias tool; random-effects model

## 2. Key Contributions
- Updated SR+MA including RCTs up to November 2025, capturing newer bulk-fill formulations including self-adhesive variants
- Primary outcome: restoration failure (RR); secondary outcomes: marginal adaptation, discoloration, postoperative sensitivity
- Subgroup analysis by follow-up duration (<24 months vs. ≥24 months) showed no significant group differences
- Confirmed that bulk-fill composites' simplified single-increment placement (up to 4–5 mm) does not compromise clinical quality vs. incremental technique

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **PICO**: Permanent posterior teeth with Class I/II restorations (P); bulk-fill composite (I); incremental composite (C); clinical performance/survival over ≥12 months follow-up
- **Databases**: Comprehensive search across multiple databases
- **Inclusion**: RCTs with ≥12 months follow-up comparing direct Class I and II restorations
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane RoB 2 tool
- **Statistical synthesis**: Random-effects model; risk ratios (RR) with 95% CI
- **Studies included**: 9 RCTs, 632 restorations

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Failure rate RR = 0.82** (95% CI: 0.33–2.01; p = 0.67): no statistically significant difference
- Subgroup analysis (<24 months vs. ≥24 months follow-up): no significant between-group variation
- Secondary outcomes (marginal adaptation, discoloration, postoperative sensitivity): comparable across most studies
- Bulk-fill categories: low-viscosity (flowable) requiring a capping layer; high-viscosity (full-body); self-adhesive (eliminates bonding step)
- Conventional incremental technique remains challenged by air voids, time cost, and bond failure risk between increments; bulk-fill addresses these operationally

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Limited number of eligible RCTs (n = 9) reduces statistical power
- Short-to-medium follow-up duration limits conclusions about very long-term survival
- Heterogeneity in bulk-fill material types (flowable vs. full-body vs. self-adhesive) may mask material-specific differences
- Need for more RCTs specifically evaluating self-adhesive bulk-fill restoratives
- Larger cavities and endodontically treated teeth require dedicated study

## 6. Related Work
- Prior SR+MAs have reached similar conclusions (Veloso et al. 2019; Boaro et al. 2019); this review updates with post-2022 RCTs
- Silorane/ormocer trials (Kruly 2018; Magno 2016) contextualize modified-monomer clinical equivalence
- Chaple-Gil et al. 2026 NMA on Class II restorations corroborates findings using network meta-analysis approach

## 7. Glossary
- **Bulk-fill composite**: Resin composite designed for placement in single increments up to 4–5 mm depth
- **Polymerization modulator**: Additive that slows gel-point kinetics to reduce shrinkage stress at cavity walls
- **Self-adhesive bulk-fill**: Bulk-fill material that bonds without a separate adhesive step
- **RR (Risk Ratio)**: Ratio of failure probability in bulk-fill group vs. incremental group
- **RoB 2**: Cochrane Collaboration's revised tool for assessing risk of bias in randomized trials
- **PRISMA**: Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses guidelines
