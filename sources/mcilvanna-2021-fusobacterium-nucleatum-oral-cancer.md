## Why Ingested
Directly extends the oral–cancer axis introduced in [[oral-microbiology/lamont-2018-oral-microbiota-dynamic-communities-host]] by critically reviewing the mechanistic and epidemiological evidence linking F. nucleatum — the bridging organism central to polymicrobial biofilm architecture — to OSCC, including paradoxical prognostic data and STAT3-driven carcinogenesis mechanisms.

## One-line Summary
Narrative critical review synthesizing evidence that F. nucleatum is enriched in OSCC biofilms and oral potentially malignant disorders, promotes carcinogenesis via STAT3/cyclin D1/MMP-9 activation, FadA/Fap2 adhesins, and immune evasion, with paradoxical findings that F. nucleatum positivity associates with better survival in OSCC (unlike in colorectal cancer).

## 2. Key Contributions
- Synthesized evidence that F. nucleatum is enriched in OSCC tissue and oral potentially malignant disorders (OPMDs), supporting a role in early malignant transformation.
- F. nucleatum proportion ~25% in both healthy and disease-associated dental plaque (core species), but total microbial load increases ~3 logs in periodontal inflammation.
- Identified key carcinogenic mechanisms: STAT3/cyclin D1/MMP-9 activation; FadA adhesin; Fap2-mediated binding to Gal-GalNAc on cancer cells; immune evasion.
- Reported paradoxical prognostic data in OSCC: F. nucleatum positivity (82.1% in Neuzillet cohort; 55.8% in Chen cohort) associated with better overall survival, relapse-free survival, and metastasis-free survival — opposite of colorectal cancer data.
- Murine OSCC model: P. gingivalis + F. nucleatum-infected tumors were 2.5× larger and significantly more invasive than in uninfected mice; STAT3 identified as key mediator.
- Tumor biology: F. nucleatum CRC strains identical to oral isolates — suggesting oral-to-distal dissemination.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Narrative critical review (non-systematic); covers OSCC epidemiology, F. nucleatum microbiology, CRC mechanistic literature extrapolated to OSCC, and prognostic studies.
- Two prognostic studies reviewed:
  1. Neuzillet et al. (2021): fresh-frozen OSCC, qPCR, n=151, 82.1% Fn-positive (124/151); Fn positivity associated with better OS, RFS, MFS; associated with older age (>56), non-drinkers, low pN stage.
  2. Chen et al. (2020): fresh-frozen HNSCC, qPCR, n=68, 55.8% Fn-high (38/68); better cancer-specific survival and RFS; non-smokers, lower tumour stage, LXN/SMARCA2 hypermethylation.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- F. nucleatum most abundant species in OSCC in Al-Hebshi et al. NGS study; followed by Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
- Genes enriched in OSCC bacteriome: bacterial motility, flagellar assembly, chemotaxis, LPS biosynthesis — relevant to Gram-negative virulence.
- Murine OSCC: F. nucleatum infection → tumors 2.5× larger; significantly more invasive; induced cyclin D1, MMP-9 upregulation; STAT3 key pathway.
- Harrandah et al.: infected oral cancer cells upregulated MMP1, MMP9, IL-8; MYC, JAK1, STAT3; ZEB1, TGF-β (EMT markers); more and larger lesions vs. uninfected controls.
- Gal-GalNAc–Fap2 axis: PNA staining (surrogate for Gal-GalNAc) weak in normal mucosa, intermediate in dysplasia, strong in malignant SCC — matches F. nucleatum abundance increase along normal → dysplasia → malignancy pathway.
- Neuzillet: 82.1% (124/151) OSCC Fn-positive; better OS + RFS + MFS; older (>56), non-drinkers, low pN stage.
- Chen: 55.8% (38/68) HNSCC Fn-high; better CSS + RFS; non-smokers, lower pT/pN stage; LXN/SMARCA2 hypermethylation.
- F. nucleatum as bridging organism: ~25% of dental plaque in health and disease; facilitates S. gordonii (early colonizer) → P. gingivalis (late colonizer) transition; produces ammonia (from glutamate/aspartate fermentation) to neutralize acid for P. gingivalis colonization.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- No Fap2/Gal-GalNAc mechanism confirmed specifically in OSCC to date.
- Most mechanistic OSCC studies inferred from CRC F. nucleatum literature.
- Paradoxical favorable prognosis in OSCC vs. CRC is unexplained; may relate to HPV-negative biology and patient subgroup (older, non-drinkers/non-smokers).
- Whether F. nucleatum is causal or a bystander exploiting the hypoxic tumor microenvironment remains debated; OPMDs enrichment supports early causality.
- Only one completed systematic review/meta-analysis (confirms higher Fusobacterium abundance in oral/HNSCC vs. non-cancer).

## 6. Related Work
- Landmark 2012 CRC papers (Castellarin et al.; Kostic et al.): first identified F. nucleatum enrichment in colorectal tumors.
- Nagy et al. 1998: first association study of Porphyromonas and Fusobacterium in OSCC.
- Al-Hebshi et al.: F. nucleatum most abundant species in OSCC by NGS.
- McIlvanna et al. note systematic review: Fusobacterium higher in oral cancer/HNSCC vs. non-cancer.

## 7. Glossary
- OSCC: oral squamous cell carcinoma.
- OPMD: oral potentially malignant disorder (e.g., leukoplakia, erythroplakia).
- FadA: Fusobacterium nucleatum adhesin A — promotes E-cadherin/Wnt-β-catenin signaling and invasion.
- Fap2: Fusobacterium autotransporter protein 2 — binds Gal-GalNAc on cancer cells; also mediates TIGIT engagement for immune evasion.
- Gal-GalNAc: galactose-N-acetyl-galactosamine — oligosaccharide overexpressed on cancer cells; Fap2 ligand.
- STAT3: signal transducer and activator of transcription 3; oncogenic pathway controlling proliferation, angiogenesis, invasion, metastasis.
- MMP-9/MMP-1: matrix metalloproteinases involved in tumour invasiveness.
- EMT: epithelial–mesenchymal transition (ZEB1, TGF-β markers).
- PPAD: not in this paper — see lamont-2018 (P. gingivalis RA link).
- CRC: colorectal carcinoma.
- HNSCC: head and neck squamous cell carcinoma.
