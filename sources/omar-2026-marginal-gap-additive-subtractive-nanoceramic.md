## Why Ingested

CAD/CAM 보철재료 카테고리에 milling vs 3D-printing 비교 근거를 보강하기 위해 인제스트. 기존 [[wiki/dental-materials/el-shazly-2026-3d-printed-hybrid-plasma-bonding]]가 3D프린팅 하이브리드 세라믹의 접착 취약성을 보였다면, 본 논문은 같은 재료 계열(Nanoksa)에서 밀링이 변연적합도 면에서도 3D프린팅보다 우수함을 보여 3D프린팅 하이브리드 나노세라믹의 한계를 다각도로 뒷받침한다.

## One-line Summary

In-vitro comparative study (BMC Oral Health 2026, 20 implant fixtures): milled Nanoksa G-Plus hybrid nanoceramic crowns showed significantly smaller marginal gaps (34.08→37.76 µm before/after thermomechanical aging) than 3D-printed Nanoksa BioGuard crowns (59.71→63.83 µm), both within McLean & von Fraunhofer's 120 µm clinical acceptability threshold, but milling significantly superior for this novel material class.

## 한줄요약

In-vitro 비교연구(BMC Oral Health 2026, 임플란트 고정체 20개): 밀링 Nanoksa G-Plus 하이브리드 나노세라믹 크라운의 변연격차(34.08→37.76 µm, 열역학적 노화 전후)가 3D프린팅 Nanoksa BioGuard(59.71→63.83 µm)보다 유의하게 작음 — 둘 다 McLean & von Fraunhofer의 임상 허용 기준 120 µm 이내지만, 밀링이 이 신규 재료군에서 변연적합도 면에서 유의하게 우수.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First direct comparison of marginal gap between milled (Nanoksa G-Plus) and 3D-printed (Nanoksa BioGuard) implant-supported crowns of the same novel hybrid nanoceramic material family.
- Demonstrates milling produces significantly lower marginal gaps than 3D printing both before cementation and after thermomechanical aging, for this newly introduced material class.
- Confirms marginal gap increases significantly after thermomechanical accelerated aging in both fabrication methods, attributed to cement degradation during thermocycling.
- All measured values (34–64 µm) remained under the classic McLean & von Fraunhofer 120 µm clinical acceptability threshold, regardless of fabrication technique.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- 20 dental implant fixtures (Implance, 4.8 mm diameter × 10 mm length) embedded in epoxy resin blocks (elastic modulus ~16.8 GPa, simulating bone) via custom metallic mold with surveyor-standardized long-axis alignment.
- Stock straight titanium abutments (7 mm height, 2 mm collar) torqued to 25 Ncm.
- n=10/group: Group I milled from Nanoksa G-Plus discs (i mes-icore milling machine); Group II 3D printed with Nanoksa BioGuard resin (Inox S2 printer, SLA/DLP-compatible, ethanol-cleaned, UV/N2 post-cured).
- Digital workflow: DOF Freedom HD extraoral scanner → STL → ExoCad design, 60 µm circumferential cement space starting 1 mm above margin, standardized molar crown geometry (10×10×10 mm).
- Marginal gap measured via stereomicroscope (Zeiss, 35X) at 6 standardized points/crown (mesio-/mid-/disto-buccal and -lingual), single blinded examiner, averaged per crown; measured before cementation and after thermomechanical aging.
- Cementation: airborne-particle abrasion (50 µm Al2O3), Z-primer + silane, Duo-Link resin cement, 5 kg static load (49 N) × 10 min seating.
- Aging: 5,000 thermal cycles (5–55°C) + 75,000 mechanical cycles (50 N, 1–1.6 Hz, wet) — each protocol approximating ~6 months clinical service.
- Statistics: paired t-test (within-group before/after aging), unpaired/Student t-test (between-group), SPSS v25, α=0.05.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

| Stage | Milled (Nanoksa G-Plus) | 3D-printed (Nanoksa BioGuard) | T | p |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Before cementation | 34.08 ± 0.85 µm (range 32.98–35.36) | 59.71 ± 0.79 µm (range 58.49–60.51) | 69.50 | <0.001 |
| After aging | 37.76 ± 0.64 µm (range 36.99–38.88) | 63.83 ± 0.57 µm (range 62.54–64.56) | 94.81 | <0.001 |
| Within-group Δ (before→after) | 3.67 ± 0.86 µm | 4.12 ± 1.08 µm | 13.48 / 12.00 | <0.001 (both) |

Milling significantly outperformed 3D printing at both timepoints; both methods showed significant gap increase after aging (attributed to cement degradation via thermocycling), but all values stayed under the 120 µm clinical acceptability threshold.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- No internal (intaglio) fit assessment — only marginal gap evaluated.
- Only one aging duration (~6 months equivalent); longer aging may reveal different long-term behavior.
- Laboratory scanner used (not intraoral), so clinical factors (gingival bleeding, moisture, limited mouth opening) not captured.
- Single material representative per fabrication method (Nanoksa G-Plus / BioGuard only) — generalizability to other hybrid nanoceramic brands untested.
- Authors call for clinical studies to confirm relevance under real conditions.

## 6. Related Work

- Donmez et al. (cited): found 3D-printed resin composite crowns had smallest marginal gap vs milled reinforced composite/PICN/hybrid ceramic — contradicts current findings, attributed to differing resin/filler chemistry.
- Sayed et al. (cited): milled Nanoksa G-plus vs 3D-printed BioGuard — same direction (milled lower) but not statistically significant in that study; also reported thermocycling-driven cement degradation increasing gap, consistent with this study.
- Suksuphan et al. (cited): found 3D-printed polymeric crowns had lower marginal gap vs milled polymeric/PICN — opposite direction, material-dependent.
- Soliman et al. (cited): milled hybrid ceramic (Vita Enamic) gave smoother margins than other CAD/CAM ceramics milled, supporting milling's marginal-fit advantage for hybrid materials generally.

## 7. Glossary

- **CAD/CAM**: Computer-aided design / computer-aided manufacturing.
- **SM / AM**: Subtractive manufacturing (milling) / additive manufacturing (3D printing).
- **Hybrid nanoceramic**: Polymer matrix reinforced with nano-zirconia/nano-ceramic/nanocarbon fillers, combining ceramic and resin properties.
- **Marginal gap**: Vertical distance between restoration margin and abutment/tooth finish line; a key determinant of cement dissolution, microleakage, and peri-implant bone health.
- **Thermomechanical aging**: Combined thermal cycling + mechanical chewing-simulation loading used to simulate in-vivo aging in vitro.
- **STL**: Standard Tessellation Language file format used for 3D digital models in CAD/CAM workflows.
