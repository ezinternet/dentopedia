## One-line Summary

Narrative review (in vitro/in vivo/clinical evidence) detailing how diabetes (chronic hyperglycemia, AGE-RAGE, oxidative stress, prolonged inflammation, impaired angiogenesis) and osteoporosis (resorption-shifted remodeling, low BMD, weak peri-implant bone) impair peri-implant bone healing, and how titanium surface modifications—especially nanotopography (<25 nm nanoporous)—may rescue deficient healing pathways.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Establishes a baseline of normal bone healing (endochondral in long bones vs intramembranous in craniofacial bones) as a reference for impaired healing.
- Contrasts the *distinct* pathophysiologies: diabetes alters bone *repair* via metabolic dysregulation; osteoporosis directly degrades bone *quantity and quality* (structural integrity).
- Maps the molecular pathways governing osseointegration (Wnt/β-catenin, BMP/Smad, MAPK, PI3K/Akt, VEGF, Nrf2, RANKL/OPG, AGE-RAGE) and how each shifts under diabetic vs osteoporotic conditions.
- Reviews preclinical models (STZ-induced diabetic rats/pigs; OVX rats for osteoporosis) and their osseointegration readouts (BIC, BMD, BV/TV, removal-torque/pull-out strength).
- Positions surface modification—physico-chemical, biological, and especially nanoscale—as a strategy to rescue specific impaired pathways in compromised hosts.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative review structured in six parts: (1) normal bone formation in craniofacial and long bones; (2) focus on osteoporosis and diabetes as prevalent, well-characterized models; (3) in vitro and in vivo models; (4) normal vs pathological bone healing around dental/orthopedic implants; (5) role of surface modifications; (6) future perspectives for medically compromised patients. Evidence drawn from biomaterials science, bone cellular biology, and clinical studies.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Epidemiology**: ~415 million adults (≈9% aged 20–79) have diabetes (T2DM = 90%); osteoporosis affects >200 million globally.

**Diabetes / hyperglycemia mechanism**:
- Chronic hyperglycemia disrupts the healing cascade: impairs osteoblast function, promotes inflammation, delays angiogenesis.
- In vivo: diabetic pigs (von Wilmowsky) showed lower BIC and BMD at both 4 and 12 weeks; STZ rats (McCracken) showed increased peri-implant bone volume but reduced direct BIC (dysregulated turnover—bone forms but fails to integrate with the implant).
- Compromised osteogenic environment: impaired angiogenesis, persistent inflammation, increased resorption; thinner trabeculae, decreased cortical thickness, lower pull-out strength.
- Downstream: AGE accumulation, oxidative stress, elevated inflammatory cytokines → diminished osteoblast function, increased osteoclastogenesis.

**Osteoporosis mechanism**:
- OVX models: marked decreases in BIC, peri-implant bone volume (BV/TV), and mechanical anchorage; delayed new bone formation; progressive trabecular deterioration; reduced removal-torque/push-out strength.
- Remodeling balance shifts toward resorption; decreased osteoblast activity, diminished turnover; low-density, mechanically weak bone unable to sustain implant loading; limited osteoprogenitor recruitment/differentiation.

**Clinical outcomes**:
- Diabetes: poorly controlled glycemia → higher risk of impaired healing, peri-implantitis, marginal bone loss; but well-managed diabetics can approach healthy success rates.
- Osteoporosis: lower BIC and primary stability, slightly increased marginal bone loss, but overall dental implant survival often >90% with appropriate protocols/patient selection.
- Shared driver: systemic inflammation (elevated TNF-α, IL-1β, RANKL/OPG imbalance) at the bone-implant interface.

**Surface modification / rescue**:
- Physico-chemical (sandblasting, acid etching, laser texturing, MAO, anodization, hydrothermal) optimize topography (micro/nano roughness, porosity) and chemistry (oxide layer, wettability, bioactive ions).
- Molecular rescue: modulate M1→M2 macrophage polarization, osteoblast differentiation, oxidative-stress response, ECM organization.
- Nanoscale: nanoporous titanium (<25 nm) reduced inflammatory response and provided alternative adhesion mechanisms in genetically impaired cells—via intrinsic physicochemical/mechanotransductive signaling, without bioactive coatings ("reprogramming cell activity"); nanostructured implants maintained high bone formation under loading in healthy animals.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (not systematic) review—no pooled estimates or formal bias assessment.
- A large proportion of differentially expressed genes at the compromised bone-implant interface remain unannotated ("dark" genome); non-coding RNAs (miR-214-3p, miR-26a, lncRNA H19) are emerging but unproven targets.
- Site-specific interpretation needed: biological impact and surface-modification efficacy may differ between dental and orthopedic implants.
- Future directions emphasize personalized, patient-specific implant therapy responsive to biological cues; comorbidities/medications and mechanical loading in compromised conditions need dedicated study.

## 7. Glossary

- **BIC**: Bone-Implant Contact — fraction of implant surface in direct contact with bone.
- **BMD / BV/TV**: Bone Mineral Density / Bone Volume to Total Volume ratio.
- **AGE-RAGE**: Advanced Glycation End-products and their Receptor — hyperglycemia-driven inflammatory/oxidative axis.
- **OVX**: Ovariectomized (estrogen-deficient) animal model of postmenopausal osteoporosis.
- **STZ**: Streptozotocin — chemical used to induce experimental diabetes.
- **RANKL/OPG**: Receptor Activator of NF-κB Ligand / Osteoprotegerin — osteoclastogenesis regulatory axis.
- **MAO**: Micro-Arc Oxidation — electrochemical titanium surface treatment.
