## One-line Summary
Experimental study examining the effects of silane application timing and saliva contamination on bond strength between ceramic restorations and resin cement.

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Research article (Korean dental journal)
- **Year**: 2009
- **Study Design**: In vitro adhesive strength testing
- **Primary Variables**: Silane treatment timing, saliva contamination exposure
- **Test Material**: Ceramic-resin cement bond interface

## 2. Key Contributions
- Quantifies the impact of saliva contamination on ceramic-resin cement bonding
- Evaluates optimal timing of silane application in clinical workflow
- Demonstrates importance of silane protection against salivary peptides
- Provides evidence-based approach to moisture control during try-in and cementation
- Addresses common clinical problem of restoration surface contamination

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Adhesive System**: Silane coupling agents with resin cement
- **Ceramic Material**: Dental porcelain/ceramic used in restorations
- **Saliva Exposure**: Simulated salivary contamination post-try-in
- **Variables Tested**:
  - Timing of silane application (pre vs. post try-in)
  - Duration of saliva exposure (seconds/minutes)
  - Presence/absence of silane protective layer
- **Testing Method**: Shear bond strength testing (tensile strength measurement)
- **Comparison Groups**: Silane-protected vs. unprotected ceramic surfaces exposed to saliva

## 4. Key Results and Findings
- Saliva contamination for >1 second significantly reduces ceramic-resin cement bond strength
- Silane coupling agents form protective barrier against salivary peptides and organic matrix
- Hydrolysis of silanol groups creates siloxane bridges to both ceramic (inorganic) and resin (organic) phases
- Pre-try-in silane application followed by protective measures maintains superior bond strength
- Silane treatment appears more effective than attempting post-contamination decontamination

## 5. Clinical Implications
- Try-in of ceramic restorations poses significant bonding risk through salivary contamination
- Silane should be applied to ceramic surfaces before try-in when possible
- If contaminated during try-in, restoration must be thoroughly cleaned and silane reapplied
- Moisture isolation critical during final cementation after silane application
- Extended intraoral try-in times increase risk of bond failure if proper protocols not followed

## 6. Limitations and Future Work
- In vitro testing does not replicate dynamic oral environment
- Saliva composition varies among individuals; study used standard salivary substitute
- Long-term aging/hydrolytic degradation of bond not assessed
- Future studies needed on different ceramic types and cement systems
- Clinical outcome studies comparing protocols needed

## 7. Glossary
- **Silane coupling agent (실란 결합제)**: Bifunctional molecule with silanol group (ceramic bonding) and organic functional group (resin bonding)
- **Hydrolysis (가수분해)**: Chemical reaction where silane alkoxy groups convert to silanol (Si-OH) groups
- **Saliva contamination (타액오염)**: Exposure of prepared surface to oral fluids containing proteins and enzymes
- **Ceramic-resin cement (도재-레진 시멘트)**: Resin-based cement used for bonding all-ceramic restorations
- **Bond strength (결합강도)**: Adhesive force measured between two bonded materials
