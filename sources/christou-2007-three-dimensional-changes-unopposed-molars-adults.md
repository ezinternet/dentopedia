## Why Ingested

Provides the long-term adult three-dimensional movement evidence underpinning the overview's "unopposed-molar movement is a 3D process (vertical + mesial + palatal), fastest early then slowing" claim — a prospective ~10-year human follow-up that complements the implant-referenced cross-sectional CBCT data. Reinforces [[wiki/occlusion/wang-2022-unopposed-molar-overeruption-implant-cbct]] and extends [[wiki/overviews/unopposed-tooth-overeruption-overview]] with directly measured longitudinal displacement vectors in periodontally healthy vs affected adult molars.

Note: only abstract-level detail available from the source PDF (publisher landing-page print).

## One-line Summary

Prospective ~10.6-year follow-up of 22 unopposed maxillary molars in 12 healthy adults showing slow three-dimensional displacement (vertical overeruption, mesial drift, palatal movement), clinically insignificant in periodontally healthy patients but accelerated by periodontal involvement.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Longitudinal (not cross-sectional) human evidence that unopposed maxillary molars in adults displace in all three planes of space over a multi-year horizon.
- Quantifies the displacement vectors: vertical overeruption, mesial drift (when no mesial neighbor), and palatal movement.
- Shows periodontally affected molars overerupt significantly more than periodontally healthy ones — identifying periodontal status as an effect modifier.
- Concludes the displacement, while statistically present, is clinically insignificant in periodontally healthy adults.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- 12 healthy mature adults (six males, six females; mean age 45 years 11 months) with maxillary molars unopposed for more than 10 years.
- Mean follow-up period 10 years 7 months (~10.6 years).
- 22 unopposed maxillary molars present at both the first and last examination.
- 14 posterior teeth with antagonists (at least one per subject) served as internal controls, selected for no significant change during the study period.
- Plaster casts taken at first and last examination; casts scanned in three dimensions.
- Position change measured as displacement of the centroid of the occlusal surface in the three dimensions.
- Changes compared with a t-test.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Vertical displacement (overeruption): unopposed molars 0.8 mm (SD 0.65 mm) vs controls 0.4 mm (SD 0.2 mm); unopposed teeth overerupted more than controls (P ≤ 0.06).
- Periodontally affected molars erupted more than periodontally healthy molars (P ≤ 0.01).
- Mesial displacement: significant in unopposed molars without a mesially adjacent tooth vs their controls (P ≤ 0.04).
- Palatal movement: unopposed teeth 0.9 mm (SD 0.6 mm) vs controls 0.5 mm (SD 0.3 mm); significantly greater (P ≤ 0.02).
- Overall: displacement of unopposed teeth occurs in three dimensions in the long term, but is clinically insignificant in periodontally healthy adults.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Small sample (12 subjects, 22 unopposed molars) limits power; several comparisons sit at borderline significance (e.g. vertical P ≤ 0.06).
- Plaster-cast scanning measures crown/occlusal-surface centroid movement, not distinguishing true eruption from tipping/remodelling at the root level.
- Adults only and maxillary molars only — not generalizable to younger patients or mandibular teeth.
- Authors attribute changes to either late growth remodelling or altered dental equilibrium after antagonist loss, but the mechanism is not resolved.
- Abstract-only detail retrievable here; full methods, per-tooth data, and discussion were not accessible from the source PDF.

## 6. Related Work

## 7. Glossary

- Unopposed molar: a tooth that has lost its occlusal antagonist, removing the functional contact that normally restrains its position.
- Overeruption: continued eruptive/vertical movement of a tooth beyond the occlusal plane after loss of its antagonist.
- Centroid: the geometric center of the scanned occlusal surface, used as the reference point for measuring positional change.
- Control teeth: posterior teeth retaining antagonists, used as an internal baseline for normal positional stability.
- Dental equilibrium: the balance of forces (occlusal, eruptive, soft-tissue) that maintains a tooth's position; disrupted by antagonist loss.
