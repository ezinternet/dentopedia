## Why Ingested

기존 aerosol-hazard 근거([[dental-handpiece/lahdentausta-2022-aerosol-concentrations-size-distributions-clinical]]의 입자 농도·크기 물리측정, [[dental-handpiece/matys-2020-dental-aerosol-hazard-risk-dental]]의 핸드피스 위험도)는 "얼마나 많이·어떤 크기의 aerosol이 나오는가"에 초점이 있었으나, 그 aerosol이 실제 병원체를 실어 나르는 bioaerosol로서 공기전파(airborne transmission)를 일으키는지, 그리고 어떤 통제수단이 실제로 미생물 분산을 줄이는지는 별도 근거가 필요하다. 본 narrative review (Allison 2024, International Dental Journal COVID-19 supplement)는 bioaerosol의 airborne-transmission 근거·측정법·통제수단(핸드피스 종류, 러버댐, 술전 mouthrinse, HVE/LEV, 환기·fallow time, PPE)을 종합해 위 두 물리측정 페이지의 hazard 근거를 감염전파 프레임으로 확장·보강한다.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review synthesising the evidence on dental bioaerosols and airborne transmission — how bioaerosols are produced (air-turbine handpiece produces ~1000× the bacterial CFU of quiet breathing), why the classical <5 µm droplet/airborne dichotomy is flawed (particles up to 100 µm can stay suspended and travel >2 m), and how a 3-tier control hierarchy (reduce aerosol production, reduce microbial load, capture/remove) plus PPE lowers exposure risk.

## 한줄요약

치과 bioaerosol과 공기전파 근거를 종합한 서술적 리뷰 — 에어터빈 핸드피스는 조용한 호흡의 약 1000배 세균을 분산시키고, 5 µm 미만만 공기전파된다는 통념은 틀렸으며(최대 100 µm 입자도 부유·2 m 이상 이동), 러버댐·술전 가글·고용량 흡인·국소배기·환기·PPE 등 3단계 통제 위계로 노출 위험을 낮춘다.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Reframes dental **aerosols** (physical particles) as **bioaerosols** (aerosols carrying biological material) and situates dentistry within the broader airborne-transmission-of-infectious-disease literature.
2. Explains why high-speed dental procedures are classed by WHO as **high-risk aerosol-generating procedures (AGPs)** and why the COVID-19 disruption to dental services stemmed from *unknown* (not necessarily high) risk.
3. Challenges the conventional contact/droplet/airborne IPC trichotomy: the 5 µm cut-off is a misinterpretation — particles up to 100 µm can remain suspended and travel >2 m.
4. Reviews the three bioaerosol **measurement** paradigms (dispersed oral microbes, physical particle counting, tracer studies incl. bacteriophages) with their benefits/limitations.
5. Organises **control measures** into a 3-tier hierarchy + PPE, with quantitative effect estimates for each, and covers ventilation/fallow-time reasoning.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative (non-systematic) review drawing on primary aerobiology studies, dental bioaerosol studies dating from the 1960s–70s, and multiple systematic reviews of dental bioaerosol control. Structured thematically: (a) respiratory aerosols and airborne transmission of disease; (b) the fate of aerosols (physics — Stokes' law, evaporation, deposition site); (c) bioaerosols in dentistry (oral + dental-unit-waterline microbial sources); (d) measurement methods; (e) control measures (3-tier + ventilation); (f) PPE; (g) future research needs. No pooled quantitative synthesis — effect sizes are cited from the underlying primary/SR literature.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Aerosol production / physics**
- Talking produces **~35×**, coughing **~370×** more respiratory aerosol than quiet breathing.
- Stokes' law: a 100 µm particle settles from 1.5 m in ~5 s, but a 5 µm particle takes ~33 min.
- Deposition by size: >5 µm → nasopharyngeal airways; <5 µm → deeper bronchial/alveolar; <0.1 µm → bronchiolar/alveolar. Smaller-airway deposition → more severe disease (animal data).

**Dental bioaerosol magnitude / risk**
- Micik: air-turbine handpiece produced **1000× the bacterial CFU** of quiet breathing and **2× that of a sneeze**; high-volume suction cut bacterial dispersion by **99.4%**.
- Bacteria detectable on surfaces **1.8 m** from an air-turbine procedure; highest droplet/aerosol settling on the patient's chest (closest zone = highest contamination). DNA sequencing shows bioaerosol bacteria arise from **both oral cavity and dental unit waterlines**.
- Viruses in dental bioaerosols: SARS-CoV-2 RNA on surgery surfaces in **5/24** COVID-19 patient cases; SARS-CoV-2 RNA in **36%** of air samples in Iranian clinics during high prevalence; HBV DNA in aerosols from **1/3** HBV carriers after orthodontic debonding.
- SARS-CoV-2 seroprevalence: **~16%** in clinical dental team vs **~6%** in non-clinical staff/general population (pre-vaccination NW England).

**Control measures (effect estimates)**
- **Reduce production**: electric micromotor (water-only cooling) produces considerably less aerosol and fewer carried microbes than the compressed-air air-turbine handpiece.
- **Reduce microbial load**: rubber dam ↓ bacterial dispersion up to **99% at 1 m, 70% at 2 m**, little effect beyond; bacteriophage tracers show **92–100%** viral-aerosol reduction. Pre-procedural mouthrinse ↓ bacterial dispersion **33–94%** (viral-dispersion effect unknown despite salivary viral-load reduction). Antimicrobial irrigants (chlorhexidine/NaOCl/H₂O₂) ↓ bacterial dispersion **43–88%**; waterline disinfectant ↓ **99%** infectious phage at 0.5 m.
- **Capture/remove**: high-volume suction ↓ bacterial dispersion **80–90%** (esp. ≤0.3 m); imaging **92%**, fluorescent tracer **75% at 0.5 m / 67% at 1.5 m**. Local exhaust ventilation (LEV / extra-oral suction, ~**3,000–5,000 L/min**) ↓ bacterial dispersion **89–93%** at 0.1 m vs 0.2 m placement; simulation ↓ up to **90% at 0.5 m, 75% at 2 m**.
- **Ventilation / fallow time**: ~**10 ACH** proposed; small-aerosol clearance ~**30 min at 10 ACH, 60 min at 5 ACH**; large droplets settle within ~10 min. HEPA filtration effective where ventilation cannot be upgraded.

**PPE**: standard precautions (gloves, eye protection, fluid-resistant surgical mask) baseline; transmission-based precautions with **FFP3/N95** for airborne (and droplet-route AGP) infections; PPE evidence largely low-certainty simulation (contamination, not infection rates).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review — no formal search protocol or pooled effect estimates.
- Underlying evidence is heterogeneous and of mixed quality; almost all clinical data concern **bacteria**, with viral dispersion in dental settings largely unstudied (much reliance on bacteriophage surrogates and simulation/mannequin models).
- Very little high-quality epidemiological evidence on actual infection risk to dental professionals from bioaerosols.
- Fallow-time and ventilation recommendations rest on limited evidence + expert opinion.
- Future research should address fundamental aerobiology plus patient/microorganism/procedure/environmental risk factors and real-world effectiveness of control measures.

## 6. Related Work

- Physical aerosol measurement of dental procedures (particle counters, size distributions) — quantifies the *aerosol* that bioaerosol studies then attribute biological content to.
- Dental aerosol hazard/risk framing for handpieces — the occupational-hazard side of the same phenomenon.
- Systematic reviews of dental bioaerosol control cited throughout for rubber dam, mouthrinse, HVE, and LEV effect estimates.

## 7. Glossary

- **Aerosol**: liquid/solid particles suspended in air.
- **Bioaerosol**: aerosol carrying biological material (microbes, tooth/biofilm debris).
- **AGP (aerosol-generating procedure)**: procedure reported to generate aerosols and associated with increased pathogen-transmission risk; high-speed dental procedures are WHO high-risk AGPs.
- **Stokes' law**: physics relating particle size to settling velocity (smaller → suspended longer).
- **Fallow time**: interval after an AGP before a room can be re-entered without protective equipment, to allow aerosol clearance.
- **HVE (high-volume evacuation / suction)**: intraoral suction capturing aerosol near the source.
- **LEV (local exhaust ventilation)**: extra-oral suction / aerosol-scavenging inlet placed near the mouth (~3,000–5,000 L/min).
- **ACH (air changes per hour)**: ventilation rate metric governing aerosol clearance and fallow time.
- **FFP3 / N95**: respiratory protective masks for airborne-route infections.
- **Bacteriophage tracer (phi6, MS2, phiX174)**: human-harmless viruses used as viral-dispersion surrogates in clinical simulation.
