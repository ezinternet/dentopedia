## Why Ingested

치성 피부 누공(Odontogenic Cutaneous Sinus Tract, OCST)은 치아 증상 없이 피부 병소로만 나타나 흔히 오진되는데, 본 증례는 정확한 치성 진단과 근관치료(RCT)만으로 외과적·항생제 치료를 회피할 수 있음을 보여 근관치료 진단·감별의 임상적 가치를 강화한다. [[endodontics/anatomy/mohammadi-2016-methods-decrease-missed-root-canals]]가 다루는 누락 근관·불완전 처치로 인한 지속 감염 문제와 맥을 같이하며, 근단 감염 원인 치아 식별의 중요성을 확장한다.

## One-line Summary

Case report (n=1, 11-year-old boy): an odontogenic cutaneous sinus tract on the left cheek, secondary to chronic periradicular periodontitis of mandibular first molar (tooth 36), was misdiagnosed/treated dermatologically for 6 months but resolved fully after root canal therapy plus granuloma debridement, with complete healing at 6 months.

## 한줄요약

증례보고 (1명, 11세 남아): 좌측 뺨의 치성 피부 누공(OCST)이 하악 제1대구치(36번) 만성 치근단주위염에서 비롯되었으나 6개월간 피부과적으로 오진·오치됐고, 근관치료(RCT)와 육아종 변연절제술 후 완전 치유되어 6개월 추적에서 병소 소실됨.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Documents an odontogenic cutaneous sinus tract (OCST) in a pediatric patient — a rare presentation that is frequently misdiagnosed because dental symptoms are usually absent.
- Reinforces that an endodontic origin should be the FIRST consideration in differential diagnosis of any draining facial/cervical lesion.
- Shows that precise root canal therapy (RCT) of the offending tooth, plus debridement of chronic granulation tissue, can fully resolve the cutaneous lesion without extraction.
- Highlights the diagnostic workup (exclusion of tuberculosis, bacterial culture, radiographic identification of the periapical source).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Presentation**: Non-healing extraoral swelling on the left cheek for 6 months, periodically discharging pus, ~2.5 cm reddish fluctuant abscess located 2 cm above the inferior border of the mandible, with an unobvious draining sinus tract. No pain/fever.
- **Prior care**: Patient had undergone dermatological surgery and multiple courses of antibiotics without resolution before dental referral.
- **Intraoral findings**: Glass-ionomer-cement filling on occlusal surface of tooth 36; tooth tender to percussion, non-responsive to electric pulp test and heat test (necrotic pulp); periodontal pocket within physiological limits; no intraoral sinus.
- **Diagnostic exclusion**: Negative Mycobacterium tuberculosis nucleic acid quantitative detection and TB antibody test; pus bacterial cultures showed 0% specific aerobic/anaerobic infection rate.
- **Radiographs**: Radiopaque mass inside crown/pulp chamber; irregular radiolucent periapical lesion around the distal root apex.
- **Diagnosis**: Odontogenic subcutaneous abscess with cutaneous fistula secondary to chronic periradicular periodontitis of tooth 36.
- **Treatment protocol (RCT, multi-visit)**:
  - Visit 1: trapezoidal access; 4 canals (MB, ML, DB, DL) cleaned with ISO #8/#6 stainless steel K-files and 3% hydrogen peroxide; pulp chamber kept patent; abscess incision/drainage.
  - Visit 2 (5 days later): rubber dam isolation; crown-down shaping with ProTaper Ni-Ti rotary to S2, working length 19.5 mm (measured with Raypex5 electronic apex locator); irrigation 3% sodium hypochlorite + 3% hydrogen peroxide; intracanal dressing with Vitapex (calcium hydroxide/iodoform).
  - Visit 3 (2 weeks later): OCST closed; obturation with gutta-percha + Cortisomol sealer, cold lateral condensation.
  - ~3 weeks later: residual red granulation tissue on cheek → chronic granuloma debridement under general anesthesia + Scandonest local anesthesia.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Abscess remarkably reduced by the second visit after incision/drainage and pulp-chamber drainage.
- Cutaneous sinus tract closed by the third visit (2 weeks).
- Granulation area converted to smooth tissue; lesion almost healed 2 weeks after debridement.
- At 6 months: cheek defect indistinguishable from surrounding tissue except slight skin hyperpigmentation; periapical radiolucency resolved on radiograph — satisfactory healing.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Single-patient case report (n=1) — lowest evidence weight; no generalizable outcome data.
- Outcome follow-up limited to 6 months.
- Additional granuloma debridement under general anesthesia was required beyond RCT alone, so the cutaneous component did not resolve from canal disinfection alone in this case.
- Emphasizes need for clinician awareness to reduce diagnostic delay (only ~50% of OCST patients report dental symptoms; many first consult dermatologists).

## 6. Related Work

- Cites OCST etiology literature: periapical infection from pulpal necrosis, caries, pericoronitis, or trauma perforating the cortical plate along the path of least resistance.
- Differential diagnoses to exclude: pyogenic granuloma, foreign body reaction, deep fungal infection, squamous cell carcinoma, osteomyelitis.
- Notes ~80% of mandibular OCSTs drain to the chin/submandibular area; lesions may also involve nose, nasolabial folds, inner canthi.

## 7. Glossary

- **OCST (Odontogenic Cutaneous Sinus Tract)**: An extraoral draining tract on facial/cervical skin arising from a chronic odontogenic (usually periapical) infection.
- **RCT (Root Canal Therapy)**: Endodontic disinfection, shaping, and obturation of the root canal system to eliminate the infection source.
- **Periradicular / periapical periodontitis**: Inflammation of tissues around the root apex secondary to pulpal necrosis.
- **Vitapex**: Calcium hydroxide / iodoform intracanal medicament.
- **Tooth 36**: Left mandibular first molar (FDI notation).
- **Electronic apex locator**: Device (here Raypex5) measuring working length electronically.
