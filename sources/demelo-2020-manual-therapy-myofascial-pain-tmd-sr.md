## Why Ingested

Part of a TMD physical-therapy ingest batch building out the manual-therapy / physiotherapy evidence cluster. It reinforces [[wiki/tmj/gonzalez-sanchez-2023-tmj-physiotherapy-treatment-sr]] by supplying an RDC/TMD-anchored, RCT-only synthesis specifically for masticatory myofascial pain, and complements [[wiki/tmj/chan-2022-myogenous-tmd-diagnosis-treatment-review]] (myogenous TMD management) by quantifying where manual therapy sits relative to counseling and botulinum toxin. The key nuance worth capturing: manual therapy relieved pain in all 5 RCTs but was NOT superior to counseling or botulinum toxin head-to-head — a comparative-effectiveness caveat that matters when sequencing conservative TMD care.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (5 RCTs, 279 patients, RDC/TMD-diagnosed masticatory myofascial pain): manual therapy relieved pain in all studies but was not superior to counseling or botulinum toxin; no meta-analysis due to heterogeneity and across-the-board risk of bias.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Synthesizes the RCT evidence (5 trials, 279 patients) on **manual therapy (MT)** for **TMD-related masticatory myofascial pain**, restricting inclusion to studies using the **RDC/TMD** diagnostic criteria for diagnostic homogeneity.
- Establishes that **MT was efficient for pain relief in all 5 studies**, but that across head-to-head comparisons MT was **not superior** to counseling or to a single botulinum toxin injection.
- Positions MT as a **low-cost, reversible, noninvasive, effective** first-line conservative option whose efficacy was statistically similar to (but not better than) higher-cost botulinum toxin.
- Highlights that MT effectiveness is **closely tied to counseling/patient education**, and that combining the two tended to produce better results (though not always statistically).
- Documents the weak evidence base: all 5 included RCTs carried **unclear risk of bias** (Cochrane tool), and methodologic heterogeneity prevented meta-analysis.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: PRISMA-guided systematic review of controlled and randomized clinical trials. Review question: "Is manual therapy efficient in treating patients with TMD-related myofascial pain?"
- **Databases**: Cochrane Library, MEDLINE, Web of Science, Scopus, LILACS, SciELO; searched independently by three reviewers (Feb–Apr 2019); reference lists hand-checked.
- **Descriptors**: temporomandibular joint disorders; craniomandibular disorders; myofascial pain syndromes; myofascial pain; exercise therapy; myofunctional therapy; physical therapy modalities; clinical trial; prospective studies; longitudinal studies.
- **Inclusion criteria**: adolescents/adults/elderly diagnosed with myofascial pain by **RDC/TMD**; ≥1 intervention group receiving manual therapy (TMJ/soft-tissue mobilization, passive/active stretching, isometric tension against resistance, guided opening/closing, and/or massage); ≥1 control group with another treatment (medicines, physical therapy, guidelines/counseling, occlusal devices) or no intervention.
- **Exclusion**: TMJ changes as variable of interest; headache associated with myofascial pain or non-RDC/TMD diagnosis; myofascial pain with neurogenic-origin pain; test-group treatment combined with medications + physiotherapy.
- **Screening flow**: 167 records → 143 after duplicates removed → 121 excluded by criteria → 23 full-text read → 18 excluded (10 not RDC/TMD; 2 non-English/Portuguese/Spanish; 2 MT with medications; 2 book chapter/clinical protocol or case-control; 1 duplicate) → **5 included**.
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (6 domains: sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants/personnel, blinding of outcome assessors, incomplete outcome data, selective reporting, other) → each study classified low/unclear/high; all 5 classified overall **unclear**.
- **No meta-analysis**: studies too methodologically heterogeneous and compared MT to different interventions.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Population**: 279 individuals, ages 12–69 y. 156 received MT alone or MT + counseling; 123 in control groups (15 botulinum toxin, 20 home physical therapy alone, 31 no treatment, 57 counseling).
- **Included studies (5)**:
  - **Guarda-Nardini et al (Italy)** — MT (n=15) vs single botulinum toxin injection (150 U/side masseter + temporalis) (n=15), 3-mo follow-up. Both improved pain; MT slightly superior in subjective pain perception but **not statistically significant (P > .05)**.
  - **Michelotti et al (Italy)** — counseling only (n=34) vs MT + counseling (n=36), 3-mo follow-up. Pain-reduction success rate 57% vs 77% — numerically higher with MT + counseling but **not statistically significant (P = .157)**.
  - **Tuncer et al (Turkey)** — MT (specialist) + home physical therapy (n=20) vs home physical therapy alone (n=20), 4-wk follow-up. Added specialist MT was **significantly better** for pain at rest and during stress (**P < .001**).
  - **Kalamir et al 2012 (Australia)** — MT (n=31) vs MT + counseling (n=31) vs no treatment (n=31), 6-mo & 1-y follow-up. Both intervention groups significantly better than no treatment (**P < .05**); at 1 y, MT + counseling > MT alone (**P = .016**).
  - **Kalamir et al 2013 (Australia)** — MT (n=23) vs counseling alone (n=23), 6-wk follow-up. MT **significantly better** at reducing myofascial pain (**P < .001**).
- **Risk of bias**: all 5 studies rated overall **Unclear**; blinding of participants/personnel was "Unclear" in every study.
- **Bottom-line pattern**:
  - MT relieved pain in **all** studies.
  - MT **better than no treatment** (Kalamir 2012) and **better than counseling alone** (Kalamir 2013).
  - MT + counseling **not statistically better than counseling alone** (Michelotti).
  - MT alone **not better than botulinum toxin** (Guarda-Nardini).
  - MT + home therapy **better than home therapy alone** (Tuncer).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Only **5 RCTs** met criteria — small evidence base reflecting low overall scientific quality of the literature.
- **No meta-analysis** possible due to methodologic heterogeneity and differing comparators (medications, physical therapy, counseling, botulinum toxin).
- **All studies carried unclear risk of bias**, requiring cautious interpretation.
- Divergent findings on MT vs counseling (superior in Kalamir 2013, not in Michelotti) leave **no clear evidence** of one treatment over another.
- Studies did not test whether **longer MT courses** would change comparisons vs botulinum toxin or counseling.
- Authors call for further well-designed RCTs to confirm findings.

## 7. Glossary

- **Manual therapy (MT, 도수치료)**: hands-on physical therapy techniques — TMJ/soft-tissue mobilization, massage, passive/active stretching, isometric tension against resistance, guided mandibular opening/closing, intraoral myofascial release.
- **Myofascial pain (근막통증)**: most common form of TMD; pain originating in masticatory muscles (masseter, temporalis, pterygoids).
- **RDC/TMD (Research Diagnostic Criteria for TMD, TMD 연구진단기준)**: standardized diagnostic questionnaire/protocol used as the inclusion anchor for all 5 studies.
- **Counseling (상담/환자교육)**: information on self-care, etiology, parafunction avoidance, soft diet, relaxed mandibular posture, psychosocial-stress education.
- **Botulinum toxin (보툴리눔 독소)**: comparator in Guarda-Nardini (single 150 U injection per side, masseter + temporalis).
- **Cochrane Risk of Bias tool**: 6-domain instrument classifying each study as low/unclear/high risk of bias.
