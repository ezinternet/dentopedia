## Why Ingested

Adds immersive-VR distraction evidence to the pediatric dental-anxiety category, complementing [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/alhomoud-2023-behavior-anxiety-levels-pediatric-patient]]: where that paper characterizes anxiety levels/behaviour in pediatric patients, this 22-RCT SR supplies the management arm — quantifying that immersive 3D VR distraction reduces anxiety and pain during treatment, often superior to conventional Tell-Show-Do/audiovisual techniques. Pairs with the adult VR implant-surgery RCT ingested concurrently to give the wiki a VR-distraction thread spanning pediatric and adult dentistry.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA/PICO) of 22 RCTs (n=2,558 children) finding immersive 3D VR distraction effectively reduces dental anxiety and pain during treatment, generally surpassing conventional behaviour-management techniques, with a few exceptions where tablets/screen distraction matched or beat VR.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Synthesizes 22 RCTs (n=2,558 children, ~5–12 y) on **immersive 3D VR** distraction as a behaviour-management tool in pediatric dentistry, deliberately excluding 2D audiovisual glasses (32 such studies excluded at full-text stage).
- Finds **strong, consistent evidence** that VR reduces anxiety and pain during dental treatment, frequently superior to conventional Tell-Show-Do/passive distraction; **no study** found traditional non-screen techniques superior to VR.
- Documents head-to-head comparisons against tablets/screens and audio: VR generally wins, but with notable exceptions (tablets matched/beat VR during inferior alveolar nerve block in some studies).
- Flags a **safety/regulatory gap**: nearly all included trials used VR headsets in children below manufacturers' minimum-age recommendations (Oculus/Sony/Samsung ~12–13 y), and cybersickness was assessed in only 1 of 22 studies.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: PRISMA-guided systematic review; PICO — P: pediatric dental patients, I: VR, C: alternative behavioural control techniques, O: anxiety and pain management.
- **Databases**: PubMed/Medline, SCOPUS, Web of Science; searched January 2023; Parsifal reference manager for de-duplication.
- **Screening**: Two independent reviewers (DP, PC), third reviewer (AM) for disagreements; Cohen's Kappa = 1.0 (excellent agreement).
- **Selection flow**: 525 abstracts → 446 after removing 79 duplicates → 54 full-text → 22 included (32 excluded for 2D audiovisual glasses without 3D immersion).
- **Risk of bias**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS) adapted for RCTs — 2 very low, 13 moderate, 7 high risk of bias.
- **No meta-analysis** performed (heterogeneous anxiety/pain scales precluded pooling).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Included studies**: 22 RCTs across Turkey, Iran, India, China, Jordan, Spain, Syria, Italy, Indonesia; total n = 2,558; mostly ages 5–12 y.
- **Outcomes measured**: anxiety in 16 studies, pain in 12; anxiety scales (CPMAS, MCDAS, MCDAS(f)-r, FIS, VPT), pain scales (WBFS, VAS, FLACC, MBPS); plus physiological measures (salivary cortisol, pulse oximetry, pulse rate).
- **Overall effect**: VR's benefit on anxiety and pain was statistically significant vs control in most studies.
- **VR devices**: Oculus Go (2 studies, 9%), VR Box (4 studies, 18%), HTC (2 studies); device unspecified in 4–5 studies.
- **Exceptions (where VR did not win)**:
  - Felemban et al. — VR comparable to screen distraction on heart rate/pain during buccal infiltration.
  - Al-Halabi — tablets outperformed VR for anxiety/pain during inferior alveolar nerve block.
  - Alshatrat et al. — no significant pain reduction in non-painful procedures.
  - Zaidman et al. — VR reduced pain during rubber-dam placement but limited benefit during local anaesthesia.
- **Positive signals**: reduced salivary cortisol (p<0.001) and shorter treatment time (Shetty/Ran/Kaswindiarti); high satisfaction with 3D video glasses (Nuvvula); increased "fun" (Atzori); less anxiety in MIH-affected children using 3D VR games (Özükoç, p<0.05).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Heterogeneous scales** (CPMAS/MCDAS/FIS for anxiety; WBFS/VAS/FLACC/MBPS for pain) prevented meta-analysis and direct cross-study comparison.
- Wide and overlapping **age ranges** mixed developmental stages within samples; prior VR experience and baseline behaviour often unrecorded.
- 4–5 studies did not specify the VR device, blocking device-spec comparisons; **off-label pediatric use** below manufacturer age limits was near-universal.
- Cybersickness assessed in only 1 study; no included trial used an eye-injury prevention protocol.
- Authors call for: qualitative/patient-reported outcomes, long-term effects, standardized scales, and head-to-head VR-vs-pharmacological sedation comparisons (potential to reduce general-anaesthesia referrals).

## 7. Glossary

- **VR (Virtual Reality)**: computer-generated immersive 3D environment delivered via head-mounted display, motion sensors, headphones.
- **Immersive vs non-immersive VR**: immersive = full head-mounted 3D with positional tracking; non-immersive = flat-screen/2D content.
- **Tell-Show-Do (TSD)**: conventional non-pharmacological behaviour-shaping technique used as the primary comparator.
- **Distraction**: diverting the child's attention away from the noxious procedure to reduce perceived pain/anxiety.
- **Cybersickness**: nausea, dizziness, eyestrain from VR exposure (sensory-conflict motion sickness).
- **NOS (Newcastle-Ottawa Scale)**: risk-of-bias / quality-appraisal tool (adapted here for RCTs).
- **MCDAS / WBFS / VAS / FLACC / MBPS**: pediatric anxiety/pain measurement scales used across the included trials.
- **MIH**: Molar Incisor Hypomineralisation (subgroup in one included study).
