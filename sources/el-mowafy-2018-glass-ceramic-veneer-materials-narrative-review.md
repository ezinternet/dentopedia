## Why Ingested

Provides a concise clinical narrative review of porcelain veneer materials, indications, preparation, cementation, and longevity through 2018, serving as a mid-evidence bridge between the early in-vitro technique studies (Sorensen 1992, Sim 1993) and the contemporary SR+MA survival data captured in [[veneers/klein-2025-ceramic-laminate-veneer-survival-complications-sr-ma]].

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Dent Med Probl 2018; 5 pages) covering porcelain veneer materials (feldspathic, pressed, CAD/CAM glass-ceramic), indications, preparation principles, cementation, and reported survival rates up to 12 years; notes 89% survival at 9 years (SR by Morimoto et al.) with glass-ceramic outperforming feldspathic.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Consolidated state-of-the-art on porcelain veneer materials as of 2018 including the emergence of CAD/CAM milled glass-ceramic veneers.
- Summarized indications and contraindications with clinical decision criteria.
- Described surface treatment protocol: HF etching + silane + resin cement (specifically that sandblasting prior to HF is recommended for optimal bond).
- Reviewed survival data from multiple studies (up to 12 years); cited Morimoto SR: 89% at 9 years (n = 13 studies), glass-ceramic 94% vs. feldspathic 87%.
- Identified fracture and debonding as primary failure modes; margin on dentin increases failure risk 10-fold.
- Advocated ultrasonic seating for optimal cement adaptation and minimum film thickness.
- Discussed gingival health advantages of supragingival veneer margins.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Narrative review — unsystematic, no meta-analysis. Authors searched and summarized relevant literature on porcelain veneers covering:
- History (Calamia 1980s, Buonocore acid-etch, Bowen BisGMA)
- Indications/contraindications
- Preparation guidelines (feather-edge finish line, enamel-only preparation)
- Impression and temporization
- Material types and fabrication
- Surface treatment and cementation
- Gingival response
- Longevity data

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Survival rates from reviewed studies:**

| Study | Veneers (n) | Follow-up | Survival |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fradeani et al. | — | 5 years | 96–98% |
| D'Arcangelo et al. | 119 | 7 years | 97.5% |
| Gurel et al. | 580 | 12 years | 86% |
| Layton & Walton | — | 5/10/12 yr | 96%/93%/91% |
| Morimoto et al. (SR) | — | 9 years | 89% overall; glass-ceramic 94%, feldspathic 87% |

**Failure modes (Morimoto SR):** debonding 2%, fracture 4%, caries 1%, discoloration 2%.

**Risk factors for failure:**
- Preparation margin on dentin → 10× higher failure risk vs. enamel margin.
- Bruxism → survival drops to ~60%.
- High-viscosity cement → increased fracture risk during seating.

**Materials:**
- Feldspathic: optimal esthetics via manual layering; technique-sensitive, voids possible.
- Pressed ceramic: higher accuracy, fewer internal defects.
- CAD/CAM milled (e.g., e.max lithium disilicate): stronger than feldspathic; single-opacity blocks historically limiting, multi-opacity blocks emerging.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review; selection bias possible; no quality appraisal of included studies.
- Only 5 pages; condensed coverage without quantitative synthesis.
- Does not address ultrathin (<0.5 mm) or no-preparation veneers.
- Did not cover digital workflow (IOS impression) as a distinct topic.
- Longevity data largely from feldspathic and early pressed ceramic; CAD/CAM long-term data limited as of 2018.

## 7. Glossary

- **Feldspathic porcelain**: Conventional dental porcelain based on feldspar; built up in layers; etches well with HF.
- **Pressed ceramic**: Ceramic material pressed under heat/pressure using lost-wax technique (e.g., IPS Empress, e.max Press); more uniform and stronger.
- **CAD/CAM milled glass-ceramic**: Computer-designed and milled from ceramic blocks (e.g., e.max CAD); strongest option.
- **Silane coupling agent**: Bifunctional molecule bonding inorganic ceramic surface to organic resin cement.
- **Ultrasonic seating**: Vibration-assisted cementation to reduce film thickness and improve marginal adaptation.
- **Feather-edge finish line**: Minimal margin preparation that tapers to zero thickness at the gingival edge.
