## Why Ingested
Provides the empirical dose-response foundation for the WHO 2015 free-sugars guidelines (<10% and <5% energy), directly informing [[caries/hajishafiee-2023-ssb-taxation-sugars-caries-umbrella]] which uses the same thresholds to model SSB-tax impact on caries; both papers by Moynihan.

## One-line Summary
Narrative review (65 papers, 54 studies; 8 cohort studies; children and adults; all countries) synthesizing the evidence base for the WHO 2015 free-sugars guideline, showing moderate-quality cohort evidence that limiting free sugars to ≤10% of energy intake reduces — but does not eliminate — dental caries, with low-quality ecologic evidence supporting an additional benefit below 5% energy.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Synthesizes the evidence base that informed the March 2015 WHO free-sugars guideline (strong recommendation: ≤10% energy; conditional: <5% energy).
- Distinguishes "free sugars" (WHO definition: all mono-/disaccharides added by manufacturer/cook/consumer + naturally present in honey, syrups, fruit juices) from intrinsic sugars (no important caries contribution due to fiber, water, protective factors).
- Documents that 42/50 studies in children and 5/5 studies in adults showed ≥1 positive association between amount of sugars and dental caries.
- Provides dose-response cohort evidence: highest consumers (>10%E) developed 0.9 more decayed surfaces/year vs. lowest (≤10%E) (Rugg-Gunn et al. 1984); children at 16%E were almost 3× as likely to develop high caries as those at ≤10%E (Rodrigues et al. 1999); each additional 5 g sugars associated with a 1% increase in probability of developing caries (Burt et al. 1988).
- Notes that even ≤10%E does not eliminate caries (important caveat for policy framing).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: narrative review; commissioned to accompany the WHO guideline process; reports the underlying systematic review (Moynihan PJ, Kelly SAM 2014) with GRADE assessment.
- Evidence base: 65 papers from 54 studies; 50 in children, 5 in adults; included nonrandomized intervention (n=3), longitudinal cohort (n=8), cross-sectional (n=24), population-based surveys (n=20).
- No RCTs found; best evidence from 8 cohort studies.
- GRADE used to rate evidence quality: cohort evidence for ≤10%E threshold rated moderate; ecologic evidence for <5%E threshold rated low.
- No date restriction, no language restriction; all countries, all age groups.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- 7/8 cohort studies showed lower dental caries with lower sugars intake; dose-response confirmed; GRADE: moderate quality.
- 5/5 cohort studies enabling ≤10%E vs. >10%E comparison: all showed lower caries at ≤10%E.
- Ecologic data: lower caries when free sugars <5%E vs. 5–10%E — GRADE: low quality.
- Rugg-Gunn et al. 1984 (UK, ages 12–14): >10%E → 0.9 more decayed surfaces/year than ≤10%E group.
- Rodrigues et al. 1999 (Brazil, age 3): 16%E intake → ~3× risk of high caries vs. ≤10%E.
- Stecksen-Blicks & Gustafsson 1986 (Sweden, age 13): low-caries group ~9.8%E vs. high-caries group 14.5%E.
- Karjalainen et al. (Finland, age 3): children who developed caries had >10%E intake; caries-free children had ≤10%E.
- US epidemiology: 15.7% of children (2007–2010) and 23.7% of adults (2005–2008) had untreated caries.
- Caries costs: up to ~10% of health-care budgets in industrialized countries; fourth most expensive disease to treat.
- Dunedin Longitudinal Study: children with <3 DMFS at age 12 had developed 15 DMFS by age 32.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Author-stated: crude meta-analysis only (data heterogeneity prevented formal pooling); no RCTs available; ecologic evidence for <5%E threshold is low quality.
- Additionally identified: narrative format introduces author interpretation; no systematic search protocol described for this specific paper (references separate SR); 2016 data may not capture recent industrializing country trends; fluoride use as confounder not always controlled.
- Future: need for prospective cohort studies specifically designed to test the <5%E threshold; population-level intervention studies.

## 7. Glossary
- Free sugars (WHO): all monosaccharides and disaccharides added to foods by manufacturer, cook, or consumer, plus sugars naturally present in honey, syrups, fruit juices and concentrates.
- %E / E%: percentage of total dietary energy intake.
- GRADE: Grading of Recommendations Assessment Development and Evaluation — rates evidence quality as high/moderate/low/very low.
- NUGAG: WHO Nutrition Guidance Expert Advisory Group.
- DMFS/DMFT: decayed-missing-filled surfaces/teeth — standard caries severity indices.
- Strong recommendation (WHO): desirable effects outweigh undesirable consequences; can be adopted as policy in most situations.
- Conditional recommendation (WHO): less certainty about balance of benefits and disadvantages; requires substantial policy debate.
