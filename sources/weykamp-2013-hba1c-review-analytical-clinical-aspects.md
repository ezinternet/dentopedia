## One-line Summary
Single-author narrative review (Ann Lab Med 2013) covering five HbA1c measurement methodologies (ion exchange chromatography, capillary electrophoresis, affinity chromatography, immunoassay, enzymatic), IFCC/NGSP standardization, common interferences (Hb variants S/C/D/E, HbF, carbamylated/acetylated Hb), and clinical applications including diagnosis (≥6.5%), monitoring, pregnancy, and POC testing.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Comprehensive classification of HbA1c analytical methods into **two principles**: separation methods (IEC, CE, AC) vs chemical reaction methods (IA, enzymatic), each with strengths/weaknesses table.
- Detailed treatment of **standardization architecture**: IFCC Reference Measurement Procedure (RMP) + NGSP harmonization, including the dual-reporting controversy (% vs mmol/mol).
- Practical framework for **quality management** in clinical labs: accreditation, internal QC design, External Quality Assessment (EQA) / Proficiency Testing (PT), pre/post-analytical controls.
- Summary of **clinical interpretation**: A1C as risk predictor for retinopathy/nephropathy/neuropathy; diagnostic cutoffs (≥6.5%); applications in pregnancy and POC settings.
- Discussion of **POC HbA1c** strengths (convenience, no lab visit) vs weaknesses (variable quality, non-lab staff handling) — relevant to dental chairside testing.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
**Five analytical methods reviewed**:

1. **Ion Exchange Chromatography (IEC)** — pI difference of 0.02 units between HbA1c and Hb sufficient for HPLC separation; detects HbF, HbA1a/b, carbamylated Hb, and genetic variants (S/C/D/E) — variant detection is both strength (carrier identification) and weakness (interference).
2. **Capillary Electrophoresis (CE)** — charge-based separation under high-voltage field with electroosmotic flow; better variant separation than IEC due to longer runtime; requires parallel capillaries for throughput, with consistency challenges.
3. **Affinity Chromatography (AC)** — boronic acid column captures cis-diol of glycated Hb; recognizes total glycated Hb (not specifically the β-chain N-terminal valine), calibrated to express as %HbA1c; does NOT detect Hb variants.
4. **Immunoassay (IA)** — anti-HbA1c antibodies bind glycated N-terminal valine; agglutination measured turbidimetrically; total Hb measured separately by photometry; requires two parallel tests.
5. **Enzymatic** — protease cleaves β-chain peptides → fructosyl peptide oxidase → H₂O₂ used to quantify HbA1c; Hb variants generally do not interfere.

**Standardization framework**: IFCC RMP as gold standard; NGSP traceable to DCCT; current dual reporting in % (NGSP) and mmol/mol (IFCC).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **HbA1c reference range**: 4–6% (20–42 mmol/mol).
- **Diagnostic cutoff**: ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol) for diabetes; 5.7–6.4% prediabetes.
- **Hb variant prevalence (99% of all variants fall into 4 categories)**:
  - **S**: high in Black Africans/Americans, Mediterranean, Indian populations.
  - **C**: Black Africans/Americans.
  - **E**: Southeast Asian.
  - **D**: globally distributed.
- **β-thalassemia**: β-chain production inhibited → HbF, HbA2 elevated → method-specific interference.
- **HbF interference**: γ-chain has terminal glycine (not valine) → glycated only at lysines; with IA, HbA1c decreases by ~1%; with AC, glycation rate ~1/3 of HbA.
- **Hb derivatives (HbCarb, pre-HbA1c Schiff base)**: post-translational modifications creating measurement artifacts.
- **Analytical performance goals**: total CV <2% (NGSP optimum); bias from RMP <2%.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Single-author narrative review without systematic search methodology — selection bias possible.
- Published 2013 — newer methods (Capillary Electrophoresis improvements, novel POC devices, HPLC variants) and updated NGSP/IFCC consensus (post-2017) not covered.
- Brief treatment of clinical decision limits in special populations (pediatric, pregnancy, CKD, elderly).
- Does not address HbA1c discrepancy in race/ethnicity beyond variant prevalence (later issue raised by Bergenstal 2017).
- Future challenges identified: improving POC quality, harmonization of dual reporting units, expansion of HbA1c diagnostic application to populations where it was previously unreliable.

## 6. Related Work
- **DCCT (1993)** and **UKPDS (1998)**: foundational A1C-complication relationship in T1DM and T2DM.
- **EDIC (Epidemiology of Diabetes Interventions and Complications)**: long-term follow-up of DCCT.
- **NGSP** (Little 2011): assay standardization framework.
- **IFCC RMP** (Hoelzel 2004; Jeppsson 2002): reference method development.
- **ADA Standards of Care**: HbA1c targets <7.0% for most adults, <6.5% for select; <8.0% for high-risk/elderly.
- **Bergenstal 2017**: race-specific A1C-AG differences.

## 7. Glossary
- **HbA1c**: Glycated hemoglobin formed by Amadori product of glucose with N-terminal valine of Hb β-chain.
- **IEC**: Ion Exchange Chromatography — separation by isoelectric point difference.
- **CE**: Capillary Electrophoresis — separation by charge/mass under electric field.
- **AC**: Affinity Chromatography — boronic acid captures all cis-diol glycated Hb.
- **IA**: Immunoassay — anti-HbA1c antibody-based detection.
- **NGSP**: National Glycohemoglobin Standardization Program (USA).
- **IFCC**: International Federation of Clinical Chemistry and Laboratory Medicine.
- **RMP**: Reference Measurement Procedure (gold standard for assay calibration).
- **EQA / PT**: External Quality Assessment / Proficiency Testing.
- **HbF**: Fetal hemoglobin (α2γ2) — predominant in newborns, elevated in β-thalassemia.
- **HbCarb**: Carbamylated hemoglobin — derivative formed in uremia (CKD).
- **POC**: Point-of-Care testing — bedside/office-based instrument.
