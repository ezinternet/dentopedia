## Why Ingested
Directly extends [[caries/moynihan-2016-sugars-dental-caries-threshold-intake]] (same lead policy author, same dose-response framework) to quantify the public-health impact of SSB taxation on caries prevention in both high-income and low-middle-income countries — providing the upstream policy evidence that contextualizes why managing the cariogenic environment around [[resin/melo-2023-bioactive-dental-resins-restorative-dentistry]] restorations matters.

## One-line Summary
Umbrella review of systematic reviews (14 SRs for SSB-tax/consumption questions; 5 SRs for sugars-caries dose-response) estimating that a 20% volumetric SSB tax would reduce free sugars by ~4.0–4.4 g/day and prevent 0.03 carious teeth per adult and 2.7–2.9% caries occurrence in children over 10 years in both HIC and LMIC — a statistically modest but population-scalable effect.

## 2. Key Contributions
- First umbrella review to address SSB taxation impact on dental caries in both HIC and LMIC within a single framework.
- Provides pooled price-elasticity-of-demand (PED) estimates for SSB: 10% tax reduces SSB consumption by 10.0% (95% CI: −5.0, 14.7%) in HIC; 9% (range −6.0 to 12.0%) in LMIC.
- Translates tax → sugars reduction: 20% tax reduces free sugars by ~4.4 g/day (HIC) and ~4.0 g/day (LMIC).
- Applies Moynihan 2016 / Moynihan & Kelly 2014 dose-response data to estimate caries impact over 10 years.
- Demonstrates convergent findings across HIC and LMIC for the caries endpoint.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: umbrella review (review of systematic reviews) per JBI Umbrella Review methodology.
- Questions: (1) SSB tax → SSB consumption (PED); (2) SSB tax → sugars intake; (3) sugars amount → dental caries; (4) modelled impact of 20% volumetric SSB tax on caries prevented over 10 years.
- Databases: PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus, CINAHL, Dentistry and Oral Sciences Source, Cochrane Library, JBI SR Register, PROSPERO. Date limit: 2000–end 2021.
- Quality assessment: AMSTAR (11-point scale; 8–11 high; 4–7 moderate; 0–3 low).
- Included: 14 SRs for Q1+Q2 (4 high-AMSTAR, 9 moderate, 1 low); 5 SRs for Q3 (2 high, 3 moderate).
- Impact estimation: PED data + SSB consumption levels applied to dose-response estimates to model 10-year caries outcomes separately for HIC and LMIC.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Q1 (SSB consumption — HIC): Teng et al. [high AMSTAR]: 10% tax → 10.0% reduction (95% CI: −5.0, 14.7%); PED = −1.0 (95% CI: −0.5, −1.47). Afshin et al. [high AMSTAR, US only]: 10% tax → 7.0% reduction (95% CI: −3.0, −10.0%). Powell et al. [moderate AMSTAR]: OPE = −1.21 (95% CI: −0.7, −2.26), implying 7.1–22.6% reduction.
- Q1 (LMIC): 10% tax → ~9% reduction in SSB consumption (range −6.0 to 12.0%).
- Q2 (sugars intake): 20% tax → ~4.4 g/day reduction in free sugars (HIC); ~4.0 g/day (LMIC).
- Q4 (modelled caries impact of 20% volumetric tax over 10 years): adults (HIC and LMIC): 0.03 fewer carious teeth per person; children (LMIC): 2.7% reduction in caries occurrence; children (HIC): 2.9% reduction.
- Conclusion: effect is statistically modest but population-wide impact is non-trivial given global caries prevalence.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Author-stated: within-country modelling studies show vast between-study differences in effect sizes; LMIC evidence is underrepresented in literature; assumptions required for modelling (dose-response extrapolation); caries data from children (primary + permanent) cannot always be separated.
- Additionally identified: umbrella review inherits limitations of included SRs; 2000–2021 search misses most recent tax implementations (e.g., UK SDIL 2018 long-term outcomes); no RCT data available for any tax-caries link; confounders (fluoride access, oral hygiene, socioeconomic status) not adjustable at umbrella-review level.
- Future: long-term longitudinal studies of implemented SSB taxes tracking dental outcomes; LMIC-specific modelling; combined effect of SSB tax + fluoride programs.

## 7. Glossary
- PED: price elasticity of demand — percentage change in consumption per 1% change in price; PED of −1.0 means 10% tax causes 10% consumption reduction.
- OPE: own price elasticity — change in demand for a product given change in its own price.
- CPE: cross price elasticity — change in demand for a product given change in price of a substitute/complement.
- SSB: sugar-sweetened beverage — beverages with added sugars (carbonated drinks, still drinks, sweetened fruit juices); excludes beverages sweetened only with artificial sweeteners.
- AMSTAR: A Measurement Tool to Assess Systematic Reviews (11-point scale).
- HIC: high-income countries.
- LMIC: low- and middle-income countries.
- Volumetric tax: tax applied per unit volume (e.g., per litre) regardless of sugar content (vs. ad valorem tax on price, or nutrient-based tax on sugar content).
- Umbrella review: a review of systematic reviews synthesizing multiple bodies of evidence on a defined topic.
