## Why Ingested

Documents how to anesthetize a patient with a genuine (anaphylaxis-confirmed) amide local-anesthetic allergy — a rare but high-stakes scenario absent from our standard agent-selection pages. Refines the agent-choice framework in [[wiki/drug/hwang-2023-proposal-clinical-guidelines-local-anesthesia-dentistry]] by adding the "no amide possible" edge case (1% diphenhydramine + 1:100,000 epinephrine as a fallback LA).

## One-line Summary

Case report + literature review (NYU Lutheran): a 43-year-old woman with allergist-documented anaphylaxis to lidocaine completed 13 dental visits (extractions, restorations, root canals, crowns) using 1% diphenhydramine + 1:100,000 epinephrine as an alternative local anesthetic; effective for maxillary infiltration but short-acting (15–75 min) with postoperative swelling and drowsiness, requiring volume limits (<5 mL/visit) and occasional intrapulpal supplementation.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Clarifies that "local anesthetic allergy" is usually NOT true allergy**: most adverse reactions are to epinephrine, vasovagal syncope, or overdose toxicity; true amide allergy is extremely rare.
2. **Identifies the real allergens** in dental cartridges: not the amide itself but additives — **metabisulfite** (antioxidant in all epinephrine/levonordefrin solutions) and historically **methylparaben** (bacteriostatic, related to para-aminobenzoic acid; now removed from single-use cartridges).
3. **Documents diphenhydramine (DPH) as a fallback LA**: 1% DPH + 1:100,000 epinephrine, no cross-reactivity with caine anesthetics, inexpensive, structurally similar to neural-blocking agents.
4. **Provides a real-world dosing/preparation protocol** and complication profile (swelling, drowsiness, burning on injection).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Patient**: 43F with an allergist's report of anaphylaxis to lidocaine >5 years prior; ester testing declined by allergist (prior anaphylaxis during amide testing) → esters not used. Patient had previously needed general anesthesia for dental care and wished to avoid it.
- **DPH preparation (Malamed protocol)**: 2 mL DPH 50 mg/mL + 7.9 mL normal saline + 0.1 mL epinephrine 1:1000 → 10 mL of 1% DPH with 1:100,000 epinephrine.
- **Delivery**: tuberculin/10-mL BD syringe, 25-G needle, buccal-vestibule infiltration (± palatal).
- **13 visits**: extractions (teeth 3, 15), composites, RCT/retreatment (10, 12, 13, 8, 9), posts/cores, crown prep and cementation. Anesthetic effect judged by patient report (no EPT used).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

[근거강함] (reported case facts):
- Adequate anesthesia achieved for maxillary infiltration across all visits; extractions and restorations completed with no-to-minimal discomfort.
- Root canal therapy required supplemental **intrapulpal** DPH (0.5–1.0 mL) — pulpal anesthesia incomplete with infiltration alone in some teeth.
- **Postoperative complication**: after visit 1 (8 mL DPH), 24 h diffuse right facial swelling; presented to ER at 48 h — CBC normal, CT unremarkable, afebrile, no infection; resolved within 24 h. → max volume thereafter limited to 5 mL/visit.
- **Drowsiness** ~15 min after administration (visit 1), resolved by end of procedure.
- Mild burning on injection at every visit.

[claude해석] (generalization beyond this case): literature cited reports DPH duration 15–75 min; adequate anesthesia for erupted/minimally impacted third molars ~80% of the time (operator-judged); DPH is less effective than lidocaine for pulpal anesthesia. The single-case efficacy is encouraging but not a substitute for controlled trials, and outcomes were subjectively assessed (no EPT).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **n=1**, subjective endpoint (patient/operator report, no electric pulp testing).
- Some "successful" procedures were on nonvital/previously-treated teeth that may not have required pulpal anesthesia.
- DPH limitations: short duration (poor for long procedures), dose-dependent postoperative edema and drowsiness, burning on injection, non-ideal aspiration with the syringe used.
- Authors recommend: confirm pulpal anesthesia with pre/post EPT in future use; limit to 1 tooth per session; reserve IV sedation/GA for extensive cases; cap volume <5 mL of 1% DPH + 1:100,000 epi to limit swelling/drowsiness.

## 6. Related Work

- Malamed 1973 — original DPH-as-LA protocol (25 patients, ≤50 mg/sitting; burning sensation common).
- Welborn & Kane 1964 — DPH mandibular block in 25 third-molar extractions.
- Uckan 1998 — DPH vs prilocaine, 16 extractions: adequate pulpal anesthesia but prolonged onset/decreased duration.
- Willett 2008 (J Endod) — DPH and DPH/lidocaine for IANB; DPH less effective than lidocaine.
- [[wiki/drug/hwang-2023-proposal-clinical-guidelines-local-anesthesia-dentistry]] — Korean LA guideline; this case adds the rare "amide-allergic patient" fallback pathway.

## 7. Glossary

- **DPH (diphenhydramine)**: first-generation antihistamine with local-anesthetic properties (aromatic ring + intermediate chain + amino terminus); used as an alternative LA in caine-allergic patients.
- **Amide LA**: lidocaine, articaine, mepivacaine, bupivacaine, prilocaine — metabolized in the liver; true allergy extremely rare.
- **Ester LA**: procaine, tetracaine — hydrolyzed to para-aminobenzoic acid (a known allergen); no longer in dental cartridges.
- **Metabisulfite**: antioxidant in all epinephrine/levonordefrin solutions; a common true-allergen culprit.
- **Methylparaben**: bacteriostatic additive (PABA-related); removed from single-use dental cartridges.
