## One-line Summary

Scoping review (PRISMA-ScR, Arksey & O'Malley framework) of 30 studies (27 in-vitro, 3 case reports; 2000–Jun 2025) mapping clinical-outcome evidence for CAD/CAM-milled vs 3D-printed inlays, onlays, and veneers — both methods reach clinically acceptable marginal fit (<120 µm), CAD/CAM is faster (45–90 min) with higher ceramic strength, 3D printing offers better adaptation/customization (especially veneers) but longer fabrication (90–150 min) and lower hardness/color stability; no long-term clinical trials exist.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Maps clinical-outcome evidence across the **fabrication-method axis** (CAD/CAM subtractive milling vs 3D-printing additive manufacturing) for three restoration classes (inlays, onlays, veneers) — distinct from survival or fracture-design scoping reviews.
- 30 studies included (27 in-vitro, 3 case reports; **no large-scale clinical trials**). Inlays most studied, then onlays, then veneers.
- Both methods produce restorations within the clinically acceptable marginal-gap threshold (≤120 µm, ISO-aligned).
- Identifies veneers as the strongest opportunity for 3D printing; CAD/CAM as the validated default for ceramics.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Frameworks: Arksey & O'Malley + Levac et al.; reported per PRISMA-ScR.
- Databases: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library + Google Scholar (gray literature). Jan 2000–Jun 2025, English only.
- Search yield: 1566 records → 452 duplicates removed → 1114 title/abstract screened → 330 full-text → **30 included**.
- Two independent reviewers (Basheer, Peeran) screened and charted; third reviewer for disputes; expert consultation stage.
- Inclusion: in-vitro experimental models simulating clinical conditions, clinical investigations (RCT/cohort/case-control/observational), case reports reporting clinical end points. **Excluded**: narrative/systematic reviews, meta-analyses, editorials, conference abstracts, non-English.
- No meta-analysis (heterogeneity); narrative thematic synthesis by restoration type, fabrication method, material, outcome.
- **No formal risk-of-bias assessment** (stated as beyond scoping-review scope).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Aggregate numbers (cross-study):**
- Marginal/internal fit: both methods within ≤120 µm; 3D-printed marginal fit values 40–100 µm.
- Fracture resistance: milled lithium disilicate ceramics highest (~1800–2500 N); printed resin/hybrid composites lower (900–1600 N / 900–1800 N).
- Fabrication time: CAD/CAM 45–90 min; 3D printing 90–150 min (incl. postprocessing). One study (Paqué 2024) reported CAD/CAM 67 min vs 3D-printing 701 min for veneers.

**Inlays** (Table 2; e.g., Ahlholm 2019, Lim 2023, Homsy 2018, Sener-Yamaner 2017, Kim 2021, Özden 2025, Karaoğlanoğlu 2023):
- 3D printing achieved equal or superior internal fit vs milling (Ahlholm: 40–60% smaller internal gaps; Lim: 3D-printed + zirconia best fit/accuracy).
- Milled ceramics (lithium disilicate, zirconia) had higher hardness and color stability; CAD/CAM blocks better hardness/color stability than printed resins (Karaoğlanoğlu — printed resins stained more).
- CAD/CAM faster; conventional pressing retains customization (Buduru case report).

**Onlays** (Table 3; e.g., Yildiz 2013, Revilla-León 2018, Lima 2018, Cantó-Navés 2023, Pasha 2023, Waz & Alim 2025):
- Mixed: pressable ceramics > milled lithium disilicate in fracture resistance (Yildiz). Etch-and-rinse cement > self-adhesive.
- 3D-printed onlays often showed better fit/reproducibility (Cantó-Navés p<0.05), but material/printing/prep-dependent; Pasha found CAD/CAM better internal adaptation despite 3D-printed higher accuracy.
- Prep design and scanning modality matter (Lima: indirect scan smaller gaps; Li 2024: shoulder prep + digital impression best).

**Veneers** (Table 4; e.g., Ioannidis 2020/2022, Paqué 2024, Daghrery 2025, Elkaffas 2025, Vichi 2023):
- Greatest 3D-printing potential. 3D-printed zirconia highest load-bearing (Ioannidis: F_initial 1650 N, F_max 2026 N), exceeding functional thresholds for ultrathin occlusal veneers.
- 3D-printed veneers showed lower marginal discrepancy / better adaptation than milled (Daghrery p=0.001). CAD/CAM zirconia best overall fit in another comparison (Ioannidis 2022).
- Build angle critical (Metin: optimal 30°, worst 90°). Composite translucency highly variable (Vichi) — material-selection caution. Cement type significant (Elkaffas p<0.001).

**Material hierarchy:** lithium disilicate = esthetic/mechanical gold standard (pressed > milled for fracture); zirconia (esp. printed) high load-bearing; hybrid ceramics/printable composites = workflow flexibility, moderate fit, lower hardness/optical stability; PEEK/PEKK = moderate, provisional/implant niche.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Predominance of in-vitro studies → limited clinical translation (no saliva, occlusal stress, patient factors).
- **No long-term clinical trials** comparing CAD/CAM vs 3D-printed; mostly short-term case reports/pilots.
- Rapid technological turnover (outdated printers/resins/software in older studies).
- High heterogeneity in outcome definitions (micro-CT vs silicone replica vs digital scan; fracture load vs flexural strength; visual vs spectrophotometric esthetics) → no quantitative synthesis.
- No formal risk-of-bias assessment.
- Future: multicenter RCTs with standardized protocols, printable-ceramic material innovation, hybrid CAD/CAM + 3D-printing workflows, standardized reporting.

## 7. Glossary

- **CAD/CAM**: Computer-Aided Design / Computer-Aided Manufacturing — digital design + (here) subtractive milling of ceramic/hybrid blocks.
- **3D printing / additive manufacturing**: layer-by-layer build-up of restorations from resin or ceramic slurry (SLA/DLP, slurry/powder-bed for ceramics).
- **PRISMA-ScR**: PRISMA extension for Scoping Reviews.
- **Marginal gap threshold ≤120 µm**: ISO-aligned clinical-acceptability limit for indirect-restoration marginal adaptation.
- **PEEK / PEKK**: polyether-ether-ketone / polyether-ketone-ketone high-performance polymers.
- **Build angle**: orientation of restoration on the 3D-printer build platform; affects trueness/precision (optimal ~30° for veneers).
