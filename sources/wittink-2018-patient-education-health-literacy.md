## Why Ingested

[[behavioral-dentistry/communication-relationship/khalifah-2019-teaching-assessment-dentist-patient-communication]] maps *which* communication skills exist and how to teach/assess them, but stops short of a concrete chairside protocol. This narrative review operationalizes the health-literacy evidence into a deployable checklist of **universal health-literacy precautions** — no jargon, small concrete steps, **three key points per visit**, and the **teach-back** comprehension cycle — turning abstract "communicate clearly" guidance into specific techniques a clinician can apply to every patient regardless of literacy level.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (abstract-only — full text not retrieved): patient education has shifted from ineffective biomedical knowledge-transfer to patient-centred care; because low health literacy is common, professional bodies recommend universal precautions for all patients — avoid jargon, break information into small steps, limit each visit to three key points, confirm understanding with teach-back, keep print at ≤6th-grade level, and use visual aids.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames patient education as having shifted from a paternalistic "doctor knows best" knowledge-transfer model — shown ineffective and sometimes counterproductive — to **patient-centred care** tailored to the individual.
- Defines **health literacy**: knowledge, motivation, and competences to access, understand, appraise, and apply health information for decisions on care, disease prevention, and health promotion.
- Highlights that **low health literacy is common** and spans reading, writing, numeracy, communication, and electronic-technology difficulties.
- Consolidates the **universal health-literacy precautions** recommended by professional bodies into a chairside checklist applicable to *all* patients regardless of literacy/education.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Narrative review / masterclass article (not a systematic review or meta-analysis); confidence `narrative-review`.
- Synthesizes professional-organization recommendations and health-literacy literature rather than primary data.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

Universal health-literacy precautions (apply to every patient):

| Precaution | Detail |
|---|---|
| Avoid jargon | Use plain language, not medical terminology |
| Chunk information | Break into small, concrete, actionable steps |
| Limit scope | **No more than three key points/tasks per visit** |
| Confirm understanding | **Teach-back** cycle — patient restates in own words |
| Reading level | Printed material at **≤ sixth-grade** reading level |
| Visual aids | Use visuals to enhance comprehension |

## 7. Glossary

- **Health literacy**: capacity to access, understand, appraise, and apply health information to make health decisions.
- **Universal precautions (health literacy)**: applying clear-communication techniques to *all* patients, not only those presumed to have low literacy.
- **Teach-back**: asking the patient to restate information in their own words to verify (and, if needed, re-explain) comprehension.
