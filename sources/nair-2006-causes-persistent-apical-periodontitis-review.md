## Why Ingested

This is the landmark review that systematized the six biological causes of post-treatment (persistent) apical periodontitis, anchoring the etiology side of the periapical-diagnosis category. It extends [[wiki/endodontics/diagnosis/karamifar-2020-endodontic-periapical-lesion-an-overview]] (which describes lesion types) by explaining *why* a radiolucency fails to heal after endodontic treatment, and it sharpens the vital-vs-nonvital framing in [[wiki/endodontics/diagnosis/asgary-2024-apical-periodontitis-vital-nonvital]] by detailing the microbial and non-microbial mechanisms that sustain disease.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Nair 2006, Int Endod J) consolidating the six biological causes of persistent apical periodontitis after root canal treatment: (i) intraradicular infection, (ii) extraradicular infection (periapical actinomycosis), (iii) foreign-body reaction to extruded materials, (iv) endogenous cholesterol crystals, (v) true (apical) cysts, and (vi) fibrous scar tissue healing.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Defines **persistent apical periodontitis** as apical periodontitis that endures because root canal treatment has not adequately eliminated intraradicular infection — distinct from primary (untreated) apical periodontitis in etiology, therapy, and decision-making complexity.
- Establishes the now-canonical **six biological causes** of asymptomatic post-treatment radiolucencies (the "Nair six"):
  1. **Intraradicular infection** persisting in the complex apical canal system (the dominant cause).
  2. **Extraradicular infection**, generally as periapical **actinomycosis** (cohesive colonies of *Actinomyces*/*Propionibacterium propionicum* surviving in periapical tissue).
  3. **Foreign-body reaction** to extruded root filling or other exogenous material (e.g., gutta-percha, sealer, cotton, cellulose from paper points).
  4. **Endogenous cholesterol crystals** that accumulate and irritate periapical tissue, sustaining a macrophage/giant-cell response.
  5. **True (apical) cysts** — pocket cysts may heal after orthograde treatment, but apical *true* cysts (lumen fully enclosed by epithelium, self-sustaining) are unlikely to resolve non-surgically.
  6. **Scar tissue (fibrous) healing** — a benign radiolucency mimicking failure, not a disease.
- Stresses that even technically excellent treatment may leave residual biofilm in **uninstrumented recesses, isthmuses, accessory and apical ramifications** because of anatomical complexity.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Synthesis of the author's own histobacteriological work (correlative light + transmission electron microscopy on anatomically intact apical block-biopsies, serial/step-serial sectioning) and the broader literature.
- Emphasizes methodological rigor: random paraffin sections systematically *underdetect* intraradicular infection; biofilm in apical ramifications is missed by conventional histology. Properly studied block-biopsies (Nair et al. 1990a) found intraradicular microbes in 6 of 9 cases — far higher than older paraffin-only estimates (10–15%).
- Cites the one-visit study (Nair et al. 2005): residual infection persisted in 14 of 16 root-filled mandibular molar mesial roots, located in uninstrumented main-canal recesses, isthmuses, and accessory canals, organized as biofilms unremoved by instrumentation + NaOCl.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Biofilm resistance**: organisms in sessile biofilms are >1000× more resistant to biocides than the same organisms as planktonic cells (Wilson 1996; Costerton & Stewart 2000) — biological basis for treatment failure.
- **Intraradicular infection** is the most frequent and important cause; the majority of root-filled teeth with asymptomatic apical periodontitis harbor persistent apical-canal infection.
- **Extraradicular causes** (actinomycosis, foreign-body, cholesterol, true cyst, scar) account for the minority of cases that resist orthograde retreatment and may require surgery.
- Clinical corollary: a persistent radiolucency is not automatically an extraradicular problem — intraradicular infection remains the first thing to address (retreatment before surgery), while genuinely extraradicular causes justify apical surgery.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative review by a single author, weighted toward his own histobacteriological case series (small n for block-biopsy studies); not a systematic review or quantitative synthesis.
- The relative frequency of each of the six causes is not precisely quantified — estimates derive from selected surgical/extraction specimens, subject to selection bias.
- Distinguishing true cysts from pocket cysts requires serial histology of the whole lesion and cannot be made radiographically, limiting chairside applicability.

## 7. Glossary

- **Persistent (post-treatment) apical periodontitis**: apical periodontitis remaining after root canal treatment due to incomplete elimination of, or re-infection by, intraradicular microbes.
- **Periapical actinomycosis**: extraradicular infection by *Actinomyces* spp. / *P. propionicum* forming cohesive colonies that survive host defenses in periapical tissue.
- **Foreign-body reaction**: chronic macrophage/giant-cell inflammation against extruded exogenous material.
- **Cholesterol crystals**: endogenous lipid crystals from disintegrating cells/serum, eliciting a non-resolving foreign-body-type response.
- **True (apical) cyst**: epithelium-lined cavity with a lumen fully enclosed by epithelium, self-sustaining and unlikely to heal non-surgically; vs **pocket (bay) cyst**, whose lumen communicates with the root canal and may heal after orthograde treatment.
- **Apical scar**: benign fibrous (collagen) healing producing a persistent radiolucency without disease.
- **Biofilm**: microbial community in an exopolysaccharide matrix adherent to a moist surface; far more biocide-resistant than planktonic cells.
