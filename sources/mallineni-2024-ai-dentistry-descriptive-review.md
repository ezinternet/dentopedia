## One-line Summary
Bioengineering 2024;11:1267 — descriptive review of AI across all dental disciplines (oral medicine, operative, pediatric, periodontology, orthodontics, OMFS, prosthodontics, forensic odontology); core conclusion — radiograph/optical image-based diagnosis dominates current deployments; data availability/uniformity/compute are bottlenecks.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Cross-discipline mapping: all major dental specialties + forensic odontology
- Identifies radiographic/optical imaging as dominant AI substrate in dental practice
- Frames evidence-based dentistry vs ML-derived recommendations as parallel decision frameworks

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Descriptive (non-systematic) literature review
- Scope: imaging/diagnosis, treatment planning, robotics/automation, AR/VR, data analytics, administrative support
- No PRISMA, no quality grading

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- AI deployed across: oral medicine, operative dentistry, pediatric dentistry, periodontology, orthodontics, OMFS, prosthodontics, forensic odontology
- Most AI applications: radiograph/optical-image diagnosis
- Other tasks lag due to: data availability, data uniformity, computational power constraints
- AI assists less-skilled practitioners in reaching more precise diagnoses (skill-equaliser framing)

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Data heterogeneity across institutions limits model generalisability
- Constraint of compute resources especially in non-tertiary settings
- Implementation gap between research models and real-world clinical workflow
- Authors emphasise AI as adjunct to evidence-based dentistry, not replacement
