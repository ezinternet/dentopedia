## Why Ingested

In-vitro benchmark study comparing fracture resistance of four CAD/CAM occlusal veneer materials (LD, LS, PI, PM) with and without thermodynamic chewing simulation — provides in-vitro fracture load data that underpins occlusal veneer material selection discussions in [[wiki/overviews/veneer-material-survival-protocol-overview]].

## One-line Summary

In-vitro study (n=64 premolars, 4 groups) showed that lithium disilicate and zirconia-reinforced lithium silicate occlusal veneers (0.5/0.8 mm) exhibited significantly higher fracture resistance than polymer-infiltrated ceramic and PMMA; all materials survived 1.2 million chewing cycles and exceeded average posterior occlusal forces.

## 한줄요약

체외실험(n=64 소구치, 4군): 리튬디실리케이트(Lithium Disilicate, LD) 및 지르코니아강화 리튬실리케이트(Zirconia-Reinforced Lithium Silicate, LS) 교합 비니어(0.5/0.8 mm)가 고분자침투세라믹(Polymer-Infiltrated Ceramic, PI) 및 폴리메틸메타크릴레이트(PMMA, PM)보다 파절저항(Fracture Resistance, FR)이 유의하게 높았으며, 4개 군 모두 120만 회 저작시뮬레이션 후 생존하였다.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. First-of-kind direct head-to-head comparison of 4 major CAD/CAM occlusal veneer material classes under identical preparation geometry (0.5 mm fissure / 0.8 mm cusp thickness) and thermodynamic chewing simulation.
2. Demonstrated that thermodynamic loading (artificial aging) paradoxically *increases* fracture resistance for LS, PI, and PM groups (possibly due to residual stress redistribution or interfacial strengthening), while LD showed no statistically significant change.
3. All four CAD/CAM materials showed fracture resistance values substantially exceeding average in-vivo posterior occlusal forces, supporting their use as ultra-thin occlusal veneer restorations.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

**Specimen preparation:**
- 64 intact, noncarious, unrestored maxillary first premolars extracted for orthodontic reasons.
- Selected for dimensional similarity; cleaned of calculus/soft tissue; stored in 0.1% thymol solution.
- Occlusal surfaces prepared within the enamel layer only; fissure/cusp thickness: 0.5 / 0.8 mm.

**CAD/CAM Materials (4 groups, n=16 each):**
| Group | Material | Brand |
|---|---|---|
| LD | Lithium disilicate (e.max CAD) | Ivoclar Vivadent |
| LS | Zirconia-reinforced lithium silicate (Vita Suprinity) | Vita Zahnfabrik |
| PI | Polymer-infiltrated ceramic network (Vita Enamic) | Vita Zahnfabrik |
| PM | PMMA (Telio CAD) | Ivoclar Vivadent |

**Luting protocol:**
- Enamel etched with phosphoric acid.
- Adhesive luting system: Multilink Primer A/B + Multilink Automix luting resin (Ivoclar Vivadent).

**Thermodynamic loading:**
- Half of each group (n=8) subjected to chewing simulation: 1.2 million cycles at 98 N.
- Remaining half (n=8) tested without chewing simulation (control).

**Fracture testing:**
- Quasi-static load to fracture applied to all specimens after aging/no-aging.
- Statistical analysis: t-test and one-way ANOVA + Tukey HSD post-hoc test (α = 0.05).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Survival of thermodynamic loading:**
- All aged specimens (all groups) survived 1.2 million chewing cycles without fracture, ceramic chipping, or crack formation — 100% survival rate across all materials.

**Effect of thermodynamic loading on fracture resistance:**
- LS, PI, PM: thermodynamic loading *significantly raised* fracture resistance (P ≤ 0.031).
- LD: thermodynamic loading did not significantly affect fracture resistance.

**Fracture resistance comparisons (without thermodynamic loading):**
- LD > PI (P ≤ 0.015)
- LD > PM (P ≤ 0.015)
- LD vs LS: trend toward higher (P = 0.051, NS)

**Clinical relevance:**
- All four materials exhibited fracture resistance considerably exceeding average occlusal forces in the posterior dentition, supporting their viability as long-term occlusal veneer restorations.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **In-vitro design**: Results cannot be directly extrapolated to clinical longevity; clinical variables (salivary environment, patient occlusal force variation, thermal cycling parameters, cementation errors) are not replicated.
- **Small n per subgroup (n=8)**: Underpowered for detecting small inter-group differences in fracture resistance after thermodynamic loading.
- **Premolar only**: Results may not generalize to molars (higher occlusal loads, different morphology).
- **Enamel preparation only**: Clinical cases with dentin exposure (e.g., erosion-worn teeth needing OVDs) may show different bonding performance and fracture patterns.
- **No follow-up beyond single test**: Does not assess fatigue crack propagation, cyclic failure initiation, or R-curve behavior under sub-fracture-threshold loads.
- Future studies recommended: clinical RCTs, molar specimens, dentine-bonded specimens, longer chewing simulation, 3D-printed resin alternatives.

## 6. Related Work

- Schlichting et al. (2011): established clinical feasibility of ultra-thin CAD/CAM occlusal veneers for erosion treatment — the clinical counterpart to this in-vitro benchmark.
- Clausen et al. (2010): dynamic fatigue of non-retentive all-ceramic full-coverage molar restorations — key predecessor in chewing simulation methodology.
- Guess et al. (2013): influence of preparation design and ceramic thickness on fracture resistance of premolar partial coverage restorations — preparation thickness context.
- Elsaka & Elnaghy (2016): mechanical properties of zirconia-reinforced lithium silicate — material characterization underpinning LS group.

## 7. Glossary

- **CAD/CAM**: Computer-Aided Design / Computer-Aided Manufacturing — milling or printing of restorations from pre-fabricated blocks.
- **LD (Lithium Disilicate)**: Glass-ceramic with interlocking needle-like Li₂Si₂O₅ crystals; brand e.max CAD (Ivoclar Vivadent).
- **LS (Zirconia-Reinforced Lithium Silicate)**: Lithium silicate matrix containing ~10% ZrO₂; brand Vita Suprinity; claimed more translucent and stronger than conventional LD.
- **PI (Polymer-Infiltrated Ceramic Network)**: Interpenetrating ceramic-polymer network (PICN); brand Vita Enamic; combines ceramic rigidity with polymer toughness.
- **PM (PMMA)**: Polymethylmethacrylate resin; brand Telio CAD; lowest stiffness, high resistance to dynamic fatigue.
- **Thermodynamic loading**: Combined thermal (temperature cycling) + mechanical (chewing load cycles) aging to simulate intraoral fatigue — here 1.2 million cycles at 98 N.
- **Occlusal veneer**: Ultrathin (0.5–0.8 mm) indirect restoration covering only the occlusal surface; minimally invasive; used for wear/erosion rehabilitation.
- **Tukey HSD**: Tukey Honestly Significant Difference post-hoc test for pairwise comparisons following significant ANOVA.
