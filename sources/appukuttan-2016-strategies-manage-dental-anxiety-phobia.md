## Why Ingested

치과불안 관리의 **성인 중심** 의사결정 프레임워크 — 식별(주관적 척도 + 객관적 생리지표) → 심리치료(행동/인지/CBT) → 약물(진정/전신마취)의 단계적 논리를 한 페이지에 정리한 기준 종설. 기존 [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/pediatric-2026-dental-anxiety-contemporary-assessment-management]]가 소아 평가·관리에 치우친 것을 성인 결정 프레임워크로 보완하고, 단일 modality 종설인 [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/cernega-2022-efficacy-hypnosis-dental-anxiety-phobia]](최면)를 상위 의사결정 맥락 안에 배치한다.

## One-line Summary

Narrative literature review (adult-focused) laying out a stepwise framework for dental anxiety/phobia: identify with self-report scales (CDAS/MDAS/DFS) + objective physiologic measures, then manage via psychotherapeutic (behavioral/cognitive, with CBT most supported) → pharmacological (sedation/GA) interventions matched to anxiety severity.

## 한줄요약

성인 중심 서술형 종설: 치과불안·공포를 자가보고 척도(CDAS/MDAS/DFS)와 객관적 생리지표로 식별한 뒤, 불안 정도에 맞춰 심리치료(행동/인지, CBT가 가장 근거 우세) → 약물(진정/전신마취)로 단계적으로 관리하는 의사결정 틀을 제시.

## 1. Document Information

- **Type**: Narrative literature review ("concise review"), open-access (Dove Press).
- **Journal**: Clinical, Cosmetic and Investigational Dentistry 2016;8:35–50.
- **Author/affiliation**: D.P. Appukuttan (Department of Periodontics, SRM Dental College, Chennai, India).
- **Scope**: Etiology of dental anxiety; identification (subjective + objective); psychotherapeutic management; pharmacological management; technological adjuncts for LA and restorative care.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Consolidates a **single decision framework**: psychotherapeutic vs pharmacological vs combined, chosen by anxiety level + patient characteristics + clinical situation + dentist expertise.
- Catalogs the **assessment ladder** — semistructured interview, validated self-report scales, and objective physiologic measures (BP, pulse, pulse oximetry, finger temperature, galvanic skin response).
- Names **CBT as the currently most accepted/successful psychological treatment** for dental anxiety and specific phobia.
- Lays out the **psychotherapeutic toolbox** in depth (relaxation, guided imagery, biofeedback, hypnosis, acupuncture, distraction, enhancing control via tell-show-do/modeling/stop-signal, systematic desensitization, positive reinforcement, cognitive restructuring).
- Maps **pharmacological tiers** to anxiety severity (minimal/anxiolysis → moderate/conscious → deep sedation → GA), with the IOS ("Indicator of Sedation Need") and ASA-status gating tools.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Non-systematic narrative review — no search strategy, inclusion criteria, or quality appraisal reported. Synthesizes prior literature (scales, RCTs, SR/MAs) into a clinically organized overview. Evidence weight is therefore that of a narrative review; specific cited findings (e.g., hypnosis meta-analysis, acupressure SR+MA) carry their own underlying study designs.

**Assessment architecture:**
- *Subjective*: semistructured interview (open-ended questions on reason for visit, prior experience, fears, expectations; refer to psychologist/psychiatrist if part of wider disorder) + validated questionnaires.
  - **CDAS** (Corah): 4 items, score 4–20, cutoff >15 = high/possible phobia; limitation = no LA-injection item.
  - **MDAS** (Modified): 5 items, score 5–25, cutoff ≥19 = high fear; administration does not raise anxiety (Humphris & Hull).
  - **DFS** (Dental Fear Survey): 20 items, score 20–100, cutoff ≥60; 3 dimensions (avoidance, somatic symptoms, fear of stimuli).
  - Single-item options (Dental Anxiety Question, VAS) exist; no gold standard. Only ~20% of UK dentists use scales (Dailey et al). Clinician judgment alone is unreliable — patients mask anxiety; scales both disclose and reduce anxiety.
- *Objective*: BP, pulse rate, pulse oximetry, finger temperature, **galvanic skin response** (validated as accurate via epidermal sweat conductance).
- Categorization output: mildly / moderately / extremely anxious / dental-phobic.

**Etiology**: conditioning (prior trauma, esp. childhood), vicarious learning, personality (neuroticism, self-consciousness), media portrayals, sensory triggers (needle/drill sights, drilling sounds, eugenol/cut-dentine smells, vibrations), loss of control in chair. Milgrom's "Seattle system" = 4 fear sources (specific dental stimuli, distrust of personnel, generalized anxiety, fear of catastrophe).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Decision logic** (core takeaway): match treatment to severity.
  - *Mild–moderate anxiety* → psychological interventions; occasionally anxiolytic/minimal-conscious sedation.
  - *Extreme/phobic* → most often combined approaches (CBT + pharmacology).
  - Pharmacology reserved for patients who can't/won't cooperate with psychotherapy, are phobic, or have special needs.
- **Psychotherapeutic = behaviorally or cognitively oriented**:
  - *Behavioral*: relaxation (Jacobson PMR — tense 5–7 s / relax 20 s across 4 muscle groups; diaphragmatic breathing; autogenic; Öst applied relaxation; Benson relaxation response), guided imagery, biofeedback (EMG/EEG/respiratory-rate), hypnosis (75% pain reduction in meta-analytic review; needs training; avoid in mental-health/personality/neurodegenerative disorders), acupuncture/acupressure (auricular acupuncture ≈ intranasal midazolam in one RCT; acupressure SR+MA = medium effect on immediate pre-treatment anxiety), distraction (music, video glasses — works in adults and children), enhancing control (tell-show-do, modeling, stop-signal/raised hand), systematic desensitization (Wolpe — fear hierarchy + relaxation + graded exposure; imaginal variant; flooding with caution), positive reinforcement.
  - *Cognitive*: restructure negative cognitions (encouragement, altering expectations, thought-stopping, focusing attention).
  - **CBT = behavior + cognitive therapy combined; most accepted/successful for situational anxiety and specific phobia.** Components: psychoeducation, graded exposure, cognitive restructuring, behavioral experiments, relaxation, self-assertiveness training. Requires dentist training.
- **Office environment & communication**: soft music, no bright lights, slightly cooler room, short waits; iatrosedative two-way communication, eye contact, honest answers, no false reassurance; sensory-adapted ("Snoezelen") environment; aromatherapy (lavender lowers salivary/serum cortisol, GSR, systolic BP — better for moderate than severe anxiety).
- **Technological LA adjuncts**: computer-controlled local anesthetic delivery (less threatening handpiece, slow flow), electronic dental anesthesia (TENS/gate-control, needle-free, variable efficacy), computer-assisted relaxation learning (CBT-based video program, RCT-reduced injection fear; overall evidence quality for injection-phobia psychological treatment = poor per Patel SR).
- **Restorative adjuncts**: "4 S" rule (eliminate Sight, Sound, Sensation, Smell triggers); ART, air abrasion, chemomechanical caries removal, lasers.
- **Pharmacological tiers** (ASA I–II for sedation candidacy):
  - Minimal sedation/anxiolysis — oral sedative ± N₂O/O₂; mild-moderate anxiety.
  - Moderate/conscious sedation — responds to verbal command; moderate-severe anxiety.
  - Deep sedation — not easily aroused; airway may need support.
  - General anesthesia — for dental-phobic, severe learning difficulty/psychiatric/physical-disability/comorbidity (special care dentistry).
  - **IOS tool** (anxiety + medical/behavioral + treatment complexity, each 1–4, sum 3–12): 3–4 minimal, 5–6 moderate, 7–9 high, 10–12 very high need / GA.
  - Conscious sedation routes: inhalational (N₂O 30–40% in 70% of pts; 70% ceiling), oral (benzodiazepines — diazepam/midazolam/triazolam), IV (rapid onset, titratable; benzodiazepine ± opioid; needs advanced training).
- **Psychological vs pharmacological**: both effective at reducing anxiety; psychological response is not immediate (multiple sessions) but **durable with low dropout and more return for future care**, whereas pharmacology works **short-term only** and is seen as less acceptable by patients.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review — no formal evidence grading; selection of cited studies not reproducible.
- Many modalities (hypnosis, acupuncture, EDA, jet/vibrotactile injection devices) flagged as needing more RCT validation for routine dental use.
- Author notes most listed techniques require **special dentist training and infrastructure** before clinical adoption.

## 7. Glossary

- **CDAS / MDAS / DFS** — Corah Dental Anxiety Scale / Modified DAS / Dental Fear Survey; validated self-report dental-anxiety questionnaires.
- **Galvanic skin response (GSR)** — objective anxiety measure via skin electrical conductance from sweat.
- **CBT (Cognitive Behavior Therapy)** — combined behavioral + cognitive treatment; most-supported psychological therapy for dental anxiety/phobia.
- **Systematic desensitization** — Wolpe graded-exposure technique pairing a fear hierarchy with relaxation.
- **Tell-show-do** — informational-control behavior-shaping technique (explain → demonstrate → perform).
- **Iatrosedative technique** — anxiety reduction through the dentist's communication/relationship behaviors.
- **IOS (Indicator of Sedation Need)** — 3-domain tool (anxiety, medical/behavioral, treatment complexity) scoring need for conscious sedation.
- **ASA I/II** — American Society of Anesthesiologists physical-status classes eligible for in-office sedation.
- **N₂O/O₂ inhalation sedation** — nitrous oxide anxiolytic/analgesic sedation (typically 30–40%, ≤70% ceiling).
