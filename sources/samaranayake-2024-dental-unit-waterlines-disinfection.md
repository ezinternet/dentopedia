## Why Ingested

치과 유니트 수관 (Dental Unit Waterlines, DUWL)은 임상 감염관리에서 흔히 간과되는 오염원이다. 이 리뷰는 DUWL 오염 기전·바이오필름·소독 방법·규제 준수를 종합적으로 다루며, [[infection-control/patino-marin-2025-sterilization-disinfection-dental-practices]]가 다루지 않는 DUWL 특화 내용을 확장한다.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review of dental unit waterline (DUWL) contamination origins, biofilm formation, pathogen risks (Legionella, Pseudomonas, NTM), and decontamination strategies (engineering, procedural, chemical, automated), with regulatory compliance recommendations across jurisdictions.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. Provides a comprehensive account of DUWL contamination across four domains: best-practice guidelines, origins/infectious risks, decontamination approaches, and constraints in combating biofilms.
2. Summarizes jurisdiction-specific regulatory thresholds: CDC ≤500 CFU/mL, ADA/Australia ≤200 CFU/mL, UK ≤100 CFU/mL, EU <100 CFU/mL (22°C/72h) or <20 CFU/mL (37°C/24h).
3. Reviews the engineering, procedural, and chemical control hierarchy for DUWL decontamination.
4. Identifies *Legionella* spp. and *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* as the predominant DUWL pathogens (prevalence 12% and 8%, respectively, by meta-analysis).
5. Offers eight standardized operational recommendations for DUWL compliance.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Contamination Origins
- **Municipal water**: UK tap water may contain 100–500 CFU/mL saprophytic non-pathogens entering DUWLs.
- **Biofilm amplification**: Even feed water <100 CFU/mL can produce exit water with 100,000–1,000,000 CFU/mL due to biofilm shedding.
- **Contributing factors**: Narrow-bore tubing (high surface-to-volume ratio), intermittent/slow flow, water stagnation (nights, weekends), PVC/polyurethane tubing materials, malfunctioning antiretraction valves, secondary storage tanks, water hardness (mineral deposits).
- **Suck-back phenomenon**: Faulty antiretraction valves draw oral fluids back into lines, seeding additional microorganisms.

### Microbiology
- Organisms: oral flora, saprophytic aerobic heterotrophs, algae, protozoa (*Acanthamoeba*, amoebae), fungi (*Candida* spp.).
- Key pathogens: *Legionella* spp. (12% prevalence), *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* (8%), nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM), *Stenotrophomonas* spp.
- Documented fatality: 82-year-old Italian woman died of *Legionella* serogroup 1 pneumonia; clonal identity with DUWL isolate confirmed by molecular typing.
- Occupational risk: 23% of US dental staff had IgG antibodies to *Legionella* vs 8% community baseline; 19% had IgM antibodies.

### Decontamination Approaches

**Engineering controls:**
- Independent reservoir (~1 L) with sterile/deionized/distilled water (CDC recommended)
- Point-of-use microbial filters (≤0.2 µm pore size): highly effective but require frequent replacement; no effect on existing biofilm
- Simple mesh, activated carbon, reverse osmosis, deionizer systems for feed water
- Antiretraction valves (built-in to handpieces/couplings): prevent suck-back when functioning properly
- Antistagnation devices: maintain continuous circulation during inactive periods

**Procedural (manual) controls:**
- Pre-use flush: minimum 2 minutes at day start, 20–30 seconds between patients (CDC guideline)
- End-of-day drying with compressed air or drying agents
- Limitation: flushing reduces microbial density but does NOT eliminate biofilm — must be combined with chemical methods

**Chemical controls:**
- Effective biocides: peroxide compounds, bisbiguanides (chlorhexidine-based), chlorine-based compounds
- Combined formulations often include chelating agents (citric acid, EDTA)
- Peroxide and chlorine-based compounds most effective but risk corrosion, DUWL blockage, water discoloration, and mild oral tissue irritation
- Microbial habituation risk: extended use of a single biocide may lead to preferential overgrowth (e.g., catalase-positive staphylococci with hydrogen peroxide biocides)
- Dosing strategies: continuous (more effective, prevents regrowth) vs intermittent (microbial recolonization between treatments); combined periodic low-dose + occasional high-concentration "shock treatment" optimal
- Emerging agents: electrochemically activated solutions, plasma sterilization, iodine cartridges — satisfactory bactericidal effect with minimal toxicity

**Automated systems:**
- Examples: Poseidon-S disinfectant system, Planmeca Waterline Cleaning System
- Advantages: consistent disinfection, real-time monitoring, reduced human error, minimal staff handling
- Disadvantages: reduced efficacy against spore-forming bacteria/robust biofilms, malfunction risk, potential overdependence reducing manual practice vigilance, compliance complexity, additional training required

**DUWL tubing material innovation:**
- Emerging: antimicrobial tubing with N-halamine, polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE), polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF)

### Constraints
- Feed water quality (geographical variation in municipal water, mineral hardness/deposits) must be assessed before treatment strategies
- No single control is sufficient; comprehensive approach (physical + chemical + automated) required
- Human error (e.g., handling biocides without gloves) introduces skin bacteria into the system

### Compliance Recommendations (8 key points)
1. Health and safety legislation: DCUs classified as reusable medical devices requiring premarket clearance
2. Dental education: DUWL management training for students and dentists; periodic bacterial testing of exit water
3. Staff training: simulators, record-keeping certification, standardized documentation templates
4. SOPs: regional-level standard operating procedures covering daily/periodic disinfection, monitoring, troubleshooting
5. Periodic microbiological monitoring using certified laboratories; prompt corrective action when thresholds exceeded
6. Biocide residue monitoring in output water
7. Log of DCU service dates vs manufacturer-recommended service life
8. Log of DCU and accessory maintenance dates

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative review without systematic search — subject to selection bias.
- Many published infection reports lack molecular typing to confirm causal link between DUWL and patient illness; such reports should be interpreted cautiously.
- Cost-effectiveness evaluations for automated disinfection systems remain insufficient.
- No uniformity in global regulatory standards; EU lacks DUWL-specific guidance.

## 7. Glossary

- **DUWL**: Dental Unit Waterline — small-bore tubing within dental chairs supplying water to handpieces, ultrasonic scalers, air/water syringes
- **CFU/mL**: Colony-forming units per milliliter — standard measure of microbial load in water
- **Biofilm**: Structured microbial community adherent to surfaces, enclosed in extracellular matrix, highly resistant to antimicrobials
- **Antiretraction valve**: One-way valve preventing suck-back of oral fluids into DUWL
- **DCU**: Dental Chair Unit
- **NTM**: Nontuberculous mycobacteria
- **Shock treatment**: Periodic application of high-concentration biocide to overcome established biofilm
- **Electrochemically activated (ECA) solution**: Water treated by electrolysis to generate reactive chlorine species; emerging DUWL disinfectant
- **HAI**: Healthcare-associated infection
