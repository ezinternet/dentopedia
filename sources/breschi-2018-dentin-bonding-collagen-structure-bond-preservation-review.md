## Why Ingested

Collagen-structure-to-bond-preservation review that anchors the dentin-adhesion hybrid-layer batch: it traces resin-dentin durability from dentin collagen architecture through the two HL-degradation mechanisms (endogenous enzymatic collagenolysis + resin leaching) and the preservation strategies (water removal, enzyme silencing). It reinforces [[wiki/resin-bonding/liu-2011-limitations-bonding-dentin-bond-degradation-review]], which independently catalogues the limitations and degradation pathways of dentin bonding, by supplying the collagen-/enzyme-mechanism framework underneath those limitations.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review framing resin-dentin durability: the hybrid layer degrades via endogenous MMP/cysteine-cathepsin collagenolysis and resin leaching — both enabled by residual unbound water — and is preserved by removing that water and silencing the enzymes.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames decades of effort toward durable resin-dentin bonds around three pillars: (1) understanding the mechanisms of hybrid layer (HL) degradation, (2) manufacturing improved adhesive systems, and (3) developing strategies for HL preservation.
- Identifies **two main HL-degradation mechanisms**: enzymatic degradation of collagen fibrils, and leaching of resin from the HL, both enabled by **residual unbound water** trapped at the bottom of the HL.
- Pinpoints the **endogenous dentinal enzymes** responsible — matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and cysteine cathepsins — activated by that unbound water to degrade the denuded collagen matrix.
- Reviews **dentin collagen structure** (30–50 vol% organic matrix, ~20 vol% water) as the biological substrate that determines how resin infiltrates and why HL is vulnerable.
- Describes both **E&R (etch-and-rinse)** and **SE (self-etch / etch-and-dry)** strategies and how each interacts with dentin collagen and water content.
- Organizes **preservation strategies** into two conceptual families: removal of unbound water from the inter-fibrillar gaps, and silencing of endogenous enzymatic activity (chemical agents and physical approaches).
- Discusses **resin degradation** mechanisms: hydrolytic degradation (water-mediated covalent-bond breaking) and collagen-scaffold degradation (MMPs/cathepsins cleaving collagen peptides).
- Acknowledges the historical discovery sequence: Dayan et al. (first collagenolytic activity in dentin), Tjäderhane et al. (attributed to MMPs), Pashley et al. (aseptic conditions collagen degradation → intrinsic proteases).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Critical narrative review of peer-reviewed literature (199 references) on hybrid-layer formation, degradation, and preservation. Not a systematic review or meta-analysis; no pooled quantitative estimates. The review is organized conceptually:

1. Adhesive systems and adhesion strategies to dentin (E&R vs SE; acid / primer / adhesive components)
2. Dentin collagen structure (composition, organic matrix, mineralization, proteoglycans, non-collagenous proteins)
3. Resin degradation (hydrolysis of resin polymers; water as primary enabler)
4. Degradation of the collagen scaffold/fibrils (MMP history, cysteine cathepsins, in situ evidence)
5. Strategies to reduce HL degradation (water removal; enzyme-silencing agents — CHX, enzyme inhibitors, cross-linkers; physical approaches)
6. Conclusions

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Dentin structure:**
- Dentin is a mineralized collagen matrix containing ~30–50 vol% organic material and ~20 vol% water.
- The organic matrix comprises mainly type I collagen with non-collagenous proteins (proteoglycans such as decorin/fibromodulin that bind separately to fibrillar collagens).

**Adhesive strategies:**
- Traditional adhesive systems require acid (removes mineral, exposes collagen), primer (hydrophilic resinous monomers for infiltration into demineralized dentin), and adhesive (monomer mixtures that penetrate primer-treated surfaces).
- E&R vs SE strategies differ in how much dentin is demineralized before resin infiltration; neither perfectly envelopes deepest collagen fibrils.

**Resin degradation:**
- Hydrolytic degradation: water-mediated chemical breaking of covalent bonds between polymers → loss of resin mass. Considered the primary reason for resin degradation within the HL.
- Hydrolysis causes two consequences: plasticization/swelling of the adhesive (allowing penetration by salivary esterases that accelerate hydrolysis) and exposure of collagen for enzymatic attack.

**Collagen scaffold degradation:**
- Collagenolytic activity in dentin first reported by Dayan et al.; attributed to MMPs by Tjäderhane et al.
- Pashley et al. showed collagen degrades in aseptic conditions → intrinsic matrix proteases (not bacteria).
- Intrinsic dentin gelatinolytic/collagenolytic activity was significantly lower in specimens with specific treatment conditions (detail not fully recoverable from snippet).
- Endogenous proteases (MMPs and cysteine cathepsins) are hydrolases that require unbound water to cleave collagen peptides — hence the mechanistic link between residual water and enzymatic degradation.

**Preservation strategies — two families:**
1. **Remove unbound water** from gaps between exposed collagen fibrils → denies hydrolases the water they need.
2. **Silence endogenous enzymatic activity** → chemical agents (MMP inhibitors, CHX, cross-linkers) and physical approaches. The number of available approaches is increasing.

**Significance:**
- Despite remaining hurdles, impressive progress achieved in understanding HL-degradation processes and developing preservation strategies.
- The large volume of research (199 references, 408 citations) reflects the importance of and effort toward durable resin-dentin bonds.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review: no quantitative synthesis, susceptible to selection emphasis.
- Clinically: full envelopment of the deepest collagen fibrils within the HL remains "very difficult, if not impossible" — this is the root of the durability problem.
- Authors note **many hurdles remain** in adhesive dentistry despite progress.
- Future work implied: more durable adhesive systems; more effective, clinically practical strategies for water removal and enzyme inhibition to extend resin-dentin bond longevity; growing list of preservation agents needs clinical validation.

## 7. Glossary

- **Hybrid layer (HL)**: the resin-infiltrated demineralized dentin collagen zone that forms the resin-dentin bond.
- **Denuded collagen fibrils**: collagen left demineralized but not fully resin-infiltrated, exposed to water and enzymes.
- **Unbound water**: residual water trapped between collagen fibrils that enables both enzymatic degradation and resin leaching.
- **MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases)**: endogenous dentinal collagenolytic enzymes activated within the HL by unbound water and low pH.
- **Cysteine cathepsins**: endogenous lysosomal cysteine proteases that, together with MMPs, degrade the denuded collagen matrix in the HL.
- **Resin leaching**: hydrolytic loss of polymer from the HL over time, a non-enzymatic degradation mechanism.
- **Hydrolytic degradation**: water-mediated chemical breaking of covalent bonds between polymers causing mass loss from the HL.
- **Enzyme silencing**: HL-preservation strategy of inhibiting endogenous MMP/cathepsin activity (e.g., CHX, cross-linkers, MMP inhibitors).
- **E&R (etch-and-rinse)**: adhesive strategy where phosphoric acid is applied and rinsed before primer/adhesive; more aggressive demineralization.
- **SE (self-etch)**: adhesive strategy where acidic monomers demineralize dentin simultaneously with primer infiltration; milder demineralization.
- **Decorin / fibromodulin**: proteoglycans binding to separate sites on fibrillar collagens, part of the dentin non-collagenous protein matrix.
- **Salivary esterases**: enzymes in saliva that can penetrate plasticized adhesive and further accelerate hydrolytic degradation.
