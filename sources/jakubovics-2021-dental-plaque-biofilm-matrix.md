## Why Ingested
Comprehensive narrative review of the extracellular matrix of dental plaque — the foundational scaffold that enables [[oral-microbiology/karygianni-2020-biofilm-matrixome-extracellular-components]] and underpins cariogenic virulence described in [[oral-microbiology/koo-2013-exopolysaccharides-matrix-biofilm-virulence]].

## One-line Summary
Narrative review of the molecular composition and functional roles of the dental plaque extracellular matrix (carbohydrates, proteins, eDNA, lipids), with emphasis on subgingival matrix and its relevance to periodontal disease pathogenesis.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Defines the major classes of dental plaque extracellular polymeric substances (EPS): carbohydrates (~20% dry weight of supragingival plaque), proteins, nucleic acids (eDNA), lipids, and cell-wall polymers.
- Characterises glucans (2–10% dry weight) and fructans as primary sucrose-derived virulence-associated polysaccharides in supragingival plaque.
- Reviews protein-linked bacterial glycans (S-layers, flagella, pili) and capsular polysaccharides as critical adhesion mediators.
- Highlights poly-N-acetyl-D-glucosamine as primary adhesion polymer of Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans and shows its role in inter-species positioning.
- Proposes biofilm matrix as an underexplored therapeutic target compared to microbial cell composition.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: narrative (non-systematic) comprehensive review.
- Scope: surveys matrix components across supra- and subgingival dental plaque, drawing on in vitro biofilm studies, electron microscopy, proteomic analyses, and animal models.
- No formal population/intervention/control framework; synthesises published molecular and functional data on EPS components.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Carbohydrates constitute ~20% of dry weight of supragingival plaque; water-insoluble fraction ~two-thirds; glucans 2–10%, fructans lower amounts.
- Plaque contains ~80% water and ~40% dry-weight protein (mostly bacterial and salivary origin).
- Lipoteichoic acids from S. gordonii associate with extracellular glucan polymers and enhance binding of cells to glucan aggregates.
- DNABII proteins (integration host factor, IHF) are structural components of subgingival matrix; anti-DNABII immunotherapy disperses biofilms in vivo.
- Extracellular DNA (eDNA) acts as structural scaffold, is susceptible to DNase disruption, and participates in horizontal gene transfer.
- Capsule of P. gingivalis reduces immune activation but is required for coaggregation with F. nucleatum and synergistic virulence in murine periodontitis models.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Bulk subgingival plaque matrix composition remains poorly characterised due to limited sample volumes.
- Most mechanistic data derive from in vitro monospecies or defined biofilm models; full polymicrobial complexity is not captured.
- Authors call for application of emerging proteomics, metagenomics, and spatial imaging to characterise the subgingival EPS matrix in clinical samples.
- Functional roles of (lipo)teichoic acids from periodontal-associated gram-positive species (e.g., Filifactor alocis) remain unexplored.

## 6. Related Work
- Builds directly on Flemming & Wingender (2010) foundational EPS review.
- Extends earlier caries-focused EPS analyses by Bowen & Koo (Caries Res 2011) to the periodontal context.
- Informs the 'matrixome' concept formalised by Karygianni et al. (Trends Microbiol 2020).

## 7. Glossary
- EPS: extracellular polymeric substances — the complex of polysaccharides, proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids forming the biofilm matrix.
- eDNA: extracellular DNA released by cell lysis or active secretion; structural scaffold in biofilm.
- DNABII: family of bacterial nucleoid-associated proteins (IHF, HU) that also serve as matrix structural components.
- Glycocalyx: historical term for the carbohydrate-rich layer surrounding microbial cells; now superseded by 'EPS matrix'.
- Glucosyltransferase (Gtf): enzyme that synthesises glucan polysaccharides from sucrose; major EPS source in supragingival cariogenic plaque.
