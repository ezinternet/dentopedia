## One-line Summary
Prospective study of 150 deep neck space infection (DNSI) cases at a tertiary care hospital in India; odontogenic source in 42.66%, Ludwig's angina most common site (24.66%), Streptococcus dominant organism (31.33%), I&D in 38%, emergency tracheostomy in a subset.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Indian Journal of Otolaryngology and Head & Neck Surgery 2022;74:5832-5835
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s12070-021-02439-6
- **Type**: Prospective study
- **Year**: 2022

## 2. Key Contributions
- Characterizes etiology, microbiology, clinical presentation, management, and outcomes of 150 DNSI cases at a single tertiary hospital in India
- Identifies odontogenic infections as the most common source of DNSI
- Documents Ludwig's angina as the most frequently affected deep neck space
- Provides data on microbiological profile and antibiotic sensitivity
- Evaluates surgical vs. conservative management approaches and complication rates including emergency tracheostomy

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Prospective observational study
- Setting: Tertiary care hospital (India), ENT and Oral Surgery departments
- n = 150 consecutive DNSI patients over study period
- Data: demographics, source of infection, clinical space involved, diagnostic workup (CT, ultrasound), microbiology (pus cultures), management (conservative vs. I&D vs. tracheostomy), outcomes, complications
- Statistical analysis: descriptive statistics; frequency distributions

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Etiology**:
  - Odontogenic: 42.66% (most common overall)
  - Tonsillitis/peritonsillar: second most common source
  - Other: parapharyngeal, post-traumatic, parotid origin
- **Space involved**:
  - Ludwig's angina (submandibular + sublingual bilateral): 24.66% — most common single diagnosis
  - Parapharyngeal space: frequent
  - Retropharyngeal space: seen in pediatric cases
- **Microbiology**:
  - Streptococcus spp.: 31.33% (most common organism)
  - Staphylococcus aureus: second most common
  - Klebsiella, E. coli, Pseudomonas: minority; more common in diabetic patients
  - Mixed flora with anaerobes common
- **Management**:
  - I&D (surgical drainage): 38% of cases
  - Conservative (IV antibiotics alone): remainder where no fluctuant collection
  - Emergency tracheostomy: performed in cases with airway compromise (approximately 10–12% of Ludwig's angina cases)
- **Complications**: Mediastinitis, aspiration pneumonia, airway obstruction
- **Mortality**: Low overall; higher in cases with mediastinal extension

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Single-center prospective study; may not represent all geographic regions
- Relatively small sample size limits subgroup analysis
- Limited long-term follow-up data
- Need for multicenter studies to define optimal management protocols

## 6. Related Work
- Directly related to van der Merwe (2023) on Ludwig's angina microbiology and antibiotic choice
- Relevant to antibiotic stewardship in head and neck infections
- Complements oral surgery infection management literature

## 7. Glossary
- **DNSI (Deep Neck Space Infection)**: Infection involving the fascial spaces of the neck; potentially life-threatening due to proximity to airway and mediastinum
- **Ludwig's Angina**: Bilateral cellulitis of submandibular and sublingual spaces; classic presentation with floor-of-mouth swelling, trismus, drooling, and airway compromise
- **I&D**: Incision and Drainage; standard surgical management of fluctuant neck space abscess
- **Tracheostomy**: Surgical airway establishment below the larynx; performed emergently when supraglottic/glottic edema prevents endotracheal intubation
- **Mediastinitis**: Spread of infection to mediastinum via deep cervical fascial planes; most serious DNSI complication with high mortality
- **Odontogenic Infection**: Infection originating from dental source (periapical abscess, periodontitis, pericoronal abscess); most common DNSI etiology
