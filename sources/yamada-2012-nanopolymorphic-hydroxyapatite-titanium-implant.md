## One-line Summary
Animal study (rat): nanopolymorphic crystalline HA (55% crystallinity, needle-like nano-HA, Ca/P=1.66) on microroughened Ti → BIC and push-in strength ↑ both early and late stages; soft tissue infiltration ↓; effect limited to ≤50 µm from implant surface.

## 1. Document Information
Int J Nanomedicine 2012;7:859-873. doi:10.2147/IJN.S28082. UCLA LBIS. Flame spray + low-temperature calcination method.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Mass-production-compatible nanopolymorphic HA fabrication via flame spray + calcination
- HA coating increases BIC and bone-implant integration strength at both early and late healing
- Identifies soft tissue infiltration reduction as key HA mechanism (not just increased surface area)
- Effect spatially limited to 50 µm peri-implant zone — important for bone volume interpretation

## 3. Methodology
Rat femur implant model. Sandblasted Ti vs sandblasted+nano-HA Ti. Biomechanical push-in test at 2 and 4 weeks. Histomorphometry: BIC, bone volume ≤50 µm and >50 µm, soft tissue percentage.

## 4. Key Results
- HA-coated vs microroughened: push-in force higher at both 2- and 4-week
- BIC: HA-coated significantly higher
- Soft tissue at interface: significantly less with HA
- Bone volume <50 µm: higher with HA; bone volume >50 µm: lower with HA
- Ca/P ratio 1.66 (stoichiometric); 55% crystallinity; 70% surface area increase vs non-coated

## 5. Limitations
Rat model; 4-week maximum follow-up; no human clinical data; HA coating long-term stability (dissolution) not assessed.

## 6. Related Work
Hasegawa 2020 (meso-micro-nano surface), Iwasa 2011 (TiO2 nano-hybrid), Ogawa 2025 (D2 topography frontier).

## 7. Glossary
Nanopolymorphic: variable nano-scale morphology (varying diameters, lengths, orientations); stoichiometric HA: Ca/P = 1.667.
