## Why Ingested

Provides the synthesizing in-vitro evidence base behind the connection-geometry claim that [[prosthetic-materials/velez-2020-implant-connection-abutment-design-screw]] and [[prosthetic-materials/ren-2024-morse-taper-abutment-subsidence-locking-force]] make about Morse-taper joints — this 10-study systematic review quantifies the post-fatigue torque-loss range (16.1%–39%) and consolidates that internal connection + Morse taper best resist screw loosening under cyclic loading, anchoring those single-paper findings in a body of comparative bench data.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (10 in-vitro cyclic-loading studies) finding that abutment-screw preload loss after dynamic fatigue ranges 16.1%–39%, and that internal-connection implants — especially Morse taper — best resist screw loosening in single-tooth restorations.

## 한줄요약

10편의 in-vitro 반복하중 연구를 묶은 체계적 문헌고찰. 동적 피로 후 지대주 나사의 전하중 손실(preload loss)은 16.1%~39% 범위였고, 단일치 보철에서는 내부연결(internal connection), 특히 모스테이퍼(Morse taper)가 나사 풀림에 가장 잘 견딘다고 결론.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Consolidates 10 in-vitro studies that subjected single-unit internal- and external-connection implants to cyclic loads and measured post-load removal (counter) torque as an index of preload loss.
- Quantifies the overall range of post-loading torque loss: **16.1%–39%** (literature baseline 16.1%–25%; Jorge reported 19.7%–39.0%).
- Concludes that the **internal connection together with the Morse taper** is most resistant to cyclic fatigue in terms of screw loosening for single-tooth implants.
- Identifies five factors governing screw torque loss: type of connection, screw design/material, prosthetic abutment type, settling effect, and internal (occlusal) loads.
- Highlights the **settling effect** (2%–10% initial preload loss with no load) and that **retightening ~10 min after first tightening** recovers preload (Siamos: 17%–19% reduction in torque loss).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Databases**: PubMed, EMBASE, Cochrane Library; English-language articles 1995–2016.
- **Inclusion**: studies comparing internal vs external connection implants subjected to cyclic loads, with abutment-screw loosening assessed by counter-torque measurement.
- **Exclusion**: studies not specifying connection type, not comparing internal vs external connections, no cyclic loading, multiple-unit prostheses, bacterial-filtration-only, or finite-element analyses.
- **Screening**: 545 papers identified → 70 abstracts reviewed after de-duplication → 35 fully read → **10 studies** met inclusion criteria (PRISMA).
- **Test setup variability**: implants embedded mostly in acrylic resin (E ≈ 17 GPa, near bone); some in aluminium (70 GPa), stainless steel (190–210 GPa), or unspecified metal. Some authors used a thermocycler to mimic oral conditions. In all studies except Park, screws underwent tightening/loosening sequencing before cyclic loading to define removal torque.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Cycles**: 16,667 to 1,000,000 (one study followed ISO 14801 with 5 million cycles).
- **Loads**: 0–400 N.
- **Tightening torques**: 20–45 Ncm.
- **Post-loading torque (preload) loss**: 16.1%–39% overall; Jorge's Morse internal connection 32.88% loss vs external + UCLA abutment 39%.
- **Connection geometry**: most authors found Morse taper maintained preload better than external hex (and in some cases better than internal hex); in external connections axial loads act directly on the screw, whereas internal/Morse connections transfer forces deeper → more stable, fewer micromovements, better stress dissipation. Suggested clinical mapping: internal connection for single prostheses, external connection reserved for multiple prostheses.
- **Screw material/design**: lubricant coatings (TorqTite, Gold-Tite) improved performance per some authors, but Tsuge & Hagiwara found titanium-alloy screws less prone to loosening than Gold-Tite regardless of connection. Piermatti: screw design (long rod + machined journal) was the single most important stability factor; Coppedê: conical-head screws loosen less than flat-head. Larger screw-joint diameter favors torque maintenance.
- **Settling/retightening**: all screws lose 2%–10% preload from settling even unloaded; retightening recovers it.
- **Loading effect**: in all studies except Tsuge & Hagiwara, screws were looser after cyclic loading than at baseline (statistically significant in only a few).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **In-vitro only** — no clinical/in-vivo loosening data; results may not translate directly to oral function.
- **No meta-analysis** — high methodological heterogeneity (substrate elastic modulus, cycle count, load magnitude, screw material/design, abutment type) precluded quantitative pooling and introduces confounding bias.
- **Lack of standardized cyclic-loading methodology**; ISO 14801 does not mandate a standardized torque, so comparisons are not univariate.
- Screw loosening is **multifactorial** — connection type alone is not determinative; screw design/material, abutment type/design, passive fit, and occlusal forces all contribute.

## 7. Glossary

- **Preload**: clamping force generated when a screw is tightened to a given torque; only ~10% of applied torque becomes preload, the rest overcomes thread friction. Screw loosening occurs when applied forces exceed the preload.
- **Settling effect (embedment relaxation)**: smoothing of microscopic surface irregularities between mating threads/seats, causing 2%–10% loss of initial preload even without external loading.
- **Counter torque / removal torque**: torque required to loosen a screw after loading; used as the index of residual preload (lower = more loosening).
- **Morse taper**: internal conical implant–abutment connection (8° or 11° cone) that engages by friction-lock; transfers force deeper and reduces micromovement.
- **Cyclic / dynamic fatigue loading**: repeated load application simulating occlusal function (here 16,667–1,000,000 cycles, 0–400 N).
- **ISO 14801**: standard for fatigue testing of endosseous dental implants.
