## One-line Summary
Systematic review (69 publications: 31 clinical + 38 laboratory) reporting that 81% found an association between occlusal stress and NCCLs, though no clinical study showed stress as the sole cause.

## 1. Document Information
- Am J Dent 2017;30(4):212-220. PMID 29178704. Searched PubMed, ISI Web of Science, EMBASE (keyword "abfraction").
- [미검증] Abstract-only ingest.

## 2. Key Contributions
- The most abfraction-favorable SR: 56/69 (81%) of publications found an occlusal stress-NCCL association.
- Distinguishes lab (FEA-heavy) from clinical evidence: 24 FEA studies found stress concentration at the cervix.
- Explicitly notes NO clinical study demonstrated NCCL caused by stress alone.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- SR; 372 identified → exclusions → 41 + 28 (bibliography) = 69 included (31 clinical, 38 laboratory). Keyword-limited ("abfraction"), English only. No meta-analysis.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- 56/69 (81%) reported an association; 23 studies reported stress/occlusal factors associated with NCCLs.
- 24 of 38 laboratory (FEA) studies found cervical stress concentration.
- No clinical study established stress as sole cause.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Single-keyword search ("abfraction") biases toward stress-positive literature; lab-study weighting; abstract-only ingest. Later critiqued by Dioguardi 2024 for not separating abfraction from pure erosion/abrasion lesions.

## 6. Related Work
- Senna 2012, Silva 2013 (opposing clinical SRs), Dioguardi 2024 (scoping review re-analysis).

## 7. Glossary
- FEA: finite element analysis. Abfraction: stress-induced cervical microfracture hypothesis.
