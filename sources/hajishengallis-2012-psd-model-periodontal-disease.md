## Why Ingested
Introduces the Polymicrobial Synergy and Dysbiosis (PSD) model — the paradigm-shifting framework that underpins all subsequent periodontal pathogenesis literature in this wiki, including [[oral-microbiology/hajishengallis-2014-porphyromonas-gingivalis-host-manipulation]] and [[oral-microbiology/lamont-2014-polymicrobial-synergy-dysbiosis-inflammatory]].

## One-line Summary
Narrative review proposing the PSD model of periodontitis etiology, in which keystone pathogens (exemplified by P. gingivalis at <0.01% of microbiota) remodel a symbiotic community into a dysbiotic, bone-destructive state through polymicrobial synergy and host immune subversion.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Formally named and defined the PSD (Polymicrobial Synergy and Dysbiosis) model of periodontal pathogenesis.
- Introduced "keystone pathogen" terminology: low-abundance species (e.g., P. gingivalis <0.01% of total microbiota) with community-wide pathogenic impact.
- Proposed that P. gingivalis is unable to cause periodontitis in germ-free mice — pathogenicity requires a pre-existing commensal microbiota.
- Defined "accessory pathogens" as species that elevate virulence through interspecies communication.
- Identified complement C5aR–TLR2 crosstalk as a core immune subversion mechanism exploited by P. gingivalis.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Narrative review synthesizing mouse periodontitis models, non-human primate studies, human microbiome data, and mechanistic cell/molecular studies.
- Key cited mouse experiment (Hajishengallis et al. 2011): P. gingivalis at <0.01% of total microbiota triggered dysbiosis and inflammatory bone loss; failed in germ-free mice.
- Microbiome breadth: >700 recognized oral species; ~200 present per individual; ~50 per site.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- P. gingivalis at <0.01% of total microbiota remodeled a symbiotic community into a dysbiotic state causing alveolar bone loss in mice.
- Pathogenicity is community-dependent: P. gingivalis failed to cause periodontitis in germ-free mice despite colonizing them.
- P. gingivalis induces "local chemokine paralysis": SerB serine phosphatase inhibits CXCL8 (IL-8) production by gingival epithelial cells, delaying neutrophil recruitment.
- C5aR-deficient mice resist P. gingivalis dysbiosis and periodontitis — C5aR is required.
- In non-human primates, gingipain-based vaccine reduces P. gingivalis numbers and total subgingival bacterial load.
- When in community with S. gordonii and F. nucleatum, P. gingivalis differentially expresses ~500 proteins.
- Del-1-deficient mice develop inflammatory bone loss in the absence of P. gingivalis — dysbiosis can also arise without a keystone pathogen.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Most mechanistic evidence derived from mouse models; direct causal proof in humans lacking.
- Keystone pathogen concept based primarily on P. gingivalis; other candidates poorly characterized.
- Does not specify which community members become "inflammophilic pathobionts" in advanced disease.
- Authors call for identification of additional keystone/accessory pathogens and therapeutic targeting of C5aR.

## 6. Related Work
- Socransky et al. 1998: red complex (P. gingivalis, T. denticola, T. forsythia) — the prior paradigm being superseded.
- Hajishengallis et al. 2011 (Nat Med): mouse model that empirically established keystone concept.
- Dewhirst et al. 2010; Griffen et al. 2011, 2012: 16S sequencing studies showing >700 oral species and heterogeneity.

## 7. Glossary
- PSD model: Polymicrobial Synergy and Dysbiosis model of periodontitis.
- Keystone pathogen: low-abundance organism with disproportionate community-wide impact on host–microbe homeostasis.
- Accessory pathogen: species that elevate the virulence of keystone pathogens or the community.
- Dysbiosis: shift in microbiota composition/function away from homeostasis toward disease.
- SerB: P. gingivalis serine phosphatase that dephosphorylates NF-κBp65, suppressing CXCL8.
- C5aR: complement C5a receptor; required for P. gingivalis colonization and dysbiosis in mice.
- Gingipains: Arg- and Lys-specific cysteine proteases of P. gingivalis (HRgpA, RgpB, Kgp).
