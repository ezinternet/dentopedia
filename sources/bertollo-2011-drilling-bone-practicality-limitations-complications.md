## One-line Summary
Orthopaedic-engineering book chapter (Biomechanics in Applications, InTech 2011) reviewing the mechanics of bone drilling — heat generation, drill-bit geometry, feed-rate, irrigation — with thermal-necrosis thresholds (47 °C/1 min, 50 °C/30 s, 56 °C) directly transferable to implant osteotomy.

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Book chapter / narrative review (engineering biomechanics, not dental-specific)
- **Source**: Biomechanics in Applications, V. Klika (Ed.), InTech, ISBN 978-953-307-969-1, September 2011
- **Authors**: Nicky Bertollo, William R. Walsh (Surgical and Orthopaedic Research Laboratories, UNSW Sydney)
- **DOI**: not assigned (open-access InTech chapter)
- **Sample**: Narrative — not an original empirical study
- **Primary Question**: How do drill-bit geometry, rotational speed, feed-rate, and cooling interact to determine thermal damage and mechanical outcome when drilling cortical and cancellous bone across surgical disciplines?

## 2. Key Contributions
- Establishes the thermal-necrosis thresholds that govern all bone drilling, including dental osteotomy: 47 °C for 1 minute reduces regenerative capacity (Eriksson & Albrektsson 1983); 50 °C for 30 s causes cellular necrosis (Lundskog 1972); 50 °C cortical exposure reduces healing capacity; 56 °C causes osteonecrosis (Matthews & Hirsch 1972).
- Documents how temperatures during k-wire insertion and cortical drilling routinely reach 100 °C without irrigation, and oscillating saw blades in total knee arthroplasty exceed 100 °C — providing the thermal context for why irrigation and feed-rate control matter in implant surgery.
- Identifies the principal determinants of heat generation: drill geometry (point angle, helix angle, flute design), diameter, rotational speed (rpm), feed-rate (mm·s⁻¹), axial thrust force (N), initial drill-bit temperature, and internal/external cooling.
- Notes that necrotic bone is resorbed by osteoclastic activity — explaining why thermally damaged osteotomy walls cause early implant failure even when primary stability is initially achieved.
- Sets the rationale for non-cutting (compaction / osseodensification) approaches: any conventional drill converts mechanical work to thermal energy via plastic deformation and shear failure at the machining face, and the only ways to limit this are tool design, kinematics, and irrigation.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
This is a synthesis chapter, not an experimental study. The authors review 30+ years of bone-drilling biomechanics literature spanning orthopaedics, neurosurgery, craniomaxillofacial, and ENT applications. They organise the chapter around (1) the thermodynamics of bone drilling (where heat comes from), (2) the biology of thermal damage (necrosis thresholds and time–temperature dependence), (3) drill-bit design parameters (geometry, flute, helix, point), (4) operational variables (rpm, feed-rate, thrust), (5) measurement modalities (thermocouples vs infrared imaging), and (6) complications (osteonecrosis, drill-bit breakage, soft-tissue injury).

The dental relevance is indirect but foundational: every implant osteotomy is a controlled drilling operation governed by the same thermodynamics, and the chapter's thresholds underpin all guidance on irrigation, drill sharpness, sequential drilling, and intermittent drilling for dental implants. The compaction / osseodensification literature can be read as a partial answer to this chapter's central problem: how to prepare a bony site without converting work energy into damaging heat.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Necrosis thresholds**:
  - 47 °C × 1 min — reduced regenerative capacity (Eriksson & Albrektsson 1983)
  - 50 °C × 30 s — cellular necrosis (Lundskog 1972)
  - 50 °C cortical — implicated in reduced regenerative capacity
  - 56 °C — osteonecrosis (Matthews & Hirsch 1972)
- **Observed temperatures without coolant**: k-wire / cortical drilling routinely exceeds 100 °C; oscillating saws in TKA > 100 °C.
- **Variables governing heat**: geometry, diameter, rpm, feed-rate, axial thrust force, initial temperature, irrigation.
- No effect sizes or confidence intervals — this is a narrative review.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Orthopaedic and engineering focus; dental implant osteotomy is mentioned only in passing — direct translation to 2–6 mm dental drill diameters and slow-speed (50–1500 rpm) protocols requires interpretation.
- No quantitative comparison of drill geometries — qualitative discussion only.
- Predates osseodensification (Huwais 2017); the compaction-drilling paradigm is not covered.
- No discussion of bone density classification (Misch / Lekholm-Zarb), which dominates dental decision-making.
- As an InTech open-access chapter, peer-review rigour is lighter than a journal-level review.

## 7. Glossary
- **Osteonecrosis**: Death of bone tissue from thermal, ischaemic, or chemical insult; necrotic bone is resorbed by osteoclasts, undermining implant fixation.
- **K-wire (Kirschner wire)**: Smooth or threaded stainless-steel pin used in orthopaedic fixation; cited here as a high-temperature drilling exemplar.
- **Feed-rate (mm·s⁻¹)**: Axial advancement speed of the drill — too slow = more frictional heat per unit depth, too fast = excessive thrust force.
- **Plastic deformation / shear failure**: The two principal mechanisms by which drilling converts mechanical work into thermal energy at the machining face.
- **Compaction drilling**: Non-extraction osteotomy paradigm (e.g. osseodensification) that displaces rather than excavates bone, theoretically reducing heat generation per unit volume removed.
