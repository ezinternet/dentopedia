## One-line Summary

Narrative review synthesizing 2013–2024 literature on polypharmacy (≥5 medications, 30–44% prevalence in older adults) causing xerostomia/hyposalivation and dysgeusia via distinct pharmacological mechanisms (anticholinergic M3-receptor blockade, β-blocker Na-K-2Cl cotransporter inhibition, thiazide carbonic-anhydrase inhibition), with a proposed interprofessional deprescribing-first management framework for dental practitioners.

## 한줄요약

내러티브 리뷰(2013–2024 문헌 종합) — 노인 다약제복용(polypharmacy, 5제 이상, 유병률 30~44%)이 여러 약물별 기전(항콜린제의 M3 수용체 차단, 베타차단제의 Na-K-2Cl 공동수송체 억제, 티아지드의 탄산탈수효소 억제 등)을 통해 침분비저하·구강건조증과 미각이상(dysgeusia)을 동시에 유발함을 정리하고, 치과의사 주도의 탈처방(deprescribing) 우선 다학제 관리 틀을 제시.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Maps specific drug classes (anticholinergics, ACE inhibitors, β-blockers, calcium channel blockers, TCAs, SSRIs, antipsychotics, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, NSAIDs, bisphosphonates) to distinct oral manifestations and their pharmacological mechanisms in aging tissue (Table 1).
- Distinguishes drug-specific hyposalivation mechanisms: muscarinic M3-receptor blockade (anticholinergics/TCAs), altered serotonergic salivary reflex (SSRIs), β-adrenergic/Na-K-2Cl cotransporter disruption (β-blockers), carbonic-anhydrase inhibition (thiazides).
- Proposes a conservative "deprescribing-first" model for polypharmacy-associated xerostomia, explicitly cautioning against routine use of systemic sialogogues (pilocarpine, cevimeline) outside their evidence base (post-irradiation hyposalivation, Sjögren's syndrome).
- Frames dentists as frontline detectors of medication-related oral adverse drug reactions within an interprofessional (dentist–pharmacist–physician–caregiver) care model.
- Identifies quantified compounding risk: polypharmacy (≥5 drugs) independently predicts malnutrition and accelerates ADL/oral-self-care decline (OR 1.5–2.0).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: narrative, evidence-based literature review (not systematic; no PRISMA/meta-analysis/formal quality appraisal).
- **Search**: PubMed/MEDLINE, Scopus, Web of Science; publications January 2013–March 2024; English only. Reference lists of selected articles/reviews manually screened for additional studies.
- **Synthesis**: descriptive, organized around epidemiology → mechanisms → specific complications → diagnostic/management challenges → management strategies → interprofessional collaboration → special populations → future directions.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- US polypharmacy prevalence in adults rose from 8.2% (1999–2000) to 17.1% (2017–2018); reaches 44.1% among adults ≥65y in recent years; >50% in groups with cardiovascular disease/diabetes/multimorbidity; "excessive polypharmacy" (≥10 drugs) exceeds 10% in older populations.
- Each additional prescribed drug raises medication-error odds by 16%; serious drug-drug interactions occur in ~13% of polypharmacy patients, >2/3 of those on ≥10 drugs have ≥1 potentially serious interaction.
- Storbeck et al. (cited): 38.5% of older adults (mean age 74) in a special care dentistry cohort had xerostomia, dose-dependent on medication count.
- Polypharmacy (≥5 drugs) independently predicts malnutrition (2025 cross-sectional long-term-care study cited) and ADL/oral-self-care dependency (OR 1.5–2.0).
- Low health literacy correlates with poor medication adherence/oral hygiene (r = −0.42, literacy score vs. plaque index).
- Recommends against routine systemic sialogogue use (pilocarpine/cevimeline) for polypharmacy-induced xerostomia — evidence base restricted to post-irradiation/Sjögren's populations with residual glandular function; non-pharmacological measures (sugar-free gum/lozenges, saliva substitutes, hydration) favored as first-line given safety profile despite not restoring gland function.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) design: no formal quality/bias appraisal, no meta-analysis, descriptive synthesis only — findings should be read as an expert synthesis rather than a graded evidence summary.
- Authors themselves note most interprofessional/caregiver-education evidence remains descriptive rather than interventional; call for outcome-focused, controlled evaluations.
- Authors call for: standardized medication-induced oral condition assessment tools, dedicated polypharmacy modules in geriatric dental curricula, oral health inclusion in primary-care screening policy, AI-driven risk-prediction tools, and longitudinal studies clarifying causal (not just associative) links between polypharmacy and oral health decline.

## 7. Glossary

- **Polypharmacy**: concurrent use of ≥5 medications; "excessive/hyperpolypharmacy" ≥10 medications.
- **Xerogenic medication**: a drug that reduces salivary flow/causes dry mouth as a side effect.
- **Prescribing cascade**: a new drug prescribed to treat a side effect of an existing drug, misattributed to a new disease, escalating medication burden.
- **Sialogogue**: an agent that stimulates salivary secretion (e.g., pilocarpine, cevimeline).
- **Sialometry**: clinical measurement of salivary flow rate.
- **MRONJ**: medication-related osteonecrosis of the jaw (associated here with bisphosphonates).
- **Deprescribing**: the planned, supervised process of reducing or stopping medications that may be causing harm or no longer providing benefit.
