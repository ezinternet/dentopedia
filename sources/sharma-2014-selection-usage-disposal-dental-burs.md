## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional questionnaire survey (131/150 practitioners) on dental-bur selection, usage, sterilization and disposal: diamond burs preferred (75%) over tungsten carbide (15%), most reused until worn out (85%), with awareness that cutting efficiency drops with wear/grit/duration, variable sterilization, and universal dustbin disposal.

## 한줄요약

단면 설문조사(개원의 131/150) — 다이아몬드 bur 선호(75%) > 텅스텐카바이드(15%), 대부분 마모까지 재사용(85%), 절삭효율은 마모·grit·시간에 따라 감소, 멸균 빈도 가변, 폐기는 전원 일반쓰레기.

## 1. Document Information

- Journal: Journal of Clinical and Diagnostic Research (JCDR) 2014 Jan;8(1):250-254.
- DOI: 10.7860/JCDR/2014/5916.3946 / PMID 24596788 / PMCID PMC3939566.
- Setting: Dept. of Prosthodontics, Crown & Bridge and Implantology, GITAM Dental College & Hospital, Visakhapatnam, India.
- Design: cross-sectional questionnaire (20 closed-ended items, 3 domains: selection [6 Q] / usage [8 Q] / sterilization-disposal).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Defines bur taxonomy clinically: all rotary cutting instruments with bladed heads that remove tooth structure by **cutting OR abrading**, ranging steel → tungsten carbide → diamond abrasives.
- Lists the real selection drivers: tradition, shape, clinical procedure, **substrate being cut**, sterilization, and disposal.
- Documents that cutting efficiency (CE) declines with bur wear, debris accumulation, grit size, and prolonged cutting.
- Quantifies practitioner behavior: diamond preference, reuse-until-worn, sterilization frequency, and disposal — exposing infection-control and efficiency gaps.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Cross-sectional questionnaire mailed to 150 local general practitioners (Visakhapatnam), telephone follow-up; 131 responses (87%).
- 20 closed-ended items (4 options each) across selection / usage / sterilization-disposal.
- Selection items probed grit/coarseness availability, color coding, and sequential ordering during tooth reduction.
- Results expressed as percentages.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Bur preference: diamond 75%, tungsten carbide 15%.
- Reuse until worn out: 85%; agreed CE decreases with use: 33%.
- Sterilization: once daily 35%, every patient 35%.
- Disposal: dustbin 100%.
- Color coding (coarseness): green/black = super-coarse, yellow = smoother/fine; operators favored sequential color coding during gross tooth reduction.
- CE physics noted: over short cuts medium/coarse/super-coarse cut comparably; with prolonged cutting, efficiency falls.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Low-evidence cross-sectional survey, single locality, self-reported behavior (recall/social-desirability bias).
- No measured cutting efficiency — captures attitudes/practice only.
- Universal dustbin disposal flags a cross-infection-control concern needing policy attention.
- Authors note the literature lacks bur-selection guidelines; lab cutting studies need usage databases to be clinically useful.

## 6. Related Work

- Bench cutting-efficiency studies (diamond grit size vs duration; turbine vs electric handpiece).
- Bur sterilization / cross-infection-control literature.
- Four mechanical tooth-removal methods cited: air abrasion, ultrahigh-frequency vibration, high-speed belt-driven rotary, air/water turbines.

## 7. Glossary

- Diamond bur (abrasive): grinding instrument; grit/coarseness color-coded; for enamel/ceramic/zirconia.
- Tungsten carbide bur (bladed): cutting instrument; for dentin/amalgam removal and smoother margins.
- Grit / color coding: coarseness grade (super-coarse green/black → fine yellow).
- Cutting efficiency (CE): material removal rate; declines with wear, debris, and prolonged cutting.
