## One-line Summary
Narrative review (J Dent 2014 Supplement) summarising chemical, biological, and host factors
governing enamel mineral loss from caries and erosion, with management frameworks and emerging
trends, concluding that prevention via evidence-based dietary and hygiene advice is the cornerstone
of lifelong hard-tissue management.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Unified review covering caries, erosion, abrasion, and attrition as causes of enamel mineral loss.
- Describes enamel composition (85% mineral as substituted calcium hydroxyapatite, 12% water, 3%
  organic proteins/lipids) and structural heterogeneity.
- Explains the "critical pH" concept (~5.5) below which hydroxyapatite dissolves.
- Distinguishes intrinsic (gastric acid/GERD) from extrinsic (dietary acid) sources of erosion.
- Highlights rising prevalence of erosion with age and declining caries trend in developed countries.
- Advocates evidence-based prevention (dietary counselling, fluoride, remineralisation) as primary
  management strategy over invasive restoration.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
(Narrative review — qualitative theses; no primary dataset)

**Enamel structure:**
- Mineral (substituted calcium hydroxyapatite): 85% by volume.
- Water: 12% by volume; organic (proteins, lipids): 3% by volume.
- Enamel thickness: up to 2.5 mm over cusps, tapering to knife-edge at cervical margin.

**Caries:**
- Multifactorial: dietary fermentable carbohydrates + plaque bacteria → organic acids →
  plaque pH drops below critical ~5.5 → subsurface demineralisation.
- Steady decline in caries prevalence in developed countries; management shifting toward
  minimally invasive: risk assessment → prevention → control → restoration → recall.

**Erosion:**
- Intrinsic sources: gastric hydrochloric acid (GERD, eating disorders, chronic vomiting).
- Extrinsic sources: dietary acidic foods and beverages (citric, phosphoric, carbonic acids).
- Prevalence increasing; increases with age; patient QoL impaired (function, sensitivity,
  aesthetics, tooth loss → psychological impact including depression and loss of self-esteem).
- Tooth wear involves erosion + abrasion + attrition, acting synergistically or additively.

**Clinical management principles:**
- Diagnosis + risk assessment to identify aetiology (intrinsic vs extrinsic).
- Prevention: evidence-based oral hygiene instruction + dietary counselling.
- Fluoride therapy and remineralisation agents.
- Minimally invasive restorative intervention when tissue loss is significant.

**Emerging trends:**
- Declining caries in developed countries but rising tooth wear/erosion prevalence.
- Key population: young children (deciduous dentition) and adolescents (permanent dentition).
- Long-term natural dentition retention increases cumulative exposure to physicochemical insults.

## 7. Glossary
- Hydroxyapatite: Ca10(PO4)6(OH)2; the principal mineral of enamel; dissolves below critical pH ~5.5.
- Critical pH: pH at which saliva/plaque fluid becomes undersaturated with respect to
  hydroxyapatite, initiating dissolution.
- Erosion: Chemical dissolution of tooth mineral by acids without bacterial involvement.
- Abrasion: Mechanical wear by external objects (e.g., toothbrush, diet).
- Attrition: Tooth-to-tooth wear (e.g., bruxism).
- GERD: Gastroesophageal reflux disease — major intrinsic erosion source.
- Pellicle: Acquired salivary protein film coating enamel; provides partial acid protection.
