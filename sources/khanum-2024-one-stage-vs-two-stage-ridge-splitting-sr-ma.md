## Why Ingested

This SR+MA directly informs the staging decision behind [[wiki/bone-regeneration/enislidis-2006-staged-ridge-splitting-implant-mandible]] (a two-stage staged ridge-split technique): the pooled comparative analysis finds the **one-stage** ridge split superior to the two-stage approach (SMD favouring one-stage ~0.89). It refines — but does not overturn — the enislidis staged-technique anchor, and complements the classic split-crest origin paper [[wiki/bone-regeneration/simion-1992-jawbone-enlargement-split-crest-gtr]] by providing the first quantitative one-stage-vs-two-stage synthesis.

## One-line Summary

PRISMA SR+MA (11 studies qualitative, 3 in meta-analysis; all moderate-to-high RoB) finding one-stage ridge split superior to two-stage (pooled SMD favouring one-stage ~0.89), tempered by poor heterogeneity and funnel asymmetry (possible publication bias).

## 한줄요약

PRISMA 체계적 문헌고찰+메타분석 (정성 11편, 메타분석 3편, 전부 중-고 비뚤림위험)에서 1단계 치조제 분할술(one-stage ridge split)이 2단계보다 우수(통합 SMD ~0.89, one-stage 유리)했으나, 이질성 불량·깔때기 비대칭(출판편향 가능성)으로 근거 강도는 제한적.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First systematic review + meta-analysis to **directly compare one-stage vs two-stage ridge split** procedures for horizontal alveolar ridge augmentation in adults.
- Pools standardized mean difference (SMD) across the three meta-analysable studies, yielding a quantitative direction-of-effect estimate (favouring one-stage ~0.89).
- Provides a structured risk-of-bias appraisal (Newcastle–Ottawa Scale for studies; JBI critical checklist for case reports) of the small ridge-split comparative evidence base.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Reporting standard**: PRISMA.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Google Scholar, EBSCO Host.
- **Search window**: January 2000 – December 2021.
- **Inclusion**: prospective and follow-up studies comparing one-stage and two-stage ridge split, reporting outcomes as mean ± SD.
- **Quality assessment**: Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (NOS) for included studies; Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) critical appraisal checklist for case reports.
- **Synthesis**: standardized mean difference (SMD / SDM) as the summary statistic, random-effects model, p < 0.05 as statistically significant.
- **Yield**: 11 studies in qualitative synthesis; 3 studies suitable for meta-analysis.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **All 11 included studies had moderate-to-high risk of bias.**
- **Pooled SMD favours one-stage ridge split** — on average "0.89 times more" toward one-stage relative to two-stage.
- **Heterogeneity** tests were mostly poor (high/inconsistent heterogeneity).
- **Funnel plots** showed asymmetry → possible publication bias.
- **Conclusion**: one-stage ridge split is superior to two-stage on comparative analysis.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Very small meta-analysable base (only 3 studies) — fragile pooled estimate.
- All evidence at moderate-to-high RoB; no RCT-grade comparison.
- Poor heterogeneity and funnel asymmetry weaken confidence and raise publication-bias concern.
- Outcome definition (mean ± SD of what — width gain? survival?) is heterogeneous across the small set; the abstract does not specify the unifying primary outcome.
- Larger, lower-RoB prospective/randomized head-to-head trials are needed before the one-stage superiority signal can be treated as definitive.

## 7. Glossary

- **Ridge split / split-crest technique**: longitudinal sectioning of a narrow alveolar ridge to expand it buccolingually, creating space for implant placement and/or graft.
- **One-stage ridge split**: ridge expansion with implant placement performed in the same session.
- **Two-stage ridge split**: ridge expansion in one surgery, with implant placement deferred to a second surgery after healing.
- **SMD (standardized mean difference)**: effect-size measure pooling outcomes measured on different scales.
- **NOS (Newcastle–Ottawa Scale)**: risk-of-bias tool for non-randomized observational studies.
- **JBI checklist**: Joanna Briggs Institute critical appraisal tool (here for case reports).
- **Funnel-plot asymmetry**: visual indicator of possible publication bias / small-study effects.
