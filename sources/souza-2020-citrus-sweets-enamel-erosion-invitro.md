## Why Ingested
Provides quantitative contact-profilometry wear data (μm) for citrus sweets — a clinically
underappreciated erosive agent — extending the soft-drink erosion model of
[[dental-erosion/barac-2015-erosive-effect-soft-drinks-enamel-profilometry]] and adding maleic
and lactic acid dimensions to the acid-type discussion framed by
[[dental-erosion/kanzow-2016-etiology-pathogenesis-dental-erosion]].

## One-line Summary
In-vitro profilometry study (n = 90 bovine enamel, 6 groups, 7-day/4×90 s-per-day acid challenge)
showing citrus sweets (pH 2.6–3.5) cause erosive enamel wear of 1.3–2.4 μm; Fini Diet (pH 3.3,
lactic+citric acid, median 2.4 μm) and Fini Regaliz Acid Tubes (pH 3.1, maleic+citric, median
2.2 μm) are as erosive as 0.1% citric acid (median 2.3 μm) and more erosive than Coca-Cola
(pH 2.6, median 1.4 μm).

## 한줄요약
In-vitro 연구 (n=90 소 법랑질, 7일): 시트러스 젤리/캔디(pH 2.6~3.5)가 1.3–2.4 μm의 법랑질
마모를 유발하며, Fini Diet(pH 3.3)·Fini Regaliz(pH 3.1)는 0.1% 구연산(2.3 μm) 수준으로
Coca-Cola(1.4 μm)보다 더 침식적 — 산 종류와 복합산이 pH보다 중요.

## 2. Key Contributions
- First in-vitro study to quantify actual enamel wear (μm by profilometry) for multiple citrus
  sweet brands under a clinically-modelled acid-challenge protocol.
- Showed two citrus sweets (Fini Diet, Fini Regaliz Acid Tubes) were as erosive as 0.1% citric
  acid solution and one (Fini Regaliz) was significantly more erosive than Coca-Cola.
- Demonstrated that sweets containing combined acids (lactic+citric or maleic+citric) tended to
  be more erosive than those with a single acid regardless of pH.
- Highlighted the underrecognised erosive risk of sweets vs. soft drinks (OR 2.24 vs 1.61 for ETW).
- Used CRIS guidelines for in-vitro reporting standardisation.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Design: Controlled in-vitro acid-challenge study following CRIS guidelines.
- Specimens: 90 bovine incisor enamel samples embedded in autopolymerising acrylic resin;
  polished to baseline profile with SiC papers (320, 600, 1200 grit); outer 2/3 surface
  nail-varnish protected to create reference control areas.
- Groups (n = 15 each):
  1. 0.1% citric acid solution (pH 2.5) — standard control
  2. Coca-Cola (phosphoric acid, pH 2.6)
  3. Fini Diet (lactic acid + citric acid, pH 3.3) — sugar-free
  4. Fini Jelly Kisses (citric acid + lactic acid, pH 3.5)
  5. Fini Fruit Salad Bubblegum (maleic acid, pH 2.6)
  6. Fini Regaliz Acid Tubes (maleic acid + citric acid, pH 3.1)
- Sweet dissolution: 40 g per 250 mL deionised water.
- Challenge: 4 × 90 s immersions/day in 30 mL solution at 25°C, 7 days; between challenges
  remineralisation in artificial saliva (pH 6.8) for 2 h; overnight in artificial saliva.
- Profilometry: contact profilometer (Mahr, Germany); 5 equidistant scans (6 mm, 250 μm apart)
  at baseline and after 7 days; wear = baseline – final step height (μm); detection limit 0.1 μm.
- Statistics: Kruskal-Wallis + post-hoc Dunn's test; p < 0.0001.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Enamel wear (μm), median [interquartile range], Kruskal-Wallis/Dunn p < 0.0001:**
| Group | pH | Acid(s) | Wear median [IQR] μm |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.1% citric acid (control) | 2.5 | citric | 2.3 [0.7] a |
| Coca-Cola | 2.6 | phosphoric | 1.4 [0.5] bc |
| Fini Diet (sugar-free) | 3.3 | lactic + citric | 2.4 [1.2] ab |
| Fini Jelly Kisses | 3.5 | citric + lactic | 1.4 [0.9] c |
| Fini Fruit Salad Bubblegum | 2.6 | maleic | 1.3 [0.3] c |
| Fini Regaliz Acid Tubes | 3.1 | maleic + citric | 2.2 [0.5] a |

(Different letters = significant difference; Dunn post hoc)

**Key findings:**
- Fini Diet (pH 3.3) and Fini Regaliz Tubes (pH 3.1): wear not significantly different from
  0.1% citric acid (2.3 μm); both exceeded Coca-Cola (p < 0.0001).
- Fini Regaliz Tubes (maleic + citric acid, pH 3.1) significantly more erosive than Coca-Cola
  (phosphoric acid, pH 2.6), despite higher pH.
- Single-acid maleic (Bubblegum, pH 2.6) was LESS erosive than dual-acid combinations,
  suggesting acid synergism rather than pH alone drives erosion.
- Citric acid acts both via H+ release (pKa 3.1, 4.74, 6.42) and calcium chelation by citrate ion.
- Null hypothesis rejected: significant differences between sweets and soft drink on ETW.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Bovine enamel used (different mineral density/composition vs human enamel).
- Sweet adhesion to tooth surface (clinically relevant) not modelled — actual in-mouth erosion
  may be greater.
- Acid concentrations not reported on product labels; exact acid proportions unknown.
- No salivary pellicle — underestimates protection of acquired pellicle in vivo.
- Future: in-situ studies; evaluate sweet adhesion contribution; assess deciduous enamel
  (higher prevalence in children).

## 6. Related Work
- Kanzow et al. 2016 — cited directly as reference 1; etiology/pathogenesis of dental erosion.
- Salas et al. 2015 (J Dent) — meta-analysis showing OR 2.24 for citrus sweets vs OR 1.61 for
  soft drinks in child/adolescent ETW.
- Carvalho et al. 2017 (Clin Oral Investig) — gummy candy causing 6× greater enamel softening
  than orange juice.
- Shen et al. 2017 (Aust Dent J) — 17/30 sugar-free sweets caused enamel softening.

## 7. Glossary
- ETW: Erosive tooth wear — multifactorial acid-mediated and mechanical loss of tooth structure.
- Maleic acid: Dicarboxylic acid (MW 116.1 g/mol) used in sweets; pKa 1.83, 6.07.
- Lactic acid: Monocarboxylic acid (MW 90.08 g/mol); pKa 3.86.
- Citric acid: Tricarboxylic acid (MW 192.12 g/mol); pKa 3.1, 4.74, 6.42; also a calcium
  chelator via citrate ion.
- CRIS guidelines: Checklist for Reporting In-vitro Studies (J Conserv Dent 2014).
- IQR: Interquartile range (Q3–Q1).
