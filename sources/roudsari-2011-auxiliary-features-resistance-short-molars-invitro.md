## One-line Summary
J Prosthet Dent 2011;106(5):305-309 — in vitro study of auxiliary resistance features on short molars (3.0 mm height, 22° TOC, chamfer); proximal grooves (Grv) and cervical TOC reduction (Rdc 22°→4° in cervical 1.5 mm) both restore resistance form vs Ctrl; proximal grooves achieve 156.75N mean dislodgement force.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: Journal of Prosthetic Dentistry 2011;106(5):305-309
- Type: In vitro experimental, Ivorine tooth + standardized metal dies
- Group affiliation: University of Manchester

## 2. Key Contributions
- Quantifies two practical clinical solutions for resistance failure in short, overtapered preparations
- Provides force values supporting routine use of proximal grooves
- Validates cervical-only TOC reduction as alternative when occlusal access limits full TOC correction

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Single Ivorine tooth prepared with controlled milling: 22° TOC, 3.0 mm occlusocervical height, chamfer finish line
- 3 groups:
  - Group Ctrl — control (22° TOC, no auxiliary)
  - Group Grv — Ctrl + proximal grooves
  - Group Rdc — TOC reduced from 22° → 4° in cervical 1.5 mm
- n=10 metal dies/group; Co-Cr copings; zinc phosphate cement
- Load applied at 45° angle, buccal-to-lingual, universal testing machine
- Statistics: One-way ANOVA + Bonferroni post-hoc (α = .05)

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- Group Grv (proximal grooves) — mean force 156.75 N (significantly higher than Ctrl)
- Group Rdc (cervical TOC reduction) — also significantly higher than Ctrl
- Both auxiliary features restore resistance form despite poor base TOC

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Single tooth model; Ivorine substrate
- Zinc phosphate cement (not contemporary)
- 45° load applied in a single direction; mastication is multi-vector
