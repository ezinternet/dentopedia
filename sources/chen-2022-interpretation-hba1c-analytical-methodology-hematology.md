## One-line Summary
Narrative review (Exp Ther Med 2022) cataloging three classes of HbA1c interference — methodological (Hb variants, HbF, derivatives by assay), biochemical (variable glycation rate), and hematological (RBC lifespan changes from anemia, hemolysis, transfusion, CKD) — with a unified framework for cautious clinical interpretation.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Establishes the **three-axis interference framework** (methodological × biochemical × hematological) as the conceptual structure for HbA1c interpretation in clinical practice.
- Provides systematic catalog of **method-specific vs variant-specific interference**: each Hb variant (S, C, D, E, HbF, HbA2 elevation) cross-tabulated against detection method (IEC, CE, AC, IA, enzymatic).
- Reviews **non-variant interference**: carbamylated Hb (CKD/uremia), acetylated Hb (high-dose aspirin), glycated lysines, posttranslational modifications.
- Catalogs **drug interferences**: aspirin, vitamins C and E, dapsone, sulfasalazine, HIV antiretrovirals, iron supplementation.
- Quantifies **RBC lifespan effect**: shortened lifespan (hemolytic anemia, recent transfusion, post-pregnancy, splenomegaly, erythropoietin therapy) → falsely low HbA1c; prolonged lifespan (iron deficiency anemia, B12/folate deficiency, splenectomy) → falsely high HbA1c.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Search terms**: HbA1c methods, HbA1c interferences, HbA1c interpretation, hemoglobin A glycosylated, glycated hemoglobin, hemoglobin variant, fetal hemoglobin, Hb derivatives, carbamylated hemoglobin, acetylated hemoglobin, aspirin, vitamins C and E, dapsone, sulfasalazine, HIV, iron deficiency anemia.
- **Language**: primarily English.
- **No PRISMA/AMSTAR systematic search reporting** — narrative review structure with seven sections (Introduction, Data collection, Biochemistry of HbA1c, Detection methods, Interfering factors, Summary, Conclusions).
- **Biochemistry sections** detail Amadori rearrangement, Schiff base (labile HbA1c / LA1c), N-terminal valine of β-chain, distinction from glycation at α-chain or lysine residues.
- **Detection methods reviewed**: cation/ion-exchange chromatography (IEC), boronate affinity chromatography (BAC), capillary electrophoresis (CE), immunoassay, enzymatic.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **HbA1c reference range**: 4–6% (20–42 mmol/mol).
- **Diagnostic cutoff (since 2010)**: ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol) diabetes; 5.7–6.4% (39–46 mmol/mol) high-risk/prediabetes.
- **NGSP-IFCC conversion**: NGSP (%) = 0.09148 × IFCC (mmol/mol) + 2.152.
- **Hb composition in adults**: HbA (α2β2) 95–98%, HbA2 (α2δ2) ~2%, HbF (α2γ2) trace.
- **Conditions falsely lowering HbA1c**: hemolytic anemia, hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency, recent blood loss, transfusion (<3 months), pregnancy (last trimester), post-erythropoietin therapy, chronic liver disease, certain Hb variants on specific methods.
- **Conditions falsely elevating HbA1c**: iron deficiency anemia, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, splenectomy, uremia (HbCarb), high-dose aspirin (acetylated Hb), hyperbilirubinemia, hypertriglyceridemia.
- **Method-specific sensitivities**:
  - IEC: variants visible (interference), HbF interferes.
  - BAC: variant-blind (insensitive but also less specific).
  - IA: HbF reduces HbA1c by ~1%; variants affect epitope recognition.
  - Enzymatic: largely variant-independent.
- **Drug interferences quantified**:
  - Aspirin >100 mg/day chronic — possible HbCarb-like elevation on some methods.
  - Vitamin C >1 g/day — variable effect by method.
  - Dapsone, ribavirin — hemolysis → falsely low.
  - Iron therapy initiation — HbA1c paradoxical fall (RBC turnover normalization).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Narrative review without systematic search reporting (no PRISMA flow, no risk-of-bias assessment).
- Heterogeneity in primary studies (in vitro, case reports, observational) precludes quantitative pooling.
- Several drug/disease interferences reported as "inconsistent and difficult to explain" — mechanisms unresolved.
- Future direction: alternative biomarkers when HbA1c unreliable — fructosamine (2–3 week average), glycated albumin (2–4 weeks), 1,5-anhydroglucitol (1–2 weeks), CGM-derived time-in-range.
- Authors emphasize that clinicians should **always reconcile HbA1c with concurrent fingerstick or fructosamine** when discordance with clinical picture occurs.

## 7. Glossary
- **HbA1c**: Glycated hemoglobin at Hb β-chain N-terminal valine via Amadori product.
- **Labile HbA1c (LA1c)**: Reversible Schiff base intermediate (aldodiamine); distinct from stable HbA1c.
- **Amadori rearrangement**: irreversible isomerization of Schiff base to 1-amino-1-deoxyfructose (stable ketoamine).
- **HbA0**: Non-glycated hemoglobin (or glycated at sites other than β-chain N-terminal valine).
- **HbA1a/b**: Minor fast-eluting Hb fractions (fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, glucose-6-phosphate, pyruvate adducts).
- **HbCarb**: Carbamylated Hb — formed from urea-derived cyanate; elevated in CKD/uremia.
- **HbF**: Fetal hemoglobin (α2γ2) — γ-chain has terminal glycine (not valine), reducing glycation; elevated in β-thalassemia.
- **BAC**: Boronate Affinity Chromatography.
- **CE**: Capillary Electrophoresis.
- **IEC**: Ion Exchange Chromatography (cation-exchange variant for HbA1c).
- **NGSP / IFCC**: standardization programs (US-centric vs international); related by NGSP = 0.09148 × IFCC + 2.152.
- **Fructosamine**: glycated serum proteins — alternative marker, 2–3 week average glycemia.
- **CGM / TIR**: Continuous Glucose Monitoring / Time-in-Range — alternative when HbA1c unreliable.
