## One-line Summary
In vitro study using steady-state plasma concentrations of nine NSAIDs; ibuprofen and mefenamic acid (but not the other seven) significantly reduce aspirin's antiplatelet effect when added before aspirin; naproxen and flurbiprofen have their own independent antiplatelet effects.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Journal of Clinical Pharmacy and Therapeutics 2013;38(1):12–15
- **DOI**: 10.1111/j.1365-2710.2012.01373.x
- **First published online**: 06 August 2012
- **Corresponding author**: Yasuhiko Yamada, Tokyo University of Pharmacy and Life Sciences
- **Study type**: In vitro pharmacodynamic screen

## 2. Key Contributions
- Comprehensive in vitro screen of nine clinically available NSAIDs for aspirin interaction at steady-state therapeutic plasma concentrations
- Confirmed ibuprofen as the primary interactor (48.1% reduction in aspirin's antiplatelet effect when ibuprofen added before aspirin)
- Identified mefenamic acid as a second interactor (22.7% reduction)
- Found that celecoxib, loxoprofen, indomethacin, diclofenac, etodolac, meloxicam, and others do not significantly interfere
- Distinguished two separate properties: (1) interference with aspirin vs. (2) independent antiplatelet activity; naproxen and flurbiprofen have the latter but not the former
- Confirmed timing dependence: no NSAID altered aspirin's effect when aspirin was given before the NSAID

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Model**: In vitro platelet-rich plasma (PRP) from human volunteers
- **NSAID selection**: Nine NSAIDs studied at steady-state plasma concentrations reflecting standard clinical doses (concentrations derived from published pharmacokinetic data: Japanese interview forms)
- **Aspirin concentration**: EC50 (50% effective concentration) previously estimated in vitro for aspirin in this model
- **Aggregation assay**: Collagen-induced platelet aggregation rate in PRP
- **Four conditions for each NSAID**:
  1. NSAID alone
  2. Aspirin alone
  3. Aspirin added first, then NSAID
  4. NSAID added first, then aspirin
- **NSAIDs tested**: Aspirin (reference), ibuprofen, naproxen, flurbiprofen, loxoprofen, indomethacin, diclofenac, etodolac, mefenamic acid, meloxicam

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Ibuprofen before aspirin**: 48.1% reduction in aspirin's antiplatelet effect (significant)
- **Mefenamic acid before aspirin**: 22.7% reduction (significant)
- **Other seven NSAIDs before aspirin**: No significant effect on aspirin's antiplatelet activity
- **Any NSAID after aspirin**: No significant interference (timing-dependent)
- **Naproxen alone**: Significant antiplatelet effect at clinical plasma concentrations (independent of aspirin)
- **Flurbiprofen alone**: Significant antiplatelet effect at clinical plasma concentrations (independent of aspirin)
- **Interpretation**: Naproxen and flurbiprofen may maintain platelet inhibition through their own reversible COX-1 binding, but this is distinct from blocking aspirin's irreversible acetylation
- The steric hindrance mechanism (occupation of COX-1 active site before aspirin arrives) is confirmed as the mechanism for the ibuprofen and mefenamic acid interactions

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- In vitro model; plasma concentrations are estimated from published PK data, not directly measured in subjects
- Collagen as sole aggregation stimulus; AA-induced aggregation not used (less direct for COX pathway)
- Only one concentration per NSAID (steady-state Cmax); dose-response relationship not explored
- No in vivo validation
- Mefenamic acid is less commonly used clinically; the magnitude of its interference (22.7%) is lower than ibuprofen (48.1%)

## 6. Related Work
- Catella-Lawson 2001: first human RCT; ibuprofen interference confirmed; diclofenac safe
- Renda 2006: RCT in IHD patients; celecoxib confirmed safe
- Hohlfeld 2008: dipyrone/pyrazolinone class interference via MAA
- Schuijt 2009 (cited in paper): diclofenac and aspirin interaction study in healthy volunteers

## 7. Glossary
- **Steady-state plasma concentration**: The drug concentration reached after repeated dosing when absorption equals elimination; used here as a clinically relevant test concentration
- **Collagen-induced aggregation**: Platelet aggregation triggered by collagen exposure; requires TXA2 production via COX-1 pathway; aspirin suppresses this
- **Mefenamic acid**: Fenamate-class NSAID; rarely used in dentistry but available in Japan and some Asian markets
- **Naproxen**: Propionic acid NSAID; own antiplatelet effect at therapeutic concentrations but does not block aspirin acetylation in this model
- **Flurbiprofen**: Propionic acid NSAID; similar profile to naproxen in this study
- **EC50**: Half-maximal effective concentration; the aspirin concentration producing 50% inhibition in vitro, used as reference
