## Why Ingested

기존 [[practice-management/scheerman-2024-systematic-umbrella-review-effects-teledentistry]]는 원격치의학(Teledentistry)의 정확도·비용·접근성을 낙관적으로 종합하지만, 환자 측 수용성(acceptability) 데이터가 빈약하다. 본 cross-sectional 조사(García 2023, N=91)는 미국 농촌 라티노 성인에서 57%가 무관심이라는 환자-측 현실을 보여 "가용성=이용"이라는 접근성 낙관론을 한정(refine)하는 근거로 활용.

## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional survey (N=91 rural Latina/o/x adults, southwest Virginia) found 57% had no interest in teledentistry even if available; only higher household income (>$24k) and lacking dental insurance were significantly associated with acceptability — availability alone will not close access gaps without explicit health-equity integration.

## 한줄요약

농촌 라티노 성인 91명 대상 단면조사 — 57%가 원격치의학(Teledentistry)에 무관심, 가구소득 >$24k와 치과보험 미보유만 수용성과 유의 연관; 단순 가용성만으로는 접근격차가 해소되지 않으며 보건형평성(health equity)의 명시적 통합이 필요.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First study to examine teledentistry acceptability specifically among Latina/o/x adults living in a rural U.S. area — an understudied, fast-growing, oral-health-underserved population (9% of rural U.S. populations).
- Counter-intuitive headline: a majority (57.1%) reported **no** interest in teledentistry consultations, contrasting with prior literature reporting positive pre-experience telehealth attitudes among Latinos and rural dwellers.
- Identifies **cost-related enabling factors** (income, insurance status) — not perceived oral-health need — as the significant correlates of acceptability, with the surprising direction that the **uninsured** were *more* receptive.
- Reframes teledentistry expansion as a potential **equity risk**: broad availability may leave acculturation/language-barriered subgroups behind unless equity is explicitly built into implementation.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Cross-sectional REDCap survey (80 questions) conducted May 2020–Feb 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- **Population**: Adults ≥18, self-identified Latina/o/x, residing in 14 ZIP codes in rural southwest Virginia (selected from 2020 Census Latino-density data).
- **Recruitment**: Social media, local health fairs, community organizations; English or Spanish option; $10 gift-card incentive.
- **Outcome**: Teledentistry acceptability = yes/no to interest in video/internet consultations (check-ups) with a dental provider, after a definition of telehealth was provided.
- **Covariates**: Sociodemographic (age, sex, education, household income <$24,999 vs ≥$25,000), acculturation proxies (survey language, nativity, years in U.S.), oral-health variables (dental insurance, past-year utilization, self-rated oral health).
- **Analysis**: Descriptive statistics + chi-squared bivariate tests (SAS 9.4). Participants with incomplete data (n=8) excluded → analytic N=91. No multivariable modeling (limited by sample size).
- **IRB**: Virginia Commonwealth University.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **N = 91** analyzed. Sample: 70.3% female, 63.7% aged >30, 54.9% Spanish-speaking, 58.2% born outside the U.S. (mostly Mexico/Central America), 57.2% ≤high-school education, 56% income <$24,000.
- **58.2% had no dental insurance**; **67.3% no dental visit in past year**; 71.4% self-rated oral health excellent/very good/good.
- **57.1% reported no interest in teledentistry** if available.
- Those interested were more likely women (66.7%), English speakers (53.8%), and to self-rate oral health as good+ (76.9%).
- **Significant correlates of acceptability**: household income >$24,000 (p=.04) and **not** having dental insurance (p=.01). Uninsured were *more* receptive — interpreted as perceiving teledentistry as a cheaper/more accessible route.
- Non-receptive participants were predominantly Spanish speakers (61.5%) and foreign-born (63.5%) — suggesting acculturation plays a role.
- Self-perceived oral health was **not** correlated with acceptability (contrasts prior utilization literature).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Small convenience sample (N=91), not nationally representative → descriptive only, no prediction/multivariable modeling.
- Over-representation of women (70.3% vs 29.7% men); rural Latino men under-recruited.
- Covariates limited to surveyed variables; unmeasured factors may be stronger correlates.
- Single yes/no acceptability item; unclear how participants interpreted "teledentistry" (consultation/triage vs online treatment).
- Authors call for larger samples, qualitative interviews, and an intersectional approach (race/class/gender) to teledentistry uptake among rural Latino/immigrant populations.

## 6. Related Work

- Reinforces the access/cost case for teledentistry (10–15% cost savings; COVID-era 60-fold demand surge) cited in broader reviews, while tempering it with patient-side reality.
- Tempers the access/cost optimism synthesized in [[practice-management/scheerman-2024-systematic-umbrella-review-effects-teledentistry]] (umbrella review of teledentistry effects).
- Complements diagnostic-accuracy evidence in [[practice-management/gurgel-juarez-2022-accuracy-effectiveness-teledentistry-systematic]] by addressing whether patients will actually choose to use the modality.

## 7. Glossary

- **Teledentistry**: Remote delivery of oral-health care/education via information technology; synchronous (live video consult) or asynchronous/store-and-forward (transmit images, X-rays, records).
- **Acceptability**: Pre-experience willingness to use a service if offered (here, yes/no interest in a video dental consult).
- **Acculturation proxies**: Survey language, nativity, years in U.S. — surrogate measures for degree of cultural integration.
- **Equity-centered telehealth**: Telehealth designed to be accessible to all and to center the needs of oppressed/marginalized communities across implementation.
- **Health professional shortage area (HPSA)**: Designation for areas (often rural) with too few providers relative to population.
