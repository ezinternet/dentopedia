## One-line Summary
Narrative review of NSAIDs in dentistry: WHO 1st-line analgesic; used across all dental fields; additional mechanisms (NF-kB, iNOS, oxidative stress); topical NSAIDs emerging as future direction.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: Medical Science Monitor 29:e940635 (June 2023)
- DOI: 10.12659/MSM.940635
- Design: Narrative review (preclinical + clinical studies)

## 2. Key Contributions
- Comprehensive mechanism overview: COX-1/2, NF-kB, iNOS, oxidative stress pathways
- Evidence for NSAIDs across all dental specialties
- Topical NSAIDs: reduced systemic side effects, worth exploring for local dental applications

## 3. NSAID Mechanisms in Dentistry
- **Primary**: COX-1 and COX-2 inhibition → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ pain and inflammation
- **Secondary** (some NSAIDs): NF-kB inhibition → ↓ cytokine cascade; iNOS inhibition → ↓ nitric oxide; leukocyte migration blockade

## 4. Clinical Applications by Dental Field
| Field | NSAID Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Conservative / Endodontics | Pulpitis pain, post-instrumentation pain | Ibuprofen 400–600mg most studied |
| Oral Surgery | Post-extraction pain (1st line) | Ibuprofen ≥ acetaminophen; ketorolac IM for severe |
| Periodontics | ↓ PGE2 → ↓ bone resorption (experimental) | Evidence mostly preclinical |
| Orthodontics | Bracket-induced pain | Ibuprofen timing around activation |
| Oral Mucosal Disease | Aphthous ulcers, mucosal pain | Topical diclofenac/benzydamine |
| Head/Neck Oncology | Cancer pain adjunct | Part of multimodal analgesic ladder |

## 5. Topical NSAIDs
- Benzydamine (Tantum Verde): anti-inflammatory + local anesthetic properties; mucositis
- Diclofenac gel: experimental for periodontal pockets
- Advantage: avoids GI side effects, renal toxicity, platelet effects of systemic NSAIDs

## 6. Key Clinical Cautions
- Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD): avoid all non-selective NSAIDs
- GI ulcers: add PPI or use selective COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib)
- CKD: avoid NSAIDs (renal prostaglandins maintain GFR)
- Anticoagulant patients: NSAIDs ↑ bleeding risk (↓ platelet COX-1)
- Cardiac patients: NSAIDs ↓ antihypertensive efficacy (especially ACE inhibitors, diuretics)

## 7. Limitations
- Narrative review (selection bias)
- Preclinical evidence for periodontal bone effects not yet translated to strong clinical data
