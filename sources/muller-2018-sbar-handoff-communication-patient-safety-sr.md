## Why Ingested

General-healthcare (not dental-specific) evidence ingested to anchor the dental team-communication axis: it is the systematic-review evidence base for a concrete intra-team communication TOOL — SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) — that transfers directly to dental-practice hand-offs and nurse/assistant-to-dentist escalation. It reinforces [[overviews/dental-workforce-delegation-skill-mix-overview]] by supplying the structured-handover instrument that delegation and skill-mix arrangements depend on (the more a dental team delegates, the more it relies on reliable hand-offs).

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (11 studies: 8 before–after + 3 controlled trials; general healthcare) finds MODERATE evidence that the SBAR hand-off tool improves patient safety — 8 of 26 outcomes significantly improved, strongest for structured nurse-to-physician telephone communication, but overall study quality is low.

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: Impact of the communication and patient hand-off tool SBAR on patient safety: a systematic review
- **Authors**: Müller M, Jürgens J, Redaélli M, Klingberg K, Hautz WE, Stock S
- **Journal**: BMJ Open 2018;8(8):e022202
- **DOI**: 10.1136/bmjopen-2018-022202 | **PMID**: 30139905 | **PMCID**: PMC6112409
- **Type**: Systematic review (narrative synthesis; meta-analysis not possible due to heterogeneity)
- **Field note**: GENERAL-HEALTHCARE evidence (nurses/physicians in hospitals, rehabilitation centre and nursing homes). Held here as an extrapolated anchor for dental team communication; the wiki's dental category is `practice-management`.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First systematic review to focus specifically on whether SBAR improves **hard patient-safety outcomes** (incidence of adverse events), as opposed to the "soft" outcomes (satisfaction, communication perception) where SBAR's benefit was already documented.
- Synthesises 11 studies and 26 distinct patient outcomes, classified by direction/strength of effect.
- Pinpoints the **specific circumstance** with the strongest evidence: structured nurse-to-physician **telephone** communication about deteriorating patients.
- Documents a sobering evidence-quality gap: a near-universally adopted "best-practice" tool rests largely on weak before–after studies.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Databases**: PUBMED, EMBASE, CINAHL, Cochrane Library, PsycINFO (via OvidSP), January 2017, plus reference screening. Search terms SBAR, ISBAR, SBAR-R, ISBARR, ISOBAR. No language/date/type restriction; no grey literature; no registered protocol.
- **Eligibility**: original research where (1) SBAR was implemented into clinical routine, (2) investigating SBAR was the primary objective, (3) at least one patient outcome reported. Studies where SBAR was a minor component of a larger quality-improvement bundle were excluded.
- **Selection/extraction**: two trained reviewers (JJ, MM), third reviewer (MR) for dissent; extraction checked by KK.
- **Quality assessment**: Effective Public Health Practice Project (EPHPP) tool (Cochrane-recommended), 18 criteria across 6 domains (selection bias, study design, confounders, blinding, data collection, withdrawals/drop-outs), consolidated to strong/moderate/weak. Two raters (JJ, MM), WEH as tiebreak.
- **Synthesis**: narrative (heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis); ICC for rater agreement.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Flow**: 1053 identified -> 701 de-duplicated -> 94 full-text -> **11 included**. Inclusion-agreement ICC 0.90 (95% CI 0.86–0.94); quality-rating ICC 0.85 (95% CI 0.78–0.90).
- **Designs**: 8 before–after, 2 controlled clinical trials, 1 RCT. Published 2006–2016. Settings: hospital (7), nursing home (3), rehabilitation centre (1).
- **Quality**: only the Field RCT rated **strong**; Randmaa CCT **moderate**; remaining 9 **weak**. No power calculations in any study; ~half of outcomes had no statistical test reported.
- **Outcomes (n=26)**: **8 significantly improved** (5 studies); **11 described as improved** without statistical test (4 before–after studies); **6 unchanged**; **1 descriptive increase** in adverse events (overall fall incidence rose while fall severity fell). None showed a significant reduction in safety.
- **Strongest specific results**:
  - Field 2011 (RCT, nursing home): INR time-in-therapeutic-range +4.5% (95% CI 3.1%–8.7%); but warfarin-related AE NS (OR 0.9, 95% CI 0.5–1.4).
  - Randmaa 2014 (CCT, anaesthesiology): communication-error CIRS events 31% -> 11%.
  - De Meester 2013: unexpected death 0.99 -> 0.34 per 1000 admissions (p<0.001).
  - Devereaux 2016: 30-day readmissions 0.12 -> 0.04 (p=0.012); avoidable hospitalisations 0.15 -> 0.05 (p=0.007).
  - Christie & Robinson 2009 (descriptive): mortality -11%, MRSA bacteraemia -83%, adverse events -65%.
- **Bottom line**: MODERATE evidence that SBAR improves patient safety, **strongest when structuring telephone communication**.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- No grey literature; authors not contacted for raw data; publication bias unassessable; heterogeneity prevented meta-analysis.
- Most studies before–after, so SBAR effect cannot be separated from secular trends (e.g. increased reporting awareness — likely behind the paradoxical fall-incidence increase).
- No included study measured **process** (communication-parameter) measures, weakening the causal chain SBAR -> communication -> conduct -> outcome.
- Authors warn that unreflected adoption of SBAR may paradoxically stall progress, since a "solved" problem attracts less further research. Calls for high-quality (ideally RCT) studies.

## 7. Glossary

- **SBAR** — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation: a four-part structured verbal/written hand-off framework. Derivatives: ISBAR, SBAR-R, ISBARR, ISOBAR.
- **Hand-off (handover)** — transfer of patient care responsibility and information between providers/shifts; a high-risk point for communication breakdown.
- **Adverse event** — injury caused by medical conduct resulting in prolonged hospitalisation and/or disability (Brennan).
- **EPHPP tool** — Effective Public Health Practice Project quality-assessment instrument; rates studies strong/moderate/weak across six domains.
- **CIRS** — Critical Incident Reporting System.
- **Before–after study** — uncontrolled pre/post design; cannot separate intervention effect from secular change.
