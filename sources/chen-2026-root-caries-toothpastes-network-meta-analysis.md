## Why Ingested

This SR + network meta-analysis (J Dent 2026) ranks toothpaste active agents — high-concentration sodium fluoride (5000 ppm), arginine+fluoride, and bioactive calcium/phosphate (CaSO4 + NH4H2PO4) systems — for arresting and reversing root caries, giving clinically actionable first-line non-invasive guidance for high-risk (often geriatric) patients. It extends [[caries/meyer-lueckel-2019-intervene-root-caries-process-sr-ma]], which already established 5000 ppm F toothpaste, CHX varnish, and SDF as superior lesion-arresting agents, by pooling newer bioactive and arginine formulations head-to-head against standard 1450 ppm NaF via network meta-analysis.

## One-line Summary

SR + network meta-analysis (Cochrane, 9 databases; 13 in vitro + 3 in situ + 12 clinical studies): high-concentration sodium fluoride toothpaste (5000 ppm) most effectively reduces root-caries lesion depth, hardens lesions, and prevents new caries — bioactive Ca/P and arginine+fluoride pastes are alternatives, while standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient in high-risk patients.

## 한줄요약

SR + 네트워크 메타분석 (Cochrane, 9개 DB; in vitro 13 + in situ 3 + 임상 12편): 고농도 불화나트륨 치약(5000 ppm)이 치근우식 병변 깊이 감소·경화·신규 우식 예방에 가장 효과적 — 생활성 칼슘/인산 및 아르기닌+불소 치약은 대안, 표준 1450 ppm NaF는 고위험 환자에서 불충분할 수 있음.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First systematic review with network meta-analysis to rank toothpaste active agents specifically for **management of root caries**, integrating laboratory (in vitro), in situ, and clinical evidence.
- Confirms a significant overall benefit of active toothpaste over placebo on root-caries lesion depth (MD −55.75; p=0.002) and mineral loss (MD −1.18; p=0.0006) in in vitro studies.
- Positions **high-concentration sodium fluoride (5000 ppm)** as the top-ranked NMA agent across lesion depth reduction, lesion hardening (RR 2.55; 95% CI 1.68–3.86), and prevention of new lesions.
- Identifies **bioactive calcium/phosphate toothpaste** (1100 ppm NaF + 1% CaSO4 + 1.1% NH4H2PO4) as second-ranked NMA agent, with RR 1.81 (1.39–2.36) for lesion hardening.
- Confirms **arginine + fluoride** (1.5% arginine + 1450 ppm Na2PO3F) as effective alternative — hardening 61.7–70.5% of lesions vs 56–58.1% in controls.
- Flags that **standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient** to arrest active root caries in high-risk patients.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

### Study registration and design
- Protocol registered in PROSPERO (CRD42024512171); conducted per Cochrane Handbook guidelines; reported per PRISMA four-phase diagram.
- PICO question: "What are the effects of using different toothpastes on root caries?"
- Population: permanent teeth (clinical studies); extracted permanent human root dentine (lab studies). Exclusion: deciduous teeth, animal studies, hydroxyapatite pellets, dentine powders.

### Search and screening
- Nine databases searched (no language or date limitations) plus handsearching; ClinicalTrials.gov searched for ongoing trials.
- Initial yield: 487 records (Scopus n=99, PubMed n=58, Embase n=138, Web of Science n=90, MEDLINE via Ovid n=36, Cochrane Library n=66).
- After removing 246 duplicates: 241 screened for title/abstract → 80 full-text reviewed → 42 excluded (wrong population, intervention, outcomes, setting, or design) → 1 excluded (ongoing trial, no results) → **28 studies included** (13 in vitro, 3 in situ, 12 clinical).
- Two reviewers independently; disagreements resolved by third reviewer; Covidence software used.

### Risk-of-bias assessment
- **In vitro**: quality assessment tool (7 parameters: sample size calculation, similar dimensions, randomisation, standardised procedures, in vivo mimicry, blinding, statistical analysis). 1–3 parameters = high risk; 4–5 = medium; 6–7 = low. Result: 4 high, 6 medium, 3 low.
- **In situ**: Cochrane tool plus assessment of specimen sequence generation, allocation concealment, and appliance-construction blinding. All studies showed low selective-reporting bias; all had unclear or high bias for specimen allocation or participant blinding.
- **Clinical**: Cochrane RoB 2.0 (randomisation process, deviations from interventions, missing outcome data, outcome measurement, selection of reported results). 8/12 low risk, 4/12 some concerns; 3 studies high risk (missing outcome data).
- GRADE certainty assessment: all outcomes rated **low quality** evidence.

### Statistical analysis
- In vitro and clinical studies analysed separately (three strata: in vitro, in situ, clinical).
- Outcomes for lesion depth and mineral loss: **mean difference (MD)** with 95% CI, random-effects model.
- Outcomes for lesion hardness and new root carious lesions: **risk ratio (RR)**, random-effects model.
- Heterogeneity: χ² test (p<0.05) and I² (>50% = high, 25–50% = moderate, <25% = low).
- Subgroup analyses by study duration and fluoride concentration.
- **Network meta-analysis (NMA)** constructed using cumulative ranking probabilities (SUCRA-like curves) with standard sodium fluoride toothpaste as the central reference node; topology confirmed as star-shaped (no direct head-to-head trials between test products; all indirect comparisons).

### Toothpaste categories
- **High fluoride**: >1500 ppm NaF (typically 5000 ppm)
- **Standard fluoride**: 1000–1500 ppm NaF or NaMFP
- **Arginine + fluoride**: 1.5% arginine + 1450 ppm Na2PO3F
- **Bioactive**: calcium/phosphate-releasing systems (10% hydroxyapatite; 5% HX-BGC bioactive glass; NaF + CaSO4 + NH4H2PO4; Fluoro Calcium Phospho Silicate)
- **Stannous/amine fluoride**: 1100 ppm SnF2; 1400 ppm AmF/SnF2

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### In vitro studies (13 studies; 10 in meta-analysis)

| Comparison | Outcome | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Toothpaste vs control | Lesion depth | MD −55.75 (95% CI −90.70 to −20.80), p=0.002; I²=94% |
| Toothpaste vs control | Mineral loss | MD −1.18 (95% CI −1.85 to −0.51), p=0.0006; I²=71% |

- **Subgroup by duration (lesion depth)**: 7–14 days: MD −78.28 (−151.41 to −5.16), p=0.04, I²=96%; <7 days: MD −25.81 (−39.15 to −12.47), p=0.0002, I²=0%; >14 days: MD −8.20 (−33.49 to 17.09), p=0.53 (NS).
- **Subgroup by active ingredient (lesion depth)**: standard fluoride (1000–1500 ppm): MD −43.26 (−112.63 to 26.12), p=0.22 (NS); high NaF: MD −77.32 (−190.22 to 35.58), p=0.18 (NS, I²=97%); other toothpastes: MD −25.81 (−39.15 to −12.47), p=0.0002. No subgroup difference (p=0.61).
- **Subgroup by active ingredient (mineral loss)**: standard fluoride: MD −1.47 (−2.89 to −0.05), p=0.04, I²=86%; bioactive (10% HA and 5% HX-BGC): MD −1.06 (−1.59 to −0.53), p<0.0001, I²=0%; high NaF: MD −0.30 (−1.55 to 0.95), p=0.64 (NS). No subgroup difference (p=0.43).
- Publication bias indicated by asymmetric funnel plots for lesion depth and mineral loss.

### In situ studies (3 studies; 2 in meta-analysis)

- 1000 ppm NaF toothpaste significantly reduced lesion depth vs no-brushing control: MD −33.54 (95% CI −65.48 to −1.61), p=0.04; I²=86% (both studies 5-week duration).

### Clinical studies (12 studies; 10 in meta-analysis)

**Lesion hardening — teeth:**
- Overall: RR 1.60 (95% CI 0.92–2.79), p=0.10, I²=93% (NS due to high heterogeneity).
- Subgroup <1 year follow-up: RR 1.96 (1.03–3.73) — statistically significant (includes 5000 ppm NaF and 1.5% arginine+1450 ppm Na2PO3F).
- Subgroup 1–2 year follow-up: RR 0.83 (0.54–1.26), NS (1100 ppm SnF2 vs 1100 ppm NaF).
- Subgroup fluoride >1450 ppm: **RR 2.55 (1.68–3.86)**, p<0.0001.
- Subgroup arginine+Na2PO3F: RR 1.21 (1.01–1.46), p=0.04 (limited benefit).
- Subgroup stannous/amine fluoride: RR 0.83 (0.54–1.26), p=0.37 (NS).
- Subgroup differences statistically significant (p=0.0006).

**Lesion hardening — participants:**
- Bioactive (1100 ppm NaF + 1% CaSO4 + 1.1% NH4H2PO4) or 1.5% arginine+1450 ppm F vs standard NaF: **RR 1.81 (1.39–2.36)**, I²=0%.

**New root carious lesions:**
- 5000 ppm vs 1450 ppm NaF: RR 0.43 (0.14–1.32), p=0.14, I²=97% (NS; high heterogeneity).

**Root caries increments:**
- Calcium/phosphate or 1100 ppm NaF vs standard/placebo: MD −0.74 (−2.08 to 0.60), p=0.28, I²=59% (NS).

**GRADE**: All clinical outcomes rated **low quality** (⊕OOO) — serious risk of bias, inconsistency, or imprecision.

### Network meta-analysis rankings (star-shaped topology; all indirect comparisons)

NMA cumulative ranking probabilities (clinical studies, lesion hardening):
1. **High-concentration NaF (5000 ppm)** — dominant probability curve, highest rank
2. **Bioactive toothpaste** (Ca/P delivery) — second
3. **Arginine + fluoride** — third
4. Standard NaF toothpaste — fourth
5. Stannous/amine fluoride — fifth (lowest)

No statistically significant differences between toothpastes on absolute comparisons due to indirect-evidence-only network and small study counts.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- All GRADE-assessed clinical evidence was **low quality** — risk of bias, inconsistency (I²=93–97% for several outcomes), and small study numbers.
- NMA has a **star-shaped topology** with no direct head-to-head trials between test agents; all relative efficacy is indirect evidence, limiting confidence in rank ordering.
- The pivotal comparison (high-fluoride/arginine vs standard NaF, RR 1.60, CI 0.92–2.79) is statistically non-significant; only subgroup analysis by concentration (>1450 ppm: RR 2.55) achieves significance.
- Substantial **heterogeneity** (I²=93–97%) across clinical lesion-hardening studies due to differing criteria for defining lesion activity/inactivity, varied follow-up durations (6–24 months), and different population ages (mean 56–81.7 years).
- Mixed evidence base (in vitro + in situ + clinical) limits direct extrapolation; many clinical inferences rely on just 2–4 studies per subgroup.
- Most in vitro studies used **toothpaste slurries** (simulating dilution with saliva/water during brushing) — translation to fresh toothpaste application may differ.
- Potential publication bias indicated by asymmetric funnel plots (lesion depth and mineral loss).
- All bioactive glass data is in vitro only; RCTs needed to validate clinical efficacy.
- Arginine hardening rate data (61.7–70.5% vs 56–58.1%) from two RCTs with limited risk-of-bias concern — authors flag high-risk industry funding (Colgate-Palmolive) in several included studies.
- Missing outcome data in 4 clinical studies (high dropout 16–49%) affected evidence grading.

## 7. Glossary

- **Root caries**: carious lesions on exposed root (cementum/dentin) surfaces, prevalent in ~33% of older adults globally.
- **5000 ppm NaF toothpaste**: high-concentration prescription sodium fluoride dentifrice; forms acid-resistant fluorapatite more effectively than standard formulations.
- **Arginine + fluoride**: 1.5% arginine combined with Na2PO3F (1450 ppm F); arginine metabolised to ammonia, neutralising plaque acids and raising biofilm pH.
- **Bioactive toothpaste (CaSO4 + NH4H2PO4)**: 1100 ppm NaF + 1% calcium sulfate + 1.1% ammonium dihydrogen phosphate; delivers soluble calcium and phosphate directly to the lesion.
- **Bioactive glass**: e.g., 5% HX-BGC or Fluoro Calcium Phospho Silicate; releases Ca2+, PO4, F− ions on contact with saliva, raising pH and promoting fluorapatite precipitation.
- **Stannous fluoride (SnF2)**: forms acid-resistant stannous-mineral layer on tooth surface; 1100–1400 ppm; inferior to high-NaF in this NMA.
- **NMA**: network meta-analysis — integrates direct and indirect evidence allowing ranking of multiple treatments without head-to-head trials.
- **SUCRA / cumulative ranking**: probability-based ranking curve used in NMA; higher curve = higher probability of being the best treatment.
- **RoB 2.0 / GRADE**: Cochrane risk-of-bias tool for RCTs / certainty-of-evidence framework.
- **MD / RR**: mean difference / risk ratio.
