## One-line Summary
Retrospective chart review at 분당제생병원 (Pundang Jesaeng Hospital DMC) OMFS dept, Jan–Dec 1999: 3,506 dental outpatients reviewed, 427 (12.2%) had a systemic disease — hypertension most common, followed by diabetes, heart disease, and liver disease; periodontitis the most common dental diagnosis; 114 internal medicine consultations; 4 severe complications including 1 death (toxic hepatitis post-extraction in a liver-disease elderly patient under GA) and 1 prolonged gingival bleeding in a warfarinized patient after scaling/root planing without baseline coagulation workup.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Quantifies the prevalence of systemic disease among Korean general-hospital dental outpatients in 1999**: 12.2% (427/3,506). Mean age 51.2 y (range 1–88); M:F ratio 0.79:1.
2. **Frequency ranking of systemic conditions**: hypertension > diabetes > heart disease > liver disease > others.
3. **Frequency ranking of dental diagnoses in the systemic-disease cohort**: periodontitis 130 > caries 106 > impacted teeth 47 > abscess/cellulitis 25 > prosthetic indications 33 > TMJ disorders 27 > other 59.
4. **Documents the gap between "consultation needed" and "consultation requested"**: only 114 internal-medicine consults across the 427 patients; many dental procedures performed without medical clearance (counselling-only 232, medication 80, simple periodontal therapy 42, extraction 23, RCT 9, I&D 4).
5. **Defines the most common *definitive* dental treatment as extraction** — followed by endodontic treatment, periodontal treatment, and prosthetic treatment.
6. **Reports 4 severe complications**: (a) death from toxic hepatitis after maxillofacial surgery under general anesthesia in an elderly liver-disease patient; (b) prolonged spontaneous gingival bleeding after scaling/root planing in a 40-year-old male on warfarin whose anticoagulant status was not screened pre-procedure (controlled with Surgicel + Gelfoam, warfarin held, normalized labs); (c)+(d) two post-operative infections.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

Retrospective chart review + structured questionnaire study of all dental outpatients at the Pundang Jesaeng Hospital DMC OMFS clinic over the 12-month window of 1999. Patients were stratified on whether they had a self-reported or chart-documented systemic disease. For the systemic-disease cohort the authors tabulated:
- Demographics (age, sex)
- Systemic disease category
- Dental diagnosis
- Whether internal medicine was consulted before treatment
- The dental treatment actually delivered
- Any post-treatment complications

The paper then frames recommendations for routine pre-treatment history taking, blood pressure measurement, and internal-medicine consultation for uncontrolled cases.

### Critical narrative findings on specific systemic diseases (Discussion)
- **Hypertension**: dental treatment itself raises BP; epinephrine in vasoconstrictor-containing local anesthetics adds further load. Routine BP measurement and pre-op internal-medicine referral for uncontrolled HTN. In unavoidable cases for uncontrolled HTN, only conservative/emergency care should be done, minimizing stress and pain.
- **Diabetes**: avoid mealtime appointments to prevent hypoglycemic shock; schedule morning, short visits; minimize epinephrine (hyperglycemic effect); keep oral glucose at hand.
- **Asthma / COPD / emphysema**: avoid respiratory depressants (bilateral block, rubber dam compromise); seat upright; keep bronchodilator + inhaler accessible.
- **Tuberculosis**: treat all patients as potentially infectious; if active TB treatment <18 mo or recent treatment <9 mo, request internal medicine evaluation of current status.
- **Renal disease (nephritis, pyelonephritis, nephrotic syndrome, renal failure)**: emergency care only when uncontrolled; avoid aspirin and nephrotoxic drugs; close BP monitoring; hemodialysis patients require specific scheduling and internal-medicine consult.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Single-center, 12-month retrospective design** — captures one institution's clinical mix; not generalizable to private clinic settings or to current pharmacological practice (1999 anticoagulation era preceded DOACs entirely).
- **Definition of "systemic disease" depends on patient self-report and chart documentation** — likely underestimates true prevalence; many patients with controlled HTN/DM may not report when asymptomatic.
- **No comparison to a non-systemic-disease control** — cannot infer relative risk of complications attributable to systemic disease.
- **Sample size of severe complications (n=4)** — too small for rate estimation; reported as case descriptions, not incidence rates.
- **OCR-dependent extraction** — this PDF is image-based and Korean-language; some discussion-section detail (e.g., specific drug names, dose recommendations beyond hypertension/DM) is partial in the captured text. For exact quotation, the original publication or a manual re-read of the figures/tables is required.

## 7. Glossary

- **전신질환 (systemic disease)**: in this paper's chart-review sense, any chart-documented non-dental medical diagnosis (HTN, DM, heart disease, liver disease, renal disease, asthma/COPD, TB, etc.).
- **내과 협진 (internal medicine consultation)**: pre-procedural referral to internal medicine for medical clearance and treatment planning.
- **Toxic hepatitis (독성 간염)**: drug-induced or anesthetic-induced acute hepatic injury; in this paper, the death case post-GA in a liver-disease elderly patient.
- **Warfarin**: vitamin K antagonist anticoagulant; in this paper's bleeding case, held peri-procedurally with Surgicel/Gelfoam local hemostasis until labs normalized.
- **Surgicel, Gelfoam**: oxidized regenerated cellulose / absorbable gelatin sponge — local hemostatic agents.
- **Scaling and root planing (SRP, 치주치료)**: non-surgical periodontal therapy; bleeding case occurred after this without prior coagulation screen.
