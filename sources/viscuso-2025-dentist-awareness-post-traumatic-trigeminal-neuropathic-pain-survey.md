## One-line Summary

Web-based cross-sectional survey of 634 Italian dentists: 29% were unaware that post-traumatic trigeminal neuropathic pain (PTTN) exists and >80% received no teaching about it, yet 70% had encountered suspected cases — most managed with irreversible procedures and only ~1 in 3 referred to a pain specialist, exposing a major misdiagnosis-driven public-health gap.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First large web survey quantifying Italian dentists' awareness and clinical handling of PTTN (n=634).
- Documents a striking knowledge gap: ~29% unaware PTTN exists; >80% never taught about it during under/postgraduate training.
- Shows the gap translates into harm: most encountered cases were managed with irreversible procedures (endodontic retreatment, apicectomy, extraction) rather than referral.
- Links unawareness statistically to failure to recognize, refer, or differentiate PTTN from odontogenic pain.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Anonymous Google Forms questionnaire (~3 min), distributed via social media, Oct–Dec 2021; IRB University of Cagliari PROT. NP/2021/5460.
- Eligibility: must be a dentist. 9 substantive multiple-choice items covering knowledge (existence, teaching), clinical experience (differential-diagnosis awareness, encountered cases, referral), triggering events, and management after root canal / after extraction.
- Analysis: Python (Pandas, SciPy); chi-square and Fisher's exact tests, alpha 0.05.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **n = 634 dentists.**
- **Knowledge**: 71.3% aware PTTN exists, **28.7% unaware**; ~1 in 5 (≈20%) received no teaching (discussion: >80% received no PTTN teaching during studies).
- **Differential-diagnosis awareness**: 88.3% aware of the need to differentiate orofacial neuropathic pain from primary odontogenic pain.
- **Referral**: 49% had ever referred a suspected neuropathic-pain patient to a specialist; 51% had not.
- **Encountered PTTN**: 71.7% had encountered suspected PTTN in practice.
- **Triggering events** (n=412, multiple answers): spontaneous 37.10%; well-performed root canal **60.7%** (most common); extraction **42.7%**; other procedures 21.4%.
- **Management after root canal** (n=370): pharmacologic 57.6%; endodontic retreatment 48.6%; extraction of devitalized tooth 22.4%; apicectomy 8.6%; treat/extract neighboring teeth ≈8–9%; referral <1 in 3 (~30%); "didn't know what to do" 13.8%.
- **Management after extraction** (n=339): pharmacologic 70.8%; surgical revision of site ~25%; treat/extract neighboring teeth 4.10%; "didn't know what to do" 11.4%; referral 30.7%.
- **Inferential**: unawareness of PTTN existence significantly associated (p<0.05) with negative responses across all items; referral after root canal pain significant (p=0.003); only PTTN-aware dentists referred.
- **Scale/economics**: applying 60.7% to worldwide root canal volume → >150,000,000 individuals at risk; Belgian direct cost €1396/patient/yr over 5y; indirect costs up to €14,446/patient (Germany).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Single-country (Italy) sample — limited generalizability.
- Did not assess dentists' evaluation of patients' psychological state.
- Self-report survey; no clinical verification of "well-performed" procedures or true PTTN diagnosis.
- Future work: clinical practice patterns and enabling/reinforcing factors for recognition and referral.

## 7. Glossary

- **PTTN** — Post-Traumatic Trigeminal Neuropathic pain; aka anaesthesia dolorosa / painful post-traumatic trigeminal neuropathy. Neuropathic pain in the trigeminal distribution following nerve trauma, persisting/recurring >3 months.
- **PIDP** — Persistent Idiopathic Dentoalveolar Pain ("phantom-tooth pain"); shares features with PTTN, key differential.
- **ICOP** — International Classification of Orofacial Pain.
- **Apicectomy** — surgical removal of the root apex (root-end resection).
