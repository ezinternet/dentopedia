## ⚠️ RETRACTION NOTICE
This paper has been **RETRACTED** by JAMA Internal Medicine. The original PDF carries an Article Alert: "This article has been retracted." A correction was also published. Treat all numerical findings as historical/contextual only — do not cite the effect estimates as reliable evidence.

## One-line Summary
[RETRACTED] Originally reported that physician burnout (47 studies, n=42,473) was associated with ~2× odds of unsafe care, unprofessional behavior, and low patient satisfaction, with depersonalization the strongest link — but the meta-analysis was retracted, so effect sizes should not be cited.

## 1. Document Information
- Journal: JAMA Internal Medicine, 178(10):1317–1331, Published Online Sep 4, 2018
- DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2018.3713
- Study type: SR+MA — **RETRACTED**
- Why kept: serves as a methodological lesson; was widely cited before retraction

## 2. Original Key Findings (NOT TO BE CITED AS RELIABLE)
- 47 studies, 42,473 physicians (59% men, median age 38, range 27–53)
- Originally claimed burnout → ~2× increased odds for:
  - Unsafe care (patient safety incidents)
  - Unprofessional behaviors
  - Low patient satisfaction
- Depersonalization subscale identified as the strongest predictor
- Association between unprofessionalism and burnout was particularly high in early-career physicians

## 3. Methodology
- Databases: MEDLINE, EMBASE, PsycInfo, CINAHL through Oct 22, 2017
- Quantitative observational studies
- Random-effects models, I² heterogeneity tests, publication bias tests
- Two independent reviewers

## 4. Why This Paper Is In The Wiki Despite Retraction
- It was a high-profile paper (JAMA Intern Med) and remains heavily cited even post-retraction
- Anyone working on physician/dentist burnout literature will encounter it
- Including it explicitly in the wiki with the retraction flag prevents accidental citation of withdrawn data
- It illustrates the [feedback_wiki-living-document] principle — the original ingest snapshot would have been wrong; updating to mark retraction is the right behavior

## 5. Limitations
- The paper has been **retracted** — methodology and conclusions are no longer endorsed by the publisher
- Even before retraction, observational studies cannot establish causal direction (burnout → bad care vs. bad-care environment → burnout)
- Effect estimates almost certainly overstated based on the retraction context

## 6. Related Work
- Newer (non-retracted) meta-analyses on physician burnout and patient outcomes should be sought before citing effect estimates
- Dental analogues (Moro 2022, Long 2023) study prevalence only, not the burnout → outcome link
