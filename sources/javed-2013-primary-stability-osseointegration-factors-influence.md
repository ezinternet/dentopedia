## Why Ingested

ISQ 임계값(≥65 favorable, <45 poor)이 어느 1차 문헌에서 비롯됐는지 추적하기 위해 인제스트. 이 2013 narrative review는 그 ISQ 컷오프를 명시한 초기 출처 중 하나이며, [[implants/isq/huang-2020-isq-clinical-significance-literature-review]]가 정리한 ISQ 임상 해석을 시간적으로 보강하고, [[overviews/implants-isq-stability-ladder]]의 "primary→secondary stability ladder" 서사에 bone/design/surgical-technique 결정 인자를 공급한다. ## One-line Summary

Narrative literature review (MEDLINE 1983–Oct 2013) arguing that secure primary (mechanical) stability is a prerequisite for osseointegration, and that bone quality/quantity, implant geometry, and surgical technique are the three predominant factors; cites ISQ >65 as favorable and <45 as poor, and micromotion >50–100 µm as a threshold that jeopardizes integration.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Frames implant stability as two stages: **primary stability** = mechanical engagement of the implant with surrounding bone; **secondary (biological) stability** = bone regeneration/remodelling. A secure primary stability is positively associated with secondary stability.
- Consolidates the **three predominant factors** affecting primary stability into one framework (Fig. 1): bone density/quality + quantity, implant design/geometry, surgical technique.
- Summarizes the two clinical evaluation methods — **Periotest (PT)** and **resonance frequency analysis (RFA, Osstell/ISQ)** — with their thresholds and limitations.
- States the **micromotion threshold** (50–100 µm) above which osseointegration is jeopardized by fibrous tissue formation and bone resorption.

Structured narrative literature review, not a systematic review or meta-analysis (no PRISMA flow, no pooled estimates, no quality grading reported). MEDLINE–PubMed searched 1983–Oct 2013 with keyword combinations; reference lists hand-searched; English-language human and experimental studies with control groups eligible. Content organized into thematic sections: prerequisites for primary stability; bone density and quality; implant design; surgical technique; methods of evaluation; effect of micromotions. No quantitative synthesis — findings are narratively summarized.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Bone density / quality**
- Poor bone quantity/quality = main risk factor for implant failure (excessive resorption, impaired healing vs higher-density bone).
- Mandible has higher implant survival than maxilla, especially posterior maxilla; posterior maxilla = thinner cortical + thicker trabecular bone ("soft bone"), where optimum primary stability is difficult and failure rate higher.
- Implant stability positively associated with cortical bone thickness (Miyamoto et al.); strong correlation between bone density and stability in a study of 158 implant sites / 85 patients (Turkyilmaz & McGlumphy).
- CT regarded as the best radiographic method for morphological/qualitative analysis of residual bone and distribution of cortical vs cancellous bone.
- Intraoperative bone condensing / undersizing the osteotomy improves bone density and increases primary (mechanical) stability.

**Implant design**
- Tapered implants introduced to compress cortical bone in sites with inadequate bone; the decreasing apical diameter accommodates labial concavity (vs cylindrical wide-body implants which risk labial perforation in thin ridges).
- If bone quality/quantity are optimal, they may compensate for implant-design inadequacy (Chong et al.).
- Rough surfaces (sandblasted, acid-etched) present larger surface area, promote peri-implant osteogenesis, and achieve higher BIC than machined surfaces in poor-quality bone.
- Small-diameter implants (<3.0 mm) provide sufficient primary stability in limited bone volume.
- Microthreads / "retention grooves" and progressive thread design at the implant neck may reduce stress and crestal bone loss; platform switching also associated with crestal bone preservation.

**Surgical technique**
- Undersized drilling locally optimizes bone density and improves primary stability; final drill diameter smaller than implant diameter recommended.
- Summers' bone-condensing (osteotome) technique pushes cancellous bone aside, increasing surrounding density and initial stability.
- **Insertion torque thresholds for immediate loading**: 32, 35, or 40 Ncm and higher have been chosen. In weak (poor-quality) bone, implants may still be immediately loaded with high survival when final implant–abutment torque is lower, ~15–20 Ncm.

**Evaluation methods**
- **Periotest (PT)**: gauges temporal contact of the instrument tip during repetitive percussions; narrow value range → comparatively less sensitive for implant stability.
- **RFA (Osstell)**: measures resonance frequency of a transducer attached to the implant body; displays **ISQ** values. **ISQ >65 = most favorable; ISQ <45 = poor primary stability.** Limitations: value depends on proper screwing of the transducer/magnet (loose transducer → falsely low ISQ); can only be performed before final abutment connection, not with the prosthetic restoration. Authors note "no justification for routine clinical use" of PT/RFA and that further refinements are needed.

**Micromotion**
- Micromotion >**50–100 µm** during functional loading negatively influences osseointegration and bone remodelling (fibrous tissue, bone resorption); can cause failure of osseointegration and implant loss.
- Well-controlled micromotion positively influenced bone formation; immediate functional loading of implants in healed ridges or fresh extraction sockets may improve peri-implant bone density and integration.

**Conclusion**: A successful primary stability is essential for successful implant integration; bone quality/quantity, implant geometry, and surgical technique significantly influence initial stability and overall success rate.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review: no formal search-strategy reporting beyond keywords, no risk-of-bias assessment, no quantitative pooling — claims are not weighted by evidence quality.
- English-only eligibility introduces language bias.
- Thresholds cited (ISQ >65 / <45; torque 32–40 Ncm; micromotion 50–100 µm) are drawn from heterogeneous cited primary studies rather than meta-analytic synthesis.
- Authors explicitly state PT and RFA lack justification for routine clinical use and that the ISQ technique needs further modification for precise, reliable assessment.

## 7. Glossary

- **Primary stability**: mechanical engagement of the implant with surrounding bone at placement.
- **Secondary (biological) stability**: stability from bone regeneration/remodelling after placement.
- **ISQ (Implant Stability Quotient)**: numeric output of RFA indicating firmness at the implant–tissue interface (>65 favorable, <45 poor).
- **RFA (Resonance Frequency Analysis)**: measures the resonance frequency of a transducer attached to the implant body (Osstell device).
- **Periotest (PT)**: percussion-based device gauging tip–implant contact; less sensitive than RFA.
- **BIC (Bone-to-Implant Contact)**: percentage of implant surface in direct contact with bone.
- **Micromotion**: relative movement at the bone–implant interface; >50–100 µm impairs osseointegration.
- **Undersized drilling / bone condensing**: surgical techniques that increase peri-implant bone density to raise primary stability.
- **Insertion torque**: rotational force seating the implant; used as a primary-stability surrogate and immediate-loading threshold (e.g., 32/35/40 Ncm).
