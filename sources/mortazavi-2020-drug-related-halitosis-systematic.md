## Why Ingested

Extra-oral (drug-induced) halitosis is a distinct etiologic subtype underrepresented in the wiki's current halitosis pages, which focus on intraoral causes and prevalence. This SR provides a comprehensive drug taxonomy (10 groups, 34 studies, 30,736 patients) filling a pharmacology gap. Directly extends [[halitosis/memon-2022-aetiology-associations-halitosis-systematic]] by detailing the drug-specific arm of extra-oral etiology.

## One-line Summary

PRISMA systematic review (34 studies, n=30,736) identifying 10 pharmacological groups that cause extra-oral halitosis as a side effect, with cysteamine (aminothiols) producing the highest incidence (up to 100%).

## 한줄요약

PRISMA 체계적 문헌고찰(34편, n=30,736): 약물 유발 구외성 구취를 일으키는 10개 약물군을 분류하였으며, 시스테아민(아미노티올계)에서 최대 100% 발생률이 보고됨.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. First systematic review to specifically catalog medications causing **extra-oral halitosis** (distinct from intraoral/local-factor halitosis).
2. Identified **10 pharmacological groups** implicated in drug-related halitosis: acid reducers, aminothiols, anticholinergics, antidepressants, antifungals, antihistamines and steroids, antispasmodics, chemotherapeutic agents, dietary supplements, and organosulfur substances.
3. Reported total incidence of 1,932 drug-related halitosis cases (6.2%) across 30,736 patients.
4. Systematically assessed routes of administration (oral, IV, topical, intranasal, subcutaneous, intradermal, eye drops) and their halitosis-inducing potential.
5. Establishes that pharmaceutical therapy is a clinically significant and underrecognized source of extra-oral halitosis.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design:** Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines
- **Databases searched:** PubMed, Scopus, ScienceDirect, Ovid, Cochrane Library (up to February 2019)
- **PICOS framework:**
  - Population: patients requiring medications or healthy individuals
  - Intervention: medications, pharmaceutical agents, or dietary supplements
  - Comparison: non-use or different dosage/placebo
  - Outcome: halitosis (bad breath)
- **Inclusion criteria:** Clinical trials or cohorts; patients without halitosis at baseline; halitosis reported as individual side effect in numbers/percentages; genuine halitosis segregated from pseudohalitosis/halitophobia; systemic drug effect origin; English language; human studies
- **Exclusion criteria:** Cross-sectional studies, reviews, pooled safety results; non-drug interventions; obscure halitosis reporting; missing drug dosage data; combined genuine/pseudohalitosis records; non-systemic or non-English studies; animal studies
- **Screening process:** 2,943 records after deduplication → 208 full-text reviews → 34 included
- **Quality assessment:** Cochrane Collaboration risk of bias (RoB) tool; two independent reviewers; Cohen's kappa = 0.94 (excellent agreement)
- **RoB distribution:** 18 low, 10 unclear, 6 high risk of bias
- **Halitosis assessment methods:** 11 studies objective (organoleptic or molecular analysis of exhaled air), 21 subjective (patient complaint), 1 combined, remainder unspecified

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Overall:** 34 studies, 30,736 patients, 1,932 halitosis cases (6.2% overall incidence)

**Ten drug groups and representative findings:**

| Group | Key Drugs | Incidence Range | Notable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acid reducers | Ranitidine (150 mg/day) | 0.9% (1/110) | Unclear RoB |
| Aminothiols | Cysteamine (≥15 mg/kg or 0.9–1.2 g/m²) | Up to 100% | Highest incidence; treatment of cystinosis/Huntington's |
| Anticholinergics | Oxybutynin (5–15 mg/day), Glycopyrrolate (40–175 μg/day) | 15.5% / 4.5% | |
| Antidepressants | Various | Reported | Dry mouth mechanism likely |
| Antifungals | Various | Reported | |
| Antihistamines & steroids | Various | Reported | |
| Antispasmodics | Various | Reported | |
| Chemotherapeutic agents | Multiple oncology agents | Reported | High RoB in several studies |
| Dietary supplements | Various | Reported | |
| Organosulfur substances | Garlic derivatives, DMSO, etc. | Reported | Well-established volatile sulfur compound (VSC) mechanism |

**Routes of administration:** Oral intake most common; also IV, topical, subcutaneous, intradermal, intranasal, and eye drops — demonstrating that even non-oral routes can cause systemic halitosis.

**Mechanisms proposed:**
- Dry mouth (xerostomia) from anticholinergic/antidepressant effects reducing salivary flow → increased VSC production
- Direct metabolic conversion to volatile malodorous compounds (aminothiols → sulfur-containing metabolites, organosulfur substances → allyl mercaptan)
- Chemotherapy-induced mucosal changes and altered oral microbiome

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Heterogeneous halitosis assessment methods (objective vs. subjective) across studies limit direct comparison
- Several included studies had unclear or high RoB
- Dose-response relationships could not always be established
- Causality vs. association remains uncertain for some drug groups
- Further prospective studies needed to definitively determine the role of each medication category
- Interaction between drugs and pre-existing oral conditions (periodontitis, xerostomia) not consistently controlled

## 6. Related Work

- Silva et al. (2017): estimated halitosis prevalence ~31.8% in adolescents/adults (general population baseline)
- Memon et al. (2022): aetiology and associations of halitosis — broader etiologic framework within which drug-related halitosis fits
- Previous literature review (Quirynen et al.) had identified only 9 compounds causing halitosis; this SR substantially expands the list
- Anticholinergic dry mouth literature well-established; this SR formalizes halitosis as a downstream outcome

## 7. Glossary

- **Extra-oral halitosis (구외성 구취):** Halitosis originating from systemic conditions, bloodborne causes, or pharmaceutical therapy — not from local oral factors
- **Aminothiols (아미노티올계):** Thiol-containing amino acid derivatives (e.g., cysteamine) used to treat metabolic diseases; produce volatile sulfur compounds as metabolites
- **Anticholinergics (항콜린제):** Drugs inhibiting the parasympathetic nervous system; cause xerostomia as a side effect, indirectly promoting halitosis
- **Organosulfur substances (유기황 물질):** Sulfur-containing compounds (garlic, onion, DMSO) that produce volatile sulfur compounds (VSCs) directly
- **VSCs (휘발성 황화합물, Volatile Sulfur Compounds):** Primary odorants in halitosis: hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), methyl mercaptan (CH₃SH), dimethyl sulfide ((CH₃)₂S)
- **Xerostomia (구강건조증):** Dry mouth from reduced salivary flow; most frequent oral adverse drug event; key indirect mechanism for drug-induced halitosis
- **PROSPERO:** International prospective register of systematic reviews (CRD42019129337)
- **Cohen's kappa (카파 계수):** Inter-rater reliability statistic; kappa = 0.94 in this study = near-perfect agreement
