## Why Ingested

This SR+MA directly addresses the diagnostic validity of halitometers (Halimeter, OralChroma, gas chromatography) versus the gold-standard organoleptic test (OLT) in intra-oral halitosis, establishing the correlation coefficients and ROC-based sensitivity/specificity data needed to evaluate whether objective devices can replace subjective sensory assessment. Foundational paper for the halitosis category — no existing wiki overlap yet.

## One-line Summary

SR+MA (76 studies, n=14,635) finds that no common halitometer correlates strongly with organoleptic scores (Spearman r = 0.65–0.76), concluding that current devices cannot replace the OLT for diagnosing intra-oral halitosis.

## 한줄요약

76개 연구(n=14,635)를 메타분석한 결과, 현재 사용되는 어떤 구취측정기(할리미터, OralChroma, GC)도 관능검사(OLT)와 충분한 상관관계(r=0.65–0.76)를 보이지 않아 OLT를 대체할 수 없다고 결론 내림.

- **Journal**: The Journal of Evidence-Based Dental Practice

- **Study type**: Systematic review + meta-analysis
- **PROSPERO registration**: CRD42022320024
- **Databases searched**: MEDLINE, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, CENTRAL (search date: March 23, 2022)
- **Included studies**: 76 articles (14,635 patients in qualitative analysis)
- **Risk of bias tool**: QUADAS-2 and QUADAS-C

## 2. Key Contributions

1. First SR+MA to pool Spearman correlation coefficients across all major halitometer types (electrochemical sensors, portable GC, bench GC) vs. organoleptic test (OLT).
2. Demonstrates that Halimeter and OralChroma do **not** correlate well with OLT (r = 0.65 and 0.69 respectively) — both fall below the threshold considered clinically strong.
3. Gas chromatography (GC) shows the highest correlation (r = 0.76) but remains only moderately correlated.
4. Summary ROC analysis provides sensitivity/specificity estimates for each device class, enabling AUC-based comparisons.
5. Contextualizes why OLT alternatives are urgently needed (COVID-19 safety concerns, examiner training difficulties, subjectivity).
6. Explicitly concludes that no current halitometer is significantly superior to another for replacing the OLT.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

### Population & Inclusion
- Adults (≥18 years) with or without halitosis
- Excluded: systemic disease patients, children, extra-oral halitosis
- Study types included: clinical trials reporting correlation coefficient between VSC device and OLT
- Excluded: in vitro/animal studies, non-English papers, conference papers, case reports

### Intervention (Index Tests)
- Electrochemical sensors (Halimeter): measures total VSCs together
- Portable gas chromatographs (OralChroma): measures H₂S, CH₃SH, (CH₃)₂S separately
- Gas chromatographs (bench GC): reference standard among objective methods
- eNoses: electronic nose devices (limited data)

### Reference Standard
- Organoleptic test (OLT): examiner sniffs and scores breath 0–5

### Primary Outcome
- Spearman's correlation coefficient (c.c.) between device reading and OLT score

### Secondary Outcomes
- Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV plotted on common ROC space
- Summary ROC (SROC) curves per device class

### Statistical Methods
- Random-effects meta-analysis for pooled Spearman c.c.
- SROC curves for diagnostic accuracy
- Heterogeneity assessed via I² statistic
- Cohen's Kappa for inter-rater agreement during screening

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### Pooled Spearman Correlation Coefficients

| Device Type | Pooled r | 95% CI | I² | p |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Electrochemical sensors (Halimeter) | 0.65 | [0.53–0.74] | 95% | <.01 |
| Portable GC (OralChroma) | 0.69 | [0.63–0.74] | 12% | <.01 |
| Gas chromatographs (bench GC) | 0.76 | [0.67–0.83] | 0% | <.01 |

- All correlations statistically significant but **none reach the threshold for strong clinical agreement**
- OralChroma has low I² (12%) suggesting high consistency across studies; Halimeter has very high heterogeneity (I²=95%)
- GC shows highest r and zero heterogeneity, but it is expensive and impractical for routine use

### Risk of Bias
- Assessed with QUADAS-2 (within-study) and QUADAS-C (comparative studies)
- Notable concerns around patient flow, timing, and index test execution

### SROC Findings
- SROC curves plotted for all device types on a common ROC space
- No device shows clearly superior AUC over the others
- Sensitivity and specificity data variable across studies due to differing diagnostic thresholds

### Clinical Interpretation
- Halimeter: pooled r = 0.65 — "poor to moderate" correlation; high heterogeneity limits generalizability
- OralChroma: pooled r = 0.69 — "moderate" correlation; more consistent across studies
- GC: pooled r = 0.76 — "moderate to good" but impractical; best-performing device class
- **Conclusion**: No halitometer can reliably replace OLT; better devices need to be developed

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- High heterogeneity for Halimeter (I²=95%) limits interpretation of pooled estimate
- Variability in diagnostic thresholds (OLS cutoffs) across included studies hampers comparability
- eNose data too sparse for meta-analysis
- Only intra-oral (Type 1) halitosis included — findings do not extend to extra-oral or pseudo-halitosis
- Training and standardization of OLT examiners not consistently reported
- Future need: development of validated halitometers with stronger OLT correlation; standardized OLT protocols
- COVID-19 context emphasizes urgency for contactless halitosis measurement tools

## 6. Related Work

- Gold standard OLT (Rosenberg 1991 scale; Miyazaki 1999 scale) — widely cited within included studies
- Halimeter (Interscan Corp.) — electrochemical VSC sensor; most studied device in halitosis research
- OralChroma (Abimedical Corp.) — portable GC; separates three main VSCs
- Prior reviews: Scully & Greenman (2008), Quirynen et al. (2009), Van den Broek et al. (2008) — narrative reviews of halitosis diagnosis
- Estimated prevalence of halitosis: 31.8% globally (prior SR+MA, cited within)
- QUADAS-2: Whiting et al. 2011; QUADAS-C: Emparanza et al. 2021

## 7. Glossary

- **OLT (Organoleptic Test)**: Gold-standard subjective breath odor assessment scored 0–5 by trained examiner
- **VSC (Volatile Sulfur Compounds)**: H₂S, CH₃SH, (CH₃)₂S — primary malodorous gases in oral halitosis
- **Halimeter**: Electrochemical sensor measuring total VSC concentration (ppb)
- **OralChroma**: Portable gas chromatograph measuring three VSCs individually
- **GC (Gas Chromatography)**: Bench-top reference method for precise VSC quantification
- **eNose**: Electronic nose — multi-sensor array for breath analysis
- **Spearman c.c.**: Rank-based correlation coefficient used for non-normally distributed halitosis data
- **SROC (Summary ROC)**: Meta-analytic ROC curve pooling sensitivity/specificity across studies
- **QUADAS-2/QUADAS-C**: Quality assessment tools for diagnostic accuracy studies
- **Type 1 halitosis**: Halitosis of intra-oral origin (76–90% of all halitosis cases)
- **PPV/NPV**: Positive/Negative Predictive Value — post-test probability of disease
