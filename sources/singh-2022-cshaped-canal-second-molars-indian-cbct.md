## Why Ingested

Adds an Indian-subpopulation (Greater Noida) CBCT data point and uniquely covers both maxillary and mandibular second molars, reporting a notably high overall C-shaped prevalence (22%) that contrasts with the low Israeli/Iranian sibling figures — useful tension for the prevalence cluster. It reinforces the C-shaped morphology baseline of [[endodontics/anatomy/song-2008-cross-sectional-morphology-and-minimum-canal]] and pairs with sibling prevalence studies [[endodontics/anatomy/shemesh-2017-cshaped-canals-cbct-mandibular-molars]], [[endodontics/anatomy/shekarian-2023-cshaped-canals-three-rooted-mandibular-iranian]], and [[endodontics/anatomy/yu-2012-cbct-mandibular-premolars-root-canal-morphology]].

## One-line Summary

CBCT cross-sectional study (500 Indian patients, Greater Noida): overall C-shaped canal prevalence 22% in second molars (maxillary 25.1% vs mandibular 18.9%, NS); most common configuration continuous/Category 1 (52.7%), then semicolon (37.3%) and separate (10%).

## 1. Document Information

- **Journal**: Journal of Conservative Dentistry, 2022, 25(5):531–535
- **Study type**: Cross-sectional CBCT prevalence study
- **Population**: Indian subpopulation (Greater Noida), 500 patients (292 M / 208 F), age 15–40 (mean 29.95), scans 2016–2019
- **Sample**: 500 CBCT records containing maxillary and mandibular second molars
- **Device**: CS 9300, 0.18 mm isotropic voxel, FOV 10×5; Melton et al. classification

## 2. Key Contributions

- Indian-subpopulation C-shaped prevalence for second molars — fills a noted gap in Indian data.
- Reports both maxillary and mandibular second molars in the same cohort (maxillary slightly higher, not significant).
- Distribution of C-shaped configuration by Melton category, with significant difference among configuration types.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- 500 archival CBCT records (CS 9300, 0.18 mm voxel) reviewed; ages 15–40.
- Inclusion: maxillary/mandibular second molars with fully formed apices, no resorption; excluded prior RCT, immature/open apices, developmental disorders, pathologies, restorations below pulp roof.
- C-shaped classified per Melton et al.: Category 1 continuous, Category 2 semicolon, Category 3 separate/discrete.
- Two calibrated endodontists evaluated twice (2-month interval); radiology specialist arbitrated disagreements; χ² tests.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Overall C-shaped prevalence: 22%** (110/500 patients).
- By jaw: **maxillary second molar 25.1% (62)** vs **mandibular second molar 18.9% (48)** — difference NOT significant (χ²=3.87, P=0.276).
- Configuration distribution (of 110 C-shaped): continuous/Category 1 **52.7% (58)**, semicolon/Category 2 **37.3% (41)**, separate/Category 3 **10% (11)** — difference significant (χ²=8.26, P=0.024).
- Conclusion: overall C-shaped prevalence 22%, continuous configuration most common; no significant jaw-type difference.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Prevalence reported per patient (110/500) rather than per tooth, complicating direct cross-study comparison.
- Higher figure than several other populations may reflect classification/counting differences and selection.
- Single-center archival sample, narrow age band (15–40).

## 6. Related Work

- Melton et al. — C-shaped configuration classification (Categories 1–3) used here.
- Cooke & Cox 1979 — original description of the C-shaped canal system.
- Fan et al. 2004 — alternative C-shaped anatomical classification (used by sibling studies).

## 7. Glossary

- **C-shaped canal**: Canals linked by a continuous slit/fin on the pulp-chamber floor, often a single ribbon-shaped orifice with ≥180° arc; cross-section varies along the root.
- **Melton classification**: Category 1 continuous C, Category 2 semicolon (dentin separating a distinct canal), Category 3 separate/discrete canals.
- **Continuous (Category 1)**: An uninterrupted C-shaped outline without separation — the most common configuration in this cohort.
