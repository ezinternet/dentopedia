## One-line Summary
In-vitro study: UV-PF of machined Ti → superhydrophilic surface → oral bacterial (polymicrobial) attachment and biofilm formation significantly reduced; bactericidal effect absent; liquid-storage maintains anti-adhesion function.

## 1. Document Information
Biomaterials 2015;67:84-92. doi:10.1016/j.biomaterials.2015.07.030. UCLA + UNESP. NIHMS preprint 709360.

## 2. Key Contributions
- First study on UV-PF effect on oral polymicrobial biofilm (with saliva + blood components)
- Demonstrates anti-adhesion mechanism: superhydrophilicity reduces bacterial attachment (not bactericidal)
- Liquid-storage post-UV maintains anti-adhesion function — clinically relevant for delayed use
- Community composition shifts initially but converges as biofilm matures — UV-PF delays but does not permanently prevent biofilm

## 3. Methodology
Machined Ti disks, UV-treated (254 nm, 15 min) or control. Human polymicrobial oral biofilm (DGGE, DNA sequencing). Salivary pellicle + blood component conditions. Bacterial attachment at 2h; biofilm at 24h.

## 4. Key Results
- Bacterial attachment: significantly reduced on UV-treated vs control
- Biofilm formation (24h): significantly reduced on UV-treated
- Bacterial viability: NOT affected (UV-PF ≠ bactericidal)
- Liquid storage (UV-treated in PBS): anti-adhesion function maintained
- Microbial community profiles: different at attachment phase; convergent at biofilm phase

## 5. Limitations
In vitro; machined Ti only; no microrough surface; long-term in vivo anti-biofilm effect not assessed; community convergence at biofilm stage limits long-term peri-implantitis prevention claims.

## 6. Related Work
Park 2025 (UV-PF review including bacterial interface), Minamikawa 2016 (aging mechanism), Hirota 2020 (clinical UV-PF outcomes).

## 7. Glossary
Polymicrobial biofilm: multi-species bacterial community; DGGE: denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis — microbial community profiling; superhydrophilic: contact angle ≈0°.
