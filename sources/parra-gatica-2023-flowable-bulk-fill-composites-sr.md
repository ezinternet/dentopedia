## One-line Summary
Systematic review of 53 in vitro studies on flowable bulk-fill resin composites reporting wide property variation: DC 19.41–93.71%, polymerization shrinkage 1.26–10.45%, shrinkage stress 2–3 MPa (most studies), flexural strength >80 MPa for most materials.

## 1. Document Information
- **Type**: Systematic Review (in vitro studies)
- **Published**: 2023 in Biomaterial Investigations in Dentistry (Vol. 10, No. 1)
- **Protocol**: PRISMA guidelines; QUIN risk-of-bias tool
- **Search databases**: Medline (PubMed), Scopus, Web of Science
- **Search language/date restrictions**: English only; ≤10 years of publication

## 2. Key Contributions
- First comprehensive SR focused specifically on flowable (low-viscosity) BF-RBCs, distinguishing them from high-viscosity sculptable composites
- Clarified that the "bulk-fill" label is applied inconsistently by manufacturers to a heterogeneous group of materials
- Two fundamental requirements for BF-RBC classification: (1) adequate degree of monomer conversion (DC) throughout the material; (2) ability to compensate/dissipate polymerization shrinkage stress at cavity margins
- Demonstrated that nanofillers improve mechanical properties of flowable BF-RBCs while maintaining low viscosity and self-leveling capacity

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- **Search**: PubMed, Scopus, WOS — 684 articles initially identified; 53 included after screening
- **Inclusion criteria**: In vitro studies reporting DC, polymerization shrinkage/shrinkage stress, and/or flexural strength of flowable BF-RBCs; English only; ≤10 years old; specified light-curing exposure time; light-cured materials only (no dual-cure excluded)
- **Risk of bias**: QUIN tool — moderate RoB observed in most studies
- **Outcome variables**: Degree of conversion (DC), polymerization shrinkage, shrinkage stress, flexural strength

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **Degree of conversion (DC)**: Range 19.41–93.71% — extremely wide variation across products
- **Polymerization shrinkage**: 1.26–10.45% — also highly variable
- **Polymerization shrinkage stress**: Most studies reported 2–3 MPa
- **Flexural strength**: Above 80 MPa for most materials (meeting typical mechanical requirements)
- DC achievement strategies: increased translucency; alternative/more efficient photoinitiators than camphorquinone
- Shrinkage stress reduction strategies: modified monomer chemistry; improved monomer–filler particle interaction
- Flowable BF-RBCs meet requirements for bulk-fill technique in posterior region, but heterogeneity prevents generalizing results across products

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- All included studies are in vitro — no clinical RCTs evaluated
- Moderate risk of bias in most studies limits certainty of conclusions
- Extremely wide ranges for DC and shrinkage make product-specific behavior difficult to predict
- Authors explicitly call for clinical studies to assess flowable BF-RBC performance under real working conditions
- Manufacturer inconsistency in applying "bulk-fill" label creates classification confusion

## 6. Related Work
- Boaro et al. 2019 meta-analysis on bulk-fill clinical and physico-chemical properties
- Silva et al. 2023 umbrella review comparing bulk-fill vs. conventional resins
- Meereis et al. 2018 systematic review on polymerization shrinkage stress composition strategies

## 7. Glossary
- **Flowable BF-RBC**: Low-viscosity bulk-fill resin-based composite; requires capping layer of sculptable composite
- **DC (Degree of Conversion)**: Percentage of C=C double bonds converted to C–C single bonds during polymerization
- **Polymerization shrinkage stress**: Stress developed at the bonded tooth-restoration interface as material contracts during cure
- **Camphorquinone**: Traditional photoinitiator in dental composites; alternative initiators used in some BF-RBCs for deeper cure
- **Self-leveling capacity**: Property of flowable composites to adapt to cavity margins without manipulation
- **QUIN tool**: Quality assessment tool for in vitro dental studies
