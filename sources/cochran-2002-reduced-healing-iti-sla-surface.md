## Why Ingested

Foundational early-loading evidence for the SLA surface: this is the landmark prospective multicenter trial that first established that ITI SLA solid-screw implants can be safely restored at ~6 weeks, providing the historical baseline against which later hydrophilic/modified-surface accelerated-loading protocols are judged. Reinforces [[implants/surface/kim-2016-hydrophilic-tapered-implant-6week-12week-loading]] (which tests a 6-week vs 12-week split on a modern hydrophilic surface) and anchors the surface-treatment timeline narrated in [[implants/surface/abraham-2014-historical-perspective-dental-implant-surfaces-treatments]].

## One-line Summary

Prospective multicenter clinical trial (6 centers, 4 countries; 326 SLA ITI implants in 110 patients) showing 4.1 mm SLA solid-screw implants can be restored at ~6 weeks (~12 weeks for poor-quality class IV bone) with 99.3% abutment-placement success and 99.1% life-table implant success at 1 and 2 years.

## 2. Key Contributions

- First prospective human clinical-trial evidence that the SLA (sandblasted, large-grit, acid-etched) surface permits a **reduced healing time** (~6 weeks) before restoration, versus the longer healing previously used with TPS-surface implants.
- Established a **bone-quality–stratified protocol**: reduced ~6-week healing only for healthy patients with sufficient bone volume and good bone quality (Lekholm–Zarb classes I–III); class IV (poor quality) sites waited ~12 weeks.
- Reported very high early success (>99% at 2 years), supplying the historical efficacy benchmark for SLA early loading.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Prospective, ongoing multicenter trial — 6 centers across 4 countries, planned 5-year follow-up.
- **Implants**: 4.1 mm diameter ITI® solid-screw implants with an SLA endosseous surface (one-stage, non-submerged ITI system).
- **Inclusion / protocol restriction for reduced healing time**:
  - (a) healthy patients with sufficient bone volume to surround the implant, AND
  - (b) good bone quality (classes I–III) at the recipient site → restored as early as ~6 weeks.
  - Patients with poorer bone quality (class IV) were NOT restored until ~12 weeks after placement.
- **Primary outcome variable**: successful abutment placement under a **35 Ncm tightening force with no countertorque** and no pain or rotation of the implant.
- **Secondary outcome (implant success)**: no mobility, no persistent pain or infection, and no peri-implant radiolucency.
- **Analysis**: life-table (actuarial) survival/success analysis (Cutler–Ederer method cited).
- **Surface rationale (background)**: cited cell-culture and animal data show the SLA surface stimulates bone-cell differentiation and protein production, yields large bone-to-implant contact, and produces high removal-torque values in functional testing.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Cohort completing recall**: 110 patients / 326 implants completed the 1-year post-loading recall; 47 patients / 138 implants completed the 2-year recall.
- **Early losses**: 3 implants lost prior to abutment connection.
- **Restored after shortened healing**: prosthetic restoration commenced on **307 implants** after shortened healing.
- **Abutment-placement success**: **99.3%** for these implants, with an **average healing time of 49 days** (~7 weeks).
- **Life-table implant success**: **99.1%** — for 329 implants at 1 year and for 138 implants at 2 years.
- **24-month post-restoration period**: no implant losses reported for the 138 implants.
- **Conclusion**: under defined conditions, solid-screw ITI implants with an SLA endosseous surface can be restored after ~6 weeks of healing with high predictability (abutment placement at 35 Ncm without countertorque) and subsequent implant success >99% at 2 years post-restoration.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Interim / early results** of an ongoing trial — 5-year follow-up not yet complete at publication; 2-year data limited to a subset (138 implants).
- **Single implant type/diameter**: only 4.1 mm ITI solid-screw SLA implants; results may not generalize to other diameters, designs, or surfaces.
- **Restricted patient selection**: healthy patients, sufficient bone volume, and class I–III bone for the 6-week arm — class IV deliberately excluded from the reduced-time arm — so the protocol is not validated for compromised hosts or poor-quality bone at 6 weeks.
- **Surrogate primary endpoint**: success defined by abutment placement at 35 Ncm without countertorque (a torque-stability proxy) rather than long-term marginal bone level or patient-reported outcomes.
- **No control arm in the abstract** comparing reduced vs conventional healing within the same study; comparison is implicit/historical.
- Full Methods/Results detail not recoverable from this abstract-only PDF.

## 7. Glossary

- **SLA (Sandblasted, Large-grit, Acid-etched)**: a moderately rough titanium implant surface produced by large-grit blasting followed by acid etching; promotes osteoblast differentiation and bone-to-implant contact.
- **TPS (Titanium Plasma-Sprayed)**: the older ITI bone-anchoring surface, compared against SLA.
- **ITI solid-screw implant**: a one-stage (non-submerged) screw-type endosseous implant of the ITI Dental Implant System.
- **Reduced healing time**: shortening the interval between implant placement and prosthetic restoration (here ~6 weeks vs the longer conventional intervals).
- **Lekholm–Zarb bone quality (classes I–IV)**: classification of jawbone density/quality; class IV is the poorest (thin cortex, low-density trabecular bone).
- **Abutment placement at 35 Ncm without countertorque**: clinical stability test — tightening the abutment to 35 Ncm without holding/counter-torquing the implant; no rotation or pain indicates osseointegration.
- **Life-table (actuarial) analysis**: survival-analysis method (Cutler–Ederer) accounting for varying follow-up durations across implants.
- **Removal/reverse torque**: the torque required to unscrew an osseointegrated implant; a functional measure of bone anchorage strength.
