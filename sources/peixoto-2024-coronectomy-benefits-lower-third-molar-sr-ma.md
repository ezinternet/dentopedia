## One-line Summary
Systematic review and meta-analysis demonstrating that coronectomy significantly reduces inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) injury risk compared to complete extraction, with acceptable reoperation rates and favorable root migration outcomes.

## 1. Document Information
- **Journal**: Journal of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery 2024;82(1):73-92
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.joms.2023.07.016
- **Type**: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis
- **Year**: 2024

## 2. Key Contributions
- First comprehensive meta-analysis comparing coronectomy vs. complete extraction specifically regarding IAN injury rates in high-risk mandibular third molar cases
- Quantifies the reduction in IAN injury risk with coronectomy
- Evaluates root migration patterns post-coronectomy and their clinical significance
- Assesses reoperation (root retrieval) rates and indications
- Provides evidence-based guidance for surgical decision-making in patients with close IAN proximity

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
- Systematic review following PRISMA 2020 guidelines
- Search in PubMed, Embase, Cochrane, Scopus, Web of Science
- Included: RCTs and prospective/retrospective controlled studies comparing coronectomy vs. complete extraction in mandibular third molars with high IAN proximity risk (CBCT-confirmed or panoramic high-risk criteria)
- Outcomes: IAN injury (permanent/transient), reoperation rate, root migration, infection, dry socket
- Meta-analysis with random-effects model; heterogeneity assessed via I²

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
- **IAN injury**: Coronectomy significantly reduced IAN injury risk (OR markedly favoring coronectomy; permanent IAN injury nearly eliminated vs. complete extraction)
- **Reoperation rate**: Root retrieval required in approximately 5–12% of coronectomy cases; most cases uneventful
- **Root migration**: Retained roots migrate coronally over time (average 2–3 mm over 2 years) but rarely cause clinical problems; most roots remain subosseous
- **Infection**: No significant difference in postoperative infection rates between coronectomy and complete extraction
- **Dry socket**: Comparable rates between groups
- Overall complication profile favors coronectomy in high-risk cases

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
- Variability in inclusion criteria for "high-risk" IAN proximity across studies
- Heterogeneity in surgical technique (amount of crown removal, bone coverage protocol)
- Long-term follow-up data on root migration >5 years limited
- Need for standardized CBCT-based criteria defining "high-risk" cases

## 6. Related Work
- Follows earlier systematic reviews by Cervera-Espert et al. (2016) and Povo et al. (2021) on coronectomy
- Relates to IAN injury management literature (Lee 2015, Sarikov 2014, Li 2022)
- Informs surgical indications guidelines for third molar management

## 7. Glossary
- **Coronectomy**: Intentional partial odontectomy removing only the crown of a mandibular third molar, leaving roots in situ when they are in close proximity to the IAN
- **IAN (Inferior Alveolar Nerve)**: Major sensory nerve running in the mandibular canal; injury causes numbness/paresthesia of lower lip and chin
- **Root Migration**: Spontaneous coronal movement of retained roots after coronectomy; typically 2–3 mm over 2 years
- **Reoperation**: Subsequent surgical retrieval of retained roots after coronectomy, indicated for infection, symptomatic roots, or migration into prosthetic site
- **High-risk criteria**: Radiographic features (darkening of root, narrowing of canal, deflection of canal) indicating close IAN-root relationship
