## Why Ingested

Comprehensive physiology-grounded narrative review of oral local anesthesia in pregnancy — gives the mechanistic "why" (cardiovascular/respiratory/hepatic/renal changes, placental transfer, FDA categories) behind the agent-selection conclusions of [[wiki/drug/uritu-2025-vasoconstrictor-dental-anesthesia-pregnancy-sr]]. Both converge on 2% lidocaine + 1:200,000 epinephrine as the best maternal/fetal balance, so this review reinforces the SR with physiological rationale.

## One-line Summary

Narrative review (West China Hospital of Stomatology, 182 studies included from 546 screened): standard oral local anesthesia is safe throughout pregnancy; 2% lidocaine with 1:200,000 epinephrine best balances maternal/fetal safety and efficacy; lidocaine and prilocaine are FDA category B (safe) while articaine/mepivacaine/bupivacaine are category C (caution); best elective timing is weeks 14–20 (second trimester), with semi-supine positioning and BP monitoring for high-risk mothers.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **Systems-level physiology map** of pregnancy changes that affect local anesthesia: cardiovascular (CO ↑50%, HR ↑20–30%, hypotension/supine syndrome ~10%), respiratory (hyperventilation, ↓20% residual lung volume, hypoxemia risk), hepatic (↓albumin/AAG → ↑free drug fraction), renal (↑GFR → ↑lidocaine clearance), endocrine (gestational diabetes ~45%, hypoglycemic syncope), and neuropsychological (heightened pain sensitivity, faster conduction block onset).
2. **Agent-selection conclusion**: 2% lidocaine + 1:200,000 epinephrine = best safety/efficacy balance. Lidocaine preferred over prilocaine because prilocaine's higher concentration (4% vs 2%) delivers more drug per injection.
3. **FDA category table** for dental local anesthetics.
4. **Procedural prophylaxis**: optimal elective timing weeks 14–20; semi-supine / left-tilt positioning; aspirate before injection; lowest effective dose; topical pre-anesthesia (lidocaine/prilocaine only, both category B).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Narrative (non-systematic) review across maternal/fetal physiology, local-anesthetic pharmacology, and oral-treatment application.
- Boolean keyword search (pregnancy, maternal, fetal, anesthesia, epinephrine, lidocaine, etc.) on Medline/Embase/Cochrane; gray literature and no-full-text excluded.
- 546 preliminary → 237 full-text → 182 included.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**FDA pregnancy categories for dental LAs** (FDA 1979 classification):

| Agent | FDA category | Use in pregnancy |
|---|---|---|
| Lidocaine | B | Safe (preferred) |
| Prilocaine | B | Safe |
| Articaine | C | Use with caution |
| Mepivacaine | C | Use with caution |
| Bupivacaine | C | Use with caution |
| Benzocaine | C | Use with caution |

**Epinephrine**: FDA category C (teratogenic in rabbits/mice/hamsters at organogenesis dosing). Use only if benefit justifies risk. 1:200,000 favored over 1:80,000 — efficacy difference not significant (cites Karm 2017/2018, Aggarwal 2020) but lower acute cardiovascular reaction risk. Higher concentrations (1:50,000) give no faster onset/longer duration.

**Maximum doses (ADA/FDA)**: lidocaine plain ≤4.5 mg/kg (max 300 mg); lidocaine + epinephrine ≤7 mg/kg (max 500 mg); epinephrine ≤0.2 mg healthy, ≤0.04 mg cardiac patients.

**Key physiological numbers**: hypertension clinically evident in ~8% of pregnancies (2nd trimester); supine hypotension syndrome ~10%; gingivitis 60–75% (3rd trimester); pyogenic granuloma 3–10%; pulpitis lidocaine block success only ~67%; IANB failure 20–47% in symptomatic irreversible pulpitis.

**Pain-reduction adjuncts**: sodium bicarbonate buffering (raises pH → more nonionized base → faster onset, less sting; some trials positive, some null; not FDA-approved as alkalizer → use with caution); warming (↓pKa → ↑free base); precooling injection site (A-δ fiber stimulation, analgesia ≈ 5% lidocaine gel).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- **Narrative review** — no systematic risk-of-bias assessment; conclusions reflect synthesized expert reasoning, not pooled effect estimates.
- FDA's pre-2015 letter-category system (A–X) is cited; this framework was officially retired by the FDA Pregnancy and Lactation Labeling Rule (2015), so category labels are heuristic, not current regulatory language.
- Clonidine proposed as an epinephrine alternative (improves IANB success in irreversible pulpitis, lower CV risk) but evidence base is limited/off-label.
- New topical formulations (liposomes, mucoadhesive patches, 3D-printed lidocaine patches) flagged as promising but understudied for pregnancy.

## 6. Related Work

- Karm 2017/2018 — 2% lidocaine 1:80,000 vs 1:200,000 epinephrine equivalence (cited as basis for the 1:200,000 recommendation).
- Aggarwal 2020 — 1:80,000 vs 1:200,000 epinephrine.
- ACOG 2017 — no anesthetic at standard concentration shown teratogenic in humans at any gestational age.
- [[wiki/drug/uritu-2025-vasoconstrictor-dental-anesthesia-pregnancy-sr]] — systematic review on vasoconstrictors in pregnancy; this review supplies the physiological mechanism behind that SR's conclusions.
- [[wiki/drug/karm-2017-lidocaine-epinephrine-200000-third-molar]] — the RCT-level evidence this review cites for the 1:200,000 epinephrine choice.

## 7. Glossary

- **FDA category B/C**: pre-2015 FDA pregnancy risk letter categories (B = no human risk demonstrated; C = animal teratogenicity, inadequate human data).
- **Supine hypotension syndrome**: aortocaval compression by gravid uterus → ↓venous return → hypotension/syncope; managed by semi-supine/left-tilt position.
- **GFR**: glomerular filtration rate (↑ in pregnancy → ↑renal clearance of lidocaine).
- **AAG**: alpha-1 acid glycoprotein (binds local anesthetic; ↓ in pregnancy → ↑free drug).
- **pKa / Henderson–Hasselbalch**: determines ionized vs nonionized fraction of local anesthetic; nonionized base crosses nerve/placental membranes.
- **IANB**: inferior alveolar nerve block.
