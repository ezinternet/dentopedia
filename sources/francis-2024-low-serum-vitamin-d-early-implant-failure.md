## Why Ingested

This is the contrarian / negative-evidence anchor for the vitamin D → implant-failure subdomain. Unlike the consensus signal in the systematic-review and the strongly-positive prospective findings of Mohsen 2024, this cohort found NO correlation between low serum vitamin D and early implant failure — and notably the failed implants occurred in patients with a *higher* mean serum vitamin D (42.54 ng/mL) than the successful patients (31.92 ng/mL). Holding this paper keeps the subdomain balanced and guards overview synthesis against an overstated, one-directional causal claim. It contrasts with [[implants/vitamin-d/mohsen-2024-vitamin-d-deficiency-osseointegration-prospective]], complements the failure-mechanism literature in [[implants/vitamin-d/miron-2025-vitamin-d-deficiency-early-implant-failure]], and is anchored against the foundational risk-factor reference [[implants/moy-2005-dental-implant-failure-rates-risk]].

## One-line Summary

Prospective cohort (174 implants / 109 patients) found no statistically significant correlation between low serum vitamin D and early dental implant failure; the 8 failed cases even had a higher mean serum vitamin D (42.54 ng/mL) than successful patients (31.92 ng/mL).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides a negative / null result in a literature otherwise tilted toward a positive vitamin D → osseointegration association.
- Reports the counterintuitive observation that early-failure cases had a higher mean serum vitamin D (42.54 ng/mL) than successful cases (31.92 ng/mL).
- Adds direct, day-of-placement serum measurement linked to a clearly defined failure endpoint (≥50% bone loss or implant mobility) followed to restoration (~3–6 months).

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- Design: Prospective cohort.
- Sample: 174 implants in 109 patients.
- Exposure measurement: Serum vitamin D level recorded on the day of implant placement.
- Follow-up: Until restoration, approximately 3–6 months after placement.
- Failure definition (outcome): Early implant failure reported on the basis of ≥50% bone loss or implant mobility.
- Analysis: Comparison of mean serum vitamin D between failed and successful patients; correlation between low serum vitamin D and early failure assessed.
- (abstract-only — full text not retrieved; detailed statistical methods and covariate adjustment not available)

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- Early failures: 8 individuals had an implant fail early.
- Mean serum vitamin D, failed cases: 42.54 ng/mL.
- Mean serum vitamin D, successful patients: 31.92 ng/mL.
- The difference was not statistically significant.
- Conclusion: No correlation between patients' low serum vitamin D levels and early implant failure.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Abstract-only — full text not retrieved from PMC; statistical detail, confidence intervals, and covariate handling are unavailable.
- Small number of failure events (n = 8), limiting statistical power to detect an association.
- Single-center cohort; generalizability and confounder control unclear from the abstract.
- Direction of the mean difference (higher vitamin D in failures) may reflect chance given the small failure count rather than a true protective-absence signal.

## 7. Glossary

- Serum vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D, 25(OH)D): circulating storage form of vitamin D, measured here in ng/mL on the day of placement.
- Early dental implant failure (EDIF): implant failure occurring before/at restoration, defined here by ≥50% bone loss or implant mobility.
- Osseointegration: direct structural and functional connection between living bone and an implant surface, the biologic basis of implant success.
