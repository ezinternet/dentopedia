## Why Ingested

The wiki holds the same group's extraction-pain NMA ([[wiki/drug/miroshnychenko-2023-dental-extraction-pain-network-meta]]) but lacked the upstream paired SRs that informed the ADA acute-dental-pain guideline on *which local anesthetic*. This pair (long- vs short-acting injectables; benzocaine topicals) directly extends [[wiki/drug/karm-2017-lidocaine-epinephrine-200000-third-molar]] (lidocaine/epinephrine choice) and complements the agent pharmacology in [[wiki/drug/becker-2012-local-anesthetics-pharmacological-considerations]] (bupivacaine duration, benzocaine ester topical).

## One-line Summary

Two GRADE-rated SRs informing the ADA acute-dental-pain CPG: SR1 (14 RCTs) finds long-acting bupivacaine probably reduces analgesic consumption vs lidocaine+epinephrine (MD −1.91 doses, 95% CI −3.35 to −0.46) and vs mepivacaine (MD −1.58 doses, −2.21 to −0.95); SR2 (5 RCTs) finds 20% benzocaine increases pain-reduction responders vs placebo (RR 1.47, 1.03–2.10) in symptomatic irreversible pulpitis.

- **Authors**: Miroshnychenko, Ibrahim, Azab, Roldan, Diaz Martinez, Tamilselvan, He, Urquhart, Tampi, Polk, Moore, Hersh, Carrasco-Labra, Brignardello-Petersen (McMaster / ADA SRI / U Michigan / U Pittsburgh / U Penn)
- **Journal**: Journal of the American Dental Association (JADA) 2023;154(1):53–64.e14
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.adaj.2022.10.014
- **Type**: Two systematic reviews with random-effects meta-analysis (PRISMA; GRADE); evidence base for the ADA/ADA-SRI/FDA acute-dental-pain clinical practice guideline.
- **Open access**: CC BY-NC-ND.

## 2. Key Contributions

1. **SR1**: head-to-head long-acting (bupivacaine) vs short-acting injectables (lidocaine+epinephrine, articaine, mepivacaine) for post-extraction acute pain — 14 RCTs, GRADE-rated.
2. **SR2**: topical benzocaine (10%, 20%) vs placebo / head-to-head for symptomatic irreversible pulpitis — 5 RCTs.
3. Quantified, agent-specific MDs for analgesic consumption and time-to-analgesic — the granular data the ADA CPG used for its bupivacaine and benzocaine statements.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Databases**: MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane CENTRAL, US ClinicalTrials registry, inception → 21 Nov 2020; English only.
- **SR1 population**: adolescents/adults, simple or surgical (incl. impacted mandibular 3rd molar) extractions; outcomes = rescue analgesia use, time to analgesic consumption, amount of analgesic consumption, adverse effects.
- **SR2 population**: adolescents/adults with symptomatic irreversible pulpitis; outcomes = number of responders (≥2 consecutive reduced pain scores at 20–30 min), pain levels (SPRID at 60 min), adverse effects.
- **Synthesis**: random-effects MA (RevMan 5.4); RR for dichotomous, MD for continuous, RD when event-rate low; subgroup analysis by short-acting agent type for SR1 heterogeneity.
- **Risk of bias**: modified Cochrane RoB 2.0.
- **Certainty**: GRADE, minimally contextualized with null-effect threshold (10% baseline risk / 10% scale range).
- **Screening**: 4,716 titles → SR1 14 RCTs (n 24–252; 57% split-mouth), SR2 5 RCTs (parallel, all US, n 20–576).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

### SR1 — long-acting (bupivacaine) vs short-acting injectables

| Outcome | RCTs | Effect (95% CI) | Certainty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rescue analgesia (8–48 h) vs any short-acting | 8 | RR 0.48 (0.20–1.13) | low |
| Adverse effects vs any short-acting | 4 | RD 0.0% (−4% to +4%) | moderate |
| Time to analgesic vs **lidocaine+epi** | 1 | MD **+2.56 h** (2.07–3.05) | high |
| Time to analgesic vs **mepivacaine** | 1 | MD **+3.56 h** (2.39–4.73) | moderate |
| Time to analgesic vs articaine | 3 | — | very low |
| Amount of analgesic vs **lidocaine+epi** | 5 | MD **−1.91 doses** (−3.35 to −0.46) | moderate |
| Amount of analgesic vs **mepivacaine** (24 h) | — | MD **−1.58 doses** (−2.21 to −0.95) | moderate |
| Amount of analgesic vs articaine (1–4 d) | 2 | MD +0.22 doses (−0.13 to 0.57) | low |

### SR2 — topical benzocaine vs placebo / head-to-head (symptomatic irreversible pulpitis)

| Outcome | RCTs | Effect (95% CI) | Certainty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Responders: 20% benzocaine vs **placebo** | 3 | RR **1.47 (1.03–2.10)** | low |
| Responders: 10% benzocaine vs placebo | 2 | RR 1.38 (0.74–2.56) | low |
| Responders: 20% vs 10% benzocaine | 2 | RR 0.93 (0.86–1.00) (trivial) | moderate |
| Adverse effects: 20% vs 10% (90–120 min) | 2 | RD 0.0% (−3% to +3%) | low |

**Bottom line**: Bupivacaine may be superior to lidocaine+epinephrine and mepivacaine for time-to- and amount-of-analgesic consumption. Benzocaine (esp. 20%) may beat placebo for pain reduction at 20–30 min in irreversible pulpitis. 20% over 10% benzocaine confers only a trivial difference.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Low / very-low certainty for several key comparisons (rescue analgesia, bupivacaine-vs-articaine timing, benzocaine-vs-placebo responders).
- English-only inclusion.
- Frequent high RoB in randomization process and selective reporting domains.
- Heterogeneity in short-acting comparator types within SR1.
- Benzocaine evidence limited to short (20–30 min) temporizing relief in pulpitis — not a definitive treatment.

## 7. Glossary

- **Long-acting LA** — bupivacaine (high pKa 8.1, 95% protein binding, prolonged block).
- **Short-acting LA** — lidocaine+epinephrine, articaine, mepivacaine.
- **MD** — mean difference (absolute effect); here "doses" = number of rescue-analgesic doses.
- **RD** — risk difference.
- **SPRID** — sum of pain relief + pain intensity difference.
- **Responder** — participant with reduced pain score at ≥2 consecutive 20–30 min assessments.
- **Benzocaine** — ester-class topical anesthetic.
