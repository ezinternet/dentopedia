## Why Ingested
The dental epidemiology anchor: it quantifies how often and why dental practitioners are complained about, and the heavy concentration of complaints in a few practitioners. Grounds the general pipeline in dentistry and informs risk-targeting for a clinic. Links to [[practice-management/ha-2023-dentist-issues-constitutional-court-decisions]].

## One-line Summary
National Australian cohort (2011-2016) showing dentists have the highest complaint rate of 14 health professions (42.7/1000/yr); 4% of dentists generate 49% of complaints; most concern treatment.

- Australian Dental Journal 2018;63(3):285-293.

## 2. Key Contributions
- National-scale quantification of dental complaint frequency, nature, and risk factors.
- Shows dental practitioners are ~3.5% of practitioners but ~10% of complaints.
- Documents extreme concentration: 4% of dentists account for 49% of complaints about dentists.
- Identifies male sex as a risk factor and treatments/procedures (59%) as the leading issue.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
National dataset of complaints about registered health practitioners in Australia, Jan 2011-Dec 2016. Complaints classified into 23 issues across three domains (health, performance, conduct). Complaint rates for dental vs other practitioners compared; negative binomial regression identified factors associated with complaints.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
Dental practitioners = 3.5% of practitioners but ~10% of complaints; highest rate of 14 professions at 42.7 per 1000 practitioners/year (dentists and prosthetists > allied dental). Male practitioners at higher risk. 59% of dentist complaints concerned treatments/procedures. ~4% of dentists received >1 complaint, accounting for 49% of complaints about dentists. 60% of closed cases needed no regulatory action; ~13% resulted in restrictive actions (e.g., conditions on practice).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
Australian regulatory data (AHPRA); regulation/culture-specific, not directly transferable to Korean medico-legal context. Complaint counts reflect reporting/regulatory behaviour, not pure clinical-error incidence.

## 6. Related Work
Dental-specific instance of the general complaint epidemiology; complements medico-legal analyses (Vadde 2024) and endodontic malpractice (Alrahabi 2019).

## 7. Glossary
- **AHPRA**: Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency.
- **Negative binomial regression**: count-model for over-dispersed complaint counts.
- **Restrictive action**: regulatory condition limiting a practitioner's practice.
