## Why Ingested

"고혈압약 등 약물에 의한 미각상실·침분비저하" 요청에 대응. 기존 [[geriatric-dentistry/halasabalu-kalgeri-2026-polypharmacy-oral-health-elderly]]가 항고혈압제(ACE inhibitor·β-blocker 등)를 다약제복용의 여러 약물군 중 하나로 개괄했다면, 본 체계적 문헌고찰은 항고혈압제 단독의 xerostomia/hyposalivation 영향만을 13편(RCT 5·환자대조군 8)으로 정밀 검증 — 그리고 놀랍게도 "항고혈압제가 미각/침분비를 더 저하시킨다는 확증적 근거는 부족하다"는 반직관적 결론을 제공해 다약제복용 논문의 단정적 서술에 균형추 역할을 한다.

## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA, 13 studies: 5 clinical trials + 8 case-control) finding the evidence for antihypertensive drugs causing more xerostomia/hyposalivation than controls is inconclusive and of low methodological quality overall — clinical trials show mixed/non-significant salivary flow changes, while case-control studies more consistently (though not uniformly) show lower salivary flow in antihypertensive-treated patients, with no single drug class identifiable as most xerogenic due to study heterogeneity.

## 한줄요약

체계적 문헌고찰(PRISMA, 13편: RCT 5편+환자대조군 8편) — 항고혈압제 복용군이 대조군보다 미각/침분비저하(xerostomia/hyposalivation)가 더 심하다는 확증적 근거는 부족하며 전반적 방법론적 질도 낮음; 임상시험은 침분비량 변화가 혼재되고 유의성 없음이 많았으나, 환자대조군 연구는(완전히 일관되지는 않지만) 대체로 항고혈압제군의 침분비량이 낮았음; 연구 이질성으로 어떤 약물군이 가장 침분비저하를 유발하는지 특정 불가.

## 1. Document Information

- Journal: International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health, 2020;17(7):2478
- Publication date: 2020-04-05
- Design: systematic review per PRISMA guidelines; no meta-analysis (heterogeneity too great)
- Search cutoff: November 19, 2019 (4 databases: PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, CINAHL, Cochrane Library)

## 2. Key Contributions

- The first (and to the authors' knowledge only) systematic review specifically isolating antihypertensive drugs as a class and testing three focused PICO-style questions: (1) more xerostomia/hyposalivation vs. controls? (2) lower salivary flow vs. controls? (3) which antihypertensive class is most xerogenic?
- Delivers a counterintuitive, conservative conclusion: current literature CANNOT confirm antihypertensive-treated patients have more xerostomia/hyposalivation than untreated patients — directly tempering the common clinical assumption (echoed in broader polypharmacy reviews) that antihypertensives are reliably xerogenic.
- Separately analyzes clinical-trial vs. case-control evidence, finding they point in somewhat different directions: clinical trials show mixed/mostly non-significant salivary flow changes (including one significant increase after captopril), while case-control studies more consistently (though not uniformly) show lower salivary flow in treated patients.
- Documents that NONE of the included studies used the Xerostomia Inventory — the only tool validated specifically for medication-induced salivary gland disorder assessment — highlighting a methodological gap in the underlying evidence base.
- Applies both Cochrane risk-of-bias (clinical trials) and modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale (case-control) tools, finding overall low-to-fair methodological quality across all 13 included studies.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: PRISMA-guided systematic review, three researchers (2 independent screeners + 1 arbiter), no meta-analysis due to heterogeneity.
- **Search**: PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, CINAHL, Cochrane Library, no start-date limit through November 19, 2019; combined terms (saliva, dry mouth, hyposalivation OR salivary flow) AND (hypertension, antihypertensive drug, OR antihypertensives); manual reference-list hand-search.
- **Inclusion**: original human studies (clinical trials, longitudinal, cross-sectional); antihypertensive-treated population compared against a control/placebo group without antihypertensive treatment, other disease, or other pharmacological treatment.
- **Outcome definitions**: xerostomia via questionnaire (single affirmative dry-mouth question sufficient) or quantified via VAS/Fox et al. questions/modified Xerostomia Inventory; hyposalivation via stimulated whole saliva (SWS) ≤0.5–0.7 mL/min or unstimulated whole saliva (UWS) ≤0.15–0.3 mL/min or modified Schirmer test.
- **Exclusion**: non-English/Spanish language, concomitant disease/other drugs, no placebo/CG comparator, non-numerical (graph-only) salivary flow data.
- **Screening**: 6201 records → 2905 after dedup → 26 full-text assessed → 13 included (5 clinical trials, 8 case-control studies).
- **Quality appraisal**: Cochrane Collaboration risk-of-bias tool (clinical trials); modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale (case-control), AHRQ good/fair/poor classification.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- 13 studies included: 5 clinical trials (largest sample n not specified as trial; overall sample range not pooled due to heterogeneity), 8 case-control studies (largest n=440 De la Luz et al.; smallest n=12 Nederfors et al.).
- Antihypertensive classes studied across included papers: β-adrenergic blockers, α-adrenergic blockers, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, heart glycosides, and centrally-acting antihypertensives.
- **Clinical trials**: only Nederfors et al. 2004 assessed xerostomia degree directly — thiazide/furosemide groups showed increased xerostomia vs. stable placebo. Only 1/5 clinical trials found a statistically significant UWS decrease (with α-/β-adrenergic blockers, propranolol+phentolamine); one trial found a statistically significant SPS (parotid) flow INCREASE after captopril (an ACE inhibitor).
- **Case-control studies**: xerostomia significantly more prevalent in antihypertensive-treated patients in both studies assessing it; hyposalivation (evaluated in 1 study, Nonzee et al. 2012) significantly greater in treated patients; UWS decreased in 5/6 studies (statistically significant in 3), SWS decreased in 4/5 studies (significant in 3), SPS significantly decreased with diuretics (1 study).
- **Overall verdict**: literature CANNOT confirm antihypertensive-treated patients have more xerostomia/hyposalivation than untreated patients when clinical trial evidence is weighted; case-control evidence leans toward lower salivary flow in treated patients but is confounded by low study quality and heterogeneity.
- No single antihypertensive class could be identified as most xerogenic due to heterogeneous study designs, outcome measures, and drug combinations across the 13 included studies.
- Quality assessment: all included studies rated poor-to-fair; clinical trials mostly "unclear" risk of bias due to undescribed methodological parameters; case-control studies scored 4–6 on modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale (5 poor, 3 fair).

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Author-acknowledged: language restriction to English/Spanish; exclusion of graph-only (non-numerical) salivary flow data; could not assess treatment-duration effects (case-control studies lacked duration data); excluded patients with concomitant disease/other medications, so combination-drug effects on salivary function remain unassessed.
- Available clinical trials are old and not current (most recent 2006, oldest 1981) — none used the Xerostomia Inventory (the only medication-induced-xerostomia-validated tool), and none calculated a target sample size.
- Authors explicitly call for long-term, adequately powered, standardized-dose RCTs directly comparing different antihypertensive classes against placebo/CG to determine which drug most reduces salivary flow, and for using validated xerostomia assessment tools (Xerostomia Inventory) in future studies.
- The lack of meta-analysis (due to heterogeneity) means pooled effect-size estimates are not available — the review's conclusions are qualitative/narrative rather than quantitative.

## 6. Related Work

- Cites Nonzee et al. 2012 (case-control study finding hyposalivation significantly greater in antihypertensive-treated hypertensive patients).
- Cites Nederfors et al. 2004 and De la Luz et al. as key clinical-trial/case-control anchor studies with the largest/most detailed xerostomia-specific data.
- Cites Thomson et al.'s Xerostomia Inventory as the field's only validated medication-induced-xerostomia assessment tool, and Navazesh's saliva-collection standardization methodology.

## 7. Glossary

- **UWS / SWS**: unstimulated / stimulated whole saliva flow rate — the most clinically relevant measures of overall salivary gland function (vs. individual-gland secretions, which are superior only for compositional/biochemical analysis).
- **SPS**: stimulated parotid saliva flow rate.
- **USS / SSS**: unstimulated / stimulated submandibular-sublingual saliva flow rate.
- **Xerostomia Inventory**: the only patient-reported outcome tool specifically validated for assessing medication-induced xerostomia severity (Sreebney and Fox, modified by De la Luz et al. in one included study — though not used in its originally validated form by any study in this review).
- **Modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale**: a risk-of-bias appraisal tool for case-control studies, scored on selection/comparability/outcome domains.
