## One-line Summary

Retrospective study of bisphosphonate-treated patients: alkaline phosphatase and ESR were significantly higher in the BRONJ group, and BRONJ associated with smoking, tooth extraction, anaemia and leukocytosis plus radiographic persisting alveolar socket, osteolysis, bone sclerosis and mandibular canal narrowing.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Cross-correlates three diagnostic domains — clinical, panoramic/periapical radiographic, and haematological/biochemical — within a single bisphosphonate cohort to identify which features distinguish bisphosphonate-related osteonecrosis of the jaw (BRONJ) from bisphosphonate users without osteonecrosis.
- Identifies a candidate laboratory signal (raised alkaline phosphatase and ESR) and confirms that the commonly cited bone-turnover marker CTX did **not** significantly differ between groups in this sample.
- Catalogues the radiographic signs that were significantly more prevalent in BRONJ versus those that were not, informing panoramic/periapical interpretation.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Retrospective analysis of records of patients taking bisphosphonates, grouped by occurrence vs non-occurrence of BRONJ.
- **Imaging**: Panoramic and periapical radiographs scored for presence/absence of — bone sclerosis, osteolysis, persisting alveolar socket, narrowing of the mandibular canal, widening of the periodontal ligament space, periradicular radiolucency, sequestrum, and thickening of the lamina dura.
- **Laboratory panel**: Complete blood count, fasting serum glucose, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), and serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, parathormone (PTH), and C-terminal telopeptide of collagen I (CTX).
- **Clinical**: Risk-factor features including smoking and tooth extraction recorded.
- (Sample size, age/sex distribution, bisphosphonate type/route/duration, and statistical methods are not stated in the available abstract.)

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

**Haematological / biochemical**
- Significantly higher in BRONJ group: **alkaline phosphatase**, **ESR**.
- No significant difference between groups: fasting serum glucose, **CTX**, PTH, calcium, phosphorus.
- BRONJ associated with **anaemia** and **leukocytosis** (from CBC).

**Clinical**
- BRONJ showed association with **smoking** and **tooth extraction**.

**Radiographic — significantly more prevalent in BRONJ**
- Persisting alveolar socket
- Osteolysis
- Bone sclerosis
- Narrowing of the mandibular canal

**Radiographic — no significant difference**
- Thickening of the lamina dura
- Periapical (periradicular) radiolucencies
- Widening of the periodontal ligament space
- Sequestrum

**Conclusion**: BRONJ is a multifactorial, high-morbidity disease; experimental studies are needed to clarify the role of the reported risk factors and clinical-radiographic signs to improve diagnosis.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Retrospective design — association, not causation; susceptible to selection and recording bias.
- Sample size, bisphosphonate exposure details, and statistical specifics not available in the abstract, limiting effect-size interpretation.
- CTX non-significance contrasts with its frequent clinical use as a risk-stratification marker; warrants caution in over-relying on a single bone-turnover marker.
- Authors call for experimental studies to clarify mechanistic roles of the reported signs/risk factors.

## 7. Glossary

- **BRONJ / MRONJ**: Bisphosphonate- (medication-) related osteonecrosis of the jaw.
- **ESR**: Erythrocyte sedimentation rate, an inflammation marker.
- **ALP**: Alkaline phosphatase, a bone-turnover/biochemical marker.
- **CTX**: C-terminal telopeptide of type I collagen, a bone-resorption marker sometimes used for MRONJ risk stratification.
- **PTH**: Parathormone (parathyroid hormone).
- **Persisting alveolar socket**: Non-healing extraction socket persisting radiographically beyond expected healing time.
