## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Periodontology 2000) of acute periodontal lesions — periodontal abscesses, necrotizing periodontal diseases, and other acute gingival conditions — covering etiology, classification, differential diagnosis, and emergency/definitive management.

## 1. Document Information

- **Title**: Acute periodontal lesions
- **Authors**: David Herrera, Bettina Alonso, Lorenzo de Arriba, Isabel Santa Cruz, Cristina Serrano, Mariano Sanz
- **Journal**: Periodontology 2000, Volume 65, Issue 1, pp. 149–177
- **Year**: 2014 (first published 17 April 2014)
- **DOI**: 10.1111/prd.12022
- **Type**: Narrative / state-of-the-art review (Original Article in a Periodontology 2000 thematic volume)
- **Scope**: Acute conditions affecting periodontal tissues, both biofilm-associated (abscesses, necrotizing periodontal diseases) and non–biofilm-associated (infectious, mucocutaneous, traumatic, allergic gingival lesions).

## 2. Key Contributions

- Provides an integrated update on the spectrum of **acute periodontal lesions** that present as dental emergencies.
- Distinguishes three major groups: (1) **abscesses in the periodontium**, (2) **necrotizing periodontal diseases**, and (3) **other acute conditions causing gingival lesions** (infectious processes not associated with oral bacterial biofilms, mucocutaneous disorders, and traumatic/allergic lesions).
- Emphasizes the clinical importance of the **periodontal abscess** as a frequent dental emergency that can compromise tooth prognosis, and from which bacteria can spread to cause infections at distant body sites.
- Classifies abscesses mainly by **etiology**, drawing a clear distinction between abscesses affecting a **pre-existing periodontal pocket** versus those affecting previously **healthy sites**.
- Synthesizes etiologic predisposing factors for **necrotizing periodontal diseases** (HIV infection, malnutrition, psychological stress, tobacco smoking) and frames them as the most severe, most rapidly destructive biofilm-associated conditions.
- Stresses the central role of **differential diagnosis** for the non-biofilm acute gingival lesions to guide appropriate management.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Design**: Narrative literature review summarizing and organizing the evidence base for acute periodontal lesions; not a systematic review or meta-analysis.
- Structures the topic into clinically actionable categories (abscesses → necrotizing periodontal diseases → other acute gingival lesions), each addressed by definition/clinical features, etiology/predisposing factors, diagnosis, and treatment.
- Draws on an extensive reference list (case reports, surveys, classification consensus documents such as AAP and Armitage 1999, and clinical/epidemiologic studies).

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- **Periodontal abscess**: a relatively frequent dental emergency; management = **drainage and tissue debridement**, with case-by-case evaluation of the need for **systemic antimicrobials** based on local and systemic factors. Definitive treatment of the underlying (pre-existing) periodontal condition is performed **after** the acute phase is controlled.
- **Necrotizing periodontal diseases**: three typical clinical features — **papilla necrosis, gingival bleeding, and pain**. Prevalence is low, but they represent the most severe biofilm-associated conditions with very rapid tissue destruction. Treatment = **superficial debridement, careful mechanical oral hygiene, chlorhexidine rinsing, and daily re-evaluation**; adjunctive **systemic antimicrobials (metronidazole first option)** in severe or nonresponding cases. After control, definitive therapy for the pre-existing gingivitis/periodontitis is provided.
- **Other acute conditions** (infectious, mucocutaneous, traumatic, allergic): gingival involvement is usually not severe, but the conditions are common and may prompt emergency visits. Typical appearance is an **erythematous, sometimes erosive lesion**; erosive lesions may result directly from trauma or from rupture of vesicles/bullae. Proper **differential diagnosis** is essential for adequate management.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Narrative (non-systematic) review — no formal search strategy, risk-of-bias appraisal, or quantitative synthesis; evidence weight is lower than an SR/MA.
- Predates the 2017/2018 World Workshop reclassification of periodontal and peri-implant diseases; terminology (e.g., necrotizing periodontal diseases categories) was later refined in the Caton/Papapanou 2018 consensus.
- Management recommendations on systemic antimicrobials reflect 2014 practice; antimicrobial stewardship considerations have since tightened.

## 6. Related Work

- AAP parameters/consensus on acute periodontal diseases and on treatment of plaque-induced gingivitis and chronic periodontitis.
- Armitage 1999 classification system for periodontal diseases and conditions.
- Subsequent 2018 World Workshop classification (Caton et al.; Papapanou et al.) which incorporates necrotizing periodontal diseases and periodontal abscesses into the updated framework.

## 7. Glossary

- **Periodontal abscess**: localized purulent infection within the periodontal tissues, frequently associated with a pre-existing pocket; a common dental emergency.
- **Necrotizing periodontal diseases (NPD)**: spectrum including necrotizing ulcerative gingivitis/periodontitis, characterized by papilla necrosis, bleeding, and pain, with rapid tissue destruction.
- **Mucocutaneous disorders**: immune-mediated diseases (e.g., lichen planus, pemphigoid, pemphigus) producing erosive/erythematous gingival lesions.
- **Erosive lesion**: superficial loss of epithelium, from trauma or rupture of vesicles/bullae.
- **Metronidazole**: nitroimidazole antibiotic, first-line adjunctive systemic agent for severe necrotizing periodontal disease.
