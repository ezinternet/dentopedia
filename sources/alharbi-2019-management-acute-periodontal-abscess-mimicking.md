## One-line Summary

Case report (n=1, 58-year-old man) of an acute periodontal abscess on a vital mandibular left central incisor that radiographically and clinically mimicked an acute apical abscess; resolved with antibiotics (amoxicillin + metronidazole), chlorhexidine, scaling/drainage rather than endodontic therapy — emphasizing pulp vitality testing for differential diagnosis.

## 2. Key Contributions

- Documents a clinical scenario where an acute periodontal abscess closely mimicked an acute apical (endodontic) abscess, including periapical radiolucency around the involved tooth apex.
- Highlights pulp vitality (thermal + electric) testing as the decisive differentiator: all mandibular incisors tested vital, ruling out pulpal necrosis and an endodontic abscess.
- Reinforces that acute periodontal abscesses respond to periodontal therapy (scaling, debridement, drainage, removal of local irritants) plus systemic antimicrobials, in contrast to apical abscesses where pulp extirpation ± incision/drainage is the treatment of choice and systemic antibiotics have no significant role.
- Cautions that systemic antimicrobials should be decided case-by-case, and avoided when a confirmed diagnosis cannot be established.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture

- **Patient**: 58-year-old Saudi man (82 kg, 1.81 m), nonsmoker/nondrinker, no significant systemic disorders, no drug/food allergies.
- **Presentation (April 2018)**: 2-day history of severe sharp toothache in the mandibular anterior region, swelling, refractory reddish gingiva, excessive gingival bleeding, tenderness to slight palpation; difficulty eating/brushing/speaking.
- **Vitals**: Temperature 38.0°C; BP 120/80 mmHg; pulse 75 bpm; bilateral tender submandibular lymphadenopathy.
- **Intraoral findings**: Heavy plaque and calculus, lingual gingival recession at mandibular left central incisor, normal probing depths elsewhere (3–4 mm), periapical radiolucency around the apex of the mandibular left central incisor, noncarious tooth-surface loss on disto-incisal surfaces of both central incisors.
- **Diagnostic tests**: Thermal and electric pulp sensibility tests — all mandibular incisors vital.
- **Diagnosis**: Acute periodontal abscess of the mandibular left central incisor.
- **Treatment**: Supragingival irrigation with 0.2% chlorhexidine gluconate; amoxicillin 500 mg TID × 7 days; metronidazole 500 mg TID × 7 days; naproxen 550 mg PRN; 0.12% chlorhexidine gluconate rinse BID × 2 weeks. After 3 days (less painful): careful scaling and drainage of pus, then discharge.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks

- At 3 days: marked reduction in pain, allowing scaling and pus drainage.
- At 1 week: complete healing of the affected area — no gingival redness, no swelling, no lymphadenopathy, no bleeding.
- At 3-month follow-up: periapical radiograph showed signs of healing in progress.
- Tooth was retained (no extraction), supporting good prognosis when the periodontal etiology is correctly identified and treated.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work

- Single case (n=1); no generalizability or comparative outcomes.
- Authors note the literature on surgical management of acute periodontal abscesses is scant and largely based on textbooks, case reports, and expert opinion rather than controlled trials.
- Antibiotic use was empirical per guideline; the report itself acknowledges systemic antimicrobial decisions should be individualized and avoided without confirmed diagnosis.

## 7. Glossary

- **Periodontal abscess**: Localized purulent infection of the periodontal tissues adjacent to a periodontal pocket; a dental emergency.
- **Apical (periapical) abscess**: Purulent infection of endodontic origin secondary to pulpal necrosis; treated by pulp extirpation ± incision and drainage.
- **Pulp vitality / sensibility test**: Thermal and electric tests used to determine whether pulp tissue is vital; key to distinguishing periodontal from endodontic abscess.
- **Scaling / debridement**: Mechanical removal of plaque and calculus, central to periodontal abscess management.
- **Apical suppurative periodontitis**: Suppurative inflammation in the apical region noted as a journal keyword for this differential context.
