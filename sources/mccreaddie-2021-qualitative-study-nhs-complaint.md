## Why Ingested
The 'what NOT to do' anchor of the response axis. Its discourse analysis names the linguistic moves (the 'fauxpology', subjectivising the complainant) that violate the fairness expectations in [[complaint-management/1472-6963-6-106]] and keep complaints unresolved -- a direct checklist of phrases for a dental clinic to avoid.

## One-line Summary
Discourse analysis of 59 NHS written complaint-responses showing they routinely use non-apologies ('fauxpology') and subjectivise grievances to evade responsibility.

## 1. Document Information
- BMC Health Services Research 2021;21:696. Open access.
- Data corpus: 60 complaint/response pairs from an NHS area in Scotland.

## 2. Key Contributions
- Identifies the 'fauxpology' -- a false/non-apology ('I am sorry you feel...') that blames the complainant's perception.
- Shows responses construct complainants' accounts as merely subjective.
- Documents use of unintentionality/exceptionality to mitigate sub-standard care.
- Explains discursively why many local complaints stay unresolved and escalate to the ombudsman.

## 3. Methodology and Architecture
Qualitative discourse analysis. Corpus of 60 anonymised written complaints and their responses from an NHS area in Scotland, collated over one month; this paper analyses the 59 complaint-responses. Discourse-analytic coding of language choices examined how far responses addressed complainants' grievances.

## 4. Key Results and Benchmarks
Responses rarely acknowledged the effort involved in complaining. They subjectivised complainants' accounts via specific discourse strategies, used unintentionality or exceptionality to mitigate poor care, and deployed the 'fauxpology' (e.g., 'I am sorry you feel...') that imputes distress to the complainant's possibly misguided impressions, thereby evading blame for the complainable action.

## 5. Limitations and Future Work
Single NHS area, written responses only; verbal/resolution-meeting interactions excluded. Interpretive discourse method -- not frequency-generalisable. The companion analysis of the initial complaints is reported separately.

## 6. Related Work
Empirically complements Friele's expectation work (2006, 2015) and converges with Gillespie & Reader's (2025) 'complaint handler's bind' on defensive responding.

## 7. Glossary
- **Fauxpology**: a non-apology/false apology that attributes the cause of distress to the complainant ('sorry you feel').
- **Subjectivising**: discursively reframing a complaint as the complainant's personal (possibly mistaken) impression.
- **Discourse analysis**: study of language-in-use to reveal social/relational work done by text.
