Comparative efficacy of clear aligner mandibular advancement versus traditional functional appliances for skeletal Class II malocclusion: a systematic review and meta-analysis

Introduction

Skeletal Class II malocclusion represents one of the most frequently encountered dentofacial deformities in orthodontic practice, with a global prevalence estimated at approximately 20% [–]. This malocclusion presents as a significant sagittal discrepancy, featuring increased overjet and deep overbite []. Such deformities create a dual burden, adversely affecting both stomatognathic health and psychosocial status [,].

Etiologically, skeletal Class II malocclusion may result from maxillary prognathism, mandibular retrognathism, or a combination thereof; however, extensive cephalometric research confirms that mandibular deficiency is the predominant etiological factor [,]. This deficiency not only severely compromises facial aesthetics, manifesting as a convex profile and chin retrusion, but is also frequently associated with functional impairments. These encompass reduced masticatory efficiency, airway constriction, and a predisposition to temporomandibular joint disorders [,]. Moreover, for adolescents navigating a sensitive developmental period, pronounced Class II facial traits can be a source of substantial psychological burden, compromising both self-esteem and social well-being [,]. Consequently, early treatment targeting mandibular retrognathism offers more than morphological correction, providing substantial benefits to the patient’s overall physiological and psychosocial health.

Functional appliance therapy remains the gold standard for the management of mandibular retrognathism in circumpubertal patients [,]. Its biomechanical rationale rests on posturing the mandible anteriorly to induce adaptive growth of the condylar cartilage and remodeling of the glenoid fossa [,]. This mechanism ultimately promotes true mandibular elongation, effectively correcting sagittal skeletal discrepancies [,]. Among traditional functional appliances, the Twin Block and Herbst are the most widely utilized, owing to their proven efficacy in correcting mandibular retrognathism [,]. However, the clinical utility of these modalities is often constrained by inherent design limitations. Specifically, the bulky acrylic of the Twin Block and the rigid mechanism of the Herbst appliance are frequently linked to physical discomfort and compromised esthetics [,].

Traditional functional appliances exert unavoidable reactive forces on the dentition during orthopedic correction [,]. Current literature consistently indicates that treatment with Twin Block and Herbst appliances is frequently accompanied by compensatory proclination of the mandibular incisors and retroclination of the maxillary incisors [,]. Although this dentoalveolar compensation contributes to the visual reduction of overjet, it may camouflage insufficient skeletal correction [,]. Also, excessive proclination of the mandibular incisors poses potential risks of gingival recession and alveolar bone dehiscence, thereby constraining the therapeutic limit for further mandibular advancement [,].

Clinically, functional appliances are biomechanically restricted to achieving generalized arch expansion or morphological modification, lacking the capacity for precise sagittal and vertical control of individual teeth [,]. This presents a significant disadvantage for the substantial proportion of Class II patients presenting with concomitant dental crowding or rotations. Consequently, to attain optimal occlusal interdigitation, a two-phase treatment protocol is often unavoidable. Such a sequential approach inevitably prolongs the total treatment duration and increases the burden of care on the patient [,].

By integrating mandibular advancement modules like precision wings, clear aligner mandibular advancement (CAMA) facilitate concomitant dental alignment and mandibular guidance, theoretically circumventing the staged burden of traditional functional therapy [,]. Despite this potential, evidence-based data regarding their efficacy remains fragmented and controversial []. Conflicting reports exist regarding skeletal response: while some studies advocate that CAMA achieves mandibular elongation equivalent to the Twin Block [,], others observe limited skeletal effects, attributing overjet reduction primarily to dental movement [,]. Similarly, the presumptive advantage of the full-coverage design in controlling lower incisor inclination remains debated, with findings varying from significant torque control to negligible differences compared to traditional appliances [,,]. Moreover, the predominance of retrospective designs in current literature highlights a critical need for rigorous RCTs to substantiate these claims and to differentiate between various aligner systems [,].

Consequently, the objective of this systematic review and meta-analysis was to synthesize high-quality clinical evidence to critically evaluate the therapeutic differences between CAMA features and traditional functional appliances, specifically the Twin Block and Herbst, in growing patients with skeletal Class II malocclusion. The null hypothesis tested was that there are no significant differences in skeletal and dentoalveolar treatment effects between CAMA and traditional functional appliances.

Materials and methods

Protocol and registration

This systematic review and meta-analysis was reported in strict accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines. The study protocol was prospectively registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO) (Registration number: CRD420251050592).

Protocol Deviations: While the original PROSPERO registration included soft tissue profile changes and specific mandibular dimensions (Co-Go, Go-Gn, Co-Gn) as primary outcomes, these were modified during the review process. Due to insufficient quantitative data reporting across the included studies, these parameters could not be synthesized. Consequently, the primary outcomes were adjusted to focus on the available sagittal and vertical skeletal and dentoalveolar parameters.

Eligibility criteria

The inclusion criteria were established based on the PICOS framework. Eligible participants were growing patients with skeletal Class II malocclusion diagnosed primarily with mandibular retrognathism, confirmed by cervical vertebral maturation (CVM) or hand-wrist radiographs. Studies comparing treatment with CAMA (clear aligners featuring specific mandibular advancement structures, such as Invisalign Mandibular Advancement, Angelalign A6, or similar systems with precision wings) against traditional functional appliances (Twin Block, Herbst, Activator) or untreated Class II control groups were included. Studies utilizing Class II elastics as the sole method for advancement were excluded.

The primary outcomes assessed were skeletal changes (post-treatment [T2] minus pre-treatment [T1]), including SNB angle, ANB angle, SNA angle, and Wits appraisal, while secondary outcomes included dentoalveolar modifications such as lower incisor inclination (IMPA or L1-MP), overjet, and overbite. Regarding study design, randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and non-randomized studies of interventions (NRSIs), including prospective and retrospective cohort studies, were considered. Studies were excluded if they involved patients with craniofacial syndromes, cleft lip and palate, or systemic diseases; a history of prior orthodontic treatment or orthognathic surgery; case reports, reviews, editorials, or abstracts without accessible raw data; or insufficient data to calculate T2-T1 changes.

Information sources and search strategy

A comprehensive systematic search was performed by two independent reviewers across the following electronic databases: PubMed (MEDLINE), Embase, Web of Science, and the Cochrane Library (CENTRAL). The search covered the period from inception to December 31, 2025. The specific search strategy was constructed using a combination of Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) terms and free-text keywords, adapted for the syntax of each database. Boolean operators were employed to combine three primary conceptual blocks: intervention modalities (Invisalign, clear aligner, sequential appliance), orthopedic or functional mechanisms (orthopedic, traction, functional appliance), and the target condition characterizing the skeletal and dentoalveolar malocclusion (Class II, mandibular retrognathism, skeletal deficiency). The search was restricted to Title and Abstract fields to ensure relevance. An exclusion filter was applied to remove animal studies (NOT “animal” [MeSH]). Additionally, the reference lists of eligible studies and relevant systematic reviews were manually screened to identify potentially missed citations. No restrictions were applied regarding language or publication status. The full details of all search strategies for each database are provided in Table S1.

Study selection and data extraction

Retrieved records were imported into reference management software (EndNote, Clarivate Analytics), and duplicates were removed. Two reviewers (X.H. and Y.T.) independently and in duplicate screened titles and abstracts for initial eligibility. Full-text articles of potentially relevant studies were subsequently retrieved and assessed against the inclusion criteria. Any discrepancies during the selection process were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third reviewer (X.C.). Data extraction was conducted independently by two reviewers utilizing a standardized collection form to harvest key variables, including study characteristics (author, year, country, design), sample demographics (sample size, age, gender distribution, skeletal maturation stage), treatment protocols (aligner type, wear regimen, control appliance), and outcome data. Specifically, pre-treatment (T1) and post-treatment (T2) mean values with standard deviations (SDs), or the mean changes (T2-T1), were recorded; where necessary, standard errors (SEs) or confidence intervals (CIs) were converted to SDs using standard formulas derived from the Cochrane Handbook. Inconsistencies during data extraction were resolved by reaching a consensus through discussion or involving the third reviewer (X.C.).

Risk of bias assessment

The methodological quality of the included studies was independently appraised by two reviewers utilizing valid risk-of-bias assessment tools tailored to the specific study designs. The Revised Cochrane risk-of-bias tool (RoB 2) [] was employed to scrutinize Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) across critical domains, including the randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, outcome measurement, and selection of the reported result. Conversely, Non-Randomized Studies of Interventions (NRSIs) were assessed using the Risk Of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Interventions (ROBINS-I) tool [].

Data synthesis and statistical analysis

Quantitative synthesis was executed using Review Manager (RevMan) version 5.4 (The Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark). For continuous outcomes, treatment effects were expressed as Weighted Mean Differences (WMDs) accompanied by 95% Confidence Intervals (CIs). In instances where studies utilized disparate measurement scales, Standardized Mean Differences (SMDs) were computed. Inter-study variability was gauged via the Chi-square (Q) test and the Istatistic, with substantial heterogeneity defined as an Ivalue exceeding 50% or a P value below 0.10. To account for inherent clinical diversity in patient demographics and therapeutic protocols, a random-effects model was uniformly adopted to provide a conservative estimate of the intervention effect. To further address potential sources of substantial heterogeneity, sensitivity analyses (leave-one-out approach) were prespecified to evaluate the robustness of the pooled results and identify individual studies contributing disproportionately to the variance. Furthermore, sensitivity analyses were conducted to probe sources of inconsistency by omitting trials with a high risk of bias.

Results

Literature search and screening

The initial electronic search yielded a total of 1589 potential records. Following the removal of duplicates and the screening of titles and abstracts, 60 full-text articles and reports were assessed for eligibility. Among these, 51 records were excluded for the following reasons: no quantitative outcome measures available (= 40), and conference abstracts or no full text available (= 11). A specific list of the closely related studies excluded after full-text screening is provided in Table S2. Ultimately, 9 studies satisfied the inclusion criteria and were incorporated into this systematic review (Fig.). The final selection comprised 1 randomized controlled trial (RCT) and 8 non-randomized studies of interventions (NRSIs) [–,–].

Study characteristics

The included studies cumulatively comprised a total sample of 465 growing patients diagnosed with skeletal Class II malocclusion. All investigations evaluated the comparative efficacy of CAMA (Invisalign MA, Angelalign A6, Clear Twin Block) versus traditional functional appliances (Twin Block, Herbst, Vanbeek Activator) or untreated control groups. The mean age of the participants ranged from 11.2 to 13.0 years, with treatment observation periods spanning from 11 to 20 months (Table S3 and Table S4). As noted in the protocol deviations, none of the included studies provided sufficient quantitative data for mandibular dimensions (Co-Go, Go-Gn, Co-Gn) or soft tissue profile changes; therefore, these parameters were not subjected to meta-analysis.

Risk of bias assessment

Risk of bias was evaluated for the single included RCT using the RoB 2 tool and for eight non-randomized studies of interventions (NRSIs) using the ROBINS-I tool. The RCT was assessed as having a high risk of bias, primarily due to deviations from intended interventions (Table S5). Among the NRSIs (Table S6), five studies were classified as high risk, a rating principally driven by bias due to confounding inherent in their retrospective designs [,,–]. Conversely, three studies were rated as moderate risk [,,]. Overall, the methodological quality of the current evidence is limited, largely constrained by the prevalence of retrospective data and insufficient control for confounding factors (Figure S1).

Quantitative synthesis

Stratified meta-analyses were performed to evaluate skeletal and dental changes. The results are presented below, categorized by sagittal skeletal, sagittal dental, and vertical assessments.

Sagittal skeletal assessment

In the comparison between CAMA and traditional Twin Block (TB), the meta-analysis yielded mixed results regarding sagittal skeletal changes (Fig.). Two studies [,] assessed changes in the Wits appraisal. Due to substantial heterogeneity (I= 98%), a random-effects model was applied. The analysis showed no statistically significant difference between the appliances (= 0.96), with a mean difference (MD) of -0.15 mm (95% CI: -5.68 to 5.38). Sensitivity analysis was precluded for this parameter due to the limited number of available studies. Regarding skeletal angular changes, the analysis revealed no statistically significant differences between the CAMA and TB groups. High heterogeneity was observed (I= 72%) in SNA, prompting the use of a random-effects model; the mean difference was negligible (MD = 0.01; 95% CI: -0.66 to 0.68;= 0.98). Similarly, SNB showed high heterogeneity (I= 75%) with no significant difference between groups (MD = -0.09; 95% CI: -0.93 to 0.74;= 0.83). ANB exhibited high homogeneity (I= 0%). The fixed-effects analysis confirmed comparable changes in the maxillomandibular relationship for both appliances (MD = -0.10; 95% CI: -0.36 to 0.15;= 0.42).

The comparative effectiveness of the CAMA and Herbst appliances regarding skeletal angular changes was evaluated (Fig.). The meta-analysis revealed no statistically significant differences between the two modalities across all assessed sagittal skeletal parameters. Notably, all comparisons exhibited high homogeneity (I= 0%). Specifically, the assessment of SNA in two studies [,] showed a negligible mean difference of 0.13 (95% CI: -0.35 to 0.62;= 0.59). Similarly, regarding mandibular changes (SNB), data [,] indicated no statistically significant distinction between the groups (= 0.17), with a mean difference of -0.50 (95% CI: -1.22 to 0.21). Consistent with these findings, the change in the maxillomandibular relationship (ANB) also demonstrated no significant difference (MD = 0.53; 95% CI: -0.15 to 1.21;= 0.13), suggesting that both modalities achieved similar skeletal outcomes.

When comparing the CAMA group to untreated controls (Fig.), two studies [,] were included for Wits appraisal. Despite high heterogeneity (I= 91%), the random-effects analysis indicated a statistically significant reduction in Wits values for the CAMA group compared to the control group (= 0.05). The mean difference was − 2.51 mm (95% CI: -4.98 to -0.03). For the maxilla, the fixed-effects analysis revealed no statistically significant difference in SNA angle changes between the treatment and control groups (MD = -0.05; 95% CI: -0.52 to 0.43;= 0.85). In contrast, the analysis of mandibular changes (SNB) demonstrated a statistically significant difference favoring the CAMA group (= 0.003), with a mean difference of 1.00 (95% CI: 0.34 to 1.65). Regarding the maxillomandibular relationship (ANB), the initial random-effects model indicated no statistically significant difference in ANB angle reduction between the groups (= 0.91; MD = -0.16; 95% CI: -2.88 to 2.55). However, this result was marked by extreme heterogeneity (I= 98%). A prespecified leave-one-out sensitivity analysis revealed that this heterogeneity was primarily driven by the study by Al Subaie et al. []. Upon exclusion of this study, the heterogeneity was entirely resolved (I= 0%), and the revised pooled estimate demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in the ANB angle favoring the CAMA group compared to untreated controls (MD = -1.55°; 95% CI: -2.16 to -0.94;< 0.00001).

Sagittal dental assessment

Three studies [,,] evaluated overjet reduction. The data exhibited high homogeneity (I= 0%). A statistically significant difference was observed (= 0.03) favoring the CAMA group, which achieved a slightly greater reduction in overjet compared to the TB group (MD = -0.46 mm; 95% CI: -0.86 to -0.06). However, while statistically significant, a mean difference of 0.46 mm is clinically minimal. When comparing the CAMA group to untreated controls, the analysis for overjet showed moderate heterogeneity (I= 58%). Although the CAMA group showed a greater reduction in overjet (MD = -2.16 mm), the difference did not reach statistical significance (= 0.06; 95% CI: -4.40 to 0.08). The inclination of the lower incisors (IMPA) was assessed in five studies [,,,,]. Moderate heterogeneity was observed (I= 43%). A statistically significant difference was found favoring the CAMA group (= 0.0002). The CAMA group exhibited significantly less lower incisor proclination compared to the TB group, with a mean difference of -0.90° (95% CI: -1.38 to -0.42) (Fig.).

Vertical assessment

In the comparison between CAMA and traditional TB, the meta-analysis revealed no statistically significant differences in vertical dimensions, suggesting comparable vertical control capabilities (Fig.). Two studies [,] reported on dental vertical changes via overbite. The included studies showed no heterogeneity (I= 0%). The fixed-effects analysis indicated no significant difference between the appliances (= 0.20), with a mean difference (MD) of -1.04 mm (95% CI: -2.63 to 0.54). Skeletal vertical changes (ANS-Me) were assessed in two studies [,]. The heterogeneity was low (I= 20%). The pooled analysis showed no statistically significant difference in lower facial height changes between the CAMA and TB groups (= 0.71; MD = -0.20 mm; 95% CI: -1.25 to 0.85). When comparing the CAMA group to untreated controls, the analysis focused on vertical dental changes. Two studies [,] evaluated the changes in overbite. No heterogeneity was observed (I= 0%). The fixed-effects model revealed no statistically significant difference between the treatment and control groups (= 0.16). The CAMA group showed a mean difference of -0.68 mm (95% CI: -1.63 to 0.27) compared to the controls (Fig.).

Discussion

The present systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to critically evaluate the efficacy of CAMA features compared to traditional functional appliances. The quantitative synthesis suggests that the skeletal orthopedic efficacy of CAMA is comparable to that of traditional functional appliances, with the least difference observed for sagittal skeletal parameters and vertical dimensions. Conversely, a significant difference was observed in the control of lower incisor inclination. The summary effect indicates that CAMA offers superior sagittal dental control, specifically by minimizing lower incisor proclination, while delivering equivalent skeletal correction.

Our meta-analysis demonstrated no statistically significant differences in skeletal parameters between the CAMA and traditional Twin Block groups. This finding is robustly corroborated by emerging literature, suggesting that the difference in material does not compromise the fundamental orthopedic response of the mandible [,]. A recent systematic review by Ghorbani et al. [] synthesized evidence on functional clear aligners and similarly concluded that mandibular advancement features are effective in inducing morphological changes comparable to those achieved by traditional appliances. Furthermore, regarding the timing of intervention, Khalil et al. [] validated that employing CAMA during the pre-pubertal peak effectively improves SNB and ANB angles, thereby establishing its reliability as an orthopedic tool rather than merely a dental alignment device. To further delineate the mechanism of action, Lombardo et al. [] compared CAMA against Class II elastics. Their findings were pivotal: while Class II elastics rely heavily on dentoalveolar compensation, CAMA primarily induces skeletal effects via mandibular advancement. This distinction confirms that clear aligner MA functions biomechanically as a true functional appliance, mirroring the skeletal effects observed in our study.

While traditional functional appliances are the gold standard, their biomechanical mechanisms are associated with inherent side effects [,]. The forced mandibular advancement via occlusal inclined planes inevitably concentrates reactive orthopedic forces on the dentition, resulting in significant lower incisor proclination [,]. Owayed et al. [] noted that such dentoalveolar compensation often masks the true magnitude of skeletal correction. In contrast, CAMA technology is designed to distribute orthopedic forces more uniformly through the splint effect of full-arch coverage and digital staging [,]. Unlike traditional appliances that often rely on point contacts that allow for tipping movements, clear aligners fully encase the clinical crown. This encapsulation effectively moves the point of force application closer to the tooth’s center of resistance and generates a counter-moment that actively resists the labial tipping force typically exerted on the lower incisors during mandibular advancement. This theoretical advantage is corroborated by our meta-analysis, which revealed that the CAMA group exhibited significantly less lower incisor proclination compared to the traditional Twin Block group. This suggests that the aligner material effectively reinforces anchorage, mitigating the reactive forces on the lower incisors []. Furthermore, Mecenas et al. [] demonstrated that patients treated with precision wings exhibited reduced lower incisor proclination, attributing this to the full encapsulation of the lower anterior teeth and the capability for programmed torque control.

Unlike traditional functional appliances, CAMA allows for simultaneous dental alignment during the orthopedic phase [,]. As highlighted by Chakraborty et al. [], clear aligner systems can correct Class II relationships while concurrently addressing crowding and arch expansion. This simultaneous treatment modality aligns with our finding of greater overjet reduction in the CAMA group, likely driven by the combined orthopedic and orthodontic effects. However, it is crucial to interpret this finding objectively; while the mean difference of 0.46 mm in overjet reduction was statistically significant, such a magnitude is clinically minimal. This indicates that both CAMA and traditional appliances are highly effective for overjet correction, and the statistical difference may not necessarily translate into a visibly superior clinical advantage. Additionally, compliance remains a paramount variable influencing the outcome of functional therapy []. For children in the mixed dentition phase, the bulk and discomfort of traditional appliances are primary contributors to treatment failure []. A scoping review by Rai et al. [] emphasized that compliance challenges are particularly pronounced during interceptive intervention, underscoring the need for more comfortable alternatives. CAMA features potentially address these concerns by offering a more esthetic and comfortable experience.

The interpretations of this systematic review must be tempered by several inherent limitations, reflecting the current state of primary research. First, as highlighted by the scarcity of high-quality primary data, the current analysis included only one RCT, whereas the majority of studies were retrospective in design. This reliance on retrospective data introduces inherent selection bias and limits the strength of causal inferences. Second, there is notable heterogeneity across the included studies regarding inclusion criteria, specific appliance designs, and wear protocols. As indicated by the ROBINS-I assessment, several of these studies carried a high risk of confounding bias.

Furthermore, the extreme heterogeneity observed in certain skeletal parameters cannot be solely attributed to retrospective designs. It is likely driven by substantial variability in specific appliance mechanics across studies and disparities in the precise timing of intervention relative to the pubertal growth peak (CVM stages) among the included cohorts. Another critical confounding factor is the physiologic growth of the mandible. Since the included patients were around their peak growth period, the observed skeletal changes represent a combination of appliance-induced orthopedic effects and natural physiological growth; without ideal untreated control groups across all comparisons, distinguishing these two components remains challenging. Additionally, the lack of consistently reported vertical skeletal parameters and maxillary incisor inclination across the primary studies restricted our ability to perform a more comprehensive cephalometric synthesis.

Future research should prioritize large-scale, multi-center RCTs incorporating standardized compliance monitoring, such as embedded microsensors, to further validate efficacy. Investigations should not be limited to short-term comparisons but must focus on long-term post-treatment stability and the specific impact of different appliance designs on clinical outcomes across diverse vertical facial patterns.

Conclusions

The present systematic review and meta-analysis suggest that the skeletal orthopedic efficacy of CAMA features is comparable to that of traditional functional appliances. The least difference was observed for sagittal skeletal parameters and vertical dimensions, whereas a maximum difference was observed in the control of lower incisor inclination. The summary effect of the meta-analysis revealed that CAMA offers superior sagittal dental control, specifically by minimizing lower incisor proclination, while delivering equivalent skeletal correction. Due to the observational nature of most included studies and the potential confounding effect of physiologic growth, the overall certainty of evidence is low. Consequently, these findings should be applied with caution until validated by further rigorous randomized controlled trials.
