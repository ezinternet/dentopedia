The Impact of Periodontal Instrumentation on Enamel and Cementum: A Narrative Review
Lodigkeit et al. (2026). Dentistry Journal (Dent J (Basel)).
DOI: 10.3390/dj14050259 | PMID: 42187637 | PMCID: PMC13205519
Source: PubMed Central full text (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13205519/)

Abstract
Achieving a smooth tooth surface following periodontal instrumentation is critical for maintaining periodontal health and minimizing plaque and calculus reaccumulation. This narrative review aimed to synthesize preclinical laboratory-based evidence regarding the effects of periodontal instrumentation techniques on enamel and cementum surface roughness and hard-tissue loss. A focused literature search was conducted using the PubMed database to identify relevant in vitro and in vitro/in vivo hybrid studies published within the last 15 years. This review focused on ultrasonic scaling, hand instrumentation, and air polishing of human teeth during periodontal treatment. Periodontal instrumentation was associated with surface alterations of enamel and cementum, with the extent of these changes depending on instrumentation parameters. Manual instrumentation was generally associated with greater surface irregularities and increased cementum removal, whereas ultrasonic scaling tended to produce more uniform surface characteristics. However, outcomes varied depending on instrumentation parameters. Air-polishing systems were described as less abrasive, particularly in biofilm management. Within the limitations of predominantly in vitro evidence, periodontal instrumentation appears to alter dental hard tissues to varying degrees depending on the technique and application. The clinical relevance of these findings remains uncertain, as the evidence was primarily laboratory-based and may not fully reflect clinical conditions or predict long-term outcomes.

1. Introduction
Periodontal diseases remain among the most common chronic inflammatory diseases affecting adult populations worldwide. The primary etiological factor is dental biofilm, and when left untreated, it can lead to gingival inflammation, attachment tissue breakdown, and progressive alveolar bone loss. Mechanical disruption and removal of biofilm and mineralized deposits are, therefore, the foundation of preventive and therapeutic periodontal care. Periodontal instrumentation, including hand scaling, ultrasonic debridement, and air-abrasive biofilm removal, is essential for achieving periodontal stability and long-term tooth retention.

Traditionally, root planing has involved the removal of the affected cementum to eliminate endotoxin within the contaminated cementum. Current studies question the traditional approach of root planing and emphasize the preservation of root structure and biologically active cementum wherever possible. Cementum plays a regulatory role in periodontal healing and attachment by influencing fibroblast behaviour and connective tissue reattachment. Furthermore, excessive removal of root cementum can eliminate bioactive proteins and growth factors essential for periodontal regeneration and fibrous attachment. Therefore, excessive removal may impair the regenerative potential of periodontal connective tissue.

Effective periodontal instrumentation reduces microbial load, disrupts pathogenic biofilms, and creates a smooth root surface, which promotes soft-tissue healing. However, mechanical debridement of mineralized tooth tissue is associated with some degree of hard tissue alteration, ranging from minor roughness changes to measurable substance loss. Increased surface roughness has been associated with enhanced bacterial adhesion and plaque retention, which interferes with periodontal healing.

Enamel and cementum are most directly exposed to mechanical instrumentation during both supragingival and subgingival debridement procedures. Although enamel is the most mineralized tissue in the human body, it can be damaged during professional debridement (surface scratches, micro-grooves, increased roughness). Cementum is considerably softer and thinner than enamel; therefore, it is more vulnerable to instrumentation trauma. Other clinical consequences may include hypersensitivity and loss of marginal integrity of restorations. Because periodontal instrumentation is performed repeatedly throughout a patient's life, even minor cumulative effects may have clinical significance.

No prior narrative or systematic synthesis focusing specifically on enamel and cementum alterations following periodontal instrumentation was identified. This study aimed to provide a narrative synthesis of preclinical laboratory-based evidence regarding the effects of periodontal instrumentation on enamel and cementum, with particular emphasis on surface roughness and hard-tissue loss.

2. Methods
Focused narrative review. PubMed search, conducted iteratively with Boolean combinations of keywords on instrumentation ("periodontal instrumentation", "ultrasonic scaling", "root planing", "air polishing", "periodontal debridement", "hand instrumentation"), substrate ("enamel", "cementum"), and surface alteration ("surface roughness", "surface damage", "loss of enamel/cementum", "substance loss"). English-language only; 15-year publication window; final search January 2026.

Inclusion: preclinical laboratory-based studies investigating effects of periodontal instrumentation on enamel or cementum, including in vitro studies on extracted human teeth and in vitro/in vivo hybrid studies (instrumentation performed clinically prior to laboratory analysis). Outcomes: surface roughness, morphological alterations, or hard-tissue loss; methods ultrasonic scaling, hand instrumentation, air polishing. Exclusions: animal teeth, artificial tooth models, orthodontic instrumentation, restorative procedures, implant surfaces, non-periodontal instrumentation. Data extracted and qualitatively summarized; no meta-analysis (heterogeneity). No formal risk-of-bias assessment.

3. Results
16 studies (in vitro and in vitro/in vivo hybrid). Methods examined: hand instruments, ultrasonic devices (various power settings, tip materials), rotary instruments, polishing systems, air-polishing systems. Outcomes assessed by SEM, AFM, and profilometry.

3.1. Enamel surface topography and roughness
Instrumentation of enamel consistently produced detectable surface changes regardless of method. Ultrasonic and manual scaling both increased enamel roughness; damage varied by instrument type, angulation, pressure, application duration. Stainless-steel ultrasonic scalers produced fine superficial scratches with minimal roughness; titanium tips produced deeper, more pronounced defects. Hand scaling with titanium curettes produced the most significant enamel damage (attributed to greater material hardness of titanium and greater manual force).

Arefnia et al. (2021): in vitro, roughness depth Rz. Air polishing (non-abrasive 7 µm powder) = highest mean Rz 6.22 µm; manual curettage Rz 5.04 µm; ultrasonic scaling Rz 3.44 µm. None caused a statistically significant change in Rz vs untreated enamel control (Rz 3.0-6.0 µm). Each instrument produced a distinct topography, but none significantly altered natural enamel roughness when used independently.

Yildirim et al. (2021): in vitro, surfaces standardized with carbide papers. All techniques (ultrasonic scaling, hand instrumentation, polishing paste, pumice, air-powder system) significantly increased enamel roughness. Ultrasonic scalers produced the greatest increase (vibrational forces introducing grooves/irregularities). Pumice was the least abrasive polishing method. Hand instrumentation produced a smoother surface than ultrasonic scaling.

Morphological analyses: superficial scratches, grooves, localized enamel defects. Manual instrumentation produced an "eroded" surface with partial destruction of enamel prisms. Ultrasonic scaling caused more significant alterations: partial/complete destruction of enamel prism bundles, granular enamel defects, surface defects. Manual instrumentation was less destructive than ultrasonic scaling for enamel morphology.

3.2. Cementum surface roughness
Root surface roughness consistently reported; degree varied by method/conditions. Several studies: hand instrumentation associated with increased roughness, grooves, scratches on cementum; SEM showed varying cementum removal and occasional dentin exposure. In contrast, some researchers found ultrasonic instrumentation produced a smoother, more uniform root surface than manual curettes. Piezoelectric vs magnetostrictive: both smoother than curettes; piezoelectric created slightly more cracks and substance loss, magnetostrictive resulted in least dentin exposure and structural damage. Marda et al. (2012): ultrasonic produced smoothest cementum, hand instrumentation produced scratches/irregularities. Findings comparing manual vs ultrasonic were not consistent across all studies.

Ultrasonic power setting influenced outcomes: medium power = lowest roughness, low power = higher roughness, high power = effects comparable to hand instrumentation (positive correlation between power and surface alteration). Parashar et al. (2023): linear grooves, shallow scratches, irregularities from ultrasonic vibration; surface alteration influenced by applied pressure, tip angulation, instrumentation duration. Lower pressure and shorter time = reduced damage (operator-dependent). Worn scaler tips associated with increased roughness vs new tips.

3.3. Hard-tissue loss and substance removal
Cementum loss consistently reported, extent varied. Manual curettes associated with greater removal of root substance; SEM: hand curettes gave smoother surfaces but greatest cementum removal, potentially complete cementum loss with dentin exposure. Ultrasonic devices preserved more cementum / less tissue loss than hand instrumentation, but not entirely atraumatic (varied with parameters). Rotary instruments: greater surface damage than curettes/ultrasonic (increased cementum removal, dentin exposure).

Quantitative (Arefnia et al.): cementum loss ≤20 µm after air polishing, ≥20 µm after ultrasonic, ≥40 µm after hand instrumentation. Air polishing caused no significant enamel substance loss; minimal loss (<3.0 µm) after ultrasonic and hand instrumentation.

Bozbay et al. (2018): in vivo with subsequent ex vivo analysis after extraction. Coronal cementum retained: air polishing 94% > ultrasonic 84% > ultrasonic + air polishing 80% > hand instrumentation 65%. Similar trends in apical cementum (greater preservation with air-polishing).

Overall: all methods cause some cementum loss; hand instrumentation generally greatest, ultrasonic intermediate, air polishing high preservation.

3.4. Air-polishing systems
Air polishing identified as one of the least abrasive techniques for both enamel and cementum: minimal change in surface roughness, high tissue preservation. On cementum, air polishing alone produced no significant change in roughness depth vs untreated control; Rz value (6.64 µm) close to untreated cementum (7.48 µm) vs hand instrumentation 3.82 µm and ultrasonic 2.36 µm. Profilometry confirmed less roughness than hand instrumentation; can smooth surface irregularities caused by instrumentation. SEM: general preservation of cementum, few grooves, fewer defects than ultrasonic. However, air polishing alone is ineffective at removing calculus; combining ultrasonic scaling with air polishing was effective for both preserving cementum and removing calculus. On enamel, air polishing had minimal effects and no significant change vs untreated enamel.

4. Discussion
All examined methods showed some degree of surface alteration; magnitude and clinical relevance differed. Manual instrumentation frequently associated with greater irregularities and increased cementum removal (direct mechanical scraping action, lateral pressure). Ultrasonic devices generally produced a more uniform root surface (high-frequency vibration vs scraping), often with cementum mostly intact, but not entirely atraumatic (microcracks observed). Solomon et al.: greater hard-tissue loss with hand instrumentation vs ultrasonic (weight-based method).

Ultrasonic scaling described as minimally invasive but still associated with surface alterations; extent influenced by operator-dependent variables (force, angulation, duration, instrument condition). Kruse et al. (2024): curettage and air polishing on enamel produced measurable but clinically insignificant roughness changes (high enamel resistance); no measurable enamel loss.

Air polishing = one of least-abrasive methods for enamel and cementum; conservative, removes plaque/stains, minimizes operator fatigue, accesses difficult areas. Primarily for supportive periodontal therapy / maintenance, not traditional root planing alone. Air polishing after scaling beneficial in vitro ("spreading pattern", more homogeneous topography). Caution: higher Rz after air polishing may result from cleaning depressions in the surface profile (deepening valleys), while lower ultrasonic values may indicate flattening of natural peaks.

Despite differences in surface alterations, hand and ultrasonic instruments showed comparable calculus removal effectiveness; both significantly reduced subgingival deposits (no significant difference in debridement efficacy). Ultrasonic required significantly less working time than manual scaling (lower operator fatigue). Manual curettage offers better tactile sense but is technique-sensitive, time-consuming, physically demanding.

Ideal instrumentation: effectively removes plaque, calculus, endotoxins while preserving cementum and minimizing iatrogenic damage — no single system currently fulfils all requirements. Limitations: most studies in vitro on extracted teeth; in vitro settings do not fully replicate the oral environment; heterogeneity in parameters/techniques/measurement; different measurement techniques (SEM, AFM, profilometry at different scales/resolutions) and roughness parameters (Ra mean deviation vs Rz peak-to-valley, not directly comparable); "smooth surface" inconsistently defined (smoothing may result from cementum removal rather than preserved integrity); no formal risk-of-bias assessment; limited external validity; possible selection/language bias.

5. Conclusions
Within the limitations of preclinical evidence, periodontal instrumentation was associated with enamel and cementum surface alterations. Manual instrumentation associated with greater irregularities and increased cementum removal; ultrasonic instrumentation associated with more uniform surfaces and comparatively lower substance loss; air-polishing systems described as relatively less invasive for biofilm removal and polishing in laboratory settings. Findings require cautious interpretation: predominantly in vitro, considerable heterogeneity; clinical relevance of observed surface alterations remains uncertain.
