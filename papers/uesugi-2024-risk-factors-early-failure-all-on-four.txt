A Study of the Associated Risk Factors for Early Failure and the Effect of Photofunctionalisation in Full-Arch Immediate Loading Treatment Based on the All-on-Four Concept.
Uesugi T, Shimoo Y, Munakata M, Kataoka Y, Sato D, Yamaguchi K, Sanda M, Fujimaki M, Nakayama K, Watanabe T, Malo P.
Bioengineering (Basel). 2024 Feb 27;11(3):223.
DOI: 10.3390/bioengineering11030223
PMID: 38534497  PMCID: PMC10968038

[FULL TEXT retrieved from PubMed Central (PMC10968038).]

ABSTRACT
Early implant failure occurring within 1 year after implantation has been attributed to various
factors. Particularly, early failure can lead to challenges in maintaining a full-arch prosthetic
device, necessitating prompt intervention, including reoperation. This study aimed to retrospectively
examine implant- and patient-related factors and the effects of photofunctionalisation associated
with early failure in patients who underwent treatment using the all-on-four concept in both the
maxilla and mandible. We conducted this retrospective study comprising 561 patients with 2364 implants
who underwent implant-supported immediate loading with fixed full-arch rehabilitation using the
all-on-four concept. We aimed to assess the survival rate within 1 year after implantation and
determine the risk factors influencing early failure. The 1-year survival rates after implantation
were 97.1% (patient level) and 98.9% (implant level) for the maxilla and 98.5% (patient level) and
99.6% (implant level) for the mandible. There was a significant difference in the implant-level
survival rates between the maxilla and mandible, with a lower rate in the maxilla (p = 0.043). The
risk factors associated with early implant failure according to the all-on-four concept included the
maxilla (implant level) and smoking (patient level). We could not find a significant effect of
photofunctionalisation on early failure (p = 0.25) following this treatment protocol.

1. INTRODUCTION
Implant failure is influenced by multiple factors and categorised into early and late failure
depending on the timing of implant loss. Late failure is attributed to peri-implantitis caused by
bacterial infection and overloading due to bruxism or inappropriate implant design. Conversely, early
failure results from multifaceted causes: surgeon-related factors (skill/experience, surgical
technique), patient-related factors (sex, age, location, bone quality, smoking, systemic diseases,
parafunction), and implant-related factors (diameter, length, surface characteristics).

Early failure of a single implant can lead to difficulties in maintaining the full-arch prosthesis,
imposing a significant burden, particularly on patients receiving implant-supported immediate loading
with fixed full-arch rehabilitation using the all-on-four concept.

Surface properties are an implant-related factor causing early failure. Compared to machined surfaces,
moderately rough surfaces retain fibrin matrix, facilitate osteoconductive responses and osseointegration.
Photofunctionalisation (UV treatment) was first reported in 2009 to increase attachment, diffusion,
proliferation, and differentiation of osteoblasts and to increase protein adsorption up to three times;
in vivo it promotes osseointegration approaching ~100% bone-to-implant contact. It removes hydrocarbons,
improves hydrophilicity (reverses titanium "ageing"), reduces pathogen adhesion/biofilm.

Despite being a burdensome procedure, there are few reports on risk factors affecting early failure in
all-on-four, including the presence/absence of photofunctionalisation. The objective was to determine
risk factors for early implant failure of the all-on-four concept and analyse whether photofunctionalisation
reduces early failure.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
Retrospective study. Patients who underwent tooth extraction with all-on-four-based implant placement
and immediate loading for fixed full-arch reconstruction at a private clinic (Malo Dental and Medical
Tokyo) between September 2005 and October 2020. Exclusions: no follow-up at clinic; zygomatic implants;
immunosuppression; active malignancy treatment; intravenous bisphosphonate therapy; uncontrolled systemic
disease (e.g., diabetes).

Surgical protocol: all implants had anodic-oxidation surface (Nobel Speedy Groovy, Nobel Biocare).
Photofunctionalisation performed chairside just before implantation, 1 min, Super Osseo Integration
Excimer UV system (172 nm, 20 mW/cm2), for implants placed after August 2016 (when system introduced).
All surgery/prosthetics by five surgeons with >10 years implant experience. Local anaesthesia. Standard
all-on-four protocol (posterior tilted implants ≥4.0 mm, anterior ≥3.3 mm; straight or 17°/30° angled
abutments). If implant ≥10 mm length or ≥35 Ncm initial fixation not achievable, additional implants
placed. Antibiotic: amoxicillin 250 mg 4×/day for 5 days; benzethonium chloride mouthwash; loxoprofen
analgesic. Same-day provisional (Co-Cr reinforced acrylic). Final prosthesis 6 months later.

Clinical outcome: early failure within 1 year of implantation. Failure = removal of implant within 1
year owing to mobility, persistent pain, swelling, or inflammation/abscess. Patient-level failure = ≥1
implant lost in a single patient.

Statistics: univariate chi-square; multivariate logistic regression; odds ratios. SPSS 20. p<0.05
significant.

3. RESULTS
Study included 617 patients (345 maxilla, 272 mandible) with 2453 fixtures (1376 maxilla, 1077 mandible).
320 male / 297 female; mean age 56.8 ± 10.8 years. (Abstract reports 561 patients / 2364 implants for the
all-on-four cohort analysed.)

1-year survival (early failure) rates:
- Maxilla patient level 97.1% (2.9% early failure); maxilla implant level 98.9% (1.1%).
- Mandible patient level 98.5% (1.5%); mandible implant level 99.6% (0.4%).
- Maxilla significantly lower survival at implant level (p=0.043).

Photofunctionalisation (Table 2): Maxilla — Yes 0.6% (3/486 implants), No 1.3% (12/890); patient level
Yes 2.4% (3/126), No 3.2% (7/219). Mandible implant level Yes 0.3% (1/333), No 0.4% (741/744). Reduced
early failure at all levels but NOT statistically significant.

Implant variables (Table 3, total n=2453, 19 failures): photofunctionalisation Yes 0.5% vs No 0.9%
(p=0.25); implant angulation straight 0.6% vs tilted 1.0% (p=0.24); implant length, diameter, primary
stability — no significant differences.

Patient factors: sex — male 3.4% vs female 1.0% (p=0.043, higher in males, univariate). Smoking —
smokers 4.1% vs non-smokers 1.3% (p=0.023). Systemic disease — no significant difference for diabetes/
cardiovascular; osteoporosis trend (p=0.051 <0.1).

Multivariate logistic regression (items with univariate significance + photofunctionalisation):
- Maxilla treatment area: OR 3.12, p=0.0444 (implant level) — most influential, significant.
- Smoking: OR 2.92, p=0.0296 (patient level) — significant.
- Male sex: OR 1.25, p=0.649 — NS.
- Photofunctionalisation: OR 0.51, p=0.237 (implant level) — protective trend but NS.

4. DISCUSSION (key points)
All-on-four medium-long-term survival 93.9-100% (maxilla) and 91.7-100% (mandible) over 3-18 years.
Context: Derks et al. (Swedish population) overall 9-year failure 7.6%, of which failure before
superstructure attachment 4.4%. Lin et al. (18,199 patients / 30,959 implants): patient survival 98.0%,
implant 98.7%; 194 implants (0.6%) lost before abutment connection; early failures (within first 6 months)
= 48.1% of all lost implants. Understanding/avoiding early failure is crucial.

Definition of "early failure" varies: ① before abutment connection; ② before loading; ③ within 1 year
of loading; ④ within 1 year after upper structure installation. As this study uses immediate loading in
all cases, early failure was defined as within 1 year of loading.

Maxilla as a risk factor: consistent with Wu et al. meta-analysis of early failure in immediate loading.
Higher maxillary early failure attributed to bone quality, opposing-teeth state, and parafunction
(especially with immediate loading). Smoking negatively impacts early failure; smoking cessation guidance
crucial. Osteoporosis showed trend; bisphosphonate-treated osteoporosis higher implant-failure risk (Malo).

Limitations: single implant-surface type (anodic oxidation) only, so photofunctionalisation effect by
surface type not assessable; opposing-teeth/parafunction not investigated; systemic-disease medications
not considered.

5. CONCLUSIONS
Multivariate analysis of implant- and patient-related factors plus photofunctionalisation on early failure
within 1 year of all-on-four full-arch immediate loading: maxilla (implant level) and smoking (patient
level) were significant risk factors for early failure; photofunctionalisation showed a protective trend
(OR 0.51) but was not statistically significant.
