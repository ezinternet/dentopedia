Aerosol concentrations and size distributions during clinical dental procedures
Lahdentausta L, Sanmark E, Lauretsalo S, Korkee V, Nyman S, Atanasova N, Oksanen L, Zhao J, Hussein T, Hyvärinen A, Paju S.
Heliyon. 2022 Oct 18;8(10):e11074. doi:10.1016/j.heliyon.2022.e11074
PMID: 36303931  PMCID: PMC9593181
Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9593181/
(Full text retrieved from PubMed Central via PubMed MCP.)

== ABSTRACT ==
Background: Suspected aerosol-generating dental instruments may cause risks for operators by transmitting pathogens, such as the SARS-CoV-2 virus. The aim of our study was to measure aerosol generation in various dental procedures in clinical settings.
Methods: The study population comprised of 84 patients who underwent 253 different dental procedures measured with Optical Particle Sizer in a dental office setting. Aerosol particles from 0.3 to 10 μm in diameter were measured. Dental procedures included oral examinations (N=52), restorative procedures with air turbine handpiece (N=8), high-speed (N=6) and low-speed (N=30) handpieces, ultrasonic scaling (N=31), periodontal treatment using hand instruments (N=60), endodontic treatment (N=12), intraoral radiographs (N=24), and dental local anesthesia (N=31).
Results: Air turbine handpieces significantly elevated <1 μm particle median (p=0.013) and maximum (p=0.016) aerosol number concentrations as well as aerosol particle mass concentrations (p=0.046 and p=0.006) compared to the background aerosol levels preceding the operation. Low-speed dental handpieces elevated >5 μm median (p=0.023), maximum (p=0.013) particle number concentrations, >5 μm particle mass concentrations (p=0.021) and maximum total particle mass concentrations (p=0.022). High-speed dental handpieces elevated aerosol concentration levels compared to the levels produced during oral examination.
Conclusions: Air turbine handpieces produced the highest levels of <1 μm aerosols and total particle number concentrations when compared to the other commonly used instruments. In addition, high- and low-speed dental handpieces and ultrasonic scalers elevated the aerosol concentration levels compared to the aerosol levels measured during oral examination. These aerosol-generating procedures, involving air turbine, high- and low-speed handpiece, and ultrasonic scaler, should be performed with caution.
Clinical significance: Aerosol generating dental instruments, especially air turbine, should be used with adequate precautions (rubber dam, high-volume evacuation, FFP-respirators), because aerosols can cause a potential risk for operators and substitution of air turbine for high-speed dental handpiece in poor epidemic situations should be considered to reduce the risk of aerosol transmission.
Keywords: Dental aerosol; Air turbine handpiece; High- and low-speed dental handpiece; Ultrasonic scaler; Optical particle sizer.

== FULL TEXT (key sections) ==

INTRODUCTION
Dentistry is considered one of the most aerosol-generating fields in medicine, since essential and commonly used instruments are rotary or ultrasonic. Aerosol-generating procedures (AGPs) may cause higher infection risk via inhalation. At the start of COVID-19, air turbine handpiece and ultrasonic scaler use were prohibited in most countries; later evidence showed dental procedures produce few particles <10 μm if high-volume suction is used. Aerosol is defined as solid/liquid particles suspended in air regardless of size; medical literature historically used <5 μm. Small aerosols (<5 μm) matter for infection because most respiratory viruses are found in them. Aim: (I) quantity of aerosols generated in various dental operations; (II) size distribution.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
84 patients (54 F / 30 M; mean age 53.3 ± 15.9 y), 253 procedures, Helsinki specialist clinic, Jul 2020–Jan 2021. Three experienced dentists, six operating rooms. Aerosol measured with Optical Particle Sizer (OPS) TSI Model 3330 (0.3–10 μm, 16 size channels), placed ~50 cm above patient head. Background measured 1 min before each procedure. Particle concentrations grouped into <1 μm, 1–5 μm, >5 μm and total. Particle mass concentrations also computed (assuming spherical droplets, density 1 g/cm³). Non-parametric Mann-Whitney / Kruskal-Wallis tests, SPSS v27. Minimum 6 procedures per class.

RESULTS
253 procedures in six rooms. Longest operation: periodontal treatment by hand (median 21.3 min); shortest: intraoral X-ray (median 1.2 min).
- Air turbine handpiece: significantly elevated <1 μm and total particle number concentrations vs background; also elevated <1 μm particle mass concentration. Total particle mass not significant.
- Low-speed dental handpiece: elevated >5 μm maximum particle concentrations, >5 μm particle mass, and maximum total particle mass concentrations.
- High-speed dental handpiece: did NOT significantly elevate any class vs its own preceding background, BUT significantly elevated particle concentration vs oral examination.
- Ultrasonic scaler: slightly elevated >5 μm maximum particle number and mass concentrations.
- Intraoral X-ray: no elevation.
Across procedures (Kruskal-Wallis), median <1 μm, 1–5 μm, >5 μm and total differed significantly; concentrations highest with air turbine.
Half-life: for 22 drilling operations (air turbine + low-speed), median half-life 21.7 min (range 8.5–47.9 min). Air change rate (ACR) 2.76–6.19 /h. Estimated fallow time for air turbine ~98.5 min.
Significant peaks: 7/8 air turbine procedures showed significant peak in particle number; ratio 2/30 for low-speed and 3/6 for high-speed handpieces; 6/12 root canal treatments. Mean particle number emission rate for air turbine ~5.2×10⁸ #/min; root canal ~1.5×10⁸ #/min.

DISCUSSION
Major finding: air turbine generated high levels of <1 μm particles and highest total particle concentration vs other instruments. High- and low-speed handpieces and ultrasonic scalers elevated aerosol vs oral examination. Higher rotational speed associated with higher aerosol generation. Smaller particles drift deeper into airways/lungs and carry more virus replicas. High-volume evacuators (≥300 l/min) effectively reduce spray mist. If a rubber dam cannot be used with the air turbine, the high-speed dental handpiece could be considered a safer, less aerosol-generating option during a pandemic. High aerosol levels after drilling take a long time to decline (mean indoor particle loss rate 0.031–0.035 /min; median half-life ~20 min) — longer than routine room turnover; good ventilation is the first tool; keeping a window open / cross-draught helps if ventilation cannot be optimized. Water/irrigant fluid forms the major portion of dental aerosols (dilutes saliva up to 200-fold); waterborne infections (legionellosis, pneumonia) have been transmitted from dental unit water lines. Maximum aerosol observed during root canal treatment (irrigant load).
Limitations: did not measure whether aerosols contained viruses/microbes; six rooms with differing ventilation (adjusted by background subtraction); high background levels (1970s building, not surgery-level ventilation) may underestimate smaller changes; single device, size only up to 10 μm.
Strengths: large sample, real patients in routine clinical settings with high-volume evacuators, variety of procedures.

CONCLUSION
Highest aerosol production with air turbine handpiece, mostly <1 μm. High- and low-speed dental handpieces and ultrasonic scalers elevated aerosol vs oral examination. Aerosol-generating instruments, especially air turbine, should be used with adequate precautions (rubber dam, high-volume evacuation, FFP-respirators). Substitution of air turbine for high-speed dental handpiece in poor epidemic situations should be considered.
