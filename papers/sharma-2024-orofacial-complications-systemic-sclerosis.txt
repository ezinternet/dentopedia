Orofacial Complications of the Connective Tissue Disease Systemic Sclerosis
Sharma M, Fadl A, Leask A. Journal of Dental Research. 2024;103(7):689-696.
PMID: 38779873 | PMCID: PMC11191658 | DOI: 10.1177/00220345241249408

ABSTRACT
Scleroderma (systemic sclerosis, SSc) is an autoimmune fibrosing connective tissue disease of unknown etiology. SSc patients show increased levels of autoantibodies, profibrotic cytokines, and extracellular matrix remodeling enzymes that collectively cause activated (myo)fibroblasts, the effector cell type of fibrosis. Despite these impacts, no disease-modifying therapy exists; individual symptoms are treated on a patient-to-patient basis. SSc research has been principally focused on symptoms observed in the lung and skin. However, SSc patients display significant oral complications that arise due to fibrosis of the not only skin, causing microstomia, but also the gastrointestinal tract, causing acid reflux, and the oral cavity itself, causing xerostomia and gingival recession. Due to these complications, SSc patients have impaired quality of life, including periodontitis, tooth loss, reduced tongue mobility, and malnutrition. Indeed, due to their characteristic oral presentation, SSc patients are often initially diagnosed by dentists. Despite their clinical importance, the oral complications of SSc are severely understudied; high-quality publications on this topic are scant. However, SSc patients with periodontal complications possess increased levels of matrix metalloproteinase-9 and chemokines, such as interleukin-6 and chemokine (C-X-C motif) ligand-4. Although many unsuccessful clinical trials, mainly exploring the antifibrotic effects of anti-inflammatory agents, have been conducted in SSc, none have used oral symptoms, which may be more amenable to anti-inflammatory drugs, as clinical end points. This review summarizes the current state of knowledge regarding oral complications in SSc with the goal of inspiring future research in this extremely important and underinvestigated area.

What Is Systemic Sclerosis?

Scleroderma (systemic sclerosis, SSc), an autoimmune rheumatic connective tissue disease of unknown etiology, is characterized by a hyperactive immune response and epithelial and vascular damage, which can result in progressive tissue fibrosis affecting not only the skin but also the internal organs and oral cavity. The incidence of SSc varies within populations, with the highest occurrence (47/100,000) in Canadian First Nations. SSc is more prevalent in females compared to males (4:1 ratio), with prevalence highest in middle age and in underprivileged households. Juvenile (childhood-onset) SSc is substantially more rare but has similar clinical features to adult SSc. SSc does not follow Mendelian inheritance patterns, yet numerous studies have suggested that SSc has an epigenetic component and tends to occur in families that have a history of autoimmune disorders. Genetic susceptibility is correlated with alterations in the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system.

According to the ACR/EULAR classification criteria, skin thickening of the fingers extending proximal to the metacarpophalangeal joints is sufficient for a diagnosis of SSc. Alternatively, 7 additional criteria: skin thickening of the fingers, fingertip lesions, telangiectasia, abnormal nailfold capillaries, lung involvement, Raynaud's phenomenon, and the presence of SSc-related autoantibodies. SSc is divided into 2 classifications based on degree of organ involvement: limited cutaneous SSc (lcSSc) and diffuse cutaneous SSc (dcSSc). lcSSc has localized fibrosis restricted to skin, formerly "CREST" (calcinosis, Raynaud's, esophageal dysmotility, sclerodactyly, telangiectasia). dcSSc patients have progressive thickening of skin and internal organs including kidneys and lungs, and significantly higher mortality. Pulmonary fibrosis, the major cause of mortality in dcSSc, has prevalence ~30% and 10-y mortality ~40%. Prevalence of lcSSc, dcSSc, and sine scleroderma was recently found to be 64%, 28%, and 8%.

A Mechanically Stiff, Hypoxic Microenvironment Drives SSc Fibrosis

Environmental stimuli such as silica and solvents proposed as possible triggers. One of earliest events preceding SSc fibrosis is endothelial cell injury and apoptosis causing vascular damage and tissue hypoxia. Vascular damage presents clinically as Raynaud's phenomenon and digital ulcers. Hypoxic microenvironment through HIF-1α leads to fibrosis by causing dermal fibroblasts to overproduce ECM. Endothelial/epithelial injury, with heightened immune response, causes release of fibrogenic chemokines TGF-β, endothelin 1 (ET-1), IL-1a, promoting fibroblast differentiation into contractile myofibroblasts and excessive remodeling of collagen-rich ECM. No overall disease-modifying treatment; symptoms managed by individual need. Oral complications, although they occur early and are easy to identify, are severely understudied.

Orofacial Implications of SSc

Orofacial manifestations affect up to 80% of SSc patients, involving skin, muscles, periodontium, salivary glands, TMJ, trigeminal nerve, and mandibular bone. Some oral complications arise as medication side effects: oral candidiasis (corticosteroid therapy), mouth ulcers (methotrexate), calcium channel blocker-induced gingival hyperplasia.

Facial Skin and Muscle Rigidity
Facial skin thickening and muscle rigidity result in mask-like face, loss of facial expressions, microstomia (reduced mouth opening). Microstomia observed in 52% to 80% of SSc patients, related to disease severity. It impairs food intake, promotes malnutrition (hindering mastication/deglutition), impairs oral hygiene, limits dental exams/treatment. Microstomia significantly more evident in dcSSc vs lcSSc — 2.5 times higher Mouth Handicap in SSc Scale (MHISS) score, impacting OHRQoL. Fibrosis may extend to oropharyngoesophageal mucosa, tongue, hard/soft palate → difficulty swallowing/speaking, voice changes.

Telangiectasia
Dilated superficial blood vessels on skin (hand, face) and oral mucosa; indicators of underlying vascular changes.

Periodontitis and Gingival Inflammation
SSc patients have more severe periodontitis indicators vs controls: periodontal attachment loss, gingival recession, higher plaque/gingival indices, increased inflammatory biomarkers in biofilm and GCF. Advanced periodontitis (stage III/IV) higher in dcSSc (90%) vs lcSSc (60%); increased attachment loss in dcSSc. However exacerbated gingival inflammation observed in lcSSc; in dcSSc inflammation may be masked by fibrosis. Gingiva in SSc do NOT exhibit fibrotic phenotype of skin (scar-less healing; gingival fibroblasts relatively resistant to myofibroblast differentiation). Widening of PDL affects >1/3 of SSc patients — cause unclear (excess collagen in PDL, increased occlusal force from bulky masticatory muscles). Etiology of altered periodontal conditions multifactorial: xerostomia, restricted hygiene from microstomia, microbiome alterations, microangiopathy, immune dysregulation, pathological ECM remodeling.

Salivary Gland Dysfunction
Pathological salivary gland changes from SSc fibrosis (sicca syndrome) or lymphocytic sialadenitis in secondary Sjögren's syndrome. Sicca syndrome reported in 70% of SSc patients; secondary Sjögren's syndrome (American-European Consensus Group criteria) affects up to 14% of SSc patients, significantly associated with lcSSc subtype. Reduced salivary flow (xerostomia) primarily from salivary gland fibrosis. Xerostomia increases susceptibility to periodontitis, tooth decay, erosion by reducing saliva's buffering of oral acidity from bacteria and SSc-associated gastroesophageal reflux.

Dysgeusia
Taste disturbance reported significantly in SSc — bitter, sour, or metallic. Etiology uninvestigated; possibly xerostomia, secondary Sjögren's, microbial colonization in pockets, or gustatory nerve changes.

Temporomandibular Joint Involvement
SSc patients have restricted mandibular movements, prone to TMD and jaw pain. TMJ pathological changes: condylar resorption, calcinosis, erosive synovitis, synovial fibrosis. Difficult to determine if impaired function from TMJ involvement or fibrosis of skin/muscle/subcutaneous tissue. lcSSc vs dcSSc TMJ involvement uninvestigated.

Mandibular and Facial Bone Resorption
Multifactorial; influenced by SSc microvasculopathy and excessive facial skin/muscle tension → ischemia and pressure necrosis. Evident in muscle attachment areas: mandibular angle, condylar head, coronoid process, zygomatic arch. Mandibular condyle most susceptible — prevalence 10% to 50%; cases of secondary apertognathia (open bite) due to condylar resorption reported.

Trigeminal Neuropathy
Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) more prevalent in SSc than general population. TN signs may precede SSc diagnosis (presenting symptom). Manifestations: severe, pricking, intermittent electric shock-like facial pain triggered by chewing/talking/brushing. Potential factors: neural tissue fibrosis → increased endoneurial pressure, damage to myelinated trigeminal fibers, vasculitic changes, or rarely secondary to severe mandibular resorption/compression neuropathy.

Alterations in Oral Microbiota
Studies scant. Higher subgingival pathogen levels in lcSSc vs healthy controls; correlation with severe periodontitis in lcSSc; exclusive association with specific IL-1 gene polymorphisms in lcSSc. Tongue swabs showed lower amounts in SSc group. Conversely a taxon significantly increased in gut microbiota of SSc, especially lcSSc, possibly contributing to GI involvement. Limited by sample size and scope.

Insights into Molecular Basis — Interleukins
Naive T cells differentiate into Th1 and Th2; Th1 cytokines suppress ECM deposition, Th2 promote it. In SSc, overproduction of profibrotic Th2 cytokines IL-4, IL-5, IL-6, IL-10, IL-13. IL-6 and IL-10 elevated at early disease phase, correlate with extent of skin fibrosis. Tocilizumab (TCZ), IL-6 receptor α inhibitor, alleviates fibrotic phenotype of lesional dermal fibroblasts and impairs bleomycin-induced skin fibrosis. Elevated IL-6 in saliva of periodontitis patients but does NOT correlate with progression. SSc patients show elevated IL-6 in GCF but no correlation with clinical periodontitis parameters. Serum IL-6 increases in early SSc (<3 y) promoting inflammatory phase/vascular damage, then reduces as SSc progresses with increase in IL-13 (fibrotic stage). Elevated oral IL-6 may simply reflect early phase of SSc / hyperactive immune response, may not be direct contributor to oral disease progression.
IL-1β stimulates collagen deposition and myofibroblast differentiation; increased in serum/skin, correlated with fibrotic/inflammatory skin scores; key modulator of periodontal tissue destruction. In lcSSc, periodontitis in 31 of 38 patients; IL-1β polymorphism (C3953 allele) linked to pathogenic bacteria.
Th17 produces IL-17 (proinflammatory) promoting neutrophil migration; elevated IL-17 mRNA/protein in lesional skin/PBMCs of SSc; 6-fold increase in IL-17 immunoreactivity in gingival tissues of periodontitis patients. Role of IL-17 in oral complications of SSc not yet investigated.

CXCL-4
Chemokine (C-X-C motif) ligand 4 (CXCL-4): antiangiogenic, profibrotic chemokine associated with vasculopathy and inflammatory cell recruitment; biomarker for SSc severity. CXCL-4 in GCF of SSc markedly elevated vs controls; correlates with increased periodontitis measures (clinical attachment level, probing depth, gingival index).

Matrix Metalloproteinase-9
MMPs are extracellular endopeptidases in pathological ECM remodeling, contributing to periodontal tissue destruction. Elevated serum MMP-9 (gelatinase/type IV collagenase) in dcSSc correlates with serum TGF-β1 and degree of skin fibrosis (can activate latent TGF-β). MMP-9 increased in biofilm samples of SSc, positively correlates with IL-6. High CXCL-4 and MMP-9 in GCF associated with adverse periodontal parameters (clinical attachment level, probing depth, plaque index, gingival index). MMP-9 and CXCL4, but NOT IL-6, significantly correlated with early-onset (<2 y) disease.

Role of Skin Fibrosis in Clinical Complications
Degree of microstomia (caused by cutaneous fibrosis) directly correlates with oral complications: clinical attachment loss, number of missing/decayed teeth, overall periodontal parameters. NO correlation between skin thickness score and oral features. Suggests current clinical-trial methods (skin thickness score) inappropriate for evaluating oral complications; degree of microstomia could be a future clinical trial end point.

Managing Orofacial Complications of SSc
No specific approved SSc treatment. Management ranges from palliative to dental/surgical. Mouth exercises improve interincisal distance in microstomia. Severe microstomia may hinder prosthesis construction, necessitating commissurotomy; impaired wound healing may → scarring/recurrence; foldable silicone prostheses as alternative. Xerostomia managed with sialagogues (pilocarpine tincture, anetholtrithione, salivary spray substitute). Telangiectasia typically left untreated; light-based treatments improve but do not prevent recurrence. PDL widening typically needs no intervention. Despite elevated periodontitis rates and impaired bone turnover, dental implants can be placed successfully without exacerbating morbidity or conflicting with systemic treatment; implant survival unaffected by SSc; can enhance OHRQoL. Require thorough preoperative risk assessment and regular follow-up; depend on SSc severity, vasculopathy, bone quality. Implants may be contraindicated with severe vasculopathy. Facial/mandibular bone resorption has no treatment options (routine follow-up). Surgical TMJ reconstruction after condylar resorption shows promise restoring functional occlusion and mandibular movement.

Conclusions and Future Directions
MMP-9 and CXCL4, but not IL-6, in GCF correlated with early-stage SSc and several periodontitis parameters. Need further investigation into CXCL-4 and MMPs (MMP-9) as mediators of oral/periodontal disease in SSc and as potential predictive biomarkers. Future clinical trials could incorporate oral end points such as degree of microstomia. Although an anti-IL-6 antibody did not affect skin fibrosis in SSc, such treatment improved periodontal outcomes in rheumatoid arthritis. Oral complications of SSc can indirectly cause malnutrition and negatively affect psychological well-being.
