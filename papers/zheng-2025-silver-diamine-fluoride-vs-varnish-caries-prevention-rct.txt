Randomized Clinical Trial on Caries Prevention of Silver Diamine Fluoride

Introduction

The primary upper anterior teeth exhibit a higher prevalence of early childhood caries (ECC) among preschool-aged cohorts in Hong Kong, and lesions are readily detectable through clinical examination. According to the World Dental Federation (FDI) Oral Health Atlas, most children with ECC never receive treatment. Conventional dental care in most parts of the world cannot cope with the high ECC prevalence, particularly due to a shortage of resources. Caries prevention is thus extremely important. The FDI advocates the use of fluoride for the prevention of ECC. The current standard of professional care for caries prevention is to use 5% sodium fluoride varnish (FV) 2 to 4 times per year. A systematic review by the Cochrane Collaboration concluded that the application of FV can reduce caries in primary teeth development by 37%. An alternative solution is 38% silver diamine fluoride (SDF), which contains a higher content of fluoride (44,800 ppm fluoride) than 5% FV (22,600 ppm fluoride) to promote remineralization. In addition, SDF includes silver, which inhibits growth of cariogenic biofilms. However, SDF has a metallic taste, which may cause discomfort for patients.

Clinical studies suggested that SDF prevented ECC development on adjacent sound teeth when SDF was applied to carious lesions in children. A literature search in 2020 found no well-designed clinical trial of a clinically meaningful duration assessing the caries-preventive effect of SDF on sound primary teeth. Therefore, the objective of this study was to assess the caries preventive effect of the application of SDF on sound primary upper anterior teeth among 3- to 5-y-old children for 30 mo. We hypothesized that SDF would be superior to FV.

Method

Trial Design: This stratified-randomized, active-controlled clinical trial was based on a double-blind, parallel-assigned design with 1:1 allocation to 2 arms. The local institutional review board (UW-18-619) approved the study. The trial was prospectively registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT04075474). The full protocol was published in an open access journal.

Participants, Setting, and Location: The study was conducted in kindergarten classrooms. The first child was enrolled on October 22, 2020, and the last child was evaluated on October 11, 2023. The inclusion criteria were children aged 3 to 4 y who were generally healthy and attending the first year of kindergarten. Children were excluded if they were uncooperative or were taking long-term medications such as methylphenidate. The water supply in Hong Kong contains 0.5-ppm fluoride.

Clinical Examination: One trained examiner performed the baseline and follow-up examinations. The 6 primary upper anterior teeth were the studied teeth for intervention. Caries was diagnosed at the cavitation level. The caries status of each studied tooth was recorded at the tooth surface level by assigning a dmfs score. A caries lesion was diagnosed as arrested if its surface was smooth and hard on probing; a lesion was recorded as active if it was soft on probing. Examinations were conducted at 6-mo intervals postbaseline. Cohen's kappa values for assessing the caries status and caries experience were at least 0.90 for every examination period.

Intervention: The children received an application of either 38% SDF (treatment group) or 5% FV (active control group). The SDF or FV was applied once every 6 to 30 mo since the first application. At each treatment session, a trained dentist used a microbrush to apply SDF (lot 21109, Advantage Arrest; Elevate Oral Care) or FV (Duraphat Varnish; Colgate-Palmolive). Each tooth surface (sound or decayed) of the 6 primary upper anterior teeth was dry-brushed before receiving the application. On average, each child had SDF or FV applied on 18 surfaces of the 6 primary upper anterior teeth. This means that a child could receive 4.8 mg of fluoride in the SDF group or 9.1 mg of fluoride in the FV group every 6 mo.

Outcome: The primary outcome was the increment of carious tooth surfaces, measured by the mean number of new carious tooth surfaces per child at 30 mo. The secondary outcome was the incidence of carious tooth surfaces, measured by the odds ratio of carious tooth surface incidence among the primary upper anterior teeth at 30 mo.

Sample Size Calculation: Drawn from a previous clinical trial, the mean ± SD number of new carious tooth surfaces found in the 62 children receiving SDF was 0.47 ± 0.87, and that in the 61 children receiving FV was 0.70 ± 0.84 at the 30-mo review. This difference corresponded to a prevented fraction of 33% more in the SDF group when compared with the FV group. With statistical power set at 0.9 and a type I error at 5% for a 2-sided test, the required sample size to test the primary outcome was 292 for each group. To accommodate a 20% loss to follow-up, this 30-mo clinical trial recruited a total of 730 children at baseline examination.

Randomization: The children were first categorized as having either an increased caries risk (dmft >0) or a low caries risk (dmft = 0). The children were then randomized within strata into the SDF or FV group in blocks of 8. The random number sequence was kept by a statistician, and opaque sealed envelopes were used to conceal the allocation sequence until the interventions were applied.

Blinding: Neither the examiner nor the children or their parents were informed of the treatment group allocation. Another operator applied SDF or FV after the oral examination.

Statistical Analysis: A modified intention-to-treat analysis method was used to retain data only from participants who completed the final examination. The primary outcome was the number of new carious tooth surfaces per child at 30-mo evaluation, compared between groups by Student's t test (or Mann-Whitney test for nonnormally distributed data). The secondary outcome measured the incidence of carious tooth surfaces over a 30-mo period. The tooth surfaces were categorized as smooth (buccal/lingual) or proximal (mesial/distal). These data were analyzed by generalized estimating equation (GEE) with a logit link function and exchangeable correlation structure to account for clustering of tooth surfaces within children. Statistical significance was defined as a 2-sided P < 0.05.

Results

A total of 730 children (365 per group) from 13 kindergartens were recruited at baseline. The current study analyzed 14,904 tooth surfaces from 621 children who completed the 30-mo study. The loss to follow-up at the child level was 16% (58/365) in the SDF group and 14% (51/365) in the FV group (P = 0.84). No statistically significant differences in demographic backgrounds, oral health–related habits, or clinical characteristics between the SDF and FV groups at baseline. Over the 30 mo, 176 children visited a dentist (90 in the SDF group). A total of 41 children had fillings (21 in the SDF group): there was no significant difference in the probability of receiving a restoration between the groups (21/307 vs 20/314, P = 0.492).

By the 30-mo examination, the mean incident distribution of new carious tooth surfaces was 0.35 ± 1.09 for SDF vs 0.54 ± 1.50 for FV (Mann-Whitney test, P = 0.048). A total of 110 children (110/621, 17.7%) developed new carious lesions on 274 upper anterior tooth surfaces (274/14,344, 1.9%). Caries incidence at the child level was 15.6% (48/307) in the SDF group and 19.7% (62/314) in the FV group (P = 0.262).

After adjusting for clustering and confounding (tooth surface type), a significant difference between the groups was observed in odds of developing new carious lesions at the tooth surface level at 30 mo. At 30-mo follow-up, the percentage of new carious tooth surfaces developed was 1.5% (108/7,224) in the SDF group and 2.2% (166/7,384) in the FV group. The final multivariable model of GEE analysis confirmed that the SDF group had a lower risk than the FV group in developing new carious tooth surfaces (adjusted odds ratio, 0.61; 95% CI, 0.39 to 0.95; P = 0.029). Analysis of 10,680 tooth surfaces from the 433 children who did not visit a dentist showed the same result (adjusted odds ratio, 0.58; 95% CI, 0.34 to 1.00; P = 0.048). The prevented fraction (1 – relative risk) for the SDF group as compared with the FV group was 39%.

Discussion

The present study is the first long-term clinical trial to examine the effectiveness of SDF in preventing ECC in primary upper anterior teeth. SDF has a dual mechanism of action, combining the remineralizing ability of fluoride and the antibacterial properties of silver. SDF at 38% also has a higher fluoride concentration (44,800 ppm) than 5% FV (22,600 ppm). This trial confirms that the semiannual application of SDF is more effective than FV in preventing caries in primary upper anterior teeth when used for >24 mo for proximal and smooth tooth surfaces. SDF demonstrated a significantly lower increase in caries, with an average increment of 0.35 surfaces, as compared with FV with an increment of 0.54 surfaces over 30 mo — a difference of 0.19 surfaces. The adjusted odds ratio was 0.61 (P = 0.029), meaning a 39% reduction in the odds of developing caries.

A systematic review and meta-analysis on caries-preventive effects of SDF (2019) found that annual applications of 38% SDF led to a 54% reduction (95% CI, 27% to 73%) in incidence of new caries as compared with quarterly applications of 5% FV, but with significant heterogeneity. The safety of topical SDF use in preschool children has been demonstrated; a 20-kg child would not reach toxic levels even with nearly 400 applications. The intermittent application schedule (18 applications over 30 mo) provided a substantial safety margin against dental fluorosis; no parents reported adverse effects.

Limitations: The incidence of carious tooth surfaces was relatively low in both groups, which decreases statistical power and increases risk of type II error. This study employed a 2-arm design without a no-treatment control group for ethical reasons. The results may not be applicable to posterior teeth. The WHO has included SDF in its Model List of Essential Medicines for children and adults.

Conclusion

When applied every 6 mo, SDF provided superior caries prevention after 24 mo for proximal and smooth tooth surfaces on primary upper anterior teeth when compared with FV in young children. (ClinicalTrials.gov NCT04075474)
