Background

Periodontitis is a chronic multifactorial inflammatory disease characterized by the progressive destruction of tooth-supporting tissues, including the periodontal ligament and alveolar bone (Van Dyke et al.). It is one of the most prevalent oral diseases worldwide and a leading cause of tooth loss in adults. According to the 2017 Classification of Periodontal and Peri-Implant Diseases and Conditions (AAP/EFP), periodontitis is now defined by a multidimensional staging and grading framework that replaces the outdated “chronic” and “aggressive” categories. This system allows classification by severity, complexity, and risk of progression, thereby improving diagnostic accuracy and prognostic value (Papapanou et al.; Tonetti et al.) The pathogenesis of periodontitis involves a complex interplay between pathogenic microorganisms in dental plaque and the host immune response, which results in the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), interleukin-1β (IL-1β) (de Oliveira et al.; Lee et al.). These mediators stimulate osteoclast differentiation and promote alveolar bone resorption, contributing to the progression of periodontal tissue destruction (Kim et al.).

Current treatment strategies for periodontitis include mechanical debridement and adjunctive use of antimicrobial agents or host modulation therapy. However, long-term use of antibiotics and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) can result in serious adverse effects, including gastrointestinal ulceration, alteration of gut microbiota with opportunistic infections, and the emergence of antibiotic-resistant strains. Moreover, the efficacy of these drugs often diminishes upon discontinuation, contributing to frequent recurrence of periodontal disease(Oh and Yu; Van Dyke). These limitations underscore the importance of developing safer, more sustainable therapies. In this context, natural agents such asextract (PBE), which possess anti-inflammatory and bone-protective properties, may provide promising alternatives to conventional treatment strategies. In recent years, plant-derived polyphenolic compounds have attracted significant attention due to their anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and bone-protective properties (German et al.). Natural substances such as green tea catechins (GTC) and chlorogenic acid (CGA) have demonstrated promising results in experimental periodontitis models, including the inhibition of osteoclastogenesis, reduction of oxidative stress, and suppression of pro-inflammatory cytokine production (German et al.; Lee et al.; Nishida et al.). Several studies have shown that GTC attenuates alveolar bone loss and inflammatory infiltration in ligature-induced models, while CGA has been reported to modulate NF-κB and MAPK pathways, thereby inhibiting osteoclast differentiation and inflammatory signaling (Park and Yoon; Shen et al.; Yoshinaga et al.).

(Kom.) Nakai, commonly known as “Chamnamul” in Korea, has been used as an edible vegetable as well as a traditional medicinal herb in East Asia for its anti-inflammatory, anti-bacterial, antioxidant, and hepatoprotective effects (Sathasivam et al.; Wu et al.).species, commonly used in folk medicine, are applied to the oral cavity due to their antiseptic and anti-inflammatory properties. They are used as mouthwashes and gargles to soothe inflamed gums and freshen breath (Bakhshi et al.; Lavaee et al.). The anti-inflammatory and antibacterial properties ofhave made it a valuable remedy for infections and inflammatory conditions in the oral cavity. Despite its ethnomedicinal relevance, the potential ofextract (PBE) in preventing or ameliorating periodontitis has not been thoroughly investigated. Preliminary phytochemical analyses have revealed that PBE contains several bioactive flavonoids and phenolic acids, including chlorogenic acid, luteolin-7-O-glucuronide, and apigenin-7-O-glucuronide, which are known to modulate inflammatory responses and bone metabolism (Sathasivam et al.; Shen et al.). While the individual components of PBE have demonstrated biological activity in other inflammatory conditions, their combined effects in the context of periodontal disease remain unexplored.

To our knowledge, no previous in vivo study has systematically compared the anti-periodontitis efficacy of PBE with established polyphenolic agents such as GTC and CGA. Moreover, few studies have addressed the synergistic potential of combining these compounds (Ni et al.). Recent research suggests that co-administration of polyphenols may produce additive or synergistic effects in suppressing inflammatory pathways and oxidative damage. Therefore, evaluating the comparative and combinatory efficacy of GTC and CGA alongside PBE may help position PBE within the landscape of plant-based periodontal therapeutics (Yanagimoto et al.).

In this study, we aimed to evaluate the protective effects of PBE in a rat model of ligature-induced periodontitis. We also compared its efficacy to that of GTC, CGA, and their combination (MIX) to contextualize PBE’s therapeutic potential among well-characterized natural anti-inflammatory agents. Additionally, phytochemical profiling of PBE was conducted using High performance liquidchromatography (HPLC) to identify its major active constituents. This study provides new insights into the potential ofas a candidate natural agent for the prevention and treatment of periodontal disease.

Methods

Phytochemical analysis ofextract (PBE)

(Kom.) Nakai were purchased from Gyeongdong Market (Seoul, South Korea). PBE was kindly provided by CNS Pharm Korea (Seoul, Republic of Korea) and was mixed with an equal amount of food-grade maltodextrin (batch No. CPB-001). The voucher specimen was submitted to the herbarium of the host institution for future reference. For phytochemical analysis, HPLC was performed using a Waters system (Milford, MA, USA) equipped with a quaternary pump, auto-sampler, and photodiode array detector. Separation was achieved on a C18 column (250 × 4.6 mm, 5 μm, Luna, Phenomenex, Torrance, CA) using 0.1% trifluoroacetic (solvent A) and acetonitrile (solvent B) in a gradient elution at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min as follows: 0–5 min, 5–5% B; 10–30 min, 15–40% B; 35–40 min, 100-5% B; 40–45 min, 5–5% B. The detection wavelength was 330 nm, the column temperature was maintained at 40 °C, and the injection volume was 10 µL. Chlorogenic acid, luteolin-7-O-glucuronide, apigenin-7-O-glucuronide, and luteolin-3-O-glucuronide were identified and quantified using reference standards purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). All solvents were purchased from J.T. Baker (Phillipsburg, NJ, USA).

Ligature-induced periodontitis assays

Seven-week-old male Sprague-Dawley rats (= 5–7 per group) were obtained from Koatech (Pyeongtaek, Republic of Korea). Animals were housed under standard laboratory conditions (temperature 22 ± 2 °C, humidity 55 ± 10%, 12 h light/dark cycle) with ad libitum access to food and water. All procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Jeonbuk National University Hospital (Approval No. JBNUH-IACUC-2024-22). A total of six experimental groups were included in the study. The normal control group consisted of 5 animals, whereas each of the remaining five groups included 7 animals. Accordingly, the sample size was reported as= 5–7. Rats were anesthetized, and a 4 − 0 silk braided black nonabsorbable surgical suture (WEGO-SILK, 50 cm, S43183-50, Foosin Medical Supplies Inc., Ltd., Weihai, Shandong, China) was tied around the maxillary second molars bilaterally to induce periodontitis. From the day of ligature placement, test substances were administered by oral gavage once daily for 14 consecutive days. On day 15, the animals were euthanized, and the maxillae were harvested for analysis (de Oliveira et al.; Yimam et al.). To evaluate dose-dependent effects, PBE was administered in three different doses (150, 200, and 300 mg/kg/day), and its efficacy was compared with that of ISD (250 mg/kg/day,L. extract and Magnolia cortex extract-containing herbal formulation, Insadolplus, Dongkook Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd., Seoul, Republic of Korea) used as a positive control (Kim et al.). In a separate comparative study, PBE at 150 mg/kg/day was evaluated against green tea catechin (GTC, 20 mg/kg, Sigma-Aldrich), chlorogenic acid (CGA, 20 mg/kg, Sigma-Aldrich), and their combination (MIX, 20 + 20 mg/kg, 1:1 weight ratio), all of which were administered following the same dosing schedule and oral route (Lee et al.; Nishida et al.).

Gross morphological assessment and bone loss quantification

After sacrifice, maxillae were dissected, defleshed, and photographed using a stereomicroscope. The alveolar bone loss area was measured between the cementoenamel junction and the alveolar bone crest using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA).

Histological analysis (H&E staining)

Maxillary tissues were fixed in 10% neutral-buffered formalin, decalcified, embedded in paraffin, and sectioned at 5 μm. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining was performed to evaluate tissue architecture. The vertical distance from the cementoenamel junction to the alveolar bone crest (CEJ-ABC distance) was measured to assess bone loss (Gennaro et al.).

TRAP staining for osteoclast detection

Tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP) staining was performed using the TRACP & Alkaline phosphatase (ALP) Double Stain Kit (Takara, Shiga, Japan) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. TRAP-positive multinucleated cells on the bone surface were observed under a light microscope. Osteoclast activity was quantified by manually counting TRAP-positive multinucleated cells (≥ 3 nuclei) within regions of interest (ROI) using ImageJ software (NIH) (Wu et al.).

Immunohistochemistry (IHC)

Paraffin-embedded sections were deparaffinized, rehydrated, and subjected to antigen retrieval. Sections were incubated with primary antibodies against CD45 (Abcam, Waltham, MA, USA), IL-1β (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA), and TNF-α (LSBio, Seattle, WA, USA). After incubation with appropriate HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies, the signals were visualized using the ImmPACT DAB Substrate Kit, Peroxidase (VECTOR Laboratories, Newark, CA, USA), and counterstained with hematoxylin. Quantification was based on the percentage of positively stained cells relative to the total number of cells within the ROI determined by thresholding and particle analysis using ImageJ software(NIH).

Statistical analysis

All data are expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Statistical analyses were performed using one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s post hoc test to compare each treatment group with the LIG group. Analyses were conducted based on the exact number of animals in each group (= 5–7 per group, as indicated in the figure legends), and p-values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant. *< 0.05, **< 0.01, ***< 0.001 and ***< 0.0001. GraphPad Prism version 9.0 (GraphPad Software, La Jolla, CA, USA) was used for all analyses.

Results

Quantification of marker compounds of PBE

PBE was analyzed via HPLC. Through a comparison of the retention time (Rt) and mass spectra with reference standards, we identified three phytochemicals predominantly present in the extract. It was confirmed that the quantity of each compound was 13.7 ± 0.01 mg/g of chlorogenic acid, 3.6 ± 0.06 mg/g of luteolin 7-O-glucuronide, 3.8 ± 0.04 mg/g of apigenin 7-O-glucuronide, and 2.5 ± 0.02 mg/g of luteolin 3-O-glucuronide (Fig.).

PBE attenuates ligature-induced alveolar bone loss

To evaluate the protective effect of PBE on periodontitis-induced bone loss, gross morphological analysis was conducted (Fig.A). Quantitative assessment in Fig.B showed that bone loss was reduced in all PBE-treated groups, indicating a protective role for PBE in preventing alveolar bone destruction.

PBE reduces osteoclast activity in ligature-induced periodontitis

To assess the effect of PBE on osteoclast activity, TRAP staining was performed (Fig.A). The LIG group displayed a marked increase in TRAP-positive cells, whereas PBE treatment, especially at higher doses, reduced TRAP staining intensity. This trend was confirmed by quantification in Fig.B, which showed a reduction in TRAP-positive cell numbers in the PBE-treated groups.

PBE suppresses immune cell infiltration and inflammatory cytokine expression

To investigate the anti-inflammatory effects of PBE, IHC staining was performed for CD45, IL-1β, and TNF-α (Fig.A). The LIG group exhibited strong staining for all markers, indicative of immune cell infiltration and inflammatory response. In contrast, PBE-treated groups showed visibly reduced staining, especially for CD45 and IL-1β. The quantitative data in Fig.B supported these findings, showing decreased positive staining area across all evaluated markers following PBE treatment.

To determine the roles of PBE constituents, GTC, CGA, and their combination (MIX) were tested (Fig.A). These findings suggest that both GTC and CGA may contribute to the bone-protective effects observed with PBE, possibly accounting for part of its efficacy.

Discussion

In this study, we demonstrated that PBE effectively attenuates ligature-induced alveolar bone loss and inflammatory responses in a rat model of periodontitis. In addition to PBE, GTC, CGA, and their combination were also evaluated. These treatment groups similarly reduced alveolar bone loss and inflammation, supporting the role of bioactive natural compounds in modulating periodontal disease (Gennaro et al.; Vargas-Sanchez et al.). Previous studies have demonstrated that GTC can reduce alveolar bone destruction and inflammation in periodontitis models by modulating oxidative stress and inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-1β) (Fan et al.; Gennaro et al.). Similarly, CGA has been shown to suppress LPS-induced inflammation and inhibit osteoclast differentiation, contributing to bone preservation (Huang et al.; Kwak et al.; Nishida et al.). By comparing PBE with these well-characterized agents, our study aimed to contextualize the therapeutic potential of PBE within a broader framework of established natural anti-inflammatory interventions. Numerous polyphenols, including GTC, CGA, curcumin, and resveratrol, have demonstrated anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and bone-preserving effects in periodontitis and other inflammatory conditions (Basu et al.; Jung et al.; Ni et al.; Nicolin et al.). Additionally, recent studies have suggested that combining polyphenolic compounds such as GTC and CGA may exert synergistic effects in modulating periodontal inflammation (Yanagimoto et al.). These findings provide a scientific rationale for selecting GTC and CGA as reference comparators in evaluating the efficacy of PBE. Notably, previous studies have also reported the potential of combining polyphenolic compounds such as catechins and CGA to achieve enhanced anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. For example, co-administration of these compounds has been associated with synergistic suppression of inflammatory cytokine production and oxidative stress in various inflammatory models (Colon and Nerín; Yang et al.). This supports the observed additive or synergistic effects in the MIX group and highlights the value of exploring phytochemical combinations in periodontal therapy. Notably, the selection of GTC and CGA was grounded in their relevance to periodontal disease pathology and prior validation in similar models (Lee et al.; Nishida et al.). GTC possesses well-documented anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activities and has demonstrated efficacy in reducing periodontal inflammation and oxidative stress in experimental models of periodontitis (Venkateswara et al.). CGA, a major phenolic compound found in numerous medicinal plants, has also been shown to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine expression and inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption via NF-κB and RANKL signaling pathways (Kwak et al.). These complementary mechanisms may explain the synergistic effects observed in the MIX group of our study.

The beneficial effects of PBE appear to be mediated through modulation of both osteoclast activity and inflammatory cytokine expression. TRAP staining confirmed that PBE treatment led to a reduction in the number of TRAP-positive osteoclasts on the alveolar bone surface. In parallel, immunohistochemical analyses revealed decreased expression of CD45, IL-1β, and TNF-α in PBE-treated tissues. These findings are consistent with prior studies showing that plant-derived polyphenols, such as theaflavins, effectively suppressed both osteoclast activity and inflammatory cell infiltration on periodontal tissue surfaces in ligature-induced rat models (Wu et al.), as well as ethanol extracts of medicinal plants that attenuated osteoclastogenesis and inflammatory markers (Shin and Hwang). These findings suggest that PBE suppresses the local immune-inflammatory cascade associated with periodontitis. Although our study did not investigate molecular signaling pathways directly, prior research has shown that compounds like CGA and luteolin-7-O-glucuronide can modulate key regulators such as NF-κB and MAPK pathways, which are critical in osteoclastogenesis and proinflammatory cytokine production. CGA has been demonstrated to inhibit NF-κB, AP-1, and MAPKs in inflammatory conditions, while luteolin-7-O-glucuronide effectively suppresses NF-κB activation, MAPK phosphorylation, and downstream cytokines like IL-1β and TNF-α in macrophage models. Future mechanistic studies should validate whether PBE mediates its effect via these or other signaling pathways (Cho et al.; Varela-Lopez et al.).

The observed beneficial effects of PBE may be attributed to the bioactive phytochemicals identified in its UPLC profile. Specifically, CGA, known to suppress NF-κB, MAPK, and AP-1 signaling, luteolin-7-O-glucuronide, which inhibits MAPK activation in macrophages, and apigenin-7-O-β-D-glucuronide, which downregulates pro-inflammatory cytokines via AP-1/MAPK blockade, have all been reported to possess anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Taken together, these compounds likely act synergistically to attenuate periodontal inflammation and inhibit bone resorption (Cho et al.; Hu et al.; Zhou et al.). UPLC analysis of the PBE used in this study identified CGA, luteolin 7-O-glucuronide, and apigenin 7-O-glucuronide as the principal components, with CGA being the most abundant. Notably, GTC was not detected in our extract, likely due to regional and seasonal variations in catechin content reported in a previous study (Kim et al.). Nevertheless, GTC was included as a reference compound based on its well-documented therapeutic effects in periodontal disease, allowing us to assess its potential synergistic interaction with CGA. Although luteolin 7-O-glucuronide and apigenin 7-O-glucuronide were not evaluated in vivo due to the limited availability of purified compounds, their biological relevance will be explored in future studies.

When compared with the positive control ISD which has demonstrated anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects in periodontal therapy (Sastravaha et al.; Shashikumar et al.), PBE exhibited comparable efficacy in suppressing inflammatory markers and osteoclast presence. These findings suggest thatmay serve as a promising alternative or adjunct to currently used plant-based periodontal therapeutics. From a translational perspective, the relevance of PBE in human health requires further investigation. Although precise clinical dosage extrapolation from rodents is complex, previous nutritional studies have reported thatis commonly consumed as a food and medicinal herb in East Asia. Estimated safe intake levels in humans have ranged from 100 to 300 mg/day in dietary contexts, and its use has been associated with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and hepatoprotective benefits (Kim et al.; Lu et al.). However, the pharmacological dosage used in this study (150–300 mg/kg in rats) suggests that higher concentrations may be required for therapeutic effects. Moreover, factors such as low oral bioavailability and rapid metabolism of phytochemicals in humans could limit efficacy (D’Archivio et al.,; Manach et al.). These pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic parameters must be systematically studied in translational models and clinical trials to ensure the reproducibility of effects observed in rodents.

Nonetheless, this study has several limitations. The precise molecular mechanisms underlying the effects of PBE were not elucidated, and the bioavailability or pharmacokinetics of the individual phytochemicals were not evaluated. Furthermore, the relatively short duration and small sample size may limit generalizability.

Conclusion

In conclusion, our findings provide novel evidence that PBE alleviates experimental periodontitis by suppressing inflammation and osteoclast activation. Further studies should investigate the specific molecular targets of PBE and evaluate its long-term efficacy and safety in clinical settings.
