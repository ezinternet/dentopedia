Title: Informed consent in dentistry and medicine in Spain: Practical considerations and legality
Authors: Otero M, Oishi N, Martínez F, Ballester MT, Basterra J
Journal: Med Oral Patol Oral Cir Bucal. 2022 May 1;27(3):e294-e300
DOI: 10.4317/medoral.25265
PMID: 35368004 | PMCID: PMC9054172
Source: PubMed Central full text (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9054172/)

ABSTRACT

Background
The healthcare practice of dentistry, as well as medicine, is framed within a legal environment. Patients have the right to know all the information related to any action performed on them and dental or medical doctors are obliged to obtain their patient's prior written informed consent (IC) before undertaking any healthcare procedures.

Material and Methods
Here we reviewed the legality and jurisprudence in Spain regarding IC. We also used INFLESZ text readability analysis software to analyse a sample of official Spanish informed consent documents (ICDs) from different surgical and interventional procedures related to dentistry and oral cavity interventions.

Results
It is a mistake to confound IC with ICDs. This error prevents physicians from considering the former as a care process in which the patient's authorisation signature is the last link in a chain formed, almost in its entirety, by the informative process and deliberation alongside the patient. Multiple factors can influence communication between practitioners and their patients. Importantly, treatment adherence is greater when patients feel involved and autonomous in shared decision-making and when the circumstances of their lives are adequately considered. We concluded that although the ICDs we analysed conformed to the requirements set out in international law, they were somewhat difficult to read according to the reading habits of the general Spanish population.

Conclusions
Knowledge about the legality of IC helps professionals to understand the problems that may arise from their non-compliance. This is because the omission or defective fulfilment of IC obligations is the origin of legal responsibility for medical practitioners. In this sense, to date, there have been more convictions for defective ICs than for malpractice. The information provided in ICs should include the risks, benefits, and treatment alternatives and must be tailored to the needs and capabilities of the patient to enable autonomous decision-making.

Keywords: Informed consent, legality of informed consent, principle of autonomy, defensive medicine, satisfactive medicine, health law, stomatology, oral surgery, dentistry.

FULL TEXT

Introduction

Obtaining informed consent (IC) is the end of a process of shared decision-making between health professionals and patients, in which the patient autonomously decides to take the course of action they consider to be best for them. This means that they must first receive all the appropriate information regarding their disease and all the diagnostic and/or therapeutic options available to them. The objective of this current manuscript was to take a practical approach to present a review of the theory, evolution, and Spanish legality of IC, with the aim of guiding dental or medical doctors in their daily clinical practice.

There are different types of IC. The most appropriate is the express form, which is manifested verbally, in writing, or by unequivocal signs. However, in many routine clinical acts, express consent is considered excessive, as occurs, for example, when doctors evaluate the results of an analysis or of a complementary test report. In these cases, tacit consent, understood by the professional based on the patient's behaviour, is sufficient. However, tacit consent is not valid in cases where, by law, the patient's will must be expressly indicated.

It is common to find patients who demand detailed information about their condition and treatments. The importance of IC is derived not only from its legal imperative. Patient satisfaction with the received information is a key indicator of effective communication, which itself drives patient behaviour. Providing information adapted to each case and its needs adjusts patient expectations by exposing them to the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed treatment. Indeed, a patient satisfied with the information provided to them is more likely to place greater trust in their doctor or dentist and become more involved in decision making. This correlates with a greater degree of patient involvement and adherence to treatment and therefore, with an improvement in therapeutic outcomes and prognosis. In fact, if a patient is not fully satisfied with the treatment administration mode or not convinced of its benefit, he will be less likely to comply with the prescribed regimen. Meanwhile, information is the best form of liability insurance for professionals because it helps protect them from lawsuits and convictions resulting from defective ICs.

Material and Methods

We conducted a detailed analysis of Law 41/2002 on the basic regulation of patient autonomy and rights and obligations in terms of information and clinical documentation which represents the national standard that currently regulates the use of health information in Spain. The different regional rules on IC and jurisprudence on this subject, as well as some previous historical international documents and conventions (the Nuremberg Code, Declaration of Helsinki, Oviedo Convention, and Belmont Report) and their derivatives (Code of Ethics and Medical Deontology of the Collegiate Medical Organization of Spain) were also reviewed.

In addition, we undertook an index search in PubMed using the following search terms: [informed consent AND patient information AND informed consent legal AND health literacy]. Articles published from 2002 onwards (the date on which the revised national law was published) were included in this review. The collected information was analysed without the need for statistical tests and is presented here in the form of summaries and Tables. Furthermore, we analysed a sample of official informed consent documents (ICDs) from different dental surgical and interventional procedures to verify their suitability from a legal standpoint and to check their readability by employing INFLESZ online software (https://legible.es/).

Results

The topic of IC is one of the most studied and debated areas in the field of bioethics. However, most of this work has focused on consent as a simple authorisation rather than on the 'process' of giving consent itself. The latter should be understood as the shared decision-making process between the two parties involved: the health professional and the patient. All the information is provided to the patient during the first part of this process while the patient deliberates and decides on their desired course of action in the second part.

Legislation
IC is extensively regulated at the international, national, and regional levels in many European countries. Its legal roots reside in the Nuremberg Code from 1947, after doctors were accused of conducting experiments characterised as crimes against humanity on prisoners during World War II without obtaining prior consent. This was followed by the Declaration of Helsinki (1964), the first Declaration of Patient Rights (1973), the Oviedo Convention (1977), and the Belmont Report (1978). In Spain, this right was included in the first Code of Ethics and Medical Deontology from 1978, updated most recently in 2011.

Law 41/2002 regulates patient autonomy and IC in Spain, repealing the corresponding articles of General Health Law 14/1986. Key points:
a) Patients have the right to know all available information about their health, including treatment risks, benefits, and alternatives, including the possibility of technique failure. Patients also have the right NOT to be informed, if expressed in writing. Information must be truthful, understandable, and appropriate, usually provided verbally with a written record in the clinical report.
b) Any healthcare intervention requires the free and voluntary consent of the affected person after having received information. Consent may be verbal except in surgical acts or invasive procedures, where it must be in writing. The patient can revoke consent at any time. In emergencies / risk to public health / therapeutic need, doctors may carry out essential interventions without IC, informing the patient afterward.
c) Consent by representation can be granted when the patient cannot decide for themselves, their decision-making capacity is judicially modified, the patient is under 16 and not emancipated, or unable to understand the scope of the intervention.

Regional legislation varies greatly (e.g., Valencia Law 10/2014 vs Galicia Law 3/2001 on order of priority for consent by representation; Castilla-La Mancha Law 5/2010 vs Catalonia Law 21/2000 on minors). Valencia Law 10/2014 requires information at least 24 hours before the procedure and prohibits giving information while the patient is numb, asleep, mentally altered, or already inside the operating/procedure room. National Law 41/2002 only requires that information be sufficiently in advance to guarantee an autonomous decision.

Jurisprudence
Some 90% of legal claims against doctors and dentists are related to defects in the transmission of information and in the IC. The content of information required for a correct IC is expanding under recent Spanish jurisprudence, even more so for "satisfactory medicine" (satisfactive medicine) — medicine that does not treat pathologies but is performed on patients who voluntarily desire interventions to "improve" their bodies. Dentistry has been configured mostly by the courts as voluntary/satisfactory medicine (examples: teeth whitening, some orthodontics). The recommended extent of IC is defined by the "necessity, severity, and novelty of medical interventions" — the LESS necessary an intervention, the GREATER the amount of information that must be provided.

The omission or defective fulfilment of IC generates civil liability and possible compensation when proven damage exists. Jurisprudence applies a "double standard": when there is no physical damage, not all risks/complications must be reported; when physical damage occurs with no malpractice, exhaustive information must have been provided. Compensation considers physical AND moral damage; this enhances defensive medicine. The availability of a therapeutic alternative is not necessary to find the right to information was violated — damages from lack of IC are compensated even where a single treatment exists, and even where the patient would have been treated the same way had they been fully informed. The judicialisation of health is a barrier to trust between patients and professionals, reframing ICs as a tool to protect doctors more than as a joint decision process, and fostering defensive practices and slower decision-making.

The informed consent documents (ICDs)
ICDs must be specific to each diagnostic or therapeutic procedure. Generic ICDs are not adequate or legally accepted; those whose headers do not correspond to the procedure are considered invalid. They must contain a section for the signature of the doctor/dentist (proof verbal information was transmitted), the patient/legal representative signature (acceptance), the date, a section for possible revocation, and the patient must be given a copy. The process of providing verbal information is gradual, can be carried out in one or more interviews, and CANNOT be replaced by any written document.

It is a mistake to assume that IC is equivalent to ICD. This error prevents considering IC as a care process in which the patient's authorizing signature represents the LAST LINK in a chain formed almost entirely by the informative process and the patient's deliberation. Readability of ICDs should be considered an indicator of quality of care. Medical terminology often uses long, difficult words. The authors selected a sample of ICDs from dental societies and verified specificity and legal compliance, then analysed readability with INFLESZ software (score 0-100; 0-40 very difficult, 40-55 quite difficult, 55-65 normal, 65-80 quite easy, 80-100 very easy). The analysed ICDs (orthodontics, dental implantation, tooth filling, tooth extraction, endodontics, periapical surgery, pulp treatment in immature tooth, periodontics, oral surgery) had INFLESZ indices of 41.24 to 52.18 points — all "somewhat difficult" to read and all below the 55-point cut-off for accessibility to the general Spanish public.

Discussion

The patient's right to information is independent of whether they decide to undergo an intervention, and precedes IC (the formal procedure for applying the principle of autonomy). ICDs should cover risks inherent to each intervention AND those related to individual patient characteristics (age, comorbidities). A patient who underwent the same intervention before (e.g., a second dental implant) still requires a new IC, because specific risks may have changed.

The minimum requirements for valid IC (not all described by law) are: capacity (competence), information, understanding, and voluntariness (absence of coercion). The way information is transmitted influences how it is received; empathy plays a fundamental role. For competent patients with learning disabilities, information must be adapted and support measures provided. Voluntariness is violated when consent is requested by people in a position of authority or when insufficient time to reflect is given.

Exceptions to IC: risk to public health, or an emergency posing immediate serious risk to a patient unable to express their will. Consent is verbal in most situations (blood pressure, ECG, oral cavity examination) but must be written for notable risk or foreseeable health impact (surgical interventions, invasive procedures). The more doubtful the outcome or the less necessary the intervention, the more important the written prior IC.

The ICDs proposed by Spanish dental societies conform to law (procedure-specific) but use complicated language difficult to read for the general population ("somewhat difficult" by INFLESZ), consistent with similar studies. The fact that a patient signs an ICD does not mean they read or understood it. Readability can be improved with shorter sentences and words, avoiding unnecessary technicalities, using subsections, graphics, and explanatory drawings.

The judicialisation of health and reactive-defensive medicine are barriers to trust and good doctor-patient relationships. Consent can become excessively bureaucratised, reducing it to the act of signing a difficult document the patient often did not read. Information protects professionals from lawsuits and allows patients to be protagonists of their own healthcare by involving them in decisions, improving treatment effectiveness through adherence. The communicative skills of professionals play a central role in the success of the IC process.
