Title: Customized three-dimensional-printed titanium mesh for alveolar bone augmentation: Retrospective consecutive case series.
Authors: Soares KH, King C, Palkovics D, Perez R, Mbadu M, Duarte WR, Schey KC, Duarte PM
Journal: Clinical Advances in Periodontics
Year: 2025 (epub 2025-10-10)
DOI: 10.1002/cap.70015
PMID: 41071184

ABSTRACT

Background:
This retrospective case series evaluated linear and volumetric bone changes in alveolar bone defects treated with guided bone regeneration (GBR) using a next-generation customized 3D-printed titanium mesh.

Methods:
Medical history, surgical details, and CBCT data collected from patients undergoing GBR with customized 3D-printed titanium mesh. CBCT subtraction analysis performed using 3D digital models (spatial registration + semi-automatic segmentation). Outcomes: complication rates, linear bone measurements, volumetric hard tissue gain, graft volume stability, augmentation efficacy.

Defect Classification:
- Combined large defects (n=5)
- Vertical large defects (n=2)
- Combined medium defects (n=2)
Total: n=9 cases

Results:
Complications:
- 2 cases: NO complications
- 7 cases: MESH EXPOSURE (77.8% overall exposure rate)
  - 2 cases: complete EARLY mesh exposure (22.2%) → total graft loss + premature removal
  - 5 cases: managed exposure without total graft loss

Bone Gain:
- Linear vertical bone gain (at middle plane of defect):
  - Large defects: 5.7 ± 2.3 mm
  - Medium defects: 2.7 ± 0.5 mm
  - Overall: 4.8 ± 2.4 mm
- Volumetric gain: 0.49 ± 0.20 cm³ (overall)
- Graft volume stability: 87.9 ± 19.5%
- Augmentation efficacy: 0.037 ± 0.012 cm³/mm

Key factors for success identified:
1. Thorough preoperative planning
2. Precise execution of surgical technique
3. Effective management of postoperative mesh exposure

Key factors for failure:
1. Compromised systemic health or medical history
2. Improper adaptation/seating of the mesh
3. Premature and complete mesh exposure during healing

Conclusion:
Customized 3D-printed titanium mesh is feasible and effective for augmenting defective edentulous ridges, providing predictable linear and volumetric gains. Mesh exposure is the most common complication (77.8%), and complete early exposure leads to total graft loss. Careful planning, technique, and exposure management are critical.

Note: This case series uses CBCT subtraction analysis (volumetric) — this is a methodological contribution beyond simple linear measurements, providing augmentation efficacy metrics (cm³/mm).
