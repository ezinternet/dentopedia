COVID-19 Oral Sequelae: Persistent Gustatory and Saliva Secretory Dysfunctions after Recovery from COVID-19

Hironori Tsuchiya
Medicine Principles and Practice, 2023;32(3):166-177
DOI: 10.1159/000531373
PMID: 37271130
PMCID: PMC10601698
Source: PubMed Central (PMC), retrieved via PubMed MCP get_full_text_article

Highlights of the Study

- COVID-19 oral sequelae are symptomatically characterized, and their pathogenic mechanisms are speculated.
- Gustatory and saliva secretory dysfunctions persist in up to 45% of COVID-19 survivors at follow-ups of 3 weeks to 12 months.
- Persistent ageusia/dysgeusia and xerostomia/dry mouth are pathogenically related to SARS-CoV-2 receptors expressed in the tongue and salivary glands and to SARS-CoV-2 infection-induced zinc deficiency.

Introduction

Since the emergence of coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) caused by severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), the disease has spread worldwide to reach over 617.7 million cases with over 6.5 million deaths as of October 1, 2022, according to the Johns Hopkins University Coronavirus Resource Center. Over 3 years have passed since the first case of COVID-19 was reported; it has been well recognized that COVID-19 patients commonly present with fever, cough, dyspnea, myalgia, fatigue, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia, cardiac arrest, anorexia, nausea, diarrhea, and chemosensory disorders. In addition to these clinical manifestations, several symptoms specific to oral tissues and functions are also associated with COVID-19. They include impairments of taste such as ageusia (complete taste loss) and dysgeusia (impaired taste) that is classified into severe, moderate, and mild hypogeusia (or amblygeustia), and impairments of saliva secretion such as dry mouth, xerostomia (subjective complaint of oral dryness), and hyposalivation (objective reduction of salivary flow rates). While early studies intensively investigated COVID-19 symptomatology in the acute phase of the disease, there is increasing evidence that patients who have recovered from COVID-19 complain of long-term effects of COVID-19 or sequelae such as fatigue, headache, attention disorder, neurocognitive disorder (brain fog), cough, dyspnea, and hair loss. In a follow-up study after 2 months from symptom onset, 87.4% of COVID-19 survivors reported at least one persistent symptom, and 55% of them had three or more sequelae. However, understanding of post-COVID-19 oral symptoms is relatively poor compared with that of oral symptoms in the acute phase of COVID-19 and other COVID-19 sequelae. Although gustatory and saliva secretory dysfunctions are not life-threatening, their persistence adversely affects overall quality of life of COVID-19 survivors who have recovered from the disease.

Methodology

A literature search was performed in PubMed/Medline, LitCovid, and ProQuest with a cutoff date of September 30, 2022, using terms including "SARS-CoV-2," "COVID-19," "sequelae," "persistent," "ageusia," "dysgeusia," "gustatory dysfunction," "xerostomia," "dry mouth," and "salivary secretion dysfunction." The preprint databases medRxiv and bioRxiv were also used. Exclusion criteria: non-English articles, studies without RT-PCR/serological confirmation of SARS-CoV-2 infection, and studies including patients with primary Sjögren's syndrome (which independently affects saliva/gustatory function). Cited papers in retrieved articles were further searched for additional references.

Results and Discussion

Gustatory Sequelae

Ageusia, dysgeusia, and hypogeusia were collectively expressed as "gustatory dysfunction." The literature search included 64 studies on persistent gustatory sequelae (total 23,476 COVID-19 survivors) and 86 studies on acute gustatory dysfunctions (total 42,759 patients). No meta-analysis was performed due to heterogeneity, but overall risk of bias was judged low-to-moderate based on prior meta-analyses/SRs.

Gustatory dysfunctions persist with a prevalence of 1-45% in COVID-19 survivors followed for 21-365 days. Pooled analysis of 8 observational studies (Moraschini et al.) indicated ageusia continues in 14.1% of survivors at mean 67-day follow-up. Summarizing 29 follow-up studies: gustatory dysfunctions reported by 13.6% at 29.7-day follow-up, 13.8% at 60.4-day follow-up, and 12.7% at 181.9-day follow-up — prevalence not significantly different between short-term and long-term follow-up. Impairments are presumed to continue for at least 1 year post-recovery.

Persistent parageusia (wrong/inadequate taste sensation) and phantogeusia (taste sensation without stimulus) were observed in some follow-up studies: 23.5% and 17.6% respectively in an Italian cohort at mean 64-day follow-up; 38.3% and 14.9% respectively in a British cohort at 117-199 days, alongside 34.0% ageusia.

Geographical/Ethnic Differences: Pooled prevalence (mean ± SD) of gustatory sequelae at follow-ups of 3 weeks-12 months: Europe 16.2 ± 11.01%, North America 13.8 ± 8.8%, South America 11.5 ± 5.0%, East Asia 3.8 ± 2.0%, South Asia 10.0 ± 1.4%, Middle East 20.6 ± 7.5%. East Asia prevalence significantly lower than Europe (p<0.005), North America (p<0.01), South America (p<0.01), South Asia (p<0.01), Middle East (p<0.0005). Comparison of acute-phase vs. post-recovery prevalence in European populations: 60.4 ± 14.5% acute vs. 16.7 ± 12.8% (≤1 month), 16.0 ± 11.4% (>1-2 months), 16.9 ± 10.0% (>2-6 months), 16.3 ± 8.2% (>6 months) — all significantly lower than acute (p<0.001) but not significantly different from each other, indicating sequelae can last months.

Gender: Algahtani et al. found ageusia persisted in 69.5% of female vs. 30.5% of male Saudi COVID-19 survivors (p=0.0001). Swiss cohort: ageusia in 12.5% female vs 6.0% male at 30-45 days, 18.2% vs 14.1% at 7-9 months. German cohort (Augustin et al.): ageusia prevalence 13.9% females vs 7.3% males at 1.7-6.8 months. However, South Korean (900 patients), Indian, and Turkish cohorts found no significant gender association with persistent ageusia.

Age: Moreno-Pérez et al. (Spain): dysgeusia/anosmia prevalence 24.9% for patients <65y vs 13.5% for >65y (p=0.03). Italian 12-month follow-up: risk of symptom persistence significantly higher in 40-54y age group (p=0.029). South Korean cohort: ageusia persistence associated with age >50y (p=0.024). Indian cohort found no significant age association.

Severity: In the acute phase, taste is more frequently impaired in mild/non-severe than severe/critical cases. French cohort (Garrigues et al.): ageusia persisted in 16.7% ICU vs 9.4% ward patients (not statistically compared here as significant). Chinese cohorts (Zhang et al., Fang et al.) at 1-year follow-up: taste impaired in 2.2-2.5% with severe disease vs 1.1-1.5% non-severe (not statistically significant). South Korean cohort found no association between ageusia persistence and moderate disease severity. Gustatory sequelae may occur independently of COVID-19 severity.

Saliva Secretory Sequelae

Xerostomia, dry mouth, and hyposalivation were collectively expressed as "saliva secretory dysfunction." Review included 7 studies (total 654 survivors) on saliva secretory sequelae and 13 studies (total 2,283 patients) on acute-phase dysfunction. Quality analysis of 4 studies (946 participants) on COVID-19 xerostomia showed low-moderate risk of bias.

Saliva secretory sequelae reported by 2-40% of survivors followed for 28-230 days. Prevalence of xerostomia/dry mouth lower after recovery than in acute phase (mean 18.5% vs 42.5%, p<0.001), but persists long-term. At follow-ups of 1-2 months and >2-4 months, prevalence was 16.2 ± 0.84% and 20.5 ± 6.8% (mean ± SD) respectively.

Characterization: Unlike gustatory sequelae, ethnicity/gender/age do not clearly determine saliva secretory dysfunction prevalence. Biadsee et al. (Israel, n=97, 7.6-month follow-up): dry mouth 14.4% overall, no significant gender difference (p=0.905). Italian (n=122) and Turkish (n=107) cohorts also found no significant gender association. Indian cohort (n=100): no gender or age association. Colombian cohort (n=100, 7-month follow-up): xerostomia in 25.7% ambulatory/mild, 19.5% severe, 37.5% critical COVID-19 — not statistically significant. Gender, age, and disease severity are not necessarily associated with COVID-19 saliva secretory sequelae.

Association between Saliva Secretory and Gustatory Dysfunctions: 20 studies (7 on sequelae, 13 on acute phase) reported xerostomia together with ageusia/dysgeusia. Cohorts from Italy, Colombia, China, India, Israel, Egypt, Turkey, and Saudi Arabia concluded persistent saliva secretory dysfunction can co-occur with persistent gustatory dysfunction regardless of geography/ethnicity. Prevalence of xerostomia was moderately correlated with ageusia/dysgeusia prevalence. Both symptoms are presumed to co-occur in the acute phase and persist together after recovery.

Possible Pathogenic Mechanisms

1. ACE2 responsible for cellular binding of SARS-CoV-2: Spike protein binds ACE2 receptor (S1 subunit), followed by Furin cleavage at S1/S2 site and TMPRSS2 cleavage at S2' site enabling membrane fusion and viral entry. ACE2 is expressed in human oral tissues, especially dorsal tongue (Xu et al.). Sakaguchi et al. demonstrated ACE2, Furin, and TMPRSS2 are abundantly present in taste receptor cell-containing taste buds/papillae and in ductal, acinar, and myoepithelial cells of salivary glands, co-localized in parotid, submandibular, sublingual, and minor salivary glands. Matuck et al. found ACE2/TMPRSS in ductal epithelium and serous acinar cells of parotid/submandibular glands in postmortem COVID-19 cases, with viral particles visible ultrastructurally. Taste buds and salivary glands expressing ACE2 receive collateral cytopathic damage from SARS-CoV-2, impairing taste perception and salivary secretion. Damaged taste bud cells require weeks to proliferate/recover; acinar cell turnover ranges 50-125 days — consistent with persistence of sequelae. ACE2/TMPRSS2 expression differs significantly between Asian and European populations, consistent with ethnic differences observed in gustatory sequelae prevalence. Since ACE2/TMPRSS2 expression does not differ by gender, gender differences in gustatory sequelae may instead relate to taste responsiveness and taste bud number.

2. TRPV1 interacting with SARS-CoV-2: TRPV1 contains ankyrin repeat domains; SARS-CoV-2 spike protein contains two ankyrin binding motifs. TRPV1 expression is activated by several viral infections. TRPV1 is expressed in taste papillae, palate, tongue epithelium (including human tongue basal layers per Marincsák et al.), and correlates with sour/salty taste sensation. TRPV1 is also present in myoepithelial, acinar, and ductal cells of submandibular, sublingual, and parotid glands (rat studies), and in human submandibular glands (Ding et al.). SARS-CoV-2 interaction with TRPV1 in tongue and salivary glands could impair taste and salivary secretion via cytopathic effects.

3. Zinc deficiency associated with SARS-CoV-2 infection: Zinc is essential for taste bud physiology, taste-transmitting nerves, and taste cell regeneration/maintenance; present at high levels in taste bud membranes and in glandular/myoepithelial cells of submandibular glands. Zinc deficiency decreases taste bud number/size and impairs taste sensitivity and saliva secretion. Zinc-binding proteins (carbonic anhydrase VI, metallothionein-3, zinc transporters) are localized in human taste buds and salivary glands, regulating zinc homeostasis for taste/saliva function. Serum zinc concentrations were significantly lower in COVID-19 patients (64.8 ± 7.5 μg/dL) vs healthy subjects (88.3 ± 14.0 μg/dL). Viral infection induces hypozincemia by redistributing zinc from blood to liver (as in sepsis/systemic inflammation). Yasui et al. found Japanese ICU-discharged COVID-19 patients had serum zinc near/below deficiency cutoff for 4 weeks post-onset — zinc deficiency effects could persist in survivors, causing ageusia/dysgeusia and xerostomia/dry mouth sequelae.

4. Supporting hypothetical evidence from variants: Omicron (B.1.1.529) has ≥32 spike mutations (15 in RBD) vs Alpha/Beta/Gamma/Delta. Omicron infection is associated with relatively low frequency of ageusia/dysgeusia compared to other variants; xerostomia very rarely reported (only one case). Wu et al. measured RBD affinity constants for human ACE2: significantly weaker binding for Omicron than wild type/Delta. Comparative dissociation constant (Kd) studies confirm weaker Omicron-ACE2 interaction than Delta. Omicron also shows weaker membrane fusogenicity than Delta, Lambda, Mu variants (Du et al.). Serum zinc concentrations correlate inversely with COVID-19 severity across cohorts (Turkish, Russian, Iranian data cited); since most Omicron cases are asymptomatic/mild, severe zinc deficiency and consequent oral sequelae are less likely with Omicron than earlier variants.

Conclusions

A significant number of COVID-19 survivors complain of ageusia/dysgeusia and xerostomia/dry mouth even 7 months to 1 year after recovery. Co-occurring gustatory and saliva secretory sequelae are pathogenically related to (i) expression of SARS-CoV-2 cellular entry-relevant receptors (ACE2, TMPRSS2, TRPV1) in taste buds and salivary glands, and (ii) SARS-CoV-2 infection-induced zinc deficiency essential for normal taste perception and saliva secretion. Although not life-threatening, long-term persistence negatively impacts quality of life. Hospital discharge is not the end of COVID-19; careful attention should continue to be paid to oral conditions of post-COVID-19 patients.
