Evaluation of D&C Red No. 33 as an alternative to erythrosine for plaque disclosure: a randomized crossover trial

Authors: Sooampon S et al. (Faculty of Dentistry, Chulalongkorn University)
Journal: BMC Oral Health 2026
PMID 42243816 | PMCID PMC13263934 | DOI 10.1186/s12903-026-08786-6
Trial registration: Thai Clinical Trials Registry TCTR20250313002 (13 March 2025)

Source: PMC open-access full text (CC BY).

BACKGROUND
Plaque disclosing agents facilitate visualization of dental biofilm and support oral hygiene instruction. Erythrosine (FD&C Red No. 3) is the most commonly used agent but stains oral tissues persistently and raises safety concerns — as of 15 January 2025 the U.S. FDA prohibited erythrosine in food and orally ingested drugs (carcinogenicity in male rats at high exposure). This prompted a search for safer alternatives. This study compared the clinical performance and user acceptability of three plaque disclosing formulations: 3% erythrosine, 3% D&C Red No. 33, and a mixed formulation of 3% D&C Red No. 33 with green powder (a two-tone agent intended to differentiate old vs new plaque). 3% concentration was selected (6% D&C Red No. 33 had lower solubility; pilot found no 3% vs 6% erythrosine difference).

METHODS
Randomized crossover clinical trial, partially blinded. 11 healthy adults (7F/4M, mean age 22.5 ± 1.3 y, range 20–25), convenience sample at Chulalongkorn University. Sample size 9 + 20% dropout → 11 (G*Power, alpha 5%, power 90%, primary outcome TQHPI). Each participant received all three disclosing formulations (A=3% erythrosine, B=3% D&C Red No. 33, C=mixed D&C Red No.33+green) in random order, one per visit, with a 1-week washout. Standardized modified Bass brushing + once-daily night flossing; 7-day familiarization. On testing days: brush in morning only, chew assigned tablet ~1 min, expectorate without rinsing. Primary outcome: plaque index by Turesky modification of the Quigley–Hein Plaque Index (TQHPI) on labial surfaces of anterior teeth, scored by a calibrated examiner before and after brushing/flossing. Secondary outcomes (participant-rated): plaque visibility (VAS 0–10), residual staining (teeth/lips/tongue/oral mucosa/sink; 3-point scale, immediate and 1 h post-brush), ease of stain removal (3-point), taste perception (3-point). Stats: repeated-measures ANOVA, paired t-tests, Friedman + Wilcoxon signed-rank; α=0.05.

RESULTS
- Plaque visibility (VAS): highest for 3% erythrosine, then 3% D&C Red No. 33, lowest for the mixed agent. Erythrosine significantly higher than both alternatives.
- Baseline plaque index (TQHPI): highest with erythrosine, lowest with mixed formulation; erythrosine significantly differed from both alternatives.
- After brushing/flossing: all three groups showed statistically significant plaque index reduction. When expressed as PERCENTAGE reduction in plaque index, NO significant difference among the three agents — i.e. despite different baseline staining intensity, effectiveness in facilitating plaque removal was comparable.
- Residual staining: erythrosine highest overall. On lips, erythrosine significantly greater than both others at 1 h. On oral mucosa, erythrosine significantly greater than D&C Red No. 33 at both times. D&C Red No. 33 produced the least residual staining; no significant between-agent difference on the wash basin.
- Ease of stain removal: erythrosine rated most difficult to brush off (significantly worse than D&C Red No. 33); mixed formulation intermediate.
- Taste satisfaction: D&C Red No. 33 highest, mixed intermediate, erythrosine lowest (all significantly different).
- The mixed D&C Red No.33/green two-tone formulation FAILED to differentiate plaque maturity (stained plaque appeared purplish-brown) — participants maintained daily hygiene so little mature plaque was present.

CONCLUSIONS
Erythrosine remains the most effective agent for plaque visualization but has notable disadvantages in residual staining, difficulty of removal, taste, and regulatory/safety status. D&C Red No. 33 showed comparable percentage plaque-reduction effectiveness with superior taste acceptability and reduced residual staining, and is already FDA-approved in oral-care products (mouthwash, dentifrice) — supporting it as a more acceptable, patient-friendly alternative disclosing agent. The mixed D&C Red No.33/green formulation gave no added clinical benefit.

LIMITATIONS
Small sample (n=11), convenience sampling of healthy young adults (limited generalizability), incomplete blinding (mixed formulation visibly distinct), assessment limited to labial anterior surfaces (posterior efficacy not captured), possible carryover despite 1-week washout, uncontrolled chewing behavior causing uneven dye distribution.
