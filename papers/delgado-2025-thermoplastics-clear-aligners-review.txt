Thermoplastics for Clear Aligners: A Review
PMCID: PMC12196624 | PMID: 40574208 | DOI: 10.3390/polym17121681
Journal: Polymers (2025), 17(12):1681. Published 2025-06-17.

ABSTRACT
With the worldwide spread of clear aligner treatment (CAT), a plethora of new thermoplastics is currently commercially available on the market, claiming to have superior properties and greater comfort. This review aims to summarise the properties of the materials and their effects on treatment effectiveness and comfort to ease material selection and also incorporate new emerging trends such as shape memory polymers (SMPs) and direct 3D printer aligners. First, a concise historical overview of orthodontics will be presented, along with the basic properties of thermoplastics and their importance in treatment. Following the individual properties, we present an analysis of optical, biocompatibility, and toxicity aspects, passing through others such as thermal, mechanical, and special methods to simulate in vivo measurements. We end with the impact of this technique on the environment and the advances and perspectives of CAT.

1. INTRODUCTION
Historical arc: Etruscan/Egyptian gold-silver appliances (1000 BC) → modern fixed appliances (austenitic steel + NiTi superelastic) → Remensnyder rubber gingival appliance (1923) → Kensling first tooth positioner (1946, clay impression + soft-vulcanized rubber, only final position set, only small corrections, bulky/uncomfortable) → Robert J. Ponitz (1971) first transparent thermoplastic thermoformed with heat+vacuum over clay impression (mostly small misfits / sports guards) → 1997 two Stanford students used CAM/CAT software, replaced clay impressions with oral scanner, software-planned 3D step models → 3D printed models → thermoformed sheet → demold/cut/trim/clean/sterilize = modern CAT workflow. Now another "revolution": more advanced polymers, 3D printed aligners, shape memory polymers (SMPs).

2. THERMOPLASTICS
- PET / PETG (amorphous PET copolymerized with ethylene glycol): MOST COMMON thermoplastic in CAT, both polyesters. Newer copolyesters have enhanced properties but low availability, higher price, lack of knowledge.
- TPU (thermoplastic polyurethane): SECOND most common; copolymer of hard+soft blocks joined by urethane bond. In CAT, high-hardness TPU (hTPU) used (mechanical resemblance to PETG); middle (mTPU) and soft (sTPU) used mainly in multilayers. TPU better organoleptic feel than polyesters but higher price, harder to process/handle/store, more problematic for allergies and chemical staining than PETG.
- EVA (ethylene vinyl acetate): soft transparent elastomer, elasticity rises with vinyl-acetate %; cheap/available/easy to process, but relegated to sports & bruxism mouthguards.
- PP (polypropylene): properties too low for active role; crystalline and not transparent.
- PC (polycarbonate): less common — high price, poor aging when worn, misinformation re biocompatibility (BPA reputation).
- MULTILAYERS: enabled by Invisalign patent expiration; combine 2+ plastics in layers; more costly but more effective and comfortable.
- BLENDING: rarely used; polymers usually non-miscible; PC proposed as compatibilizer between PETG and TPU → enhanced stress relaxation vs PETG.
- NEW TRENDS: SMPs (major network = stable shape + secondary network = temporary shape; recover temporary→stable under temperature/humidity in narrow window). Two SMP approaches: (1) improve effectiveness (final shape made, current mouth fixed as temporary via heat, evolves to fixed at oral temp; problem — if treatment inaccurate, temporal effect lost; most SMPs toxic, expensive, hard to manufacture); (2) PREFERRED — aligners with 2+ shapes, reshape to next step using heat+humidity ("step-shifting" — 1 aligner for 2 steps via immersion in hot water). 3D printing resins — direct printing = better device fit, faster/cheaper/greener, less waste.

3. GENERAL REQUIREMENTS
Transparent + biocompatible, sustain both ≥14 days in oral environment. Biocompatibility = low cytotoxicity (low leachate emissions, low water shrinkage). Mechanical: low but sufficient elastic modulus. Simulations: elastic modulus ~550–650 MPa is more than enough for most movements, but pain & dental-movement force thresholds are patient-dependent and variable — no single definitive value for all treatments.

4. OPTICAL PROPERTIES
Transparency + longevity. Fully amorphous preferred over crystalline. Must resist staining (coffee, black tea, soda, red wine, nicotine). Measurement: spectrophotometry (flat sheets pre-transformation); dentistry colorimeter (reflection-based, calibrated for enamel — poor sensitivity for plastic vs plastic; reference preferred); flatbed scanner + calibrated white background for direct aligner color change after 14-day immersion.
Conclusions: aging/wear + saliva/chemicals decrease transmittance after 7 days (recent analyses report improvement in recent years). Most transparent: PETG, TPU, PC, multilayers — no significant difference in naive state. Transparency decreases after thermoforming (increased surface roughness). TPU most susceptible to staining (higher water sorption + porosity); coffee most damaging substance. Invisalign multilayer has UNIQUE structure: soft outer TPU layers + inner polyester core → more prone to staining than other multilayers (which have opposite structure) → reinforces TPU = most susceptible to coloring.

5. AGING AND CHEMICAL RESISTANCE
Test: submerge in water/artificial saliva at 37°C, then mechanical test vs non-aged. Higher water sorption → decay in mechanical properties (↑ stress relaxation, ↓ initial force). PC: dramatic hydrolysis after long immersion → no longer used. TPU in acid → ↓ relaxed stress, ↑ elastic modulus (chemical reaction). Biofilm → ↑ fragility & hardness. Water/cleansing agents make PP and PETG more plastic; no big difference in cracking fracture energy among water/cleansing/dry.
Worn aligners: elastic & indentation modulus ↓ slightly after 7 days, stable after 14 days. Invisalign loses elasticity after usage (but no similar studies for other thermoplastics). 3D printed resin TH-85: no significant property change after 7 days use. Comparison: Invisalign, PETG, TC-85 similar indentation elastic modulus, BUT stress relaxation: Invisalign much lower (4% non-used, 10% after 1 wk) vs TC-85 (45% before & after use) → 3D resins easily deformable, not good for CAT.
Blending/stacking ↓ water uptake → better aging. PETG/PC/TPU blends: lower water absorption vs commercial PETG at 14 days/37°C. Multilayers controversial (only one study: PETG/TPU bilayer reinforced with rigid resin; bilayer absorbs much more water than monolayer, third layer brings it to middle) → confirms TPU is one of the most hygroscopic CAT polymers.
"Mustard test": material wrapped around cylinder (known stress/strain), submerged in mustard, bending angle/coloration/microcracks/mechanics measured after recovery. Idea: liquid penetrates amorphous polymer at stress-concentration areas → plasticization, ↓ Tg, microcracks coalesce. Disadvantage: overestimates combined effect (never fully immersed). Pop-soda variant: thermoformed PETG brands in artificial saliva 2 wks 37°C + pop soda 10 min/day, flexural modulus by 3PB — no significant difference between soda/no-soda or between PETG brands.

6. MECHANICAL PROPERTIES
Devices exert slight force ~22 h/day at 37°C in humid environment. Elastic modulus important but elastic RANGE may be more important (environment/temperature only decrease elasticity). PETG vs hTPU stress-strain curves fully different. Aligners act within elastic range near yield point. If strain < yield: effective force much lower than planned → efficiency drops. If strain > yield: device plasticized, deformed, ceases force. hTPU security range broader than PETG → forces vary less. Strains decrease gradually each time device removed/inserted. PETG & PC: first cycles painful, rest more comfortable. hTPU larger elastic range → less force variation, lower force → more comfortable. THREE crucial factors: yield stress, yield strain, elastic modulus.
PC highest yield stress + highest elastic modulus; hTPU comparable yield stress with lower elastic modulus. PETG higher modulus than hTPU + lower yield point. PET & PC similar modulus/yield; adding ethylene glycol to PET (→ amorphous PETG) ↓ modulus & yield stress, ↑ yield strain. Adding PC to PETG ↑ yield point & elastic range (better material, needs more research). SMPs & 3D resins: elastic modulus similar to PETG and hTPU.
Methods: 3PB (most common, simple, sometimes 37°C water bath, but rarely plasticizes → less informative); uniaxial tensile (more informative); indentation on used aligners (punctual viscoelastic params, relies on curve fitting). Invisalign elastic modulus ↓ after 7 days, stable after 14, relaxation modulus inverse → more plastic after usage. Hardness: PETG/PC/hTPU monolayers similar; multilayers slightly softer; PP lowest. Water sorption effect on hardness stronger than cold-working + biofilm combined. Oral pH 5–9 (generally acidic); scarce data on acidity effect; hTPU may chemically alter at low pH.

6.1 ORTHODONTIC FORCE & STRAIN MEASUREMENT
- OMSS (orthodontic measurement and simulation system): dentition model with movable teeth + 3D force/torque measurement. Cannot be immersed (only temperature); only static forces in literature.
- Wax-filled tooth-hole variant: simulate soft tissues, freeze, OMSS at 37°C constant force, compare scans. Showed SMP reforming weekly (1 aligner/2 phases) achieves comparable or larger displacement vs conventional thermoplastics (if similar soft-tissue properties).
- DIC (digital image correlation): two cameras over black/white spot-patterned aligner, strain from spot separation. No aging/temperature (affects paint); can be in vivo with spotted paper on dentition.
- Force-sensitive paper/film between aligner and model: color by force magnitude; extra layer overestimates force. OMSS measures forces nearer biological range than thin films. Multilayer force decay after aging much less than hTPU/PETG monolayers.
- Capacitive force sensor bonded to aligner: similar to OMSS, but extra layer overestimates, wiring affects fit.
- Piezoelectric sensor (minimal thickness, wireless via electromagnetic coupling): only proof-of-principle, single in-vivo immediate force.
KEY: multilayered aligner force more gentle than monolayers — 2–3× lower than PETG, 1.5–2× lower than hTPU in models; 3D resins enact almost no force.
ACTIVATION: misfit dentition vs aligner (mm); all systems designed at 0.25 mm activation. Higher activation → higher initial forces but decay to same threshold → higher activation only means over-recommended forces that quickly drop.
LDH (lactate dehydrogenase) in saliva = soft-tissue damage marker (intracellular enzyme leaks as membrane permeability rises). Measured for metallic appliances (40–80 mgf 21 days; 125 mgf) but NO study applying to clear aligners.

6.2 STRESS RELAXATION & CREEP
Stress relaxation = main property for CAT instruction. Plastics are viscoelastic → viscous force drop until stable settling force. Creep = beneficial small amount (dissipate excess force over PDL, ease fitting/removal) but excessive creep compromises effectiveness. Creep does not represent reality (strains defined by tooth-movement velocity + deformation history; creep forces too high). Stress relaxation preferred — set strain, follow force. Conventional strain 0.6% used but real aligner strain spans 0.01%–1.8%. Methods: universal testing (tensile/3PB), DMTA (lower forces, cyclic testing resembling fit/removal). Material more viscous after usage and after aging.
PETG vs TC-85 (3D resin) at 37°C & 80°C: at 37°C both decline to plateau after 5 cycles; TC-85 more pronounced (higher deformability), force retention extremely poor — 10% vs 80% in PETG (first cycle). At 80°C 3D resin shows shape recovery (negative strain → zero with cycles). SMPs: thermal cycling above Tg → reverts to programmed deformed config, ↑ recovery stress; shape memory deteriorates after limited cycles; residual strain accumulates each cycle (also seen in PETG & TC-85).
hTPU stress drop more gentle than PETG monolayers but initial stress/force too high in both (higher in hTPU). PP worst (easily deformed, highest force drop). Multilayers ↓ initial force + gentle drop (soft layer). PETG+PC blend ↓ force drop. Aging/use ↓ initial+final forces and ↓ force drop. hTPU chemical changes in acid ↓ force drop. PETG long-time (200 h): force keeps decreasing with sudden drops + slight increases (slip bands). 3D printed aligner stress relaxation is most problematic — resins do lower forces but cannot sustain force (fast softening). Nano-indentation creep/relaxation only on Invisalign multilayer: creep index & relaxation index ↑ in used samples.

7. THERMAL PROPERTIES
Tg = ability of chains to slip/move/dissipate energy → high Tg = less permanent deformation = more stable treatment; also sets thermoforming parameters. TPU more accurate at reproducing mold shape (Tg slightly higher than PETG). Measure via DSC (heat/cool profiles) or DMTA (storage modulus = elastic energy; loss modulus = viscous energy lost; Tg = max of loss modulus profile). TPU larger loss modulus than PETG (better fit, efficiency). 3D resins much higher loss moduli → deform quickly to dentition, cease force. PVC loss modulus much higher at low temp → restricted to 14 h/day to avoid plastification.
Tg values: PETG 75–80°C, TPU 80–95°C (near oral temp) → new higher-Tg materials appearing. 3D printing: only approved direct-print material has Tg ≈ 37°C → cause of extremely low efficiency & low forces. Thermoforming + water ↓ Tg in all polymers; thermal properties don't change after aging in thermoformed PETG. TPU has amorphous + crystalline phases; melting enthalpy ↓ after thermoforming, Tg shifts lower (↑ amorphous phase). No study on aligner thermal properties after usage.

8. BIOCOMPATIBILITY
8.1 In vitro: measure metabolic activity of oral cells exposed 15 days to aligner (colorimetric enzyme assays). Most materials low/null cytotoxicity; health-risk ones are novel direct-3D-print resins or SMPs (yet to be tested deeply; current findings suggest similar/better cell viability among thermoformed, SMP, 3D printed).
Microplastics/leaching concerns: histologic reactions, estrogenic effects, endocrine disruptors (bisphenols, phthalates). PC made from bisphenols but fully reacted in commercial BPA-free PC → cannot leach (BUT some non-EU/USA manufacturers mislabel BPA-free). BPA also in some TPU (Invisalign). BPA leachates low/below health risk or undetectable; TPU & PETG release almost undetectable BPA, but Invisalign material detected higher BPA. BPF: TPU & multilayers may release; negligible in PETG. BPS negligible in all. No estrogenic effect observed in Invisalign after 2 weeks. Method: liquid chromatography (+ extraction); FTIR-ATR less successful (can't detect ppm).
BPA-free plastics: ALL BPA-free polyesters (including PCTG) have detectable estrogenic effects in vitro, UNLIKE PETG (no detectable estrogenic effect). TPU not in that study. Tests used estrogen-responsive cancer cell lines (proliferation + luciferase). PCTG monomers leach no estrogenic chemicals (but more aggressive leachate method → not comparable). No definitive list of estrogenic substances / thresholds → not fully clear PCTG & BPA-free are non-estrogenic (same for TPU).
Histological reactions: none on PETG or most multilayers despite rising PEG contact allergy reports. Invisalign material may cause reactions (isocyanate in its TPU). No allergy in other multilayer brands with copolyester outer layer.
Biofilm: methods SEM, bioluminescence, CFU counting. No difference in biofilm formation between materials; cannot be avoided, only alleviated by cleaning (commercial sanitizing tablets). Gold-nanoparticle coating effective vs biofilm; hydroxyapatite on functionalized 3D-printed aligners → slight mineralization improvement but ↓ mechanical properties + ↑ cytotoxicity.
8.2 In vivo: chewing/speaking/removal may irritate oral mucosa. Invisalign & gold-nanoparticle-coated Invisalign minimal oral irritation; conventional aligner slight erythema (associated with bacteria/biofilm). Metal fixed appliances higher pathogens/plaque than clear aligners. PETG vs TPU: in rotation, PETG → large root movements, TPU → mainly crown movement (little root) → PETG generates greater orthodontic forces (useful for root displacement). PETG aligners cause significantly higher wear on bonded attachments than TPU (no attachment → enamel reduction, accelerated in acid + extensive uncleaned use).

9. ENVIRONMENTAL EFFECTS & RECYCLABILITY
Clear aligners = medical devices → cannot be recycled (health risk), must be burned in specialized facilities under sterile transport (though sorting/recycling frameworks exist). Most patients discard at home as house waste → infection risk. Composition secrecy hinders recyclability. Align Technology & others now send aligners to third company that sterilizes + remelts into hard recycled plastic (but loss of properties → undesirable as same-purpose raw material). Biodegradable products appearing (scarce data). Green polymers from plant sources: PEF (polyethylene furanoate, terephthalic acid → furan dicarboxylic acid) ↓ greenhouse gas, similar to PET (amorphous, more rigid), but no biocompatibility/toxicity data; PCTG also fully plant-sourced. Enzymatic degradation proposed (bacteria/fungi for PET/PETG; serine hydrolase for some PU) — needs homogeneous waste + economic process. No full LCA of CAT exists. 3D printed aligners & SMPs reduce thermoforming molds + material waste + carbon footprint.

10. PERSPECTIVES & RESEARCH DIRECTIONS
Main efforts: direct 3D printing (better fit, faster, cheaper) — but unknown cytotoxicity, scarce biological safety data, only few resins FDA-approved (mostly mouthguards/splints, not aligners). 3D printing may not increase process efficiency.
SMPs promising, commercially emerging, but little efficiency/cytotoxicity data.
Two specialist targets: (1) PRESCRIPTION DURATION — most prescriptions 14 days same material; some studies show 10 or 7 days same efficiency (depends on treatment ease + patient response, no clear guideline). (2) SOFT vs HARD PLASTICS for different movements — aligned teeth needing only expansion/retraction → rigid plastic (small strains); crowded teeth → softer plastic (gentle forces longer); no guidelines on when to switch stages or how many rigid/soft aligners.
Aiders under development: optical attachments (accelerate movement during sleep — fewer aligners, no pain/efficacy difference); vibrational tools (5 min/day → increased efficiency/predictability/pain perception); piezoelectric aiders (↓ pain, lower forces via faster bone regeneration).
Final gap: NO studies linking applied force level to soft-tissue damage. LDH-sensitive papers + sensors exist → could enable "personalized" force per patient via software incorporating aligner viscoelastic features + needed movements.

KEY KNOWLEDGE GAPS (author-summarized themes):
- No clinical evidence for claimed TPU/EVA comfort superiority.
- No studies of non-Invisalign thermoplastics after intraoral usage.
- No aligner thermal properties after usage.
- No LDH force-damage study on clear aligners.
- No full life-cycle assessment (LCA) of CAT.
- Unknown cytotoxicity / efficiency of 3D resins and SMPs in use.
- No guidelines for prescription duration or rigid-vs-soft staging.
