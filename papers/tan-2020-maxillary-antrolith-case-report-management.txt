Case report of a maxillary antrolith

Authors: Tan Yong Leng Thurston, Zhang Yang, Bertrand Chew Shen Hui
Journal: International Journal of Surgery Case Reports (2020)
DOI: 10.1016/j.ijscr.2020.08.011
PMCID: PMC7452633

Summary:
Antroliths are rare pathological calcifications (mineral salt depositions around a central nidus) in the maxillary sinus. First described by Bartholin in 1654. Prevalence: 0.15% to 3.2% in maxillary sinuses. Most are asymptomatic incidental findings. Gender: 55-60% female.

Case:
67-year-old lady referred for dental clearance before Denosumab therapy (breast cancer history). Incidental rounded radiopacity on panoramic radiograph in right maxillary sinus. CBCT: 3.1mm x 3.6mm circumscribed dense radiopacity at sinus floor near buccal roots of #17. No mucosal thickening or opacification.

Management: Patient chose removal+biopsy (concerned about breast cancer metastasis).
- CBCT reviewed for surgical access measurements
- Caldwell-Luc procedure under local anesthesia
- 5mm round buccal window, 20mm from cuspal edge of #17
- Antrolith clearly visible and retrieved
- Histopathology: metaplastic bone and calcification → confirmed antrolith

Etiology of antroliths:
- Endogenous nidus: tooth fragments, bony chips, blood, mucus, fungi
- Exogenous nidus: gutta percha, cotton, metal objects, dental burs, silver points
- Precipitation of calcium salts (calcium phosphate, calcium carbonate) around nidus
- Predisposing factors: long-standing infection, poor sinus drainage, foreign body presence

Symptoms (when symptomatic):
- Epistaxis, facial pain, chronic sinusitis, nasal obstruction
- Purulent/blood-stained discharge, foul-smelling postnasal drip
- Otorrhea, anosmia, cacosmia, halitosis

Imaging:
- CT appearance: homogeneous, high-density periphery with central lower density (variable)
- CBCT: circumscribed dense radiopacity (as in this case)
- CT preferred for identification, localization, and complications

Differential diagnosis:
- Impacted teeth, root fragments, osteomyelitis
- Calcified mucus retention cysts, calcified polyps
- Ossifying fibroma, fungal ball calcification
- Benign/malignant tumours

Management:
- Small/asymptomatic: periodic surveillance; prophylactic removal NOT necessary
- Complicated/symptomatic: surgical removal
  - Endoscopic sinus surgery (ESS)
  - Caldwell-Luc procedure
  - Combination of both
- Supplement with treatment of concurrent sinus infection
- Prognosis: generally good; no recurrences documented

Implications for dental/implant planning:
- Antrolith in sinus floor may be discovered on pre-implant CBCT
- Should be included in differential diagnosis of sinus floor radiopacity
- Decision: small/asymptomatic → observe; large/symptomatic → removal before sinus lift
- Histopathological evaluation justified even in asymptomatic cases (to rule out malignancy)
