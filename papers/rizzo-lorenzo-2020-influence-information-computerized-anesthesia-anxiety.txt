Influence of information concerning a computerized anesthesia system on dental anxiety: a randomized controlled clinical trial

Rizzo-Lorenzo A, Sánchez-Torres A, Noguera-Mutlló C, Pérez-Beltrán I, Figueiredo R, Valmaseda-Castellón E
Med Oral Patol Oral Cir Bucal. 2020;25(2):e217-e223
DOI: 10.4317/medoral.23315
PMID: 32040470
PMCID: PMC7103442

ABSTRACT

Background
A single-blinded randomized controlled trial among patients requiring an upper third molar extraction was performed to evaluate the anxiety degree after receiving information or not about the functioning of The Wand system. Secondarily, perceived pain and the need of re-anesthesia were assessed.

Material and Methods
Patients were randomly assigned to the experimental group (detailed explanation about The Wand) or control group (no specific information). Local anesthesia with The Wand consisted in a supraperiosteal infiltrative technique injection 1.6 mL at the buccal and 0.2 mL at the palatal side. Distinct questionnaires for assessing dental anxiety and 100-mm visual analog scales to assess pain were delivered. Demographic data, radiological parameters, operative time and type of intervention were also registered.
A descriptive bivariate analysis by non-parametric tests to detect differences in anxiety, pain and re-anesthesia was performed by SPSS 22.0 (SPPS Inc. Chicago, USA).

Results
A total of 85 patients were assessed for eligibility but 17 participants were lost due to the cancellation of the visit for the surgical intervention. Finally, sixty-eight patients were included (34 participants in each group), 47 women (69.1%) and 21 men (30.9%), with an average age of 28.8 (± 9.3) years.

Conclusions
Patients that received a detailed explanation of The Wand did not have a significant reduction of the anxiety degree and perceived pain during the anesthetic act compared to patients that received no information. The need of re-anesthesia was not related to the anxiety level but was significantly related to increasing operative time.

Keywords: Dental anxiety, pain, local anesthesia, third molar, computerized anesthesia.

FULL TEXT

Introduction

Anxiety and pain during dental procedures are well described in the literature. Despite current improvements on materials and techniques, dental anesthesia is usually one of the most feared parts of dental procedures. This could difficult our maneuvers and create discomfort to patients. Pain during anesthesia can be generated by the puncture or by the fluid release and worsened by anxiety and fear from patients. Injecting slowly at a low pressure seems to reduce pain and increase comfort during dental anesthesia.

The research into this field aims to develop less invasive and painful systems compared to conventional anesthesia. The Wand (Milestone Scientific, Deerfield, IL) was designed to decrease pain and anxiety produced by the traditional anesthetic syringes. It consists in a computerized controlled injection activated by a footswitch pedal. The hand piece is similar to a pen, which constitutes an advantage as it does not remind a conventional syringe. The device maintains the anesthetic liquid flow at a constant volume and pressure, independently from the tissue resistance, and it can be used at a slow or high speed. Presumably, it relieves few drops that precede the needle puncture, thus, creating a virtually imperceptible injection. This system delivers mild beeps during the procedure. However, some studies speculate that it could produce some patients to be more nervous because this is a new and unknown system for them compared to the traditional syringe.

The palatal injection is usually painful. As the speed of liquid flow seems to be the main cause, the low speed injection is recommended in areas of dense tissue such as palatal mucosa. A study published by Shah compared the perceived pain of palatal injections by The Wand or by traditional syringe and obtained better results with The Wand. Interestingly, some studies made in children did not find relevant differences in terms of pain for highly anxious individuals. As anxiety may reduce the threshold of pain and patients waiting for a surgical procedure experience anxiety, the misunderstanding or the lack of information about the procedures could even worsen their feelings and pain response. Into this field, there is a lack of studies addressing the benefits of giving information about the anesthetic technique.

The aim of this study was to evaluate the anxiety degree after receiving information or not about the mechanism of The Wand system, previous to an upper third molar extraction. The secondary aim was to assess the perceived pain and need of re-anesthesia.

The hypothesis was that previous explanation would reduce the anxiety levels and perceived pain after the anesthetic act. Furthermore, very anxious patients would need more re-anesthesia during the surgical procedure than less anxious individuals.

Material and Methods

This study has been carried out according to the CONSORT guidelines. All patients signed an informed consent, and the study protocol was approved by the ethical review board of the Dental Hospital of the University of Barcelona (Protocol number 11/2016). The Declaration of Helsinki guidelines were followed throughout the trial.

Experimental design
A single-blinded randomized controlled trial was performed among patients requiring an upper third molar extraction treated by postgraduate students of the Master of Oral Surgery and Orofacial Implantology at the dental hospital of the University of Barcelona.

Participants
Inclusion criteria were patients from 18 to 45 years old, coming for an upper third molar extraction (either erupted, partially or totally included) in absence of symptomatology, willing to participate in the study and signed the informed consent for treatment.

Exclusion criteria: dental professionals or students (possible knowledge of the anesthetic system), patients with systemic diseases (≥ASA III), suffering from allergy or intolerance to the local anesthesia administered, patients who needed other tooth extraction in the same surgical act, surgical interventions lasting more than 60 minutes, active infection in maxillofacial area, concomitant treatment with systemic antibiotics and/or analgesics and abnormal hemodynamic parameters (heart rate <50 or >110 bpm, systolic BP <70mmHg or >150mmHg and diastolic BP <50mmHg or >100mmHg).

Study intervention
At the 1st visit, patients answered the STAI-T (State-Trait Anxiety Inventory) questionnaire to identify their anxiety trait. Then, at a 2nd visit when patients came to perform the surgical procedure, they were randomly assigned to the experimental group (detailed explanation about the functioning of The Wand system) or control group (no specific information about the system) by means of a computer generated random sequence through www.randomization.com.

The explanation for the experimental group was verbal and standardized: "The Wand is a local anesthesia system whose main objective is to reduce pain and anxiety during the anesthetic act. It has a pen-shaped design producing less visual impact, thus reducing patient anxiety. Moreover, this device releases anesthesia regardless of tissue resistance, at a low pressure and a constant flow, which produces less pain during puncture and injection. In your case, the anesthetized area will be the buccal gingiva and the palate associated to the third molar that is going to be extracted." No explanation was given to participants belonging to the control group.

Registered variables
Anxiety degree measured by distinct questionnaires (Interval Scale of Anxiety Response (ISAR), Modified Dental Anxiety Scale (MDAS), Dental Fear Survey (DFS) and State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI)) was the primary outcome. Perceived pain measured by 100 mm VAS at 4 different times (palatal and buccal puncture and anesthetic infiltration) and need of re-anesthesia during the surgical procedure were secondary outcomes. Demographic data (age, gender), radiological parameters (Pell & Gregory, Winter, Parant classifications), operative time (minutes; incision to last suture knot) and type of intervention (with/without ostectomy and/or odontosection) were also registered.

The ISAR questionnaire: horizontal stroke on a 90 mm vertical VAS. MDAS: modification of Corah's Dental Anxiety Scale, 5 questions, max score 25, ≥19 = very anxious. DFS: 20 questions, dental-anxiety-specific, score 20 (no fear) to 100 (terrified); cut-off 63. STAI: 40 questions in two groups — state anxiety (transient) and trait anxiety (latent tendency). STAI-T administered at visit 1; STAI-S (state) administered post-anesthesia.

Surgical technique
Three blinded and previously calibrated researchers (AST, CNM, IPB) performed local anesthesia with The Wand system by a supraperiosteal infiltrative technique, injecting 1.6 mL buccally and 0.2 mL palatally, using a short 25 mm 30G needle (Artinibsa; Inibsa, Lliçà de Vall, Spain) and one cartridge of 4% articaine with 1:100,000 epinephrine (Artinibsa; Inibsa). After the anesthetic act, one investigator (ARL) delivered the questionnaires (ISAR, MDAS, DFS, STAI-S) and the four VAS scales simultaneously.

Surgeries were performed by Master fellows with similar experience and the same technique: full-thickness flap, bone removal with high-speed (40,000 rpm) handpiece and tungsten carbide round bur with constant sterile saline irrigation, as needed. Wound closed with 3/0 silk interrupted suture.

Sample size calculation and statistical analysis
Sample size: α-error 0.05, power 0.9, effect size d=0.8, calculated with G*Power 3.1.9.2. Total sample 68 patients, 34 per group. Descriptive/bivariate analysis by SPSS 22.0. Normality assessed by Shapiro-Wilk; non-parametric tests used given non-normal distribution (U-Mann Whitney for continuous, Fisher exact for dichotomic variables). MDAS and DFS results analyzed as dichotomic variables. Significance set at p<0.05.

Results

85 patients assessed for eligibility at visit 1; 17 lost due to cancellation of the surgical visit. Final sample: 68 patients — 47 women (69.1%), 21 men (30.9%), mean age 28.8 (±9.3) years (range 18–67). 30 (44.1%) right and 38 (55.9%) left upper third molars extracted; mean operating time 19.2 (±11.7) minutes. Parant classification: 55 extracted conventionally (type 1), 13 needed ostectomy (type 2).

Anxiety questionnaire scores were low overall for both the overall sample and each group; few individuals were highly anxious. Non-parametric tests (U-Mann Whitney, Fisher) showed NO significant difference between groups for any anxiety measure — previous explanation of The Wand did not influence anxiety level.

No significant differences were found for VAS pain scores during vestibular and palatal puncture and infiltration between groups.

29 (42.6%) patients required intraoperative re-anesthesia. There was a statistically significant relationship with increasing operative time (p=0.007): interventions with no need for additional anesthesia lasted 15.5 min (SD=8.8) vs 24 min (SD=13.3) for those needing re-anesthesia. No significant difference for need of re-anesthesia by anxiety level (any questionnaire) or by previous explanation (p=0.806). No significant relationship between re-anesthesia and radiological variables (distal space p=0.245; depth p=0.400; Winter's position p=0.235) or type of intervention (Parant p=0.059).

Discussion

Fear of pain from the anesthetic injection is very frequent, and these patients could report greater pain during the anesthetic act than patients without fear.

Upper third molar removal was selected as a reproducible surgical model; palate is one of the most painful areas, useful to test the intervention's efficacy. Topical anesthesia was deliberately not used to reduce potential bias in VAS pain collection. Anxiety and pain being subjective variables complicates comparison with the general population — a study limitation; multiple questionnaires were used to reduce bias.

This study showed previous explanation of the anesthetic system does not decrease anxiety level as measured by distinct questionnaires — no statistically significant between-group differences. Inclusion of individuals with a previous upper third molar extraction could bias questionnaire answers. No prior studies address anxiety depending on information delivered about the anesthetic system specifically. Recent research found information provided to patients did not change overall satisfaction after impacted third molar extraction. The amount of information is controversial — some patients could experience more anxiety from an excess of information.

Computerized controlled anesthetic delivery systems better control flow/pressure, which can reduce pain during local anesthesia infiltration. A recent literature review showed most studies comparing conventional vs computerized controlled anesthesia systems found better results in the maxilla for computerized delivery (VAS, electric pulp tester, dental anxiety scale, perceived stress scale). However, the sound/beeps emitted by The Wand, the slowness of the act, and observing an unknown device could provoke more fear than a traditional syringe — issues raised in some studies.

A study by Wang interviewed MDAS-diagnosed anxious patients for suggestions to improve anxiety; all agreed more information regarding treatment indication/steps would help understanding and allow questions, reducing perceived anxiety — but seeing real images/instruments close to the mouth ("tell-show-do") would exacerbate anxiety. A RCT by Heaton studied a computerized systematic-desensitization program (CARL) vs an informative pamphlet to reduce dental injection fear — CARL produced greater fear reduction than the leaflet. Both studies support that information/explanation could reduce perceived anxiety/pain, contradicting the present results. A limitation of the present study: few very anxious patients were included; more between-group differences might emerge with more extremely anxious patients.

No prior studies in adults assess need of re-anesthesia with computerized controlled anesthetic delivery systems. Here, need of re-anesthesia (42.6% total) correlated with increasing operative time but not anxiety level nor previous explanation — a high re-anesthesia rate given the mean operative time, possibly overestimated since surgeries were performed by fellows (some cases of intraoperative discomfort/pressure might be interpreted as pain).

A RCT by Patini (children 5–12y, contralateral extractions) observed four times more need of intraoperative re-anesthesia with conventional syringe vs The Wand (no operative-time data available). Computer-controlled delivery devices seem to achieve more diffusion of anesthetic solution than manual syringe.

Overall, computerized anesthesia systems seem preferred — reduces disruptive behavior in children and pain during injection. A prospective cohort study reported over half of patients would pay additionally to be anesthetized by a minimally invasive system, confirming positive experience vs traditional injection.

Conclusion: Patients receiving a detailed explanation of The Wand system before upper third molar extraction did not have significantly reduced anxiety or perceived pain during the anesthetic act vs patients receiving no information. Need of re-anesthesia was not related to anxiety level but was significantly related to increasing operative time.
