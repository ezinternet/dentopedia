The Use of Premixed Calcium Silicate Bioceramic Sealer with Warm Carrier-Based Technique: A 2-Year Study for Patients Treated in a Master Program

Authors: Fausto Zamparini, Andrea Spinelli, Filippo Cardinali, Pietro Ausiello, Maria Giovanna Gandolfi, Carlo Prati
Journal: Journal of Functional Biomaterials (J Funct Biomater) 2023;14(3):164
DOI: 10.3390/jfb14030164 | PMID: 36976088 | PMCID: PMC10054578
Publication date: 2023-03-18
Source: PubMed Central full text (PMC10054578). According to PubMed; DOI https://doi.org/10.3390/jfb14030164

=== ABSTRACT ===
Background: Recently several calcium silicate flowable sealers have been introduced as endodontic materials for the root canal. This clinical study tested the use of a new premixed calcium silicate bioceramic sealer in association with the Thermafil warm carrier-based technique (TF). Epoxy-resin-based sealer with the warm carrier-based technique was the control group.
Methodology: Healthy consecutive patients (n = 85) requiring 94 root canal treatments were enrolled and assigned to one filling group (Ceraseal-TF n = 47, AH Plus-TF n = 47) in accordance with operator training and best clinical practice. Periapical X-rays were taken preoperatively, after root canal filling and after 6, 12 and 24 months. Two evaluators blindly assessed the periapical index (PAI) and sealer extrusion (k = 0.90). Healing rate and survival rate were evaluated. Chi-square tests were used to analyze differences between groups. Multilevel analysis evaluated factors associated with healing status.
Results: A total of 89 root canal treatments in 82 patients were analyzed at end-line (24 months). Total drop-out was 3.6% (3 patients; 5 teeth). 91.1% healed teeth (PAI 1-2) in Ceraseal-TF, 88.6% in AH Plus-TF. No significant difference in healing outcome or survival (p > 0.05). Apical extrusion occurred in 17 cases (19.0%): 6 in Ceraseal-TF (13.3%), 11 in AH Plus-TF (25.0%). Three Ceraseal extrusions were radiographically undetectable after 24 months. All AH Plus extrusions did not change.
Conclusions: The combined use of the carrier-based technique and premixed CaSi-based bioceramic sealer showed clinical results comparable with carrier-based technique and epoxy-resin-based sealer. The radiographical disappearance of apically extruded Ceraseal is a possible event in the first 24 months.

=== 1. INTRODUCTION ===
Calcium silicate cements (CaSi) were introduced in clinical use more than twenty years ago as root-end filling materials for their hydraulic properties. Through continuous modification of chemical composition and mechanical properties, CaSi cements have been proposed as sealers for cold obturation techniques. Recently, the formulation of these materials ("bioceramics") changed from powder-liquid to premixed, flowable, "ready to use" materials. Several CaSi-based (mainly containing CaSi particles) and CaSi-containing (minor amounts of CaSi) sealers are now available.

Ceraseal is a premixed CaSi-based bioceramic sealer which includes tricalcium silicate (20-30%) and dicalcium silicate (1-10%) as bioactive components. Zirconium dioxide is the radiopacifier (45-50%). The chemical-physical properties have been investigated in only two studies, which reported lower radiopacity, lower setting time and similar flowability when compared to AH Plus. The ability to release calcium and to alkalize the environment was reported in one recent in vitro study. These properties appear attractive for use in warm filling techniques, such as the warm carrier-based gutta-percha technique.

The clinical advantages of the warm carrier-based gutta-percha technique are well documented: ease of use, short learning curve, reliability in obtaining technical results, and adequate clinical results. Traditionally, warm carrier-based procedures are associated with epoxy-resin-based sealers. Previous studies on carrier-based techniques reported success rates ranging from 81 to 96% after 3-5 years. One drawback of the warm carrier-based technique is the higher risk of sealer extrusion in the periapical bone area. In this context, a CaSi-based bioceramic sealer may be clinically attractive in case of periapical extrusions. Clinical data on CaSi-based sealers used with cold filling techniques have been reported in recent short-term studies (12 months), reporting high success. No clinical study reported the use of Ceraseal with warm carrier-based techniques.

This study analyzed the use of a new premixed CaSi-based bioceramic sealer in association with the warm carrier-based technique. Epoxy-resin-based sealer with the warm carrier-based technique was the control group. The null hypothesis was that both filling techniques and sealers provide a similar outcome after 24 months.

=== 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS ===
Study design: Prospective cohort study conducted May 2019 - March 2022 at the Endodontic Clinical Section, Dental School, University of Bologna. Patients treated by a pool of postgraduate master students under standardized protocols and strict supervision of experienced tutors. Approved by local ethical committee (OUTENDOPROSP; CE 20079). Declaration of Helsinki (2013), STROBE checklist, Dodson 2007 guidelines.

Sample size: Primary outcome = survival status of teeth after RCT with 2 different filling techniques. Assumed 24-month survival rate 0.80-0.90. Accepting probability of non-significant decrease of 0.10, calculated 40 teeth per group to test null hypothesis at alpha=0.05 with 80% power. Increased to 47 per group (94 RCTs) to compensate for follow-up losses.

Enrolment: May 2019 - February 2020. 129 patients needing RCT; 17 excluded (unable to attend regular follow-up), 8 (geographical location), 3 (multiple fixed rehabilitations), 1 (medical conditions), 15 (insufficient structural integrity / hopeless tooth).

Root canal treatment: Local anesthesia (Carboplyne 30 mg/mL Dentsply), rubber dam. Primary RCT: straight-line access, working length (WL) at 0.5 mm from radiographic apex with periapical radiographs + electronic apex locator (Root ZX, Morita). Pre-flared with K-file up to #15, then NiTi instruments (Mtwo, VDW; or Rotate, VDW). 5 mL of 5% NaOCl irrigant; for calcified canals 3.0 mL of 10% EDTA; final 2.0 mL sterile water before filling. No intracanal medication. Secondary RCT: reciprocating NiTi (Reciproc Blue, VDW) + ultrasonic tips (StartX), apical enlargement with Reciproc Blue #40.

Filling techniques: Premixed CaSi-based bioceramic sealer (Ceraseal, MetaBiomed, Cheongju, Republic of Korea) used with warm carrier-based technique (Thermafil, Dentsply). Ceraseal contains tricalcium silicate (20-30%), dicalcium silicate (1-10%), tricalcium aluminate (1-10%), zirconium dioxide (45-50%) and thickening agents. Epoxy-resin-based sealer (AH Plus, Dentsply) with warm carrier-based technique = control. RCT filling by 2 operator groups each trained for only one technique. Before the study (Feb-Apr 2019), 12 post-graduate Master in Endodontics operators were randomly assigned to a full training course on either Ceraseal-TF or AH Plus-TF. In both groups carrier was pre-heated using Thermaprep obturator oven. Sealer applied with K-file #20 to WL-3 mm, pre-heated carrier inserted at WL-0.5 mm, excess removed with round bur. Sealed with cotton pellet + hygroscopic radiopaque temporary restoration (Coltosol).

Tooth restoration: 2 weeks after filling, temporary removed, crown restored under rubber dam. Self-etch bonding (Clearfil SE BOND), flowable (G-Aenial Flow) + composite (G-Aenial) resins, 1.0 mm increments, photo-cured. All restorations occlusally loaded. No post applied. Provisional/definitive prosthetic rehab after ~6 months when indicated.

Radiological evaluation: Periapical radiographs (parallel technique). Filling "adequate" when carrier at 0-1.0 mm from radiological apex; overfilling and short filling recorded. Follow-up at 6 (T6), 12 (T12), 24 (T24) months. PAI (periapical index) scored single-blind by two additional operators at baseline, T6, T12, T24. Sealer extrusion recorded/measured (mm long axis) using ImageJ; categorized 0 mm, 0.1-1.0 mm, 1.1-3.0 mm, >3 mm. PAI calibration weighted kappa k=0.90 intra- and inter-operator.

Success/survival criteria: Success = no periapical lesion (PAI 1-2) and no clinical signs/symptoms. Failure = periapical radiolucency PAI >=3. Survival = success + surviving teeth with lesions. Cause of extractions recorded.

Statistics: Stata 17. Chi-squared and Fisher exact tests for parameter distribution. Multilevel mixed-effect logistic regression for healed status (dichotomous: PAI 1-2 healed vs PAI 3-5 non-healed). Age/gender as person-specific fixed effects; clinical parameters as tooth-specific random effects; clustering within patients with robust estimator. Significance 0.050.

=== 3. RESULTS ===
Demographics: 85 patients initially enrolled, 94 RCTs. Drop-out = 3 patients / 5 RCTs. End-line: 82 patients, 89 RCTs analyzed. Groups homogeneous except age (p=0.020; Ceraseal-TF more teeth in patients <30 y) and sealer extrusions (p=0.040; Ceraseal-TF lower percentage of extrusions).

Outcome: Cumulative 24-month survival rate 97.8% (2 teeth lost, 2.2% — one Ceraseal-TF, one AH Plus-TF; both horizontal root fracture). No pain, fistulae, swelling during follow-up. Healed teeth (PAI <3) after 24 months: 91.1% Ceraseal-TF, 88.6% AH Plus-TF. No difference (Pearson chi-square=0.9427, p=0.624; Fisher exact=0.714).
Multilevel mixed logistic regression: only initial PAI and endodontic treatment type statistically associated with different healing outcome (p<0.05). Preoperative initial PAI >3 and presence of a previous endodontic treatment were predictors of non-healing at 24 months.

Sealer extrusion/resorption: Ceraseal-TF 6 extrusions / 44 RCTs (13.3% — note: text reports out of 44 at endline); AH Plus-TF 11 extrusions / 44 RCTs (25%). Three Ceraseal apical extrusions were resorbed after 24 months, other three stable. All AH Plus extrusions stable and well-detectable after 24 months. (Overall 17/89 = 19.0% extrusion.)

=== 4. DISCUSSION ===
First clinical test of a premixed CaSi-based bioceramic sealer with warm carrier-based gutta-percha technique; similar results vs epoxy-resin-based sealer. Both ~90% success at 24 months. Procedures by post-graduate master operators after 3 months training under tutor supervision.

CaSi materials set in wet environment, release biologically active ions, expand, and nucleate apatite in phosphate solutions; positive bio-interaction with periapical tissues and mineralizing cells, osteoinductive properties. Warm carrier-based technique selected as widely considered "gold standard" obturation; easier/more reproducible in master program. Heat effect: in vitro, CaSi sealer dehydration/organic degradation at 100-225 °C affects setting time, flowability, film thickness; PEG (water-soluble solvent) may thermally degrade. But in vivo temperature much lower — Donnermeier et al. showed max rise to 58 °C with warm filling — so only limited modification of Ceraseal physical properties expected with carrier-based technique.

Periapical healing increased from preoperative status in both groups; no new periapical lesions or re-exacerbation. Ceraseal-TF lower extrusion (13.3%) vs AH Plus-TF (25%), likely from different chemical/physical properties. AH Plus with warm techniques increases fluidity/flow out of apex and deeper tubule penetration. AH Plus radiopacity markedly higher (10.00-11.8 mmAl) than Ceraseal (~6.5 mmAl) — explains higher detectability and greater diameter of extruded AH Plus; lower Ceraseal radiopacity may require greater extrusion volume to be detected.

Three Ceraseal extrusions resorbed and completely undetectable after 24 months; AH Plus extrusions stable at all times (consistent with prior studies). Only two prior articles evaluated apical over-extrusion frequency of premixed CaSi-based sealers (both cold techniques); both reported higher over-extrusion vs epoxy-resin sealer. Extrusion morphology influenced by root diameter and presence of periapical lesion: periapical bone defects (PAI 3-5) associated with circular-morphology extrusions; intact apical bone with smaller extrusion.

Effect of extrusion on healing debated. Biological response depends on chemical composition / ion or toxic release. Older study: extrusion -> unfavorable healing (chronic inflammation source). More recent studies: non-significant effect of traditional extruded sealers on healing. Different behavior supposed for Ceraseal/CaSi-based: solubility linked to high calcium release and bioactivity (apatite nucleation), modifying sealer structure apically. Epoxy-resin sealers set and remain chemically/dimensionally stable and inert. Solubility of CaSi sealers may correlate with radiographic disappearance of extruded sealer; calcium release activates mineralizing stem cells (bone marrow cells, adipose-derived MSCs). Clinically useful when CaSi sealer extruded into periapical bone defects.

CaSi-based sealers can induce interfibrillar mineralization of demineralized dentine collagen (dentin biomineralization), penetrate dentinal tubules, improve sealing over time by filling voids and forming apatite with dentine remineralization — biological rationale for CaSi sealer use given NaOCl/EDTA demineralized/deproteinized substrate that may predispose to fracture.

Limitations: short follow-up (24 months; ESE recommends >=4 years to state healing), prospective design, primary and secondary RCT not separately analyzed (potential bias in survival), operator randomization (to create homogeneous trained operator groups). No perforations or iatrogenic complications; impact of master-student skills considered negligible. Survival/success high and in line with 81-96% at 2-5 years in prior studies.

=== 5. CONCLUSIONS ===
The study supports clinical use of premixed CaSi-based sealer with warm carrier-based technique. The two techniques showed comparable clinical outcome. Premixed CaSi-based sealer had less radiopacity, lower extrusion occurrence, but higher radiographic disappearance when apically extruded (likely related to solubility and calcium release). Sealer extrusion did not affect healing outcome. Further long-term studies needed to validate these filling materials.
