Chen, Li, Hill, Baysan. "The use of toothpastes containing different active agents in managing root caries: A systematic review with network meta-analysis." J Dent. 2026;168:106582.
PMID: 41720286
DOI: 10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106582
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41720286/

ABSTRACT (verbatim):

This systematic review aimed to evaluate current evidence according to toothpastes containing different active ingredients for the management of root caries in laboratory-based, in situ and clinical studies. Conducted per Cochrane guidelines. Literature searches across nine databases (up to 31.01.2024). In vitro and in situ studies evaluated using a quality assessment tool. Cochrane RoB2.0 for clinical studies. Two reviewers independently did selection, extraction, risk of bias, and GRADE certainty. Thirteen in vitro, three in situ and 12 clinical studies met eligibility. Mean differences (MD) calculated for lesion depth, mineral loss and root caries increments. Risk ratios (RR) for changes in lesion hardness and new root carious lesions, random effects model. Meta-analysis revealed significant differences in root carious lesion depth (MD: -55.75; 95% CI, -90.70 to -20.80; p=0.002) and mineral loss (MD: -1.18; 95% CI, -1.85 to -0.51; p=0.0006) between toothpaste and placebo. In clinical studies, toothpastes with high sodium fluoride (5000 ppm) or 1.5% arginine plus 1450 ppm NaPOF, vs standard 1450 ppm NaF, appeared effective for hardening and reversing root carious lesions (RR: 1.60; 95% CI: 0.92 to 2.79). Pooled estimates: root carious lesions became hard following bioactive toothpaste (1100 ppm NaF + 1% CaSO4 + 1.1% NH4H2PO4) vs standard toothpastes (RR: 1.81, 95% CI: 1.39-2.36). Toothpaste with high-concentration sodium fluoride was most effective at reducing lesion depth, improving hardness, and preventing new caries. Bioactive components delivering calcium and phosphate and adjunctive fluoride+arginine may manage these lesions vs standard 1450 ppm NaF which might be insufficient to arrest active root caries in high-risk patients. High-concentration sodium fluoride toothpaste by prescription can be first-line non-invasive therapy for arresting active root caries; alternatively OTC fluoride+arginine or bioactive toothpastes could be optimal for patients who cannot tolerate/decline high-fluoride prescriptions.
