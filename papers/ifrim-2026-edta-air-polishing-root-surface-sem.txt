Title: Effects of Adjunctive EDTA and Air-Polishing Powders on Periodontitis-Affected Root Surfaces: A SEM Study
PMID: 42075624 | PMCID: PMC13117045

ABSTRACT
Mechanical subgingival instrumentation remains the gold standard in periodontitis treatment; however, it may leave residual debris and induce surface alterations. Adjunctive strategies such as air polishing and ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) might optimize root surface conditions. To evaluate, by scanning electron microscopy (SEM), the effects of scaling and root planing (SRP) combined with EDTA, with or without adjunctive erythritol- or glycine-based air polishing, on root surface alterations and smear layer formation. Ten extracted human teeth affected by periodontitis (yielding twenty samples) were included. Two teeth served as descriptive controls. The remaining teeth were allocated to four treatment groups. The first three groups included samples obtained from the middle portion of the roots: S (SRP + EDTA), Se (SRP + erythritol air polishing + EDTA), and Sg (SRP + glycine air polishing + EDTA). The fourth group, Js, consisted of samples from the cementoenamel junction (CEJ) treated with SRP + EDTA. SEM images were appreciated qualitatively and assessed using ordinal scores (0–3) for marks, cracks (×100), and smear layer (×1000). Non-parametric statistics were applied. A significant difference in mark scores was found among S, Se, and Sg samples (H = 13.411, p = 0.001), with Se samples showing lower mark scores than S (p = 0.001). Crack scores also differed among groups (H = 12.038, p = 0.002), with higher values observed in Se compared to S (p = 0.001). Smear layer scores did not differ among groups (H = 0.102, p = 0.950). Compared with S samples, Js differed only in marks (p = 0.009), with no significant differences in cracks or smear layer. Within the limitations of this in vitro study, root surface alterations and smear layer formation showed variable responses across treatment protocols, with comparable smear layer scores. Similar effects were observed for CEJ and mid-root samples; however, these findings should be interpreted with caution.

1. INTRODUCTION
Periodontitis is one of the most prevalent chronic inflammatory diseases worldwide, inducing progressive destruction of the tooth-supporting tissues. The gold standard of active periodontal therapy is subgingival mechanical instrumentation (scaling and root planing, SRP). Residual microislands of calculus frequently persist after SRP, contributing to sustained periodontal inflammation. Conversely, aggressive or repeated subgingival scaling may result in immediate cemental loss and the formation of surface marks. Air-abrasive powders such as glycine and erythritol enhance subgingival biofilm disruption while inducing minimal soft- and hard-tissue damage. Mechanical SRP, alone or with air polishing, may generate a smear layer composed of infected cementum, bacterial toxins, biofilm, and calculus remnants. The smear layer creates an unfavorable environment for cell attachment. EDTA is a chelating agent that binds calcium ions at neutral pH and removes the inorganic component of the smear layer, exposing the collagen matrix and improving surface permeability; it eliminates residual calculus, improves clot formation, and exerts antibacterial effects. There is limited in vitro evidence investigating the combined impact of SRP, air-polishing powders, and EDTA on root surface micromorphology.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
Study design: morphological changes in dental root surfaces of extracted human teeth affected by periodontitis following mechanical instrumentation (manual + ultrasonic) or combined with adjunctive air polishing and EDTA. Root surfaces evaluated qualitatively by SEM; post-treatment mechanical damage (instrumentation marks and cracks) and residual surface debris (smear layer) assessed semi-quantitatively.
Ethics: approved by Ethics Committee, Iuliu Hatieganu University Cluj-Napoca (160/06 Sep 2024) and Cluj County Emergency Hospital (52951/09 Nov 2023). Teeth extracted for periodontal reasons; informed consent obtained.
Sample selection: 10 extracted human teeth (premolars, molars). Inclusion: calculus on apical two thirds of root or near CEJ; no root caries; no fractures/anomalies; relatively flat root surfaces. Stored in 4% chloramine-T, used within one month.
Experimental groups and instrumentation:
- Group 1 (Ct): 2 teeth, uninstrumented controls.
- Group 2 (S): 2 teeth, SRP + 24% EDTA (mid-root).
- Group 3 (Sg): 2 teeth, SRP + AIR-FLOW PERIO Powder (glycine-based, EMS) + 24% EDTA.
- Group 4 (Se): 2 teeth, SRP + AIR-FLOW PLUS Powder (erythritol-based, EMS) + 24% EDTA.
- Group 5 (Js): 2 teeth with calculus at CEJ, treated by SRP + 24% EDTA (CEJ region).
SRP: ultrasonic scaler (1S tip, Acteon) to remove visible calculus, then Gracey curettes 5/6 (Hu-Friedy), modified pen grasp, 45-90° working angulation, 4 strokes on entire root length with light standardized force. Air polishing (groups 3, 4): erythritol- or glycine-based for 5 s at 5 mm working distance. EDTA (all except group 1): immersed in 24% EDTA for 2 min, then rinsed. Single operator (S.S.I.) trained by senior (A.R.).
Sectioning: longitudinal (apico-coronal) section ~2 mm thick; each specimen bisected transversely → two samples (final ~4×4×2 mm). Total 20 samples. Each tooth allocated to single treatment group.
SEM: rinsed, dehydrated (graded ethanol 33-100%), mounted on aluminum stubs, sputter-coated with ~200 Å gold-palladium. Examined at 100× to 15,000× by single blinded investigator. Semiquantitative scoring (ordinal 0-3) by senior investigator (A.R.) with SEM operator (L.B.T.) of two microscopic fields per sample (near mark + middle): instrumentation marks (×100), surface cracks (×100), smear layer/residual detritus (×1000). Eight evaluations per group per parameter.
Statistics: SEM scores ordinal (0-3), non-normal → median (25th-75th percentile). Kruskal-Wallis among S, Se, Sg with Bonferroni-adjusted pairwise comparisons. Mann-Whitney U for S vs Js. p < 0.05 significant. No formal power calculation (exploratory). Limited sample size noted.

3. RESULTS
Control (Ct): extensive dehydration-related polygonal cracking consistent with cementum; some elevated deposits compatible with calculus. Calculus rough, granular, "cauliflower-like" texture of agglomerated micronodules. Some samples showed porous honeycomb-like network consistent with EPS biofilm remnants.
S group: root surface covered by continuous smear layer largely obscuring the underlying substrate.
Se and Sg groups: pronounced polygonal cracking consistent with cementum + randomly distributed scratches; cementum-like pebble microtexture, slightly blurred (discrete smear layer). No microislands of calculus remnants on instrumented samples.
Js (CEJ): clear demarcation line separating two morphologically distinct tissues; polygonal cracking and pebble-like cementum surface partially obscured by smear layer; adjacent region smooth/compact (enamel-like).

KEY STATISTICS:
- Mark score: significant difference among S, Se, Sg (Kruskal-Wallis H = 13.411, df = 2, p = 0.001). Se < S (p = 0.001). Sg vs S p = 0.054; Se vs Sg p = 0.646.
- Crack score: significant difference among S, Se, Sg (Kruskal-Wallis H = 12.038, df = 2, p = 0.002). Se > S (p = 0.001). Sg vs S p = 0.314; Se vs Sg p = 0.018.
- Smear layer score: NO significant difference among S, Se, Sg (Kruskal-Wallis H = 0.102, df = 2, p = 0.950).
- S vs Js (Mann-Whitney): significant only for mark score (U = 9.000, Z = -2.604, p = 0.009). Crack p = 0.116; smear layer p = 0.913.

4. DISCUSSION
SEM of controls confirmed mature calculus and EPS remnants. Cementum appeared preserved in treated samples (pebble-like morphology + crack pattern), relevant because excessive hand-curette instrumentation may cause unnecessary root substance loss. No residual calculus after treatment, consistent with other low-calculus-score reports. Lower mark score in Se group may suggest less aggressive surface effect, but partial masking by residual smear layer cannot be excluded. JS samples explored instrumentation at the clinically sensitive CEJ; marks differed between S and Js but CEJ surfaces differ morphologically and should be interpreted cautiously. Increased surface roughness promotes bacterial adhesion (threshold 0.2 μm; increase to 0.8 μm enhances biofilm). Cracks consistently associated with cementum, likely dehydration artifacts during processing, useful to distinguish cementum from calculus/dentin. No significant smear layer differences among protocols; smear layer strongly adheres to root, multifactorial formation. Dentinal tubules not identified. EDTA attractive due to near-neutral pH limiting cytotoxicity. In vivo, smear layer eliminated biphasically (rapid reduction within 7 days, significant decrease by 28 days).
Limitations: small number of teeth per group; no formal intra/inter-examiner calibration; ex vivo design; SEM specimen preparation may influence surface appearance; semi-quantitative scoring subjective; multiple samples from same tooth introduce dependence between observations.

5. CONCLUSIONS
EDTA did not result in complete smear layer removal under the tested conditions; however, its potential role in reducing subgingival deposits supports its role as a conservative adjunctive in periodontal therapy. Root surfaces at the CEJ exhibited a similar response to instrumentation as mid-root areas, indicating that controlled mechanical debridement can be performed in this anatomically challenging region without inducing additional surface damage.
