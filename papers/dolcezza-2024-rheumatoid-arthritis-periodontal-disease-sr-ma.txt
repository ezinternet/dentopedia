Relationship Between Rheumatoid Arthritis and Periodontal Disease—Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
Dolcezza S, Flores-Fraile J, Lobo-Galindo AB, Montiel-Company JM, Zubizarreta-Macho Á
J Clin Med. 2024;14(1):10. PMID 39797091; PMCID PMC11720692; DOI 10.3390/jcm14010010
Source: PubMed Central full text (JATS).

ABSTRACT
The aim of this systematic review and meta-analysis was to determine the association between rheumatoid arthritis and periodontal disease. This systematic review and meta-analysis of the scientific literature was carried out based on the recommendations of Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA). We analyzed all studies that evaluated the relationship between the chronic inflammatory diseases through the response to non-surgical periodontal treatment, comparing the values of CAL (Clinical Attachment Level) for PD (periodontal disease) and DAS28 for RA. A total of three databases were searched in the literature search: Pubmed, Scopus, and Web of Science. After eliminating duplicate articles and applying certain inclusion criteria, of the 29 articles found, a total of 6 were included in the present study. A statistically significant difference in mean reduction of -0.56 mm was obtained for CAL, with a 95% confidence interval of the difference between -0.82 and -0.31 (z-test = -4.33; value = 0.001) in favor of the periodontal treatment group. The heterogeneity of the meta-analysis was slight (I2 = 39% and Q = 8.19; value = 0.146). For DAS28, treatment showed a mean reduction of -0.39 DAS points, with a 95% CI between -0.46 and -0.31 (z-test = -10.3; value < 0.001) among patients with PD and RA. The present study shows how the control of periodontal disease through non-surgical periodontal treatment can reduce the severity of RA. This finding consistently supports the idea that there is a pathogenic association between these two chronic inflammatory diseases.

1. INTRODUCTION
The human microbiome encompasses all microorganisms inhabiting the skin, mucous membranes, and intestinal tract. The periodontium supports the teeth through alveolar bone, root cementum, periodontal ligament, and gingival mucosa. Periodontal disease, a multifactorial inflammatory condition, progressively destroys these tissues, beginning with gingivitis-induced connective tissue degradation and mucosal disintegration.
Factors such as dental plaque and systemic conditions contribute to periodontal disease, which may lead to systemic complications like endocarditis, respiratory and coronary diseases, cerebrovascular events, diabetes control issues, pregnancy complications, and autoimmune diseases. Autoimmune conditions like Sjögren's syndrome, Crohn's disease, systemic sclerosis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and rheumatoid arthritis (RA) have significant odontostomatological impacts. RA, a chronic autoimmune disease affecting 0.5–1% of adults globally, is mediated by inflammatory cascades involving immune complexes, leading to joint destruction.
RA predominantly affects women aged 40–50 and can impact various joints, including the TMJ, spine, and dentoalveolar joint. Chronic oral inflammation shares pathogenic mechanisms with RA, including leukocyte infiltration and cytokine release (e.g., TNF-α, ILs, GM-CSF, RANKL, and MMPs). Periodontal bacteria are implicated in autoimmune pathology and found in high levels in RA patients' serum and synovial fluid, underscoring a pathogenic link between periodontitis and RA-associated osteoarticular degeneration.
The aim of this work was to determine the association between rheumatoid arthritis and periodontal disease, with a null hypothesis (H0) stating that there is no association between rheumatoid arthritis and periodontal disease.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
Study Design. The literature review was conducted following PRISMA. PROSPERO registration number: CRD42020192179. PICO question: "Is there a link between periodontal disease and rheumatoid arthritis?" Population: patients affected with periodontal disease and affected by rheumatoid arthritis; Intervention: non-surgical periodontal treatment; Comparison: patients submitted and non-submitted to periodontal treatment; Outcome: clinical attachment loss (CAL) and DAS28.
An electronic search was carried out using PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane, and Web of Science, assessing all literature published internationally up to March 2024. Five MeSH terms: "Periodontitis"; "autoimmune disease"; "rheumatoid arthritis"; "periodontal disease"; "randomized clinical trial". Boolean operator "AND". Two researchers (S.D.; A.Z.M.) searched the databases simultaneously.
Inclusion criteria: randomized clinical trials; samples of patients of any age affected by periodontitis or periodontal diseases and rheumatoid arthritis. No restriction by language or year. Exclusion criteria: systematic reviews, clinical cases, case series, editorials; studies including patients with another oral disease, another autoimmune disease, or whose periodontitis/RA was related to another pathology.
Risk of bias was assessed using the Jadad scale (5 items: randomization, researcher/patient blinding, descriptions of losses during follow-up; score 0–5; <3 = low quality).
The meta-analysis used the random effects model inverse variance method. Effect size = difference in means between the periodontal treatment group and the control group for CAL (mm) and DAS28 score, assessed with z test. Heterogeneity analyzed using Q test and I2. Significance at < 0.05. Publication bias analyzed using the trim-and-fill method of funnel plot skew adjustment.

3. RESULTS
3.1 Flow Diagram. A total of 29 articles were obtained: 21 from PubMed, 5 from Web of Science, and 3 from Scopus. 3 discarded as duplicates. After title/abstract screening, another 20 eliminated, leaving 6. The six articles were assessed in the quantitative analysis.
3.2 Qualitative Analysis. All 6 selected were randomized clinical trials. Most values expressed as mean and standard deviation, except one measured in IQR. CAL (mm) and DAS28 (standard units) compared. Sample sizes below 100 patients; no dropouts/rejections detected. Each study had a control group and an experimental group, using non-surgical periodontal treatment and medical laboratory analyses.
3.3 Quality Assessment (JADAD). Most studies described losses and retreats. Not all articles were double-blind, except Silvestre-Rangil et al. and Thilagar et al.; most were randomized.
3.4 Quantitative Analysis.
3.4.1 CAL. The six studies were combined using the random effects model inverse variance method to estimate the mean difference in CAL (mm) between the periodontal treatment (n = 124) and control groups (n = 131), obtaining a statistically significant difference of -0.41 mm with a 95% CI between -0.70 and -0.12 (z test = -2.73; p-value = 0.006) in favor of the periodontal treatment group. No heterogeneity: I2 = 7%, Q test = 5.38; p-value = 0.372.
3.4.2 Publication Bias (CAL). Trim-and-fill: by adding three studies, the effect size was estimated at -0.30 mm, 95% CI -0.55 to -0.046 (z test = -2.31; p-value = 0.021). Analysis free of publication bias.
3.4.3 DAS28. Six studies combined; statistically significant difference of -0.56 in DAS28 score, 95% CI -0.82 to -0.31 (z test = -4.33; p-value = 0.001) in favor of the periodontal treatment group. Heterogeneity slight: I2 = 39%, Q test = 8.19; p-value = 0.146.
3.4.4 Publication Bias (DAS28). Trim-and-fill: three studies added, effect size re-estimated as -0.39 DAS28 points, 95% CI -0.46 to -0.31 (z test = -10.3; p-value < 0.001). Analysis not affected by publication bias.
[Note: abstract and discussion report -0.56 as the CAL difference and -0.39 as the adjusted DAS28; the results section labels the primary -0.56 estimate under DAS28 and -0.41 mm under CAL. The pre-adjustment CAL difference is -0.41 mm (I2=7%); the pre-adjustment DAS28 difference is -0.56 (I2=39%); trim-and-fill adjusted values are -0.30 mm (CAL) and -0.39 DAS28 points.]

4. DISCUSSION
The results support the null hypothesis (H0) that no significant association exists between RA and periodontal disease [authors' framing], yet findings demonstrate the beneficial effects of non-surgical periodontal treatment in alleviating RA symptoms, evidenced by significant reduction in DAS-28 scores in the experimental groups. Non-surgical treatment decreased periodontal pathogen levels and improved/stabilized CAL parameters in individuals with both periodontal disease and RA.
While control groups exhibited worsening/progression of both inflammatory conditions, treatment groups experienced stabilization over the 2-month study period, aided by weekly periodontal exams, prophylaxis, and reinforcement of oral hygiene instructions. CAL scores (threshold 4 mm indicating active periodontal disease) increased in control groups, reflecting progression of periodontitis; treatment groups showed statistically significant CAL improvement, mean difference -0.56, 95% CI -0.82 to -0.31 (z-test = -4.33; p = 0.001), concurrent with improvements in RA indices.
A strong correlation (r = 0.97) was found between DAS28 and the original DAS (inflamed joint count). DAS28 ranges 0 to 9.4, categorizing disease activity as low (DAS28 ≤ 3.2), moderate (3.2 < DAS28 ≤ 5.1), or high (DAS28 > 5.1). Patients initially showed moderate RA activity; treatment groups shifted from moderate to low disease activity, in contrast to worsening control groups. Mean reduction in DAS28 was -0.39 points, 95% CI -0.46 to -0.31 (z-test = -10.3; p < 0.001).
Controlling periodontal infections and gingival inflammation through scaling/root planing and plaque control in individuals with moderate periodontal disease may contribute to a reduction in the signs and symptoms of active rheumatoid arthritis.

5. CONCLUSIONS
Controlling periodontal disease through non-surgical periodontal treatment may help reduce the severity of RA, supporting a potential association between these two chronic inflammatory conditions. A multidisciplinary approach is recommended for patients with RA, emphasizing diagnosing periodontal involvement and incorporating non-surgical periodontal therapy alongside conventional antirheumatic treatments. Further research is needed.
