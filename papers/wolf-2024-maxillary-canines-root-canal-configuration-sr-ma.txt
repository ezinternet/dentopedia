Morphology and root canal configuration of maxillary canines: a systematic review and meta-analysis
Wolf TG, et al. BMC Oral Health 2024. DOI 10.1186/s12903-024-04682-z | PMID 39143543 | PMCID PMC11325658

ABSTRACT
Background: This study assessed the internal morphology of maxillary canines (MxC) through a systematic review of existing literature.
Methods: Research articles up to June 2024 were retrieved from five electronic databases (MEDLINE via PubMed, Embase, Scopus, LILACS, and Cochrane). Predefined search terms and keywords were used, and potential studies were identified by cross-referencing and bibliographies of the selected articles reviewed.
Results: Two hundred studies were identified, 73 duplicates were removed, 127 records were screened, and 113 were removed after consultation of title and abstract. After full-text consultation and hand searching, finally 22 studies were included. Using the method for describing the root canal configuration (RCC) of Briseno Marroquin et al. (2015) and Vertucci (Ve) (1984), the most frequently reported RCC of MxC were 1-1-1/1 (Ve I, 75.4-100%), 2-2-1/1 (Ve II, 0.1-20%), 1-2-1/1 (Ve III, 0.1-11.6%), 2-2-2/2 (Ve IV, 0.1-0.4%), 1-1-2/2 (Ve V, 0.1-2.4%), 2-1-2/2 (Ve VI, 0.5-1.2%), and 1-2-1/2 (Ve VII, 0.1-0.2%). The meta-analysis of six studies (Europe/Asia) showed that a significantly higher number of RCC of 2-2-1/1 (Ve II) (OR [95%CI] = 1.34 [0.53, 3.41]), 1-2-1/1 (Ve III) (OR [95%CI] = 2.07 [1.01, 4.26]), and 1-1-2/2 (Ve V) (OR [95%CI] = 2.93 [1.07, 8.07]), were observed in males, and 2-2-2/2 (Ve IV) (OR [95%CI] = 0.08 [0.00, 4.00]) in females. No sex differences in the RCC of 1-1-1/1 (Ve I) and 1-2-1/2 (Ve VII) were observed.
Conclusions: Cone beam computed tomography is the most frequently used method for research on the RCC of MxC. Despite the high prevalence of type 1-1-1/1 (Ve I) RCC in MxC, clinicians should remain vigilant for more complex and sex-differentiated patterns in up to 25% of cases to prevent endodontic treatment complications or failures.

INTRODUCTION
Detailed knowledge of three-dimensional internal morphology and root canal configuration is crucial for endodontic treatment success. The most frequent RCC of single-rooted maxillary canines is a single root canal from pulp chamber to apex (1-1-1/1, Vertucci I). However, different populations studied with modern 3D imaging show that up to a quarter of teeth have anatomical variations. Past ex vivo methods: clearing technique, scanning electron microscopy, light microscopy. CBCT is a modern effective in vivo tool, superior to previous methods, though inferior to micro-CT for fine structures. Classifications by Vertucci and Weine et al. describe variations but cannot capture all morphological intricacies vs. more modern classifications (Briseno Marroquin et al.). Aim: systematically review internal morphology and RCC of MxC, and identify sex influence.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Systematic review of published literature on internal morphology and RCC of MxC up to June 2024. Five databases searched: MEDLINE via PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Database, LILACS, Scopus, plus grey literature. PRISMA guidelines followed. Protocol registered in PROSPERO (CRD42023394478). Inclusion: RCTs, cross-sectional, comparative, validation, evaluation studies of internal morphology and RCC of MxC without restrictions. Excluded: case reports and reviews. Search: ("root canal configuration" OR "root canal system" OR "root canal morphology") AND ("maxillary canine" OR "maxillary anterior teeth") AND ("morphology" OR "anatomy"). Two independent reviewers (T.R., A.L.W.). RCC classified using Vertucci, Weine et al., and Briseno Marroquin et al. Risk of bias: Anatomical QUality Assessment (AQUA) tool. Quality assessment: NHLBI customized tool. Statistics: RevMan 5.4. Odds ratio for effect size. I2 statistic for heterogeneity (I2<35% low -> fixed-effects; I2>35% substantial -> random-effects). Primary outcomes (RCC, sex, geography) as ORs with 95% CI. p<=0.05 significant.

RESULTS
Literature search through five databases: 200 articles. 127 remaining after removing duplicates, screened by title/abstract. 14 articles in full text, 4 excluded. 12 articles added after hand search. Total: 22 reviewed articles.
Most frequently observed RCC of MxC, regardless of method or sample number, is 1-1-1/1 (Vertucci I or Weine I) with frequency 75.4-100%.
Second most common: Briseno Marroquin 2-2-1/1 (Vertucci II; Weine II) with 0.1-20%.
Third common (up to 11.6%): Briseno Marroquin 1-2-1/1 (Vertucci III); Weine classification does not include this type.
Other RCCs less frequent: 2-2-2/2 (Vertucci IV / Weine III), 1-1-2/2 (Vertucci V), 2-1-2/2 (Vertucci VI), 1-2-1/2.
CBCT is the most used research method; radiographic, staining and clearing, or micro-CT methods less frequent.
Six studies reported sex-specific differences; meta-analysis differed by origin (Europe/Asia).

DISCUSSION
Briseno Marroquin 1-1-1/1 RCC is most frequent across all studies; lowest frequencies reported by Weng et al. (75.4%) and Somalinga Amardeep et al. (81.6%). In half of 22 studies, 2-2-1/1 (Vertucci/Weine II) reported as second most common (0.1-20%). Nearly all studies reported 1-2-1/1 (Vertucci III; not in Weine) as third most common (0.1-11.6%). Other RCCs (2-2-2/2, 1-1-2/2, 2-1-2/2, 1-2-1/2, 1-1-3/3 = Vertucci IV, V, VI, VII, VIII) less frequent. One of 22 studies published before Vertucci classification reported only single root 1-1-1/1.
Micro-CT is gold standard for ex vivo research of internal tooth morphology. CBCT and micro-CT non-destructive 3D imaging. CBCT enables less detailed visualization than micro-CT but allows clinical in vivo use (micro-CT not possible in humans due to high radiation).
Six of 22 studies compared sex differences; all by CBCT except one (staining and clearing). All authors: 1-1-1/1 most observed in both sexes (up to 99.5% men, up to 100% women). Sex-specific differences comparing Europe vs Asia. Differences explained by methodology or ethnic origin.
Although most common RCC is 1-1-1/1 (Ve I), clinician should be aware of complex internal morphology in up to 25% of cases. These include connecting canals or accessory root canals that cannot be prepared mechanically, emphasizing importance of chemical root canal irrigation and careful obturation.
Limitations: methodology distortions, CBCT/micro-CT artifacts, subjective evaluation criteria. Aging, caries, tooth wear cause narrowing via secondary dentin but minimal effect on main canal morphology. Some studies lacked sex/age data. Age not included in analysis.

CONCLUSIONS
Within limitations of this SR+MA: CBCT is the most frequently used method for RCC of MxC research. Despite high prevalence of type 1-1-1/1 (Ve I), clinicians should remain vigilant for more complex and sex-differentiated patterns in up to 25% of cases to prevent endodontic complications/failures.
