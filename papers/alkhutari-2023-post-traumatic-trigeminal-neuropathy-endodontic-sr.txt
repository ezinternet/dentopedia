Title: Post-traumatic Trigeminal Neuropathy Associated With Endodontic Therapy: A Systematic Review
Journal: Cureus, 2023
Retrieved via PubMed MCP get_full_text_article (PMC full text, JATS-derived).

==================================================================
INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND

Persistent dentoalveolar pain (PDAP) is an idiopathic condition of chronic
neuropathic origin that manifests as a diagnostic challenge for dental
practitioners. Three different root cause origins are discovered: psychogenic,
vascular, and neuropathic. Neuropathic origin is declared to be the primary due
to the phenomena of deafferentation. The loss of the spinothalamic tract, which
transmits somatosensory information regarding pain, itch, and rough touch, causes
deafferentation. Even after the wounds have fully healed, deafferentation of nerve
fibers, which usually occurs due to a severe injury, causes ongoing discomfort,
paresthesia, and dysesthesia. PDAP is located primarily in the teeth and jaws.

PDAP can originate after multiple invasive dental treatments, such as tooth
extraction, bone grafting, dental implantation, apicoectomy, and endodontic
treatment. It was initially discovered by McElin and Horton in 1947, and from that
point forward, there have been numerous clinical cases in the literature,
particularly related to root canal therapy (RCT).

The initial symptom is intraoral unilateral pain but, with time, the pain extends
bilaterally in up to one-third of the patients, which could be deep or superficial.
Pain may vary but is usually experienced as aching, burning, or throbbing. The
intensity of pain ranges from mild to severe. The beginning of pain that frequently
follows dental treatments, such as tooth extraction, endodontic therapy, and sinus
surgery, is triggered by a temperature change. The pain may occur without preceding
events or in the absence of dental causes.

The duration of persistent idiopathic facial pain, which includes atypical
odontalgia (AO), could last for more than two hours up to three months, despite the
absence of neurological defects. PDAP most commonly affects the upper jaw molars and
premolars. Females have been seen to predominate in 65-85% of PDAP cases. The average
age range is 15-80 years for female patients and 23-69 years for male patients.

Earlier terminology (1974: "idiopathic periodontalgia"; 1978 Marbach: "Phantom Tooth
Pain"; 1978: "atypical odontalgia"). The HIS recently proposed the name painful
post-traumatic trigeminal neuropathy (PTTN) to describe neuropathic pain with a
traumatic etiology that affects the trigeminal nerve, affecting face, mouth, and
other areas of the head.

GOAL: systematically review the possibility of developing PTTN if the patient received
endodontic therapy and the time interval between endodontic therapy and PTTN.

==================================================================
REVIEW / METHODS

Level 1 and level 2 evidence with a low-to-moderate risk of bias. All English-language
studies linking human endodontic patients and painful PTTN: cohort, cross-sectional,
prospective, retrospective, RCTs. High-quality research on this subject was lacking.
IRB Riyadh Elm University (FRP/2020/278/293 (REU)).

Databases: PubMed, Web of Science, Cochrane CENTRAL. Search terms: painful PTTN, PDAP,
AO, neuropathic orofacial pain, atypical facial pain, phantom tooth pain, persistent
idiopathic facial pain. Published 2009-2020, human subjects. Five independent reviewers.

==================================================================
STUDY CHARACTERISTICS

Of the 10 articles chosen:
- 2 (20%) retrospective clinical trials
- 4 (40%) retrospective studies
- 1 (10%) retrospective observational study
- 1 (10%) retrospective + prospective cohort
- 1 (10%) prospective cohort
- 1 (10%) prospective longitudinal cohort

Each research: 8 to 1,331 individuals. Mean age 40 to 55.4 years; gender distribution
31% male to 64% female. Median age of PTTN is 40 years. Maxillary arch prevalence
reported in 2/10 studies; mandibular arch in 2/10. The interval between onset of PTTN
and start of RCT is 3-48 months.

==================================================================
DIAGNOSTIC TESTS FOR PTTN

Clinical examination tests: extraoral exam of face/head/neck, periapical radiographs,
asymmetry, tender points, auscultation/palpation of TMJs, mandibular movement. Intraoral:
occlusion, interferences with root-filled teeth, swelling/sinus tract, periodontal
probing depths, pain to pressure/percussion, restoration margin integrity. Most-reported
diagnostic procedures in 8/10 papers. Clinical interviews (pain "rarely interrupted sleep",
"non-responsive to NSAIDs") in 1/10. Diagnostic LA for tissue hyperesthesia in 2/10. CBCT
(differentiate symptomatic apical periodontitis vs AO) in 1/10. Pain Catastrophizing Scale
+ SF-MPQ in 1/10. VAS (0-10) + S-LANSS survey (chronic pain >3 months) in 2/10. OHRQoL
(OHIP-14) in 1/10. Pain intensity in 1/10. CT for other diagnoses in 1/10.

DISCUSSION — KEY NUMBERS PER STUDY

Six of 10 papers used "atypical odontalgia". Treatment for pain: amitriptyline
(50-75 mg/day) and/or carbamazepine (400-600 mg/day) + vitamin B complex.

Van der Cruyssen et al. 2020 (1,331 patients, Belgium/England, retrospective clinical
trial): Causes of trigeminal nerve damage:
- Third molar: 519 (48%) — most frequent, females mid-40s
- Dental implants: 146 (13%)
- Third molar extraction: 136 (13%)
- Local anesthesia: 125 (12%)
- ENDODONTIC TREATMENT: 86 (8%)
- Trauma: 74 (6-7%)
Arch not mentioned. Mean age 46 years. 112 days between PTTN diagnosis and treatment.

Females (40-55.4 years) greater predominance in PTTN than males. One paper: more common
in females mid-40s, maxillary molar/premolar regions; mean age continuous neuropathic
orofacial pain 54.5 years, female predominance 86.9% (n=20). Contrast: another study =
no gender predilection, more prevalence in mandibular arch.

Oshima et al. 2009 (271/Japan, retrospective): RCT causes PTTN in maxillary/posterior;
16 endodontic follow-up patients (13 F, 3 M), mean 46.8 yrs — 14 (87.5%) maxillary pain,
2 (12.5%) mandibular. 10 (62.5%) discomfort started right after retreatment; 5 (31.3%)
right after pulp extirpation; 1 unclear. Onset 1-2 months.

List et al.: of 46 cases of PTTN, 56% upper jaw pain vs 45% lower jaw. Upper jaw ratio
8:2, majority molar area ratio 5:3.

Klasser et al. 2011 (250 adults/USA, retrospective): 3.1% persistent non-odontogenic pain.
22 patients maxillary non-odontogenic, 17 mandibular. Neuropathic pain higher in
middle-aged (mean 50.6 yrs), no sex preference, HIGHER IN MANDIBULAR ARCH. 4 years.

Time between PTTN and RCT: non-odontogenic pain after ≥6 months post-RCT = 34%
(95% CI 1.4-5.5).

Causes of PTTN/nerve injury after failed endodontic treatment: internal/external
resorption defects communicating with root canal, perforation communicating with external
root surface, root fracture, lower molar teeth with root apices close to the inferior
dental canal and/or mental foramen, dens invaginatus, periapical lesions, cysts.

Philpott et al. 2018 (198 patients/264 teeth, London): 20% of sample showed persistent
pain. Predictive factors for PTTN: after RCT, female gender, history of chronic pain,
teeth responsive to pulp test, teeth associated with preoperative pain, tooth crack prior
to treatment, diameter of preoperative radiolucency. 5-14 months post-NSRCT.

Conditioned pain modulation (CPM): PTTN patients showed less effective CPM than controls.
Duration of cold pain after removal of cold stimulus significantly longer in PTTN. Impaired
CPM linked to fibromyalgia, tension headache, TMD, migraine, IBS.

Peñarrocha et al. 2012 (63/Spain, retrospective case study): trauma antecedents — impacted
lower third molar extraction (34 patients, 54%), simple extraction (9 patients), ENDODONTIC
TREATMENT (1 patient). Pain in 4 males, 32 females mid-40s. ≥6 months.

De Poortere et al. (retrospective): treatment subgroups linked to PTTN — implant surgery
(n=9, 31%) primary; third molar extraction (n=6); facial fractures (n=4); BSSO (n=4);
ENDODONTIC THERAPY (n=3).

3-6% of endodontic treatment patients have PTTN, prevalence highest in women (40 patients),
per two studies.

Klazen et al. 2018 (Leuven, retrospective 2-year cohort): 8,845 new patients, 53 cases of
iatrogenic trigeminal nerve injury (36 F 68%, 17 M 32%). Endodontics-related: 6%. Avg age
42.9 yrs F (15-80), 40.4 yrs M (23-69). Females > males.

Renton and Yilmaz: iatrogenic inferior alveolar nerve injuries (IANI) brought on by
temporomandibular surgery (60%), LA (19%), implants (18%), ENDODONTICS (8%).

Nixdorf et al. 2015 (prospective longitudinal cohort, National Dental PBRN): pain present
6 months after RCT — non-odontogenic 8 patients (7 referred TMD, 1 PDAP), mixed 2 (1 PDAP,
1 TMD). Maxillary teeth = 53% of treated teeth, 89% posterior teeth. Females, 40s.
38/354 (11%) met pain criteria; mean age 49.

Pigg et al.: after endodontic treatment, 3.4% frequency of chronic non-odontogenic pain.

Jacobs et al. (case-control): 5.7% phantom pain; women:men 9:1. Most complaints molar area
ratio 8:2, upper jaw ratio 5:3.

Melek et al.: orofacial discomfort in PTTN 66% and TN 80%. TN pain attacks more frequent
(71%) than PPTTN (28%); TN more numbness (12%). TN patients depression signs (54%) vs PPTTN
(36%); anxiety similar (34-39%).

Benoliel et al. 2012 (145 total: 91 PTTN, 54 CTN): 96% of PPTTN cases had trigeminal sensory
abnormalities (mostly allodynia, hyper/hypoalgesia); 1% had anaesthesia. PTTN 48.60±15.2 yrs.

Diagnostic criteria (Graff-Radford and Solberg 1992) best describe the condition.

DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA FOR AO (idiopathic toothache):
- Pain in a tooth or tooth site
- Continuous or almost continuous pain
- Pain persisting more than four months
- No signs of local or referred pain
- Equivocal somatic nerve block

==================================================================
CONCLUSIONS

High-to-moderate level of evidence regarding PTTN after endodontic treatment. Pain reported
after endodontic treatment mostly arising from non-odontogenic neuropathic origin. Evident
symptoms: persistent pain after endodontic treatment caused by impaired endogenous analgesia,
pronociceptive pain modulation, and failed endodontic treatment, with more female predominance
in mid-40s. 10 articles showed a prevalence of an endodontic procedure 3-48 months following
post-endodontic treatment, mainly affecting females mid-40s, no variation maxilla vs mandible.
Distinguishing pulpal pain from PTTN helps practitioners properly diagnose PTTN without further
procedures that negatively affect patient tolerance.
