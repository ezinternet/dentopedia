Title: Immediate implant using an inverted body-shift design versus a conventional conical implant: A randomized clinical trial with 1 year follow-up
Authors: Andrea Galve-Huertas et al.
PMID: 41273736
PMCID: PMC12983383
DOI: 10.4317/medoral.27776
Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12983383/

Introduction

In the 1970s and 1980s, implants were traditionally placed only after complete healing, which consequently prolonged treatment. Tooth extraction triggers alveolar bone remodelling, including horizontal and vertical resorption of the buccal and lingual plates. In the anterior region, these changes can compromise aesthetics, particularly if the extraction is not atraumatic. In 1978, Wilfred Schulte (University of Tübingen, Germany) introduced the immediate implant concept, placing dental implants directly into extraction sockets at the time of tooth removal. This technique, later refined, aims to shorten treatment time while preserving surrounding bone and soft tissue. Success depends on atraumatic extraction, precise implant positioning for primary stability, and, when appropriate, immediate provisionalization to support peri-implant tissues. The integrity of the extraction socket-particularly the buccal plate-is critical determinant of success, and grafting may be required to manage gaps between the implant and socket walls. Another treatment option for the upper anterior region is early implant placement. In implant dentistry, this refers to placing an implant into the extraction site after a short healing period-typically 4-8 weeks to allow soft tissue healing, or 12-16 weeks for partial bone healing.

Over the past two decades, interest in immediate implant placement has grown considerably. Although technique-sensitive, it has become a routine procedure with success rates comparable to conventional implants. Its advantages include shorter edentulous periods, fewer surgical interventions, minimal invasiveness, better preservation of soft and hard tissues, and improved aesthetic outcomes. For long-term success with immediate implants, several clinical parameters must be carefully observed. Levine et al. outlined ten essential steps for aesthetic-zone cases, including assessing aesthetic risk with the patient and treatment team, using cone beam computed tomography (CBCT) to evaluate bone and root anatomy, performing atraumatic extraction while preserving the buccal plate, placing the implant in an ideal three-dimensional position, selecting a narrower implant to maintain a 2-3mm buccal gap, grafting that gap with a low-resorption biomaterial, augmenting soft tissue when required, immediately shaping the emergence profile with a provisional restoration or customised healing abutment, transferring soft-tissue contours with a custom impression coping, and finally delivering a screw-retained definitive crown rather than using stock abutments or deep cement margins.

Recent preclinical and clinical studies have analysed an innovative macro-hybrid implant design that represents a paradigm shift in biology and aesthetics, addressing limitations that can hinder immediate implants and worsen outcomes. In this design, the implant body is conical in its apical third, widens in the middle third, and then narrows coronally towards the neck. It can therefore be described as inverted and convergent. At the coronal implant-abutment junction, the profile is narrower where the bone is thinner, more delicate and relatively avascular, allowing a greater volume of graft material to be placed between the implant and the surrounding bone. The middle third widens to enhance primary stability in a region with greater bone volume and vascularisation. In addition, a 12° angled neck permits central placement within the socket, optimising alveolar bone use and improving prosthetic emergence; palatal screw access simplifies restoration, avoids cement-retained crowns, and reduces laboratory and prosthetic accessory costs.

Material and Methods

Study Design and Group: This study was a single-blind, randomized controlled clinical trial, conducted at the Universitat Internacional de Catalunya (UIC). Thirty patients were enrolled for the placement of immediate implants with immediate crown placement, to replace maxillary anterior teeth (central and lateral incisors, canines or premolars). The study was registered on ClinicalTrials.gov.

Sample Selection: Patients were randomly assigned to two groups. The test group (n=15) received the Inverta co-axis 12° implant, while the control group (n=15) was treated with a conventional internal conical implant.

The Inverta design differs from the parallel-walled internal conical implant in two main aspects: Its body expands in the midsection to achieve maximum apical anchorage, creating a 2-3mm buccal gap without compressing the coronal bone; and it incorporates a 12° angled platform, allowing central placement within the extraction socket, optimizing residual alveolar bone use, and improving the prosthetic emergence profile for better aesthetic outcomes.

Inclusion criteria: ASA type I patients; age ≥18 years; good oral hygiene (plaque index <15%); motivation and compliance; presence of a single maxillary tooth (central/lateral incisors, canines, or premolars) indicated for extraction due to poor prognosis; absence of buccal bone dehiscence and presence of intact soft tissues without recession (Elian type I alveolus); adequate bone volume for primary implant stability; absence of adjacent bridges or amalgam restorations causing CBCT artifacts.

Exclusion criteria: Acute infection at extraction site; missing posterior support leading to occlusal overload; insufficient mesio-distal space; unstable periodontal status; smoking >10 cigarettes/day; systemic medical conditions contraindicating implant surgery.

Surgical Procedure: All surgical procedures were performed by six postgraduate students from the Master's Programme in Oral Surgery at UIC. Under local anesthesia (articaine 4% with 1:100,000 epinephrine), teeth were atraumatically extracted without flap elevation. The sockets were thoroughly debrided, irrigated with sterile saline, and integrity of the buccal plate was verified with a periodontal probe. Implant dimensions were selected to provide at least 5mm of apical anchorage and maintain a 2-3mm buccal gap. Buccal gaps ≥2mm were filled with deproteinized bovine bone mineral (Bio-Oss®, Geistlich). A subepithelial connective tissue graft was obtained from the tuberosity or palate and stabilized with absorbable sutures.

Prosthodontic Procedure: Implant stability was evaluated by resonance frequency analysis (RFA) using the Penguin device (Integration Diagnostics, Sweden). When ISQ values exceeded 62, an immediate screw-retained provisional was placed; otherwise, a customized healing abutment or Maryland bridge was used. A standardized 3D-printed provisional crown (EXOCAD, Cerasmart) was left infra-occluded. Definitive restorations were delivered after three months using screw-retained E-max or zirconia crowns on titanium-base abutments, tightened at 35 Ncm.

Variables:
- Survival rate: presence of implant without extraction due to biological or mechanical complications
- Success: Misch et al. 2008 classification (four categories)
- Primary stability: insertion torque (IT) in Ncm and ISQ via RFA (Penguin device)
- Marginal bone loss: CBCT-based volumetric analysis using CBCT superposition (LimaGuide software) at T0 (pre-extraction), T1 (immediately post-placement), T2 (1-year follow-up)
- Soft tissue: Pink Esthetic Score (PES) at 1 year, 7 variables, 0-14 scale

Statistical Analysis: Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon signed-rank, Student's t-test, Chi-square, Kruskal-Wallis, Brunner-Langer. Significance p<0.05. R 4.3.1 and SPSS 15.0.

Results

Baseline: 30 patients (11 males 36.7%; 19 females 63.3%); mean age 53.4±11.8 years (range 30.8-78.8). Groups homogeneous at baseline (no significant differences in gender, age, tooth type, implant diameter, or implant length).

Implant Survival and Success:
- Survival: 100% in both groups
- Overall success: 83.3%
- Control group: 93.3% success, 6.7% satisfactory survival (2-4mm bone loss)
- Test group: 73.3% success, 26.7% satisfactory survival
- No significant difference between groups (p=0.327)

Primary Stability:
Insertion Torque (IT):
- Control: median 35.0 Ncm (95% CI: 26.17-37.16; SD=9.9); range 11-45 Ncm
- Test: median 42.0 Ncm (95% CI: 32.91-49.23; SD=14.7); range 16-60 Ncm
- Difference not statistically significant (p=0.074)

ISQ (Resonance Frequency Analysis):
- Control buccal: mean 56 (95% CI: 46.8-64.6; SD=16.1); median 56.0 (IQR: 50.0-71.0)
- Control palatal: mean 60 (95% CI: 50.2-67.5; SD=15.7); median 60.0 (IQR: 51.0-71.0)
- Test buccal: mean 62 (95% CI: 58.9-64.8; SD=5.3); median 62.0 (IQR: 60.0-64.0)
- Test palatal: mean 62 (95% CI: 61.1-64.4; SD=3.0); median 62.0 (IQR: 60.0-66.0)
- Higher ISQ medians in test group (both directions), but not statistically significant

Provisionalization:
- Control: 20% immediate aesthetic provisional, 80% customized healing abutment (lower stability)
- Test: 40% immediate aesthetic provisional, 60% customized abutment
- No significant difference (p=0.426)

Horizontal Bone Loss (T0 to T2):
- At 1mm: global median -0.83mm; control -0.19mm, test -0.97mm; significant over time (p<0.001); no intergroup difference (p=0.208)
- At 3mm: global median -0.45mm; control -0.08mm, test -0.53mm; significant over time (p=0.035); no intergroup difference (p=0.400)
- At 5mm: global median -0.25mm; control -0.29mm, test -0.20mm; marginal significance over time (p=0.070); no intergroup difference (p=0.492)

Vertical Bone Loss (T1 to T2):
- Buccal: global median -0.58mm; control -0.38mm, test -0.86mm; no intergroup difference (p=0.124)
- Palatal: global median -0.59mm; control -0.74mm, test -0.34mm; no intergroup difference (p=0.533)
- Slight buccal resorption greater in test group; palatal loss greater in control

Aesthetic Evaluation (PES):
- Control: median PES 12 (IQR 10-12), mean 11.4 (95% CI: 10.38-12.42); range 7-14
- Test: median PES 11 (IQR 9-13), mean 11.2 (95% CI: 10.13-12.27); range 9-14
- No significant differences; comparable aesthetic outcomes at 1 year

Conclusions

Both inverted body-shift (co-axis 12°) and conventional conical implants showed comparable clinical outcomes for immediate post-extraction placement in the anterior maxilla. 100% survival in both groups; 83.3% overall success. Primary stability adequate for immediate loading. Marginal bone remodeling limited at all levels with no significant intergroup differences. PES comparable. The inverted co-axis design may offer practical advantages in challenging cases: central alveolar positioning, improved stability, screw-retained restorations with optimal emergence profiles. Larger, long-term studies are warranted.
