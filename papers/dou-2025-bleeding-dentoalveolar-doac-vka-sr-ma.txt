Risk of bleeding with dentoalveolar surgery in patients taking direct oral anticoagulants or vitamin K antagonists: A systematic review and meta-analysis

Authors: Ke Dou, Yue Shi, Baoyi Yang, Zhiguo Zhao
Journal: The Japanese Dental Science Review (Jpn Dent Sci Rev) 2025;61:188-199
DOI: 10.1016/j.jdsr.2025.08.002
PMID: 40831539  PMCID: PMC12358962
Publication date: 2025-08-12
PROSPERO: CRD42024608201

== ABSTRACT ==
Objective: To assess and compare the bleeding risks in patients undergoing dentoalveolar surgery who were on uninterrupted therapy with direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) or vitamin K antagonists (VKAs).

Methods: Electronic database searches in PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and CENTRAL through 28 September 2024, following PICOS criteria. Two reviewers independently performed literature screening, data extraction, and assessed risk of bias (ROBINS-I). Data pooled to estimate risk ratios (RR) with 95% CI using a fixed-effects model. Heterogeneity assessed with Q statistic and I². Subgroup and sensitivity analyses performed. GRADE used for certainty of evidence.

Results: 29 studies enrolled a total of 29,212 patients (13 cohort, 14 case-control, 2 cross-sectional).
- DOACs vs VKAs: 23 studies, 13,825 patients. Reduced bleeding risk with DOACs RR = 0.79, 95% CI 0.68-0.92, I²=0%, P=0.002 (21% relative reduction).
  Subgroups vs VKAs:
    - Dabigatran: RR = 0.40, 95% CI 0.23-0.67, I²=0%, P=0.0006 (significantly lower)
    - Rivaroxaban: RR = 1.08, 95% CI 0.84-1.39, I²=38%, P=0.54 (NS)
    - Apixaban: RR = 0.88, 95% CI 0.64-1.3, I²=0%, P=0.46 (NS)
    - Edoxaban: RR = 0.70, 95% CI 0.45-1.11, I²=60%, P=0.13 (NS)
  Concomitant antiplatelet therapy subgroup: did not significantly increase bleeding.
  No publication bias (Egger P=0.929).
- DOACs vs Control (no anticoagulant): 10 studies, 4091 patients. Increased bleeding with DOACs RR = 3.23, 95% CI 2.18-4.78, I²=24%, P<0.0001. (Egger P=0.363)
- VKAs vs Control: 7 studies, 4565 patients. Increased bleeding with VKAs RR = 3.35, 95% CI 2.31-4.85, I²=0%, P<0.0001. (Egger P=0.382)

Bleeding incidence across studies ranged 0%-46%. GRADE certainty: very low.

Conclusions: Patients receiving DOACs or VKAs have increased bleeding risk during dentoalveolar surgery, but severe bleeding requiring hospitalization or causing irreversible damage is rare. DOAC users appear to have lower bleeding risk than VKA users, mainly driven by dabigatran etexilate; unclear for rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban. Quality of evidence is very low. Uninterrupted oral anticoagulant use during dentoalveolar surgery is a viable and relatively safer approach.

== KEY DISCUSSION POINTS ==
- Routine discontinuation of anticoagulant therapy before dentoalveolar surgery no longer recommended; local hemostatic techniques control bleeding for VKA patients with INR <=4.0.
- Dabigatran (direct thrombin inhibitor) locally inhibits thrombin at the wound; short half-life + reversal agent (idarucizumab) makes systemic effect controllable. Factor Xa inhibitors harder to reverse and balance hemostasis.
- VKA half-life >55h, no circadian variation; DOAC half-life 7-17h.
- American College of Surgeons: stop DOACs 24-48h before extraction, resume 24h after. European Heart Rhythm Association: last DOAC dose 18-24h before surgery for minimal bleeding risk, resume 6h post-op.
- Curto et al. risk classification: low-risk (uncomplicated extraction, <45 min) vs moderate-high-risk (>3 teeth or >45 min). Low risk: no need to stop dabigatran; apixaban continued. Intermediate-high risk: withhold dabigatran >=48h, apixaban >=24h; consider LMWH bridging; extend interruption in renal impairment.
- Thromboembolic risk of discontinuation outweighs bleeding risk: rebound hypercoagulation, 20-fold increase in short-term thromboembolic events after stopping dabigatran/rivaroxaban (Vene et al.).
- Hemostatic adjuncts: sutures, pressure, gelatin sponge, oxidized cellulose, chitosan-based agents, tranexamic acid sponges.

== LIMITATIONS ==
- ~1/3 retrospective (record-based), inherent bias. No RCTs (ethical constraints). GRADE very low. Bleeding definition varied across studies. Variable surgical invasiveness. Only postoperative bleeding evaluable.
