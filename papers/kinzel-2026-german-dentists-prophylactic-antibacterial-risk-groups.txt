A Survey of German Dentists on the Prophylactic Use of Antibacterial Drugs in Patients in Risk Groups: Diabetes, Joint Replacement, Risk of Endocarditis, and Immunosuppression or Organ Transplantation
Kinzel J, Seifert R. Int J Dent. 2026;2026:8452465. DOI: 10.1155/ijod/8452465. PMID 42325694. PMCID PMC13280459.

1. Introduction

Antibacterial drugs (ABDs) have been used frequently in human medicine for many years and are also an indispensable part of the range of medications prescribed in dentistry. In terms of absolute prescription figures, ABDs rank second behind fluoride preparations. Overall, prescriptions by dentists accounted for just under 9% of all ABDs prescribed. As with any medication, the desired positive effects of reducing pathological bacteria must be weighed against the potential negative effects. Of particular note here is the development of resistance, which has received increasing attention in recent years in the context of antibacterial stewardship. In order to enable doctors and dentists to weigh up the risks and benefits of prescribing ABDs on a scientifically sound basis, professional associations are in many cases developing guidelines and recommendations. However, in dentistry, the guidelines of professional associations regarding the indication, dose, and duration of medication administration are often inconsistent and leave dentists with considerable leeway. The only area where there is broad consensus is endocarditis prophylaxis, since the American Heart Association (AHA) published guidelines in 2007 recommending endocarditis prophylaxis only for high-risk patients, thereby significantly reducing the target group compared to previous guidelines. These guidelines have been adopted almost in their entirety by a large number of professional associations across countries. Surveys have shown that dentists in the USA follow these guidelines. In Germany, there is now only a broad consensus on recommendations for administering ABD during dental implantation in patients with diabetes, immunosuppression, antiresorptive therapy, or a risk of endocarditis. A study from 2024 found that some European experts recommend prophylaxis with ABD for dental implants, but overall more ABD is prescribed for prophylaxis than recommended; more education is needed to promote a more rational approach. Studies in Italy (dental students 2019; licensed dentists), Switzerland, and Saudi Arabia came to similar conclusions of wide variation and overprescription. There are also critical international voices that generally question the benefits of prophylaxis with ABD in implantology. Although bacteremia can be detected in many patients after dental procedures, this depends heavily on the type of procedure, and various studies show very different frequencies; bacteremia is also detected during chewing, tooth brushing, and flossing. This raises the question of how consistent dentists are in their everyday practice when administering ABD and in which procedures such drugs are used at all.

2. Methodology and Materials

In the online-only survey, dentists working in Germany were asked about their prescribing behavior with regard to prophylactic ABD. The survey focused exclusively on systemic ABD and was divided into four main areas: ABD for patients with endocarditis, patients with organ transplants or immunosuppression, diabetes, and joint replacements. In each area, there were questions addressing different forms and manifestations of the disease, as well as questions related to various dental activities. For each question, the response options were: never (0%), rarely (<25%), occasionally (25%-50%), frequently (50%-75%), very frequently (>75%), always (100%), and "not specified" - indicating the estimated percentage of patients in each group who received prophylactic ABD from the participant. For each main area, the question was asked about the most frequently and second most frequently used prophylactic ABD. Possible answers were limited to: amoxicillin, clindamycin, penicillin V, doxycycline, metronidazole, ampicillin, vancomycin, azithromycin, clarithromycin, and "not specified." A "yes"/"no" option asked whether the participant felt confident overall in using ABD for prophylactic purposes. Finally, the year of the state examination was indicated (1950 to 2023). Each question had to be answered. Only fully completed surveys were included, resulting in 537 evaluable questionnaires from 1070 participants. Participation was voluntary; the link was distributed to members via the dental associations of Saarland, Berlin, Baden-Wuerttemberg, Lower Saxony, Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania, Westphalia-Lippe, and Thuringia. The survey was available online on the SoSci platform for 5 months from 10.2023 to 03.2024.

Classification: If more than 80% of respondents answered "always" and "very often" OR "never" and "rarely" to a question, this was evaluated as a clear positive or negative consensus. If between 50% and 80% answered in one direction, this was a positive or negative trend. All other results were interpreted as inconclusive. Results were briefly compared with the recommendations of German professional associations.

3. Results

3.1. Prophylactic ABD for Endocarditis Prophylaxis
Clear consensus regarding administration of ABD only in cases of prosthetic valve replacement. In patients who had survived bacterial endocarditis and heart transplant patients, a positive trend toward endocarditis prophylaxis was still evident. In cases of cyanotic heart defects, congenital cyanotic heart disease, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, mitral valve prolapse, acquired valve dysfunction, and pulmonary shunts: inconclusive. No majority for/against in any of these heart diseases combined with additional risk factors (smoking, heavy alcohol). On type of procedure: no clear positive consensus; positive trend only for tooth extraction, implantation, periodontal procedures, and procedures involving bone. Rather negative trend for inflammation of oral mucosa, injury to mucosa, injury to gingiva, local anesthetic injection, and probing of periodontal pocket. Consensus only on NOT administering ABD for postoperative suture removal. Mixed for endodontic inflammation and dental/maxillofacial trauma. Compared with DGZMK and DGK guidelines: recommendations largely implemented; significant deviations only for procedures involving injury/manipulation of the gingiva and local anesthetic injections. Most frequent ABD = amoxicillin, second = clindamycin (consistent with guidelines: amoxicillin first-line, clindamycin only for penicillin allergy).

3.2. ABD Used Prophylactically in Patients With Joint Replacements
No positive consensus or positive trend for any question - generally critical approach. Negative trend in: dental procedures >2 years after joint surgery, hip replacement, joint replacement + other risk factors, tooth extraction, endodontic inflammation, periodontal, dental/jaw trauma. Negative CONSENSUS (>80% never/rarely) for: inflammation of oral mucosa, injury to mucosa or gingiva, local anesthetic injection, postoperative suture removal, probing of PA gap. Unclear (no trend) for: dental procedures within 2 years of joint surgery, implantation, procedures involving bone. The German Society for Endoprosthetics (AE) recommends ABD for invasive dental procedures (single 2 g amoxicillin preoperatively). Clear discrepancy between dentist behavior and recommendation for tooth extraction, periodontal procedures, and dental/maxillofacial trauma; even for implantation and bone procedures the recommendation is not applied in every treatment. Amoxicillin most common, clindamycin reserve.

3.3. Prophylactic ABD in Patients With Diabetes
Mixed picture, generally critical. No positive consensus or positive trend. Negative CONSENSUS against ABD in: type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, inflammation of oral mucosa, injury to mucosa/gingiva, local anesthetic injection, PA probing, postoperative suture removal. Negative TREND in: poorly controlled diabetes, diabetes + other risk factors, tooth extraction, endodontic inflammation, periodontal surgery, dental/maxillofacial trauma. Unclear for: tooth extraction, implantation, procedures involving bone. (Note: text lists both negative trend and unclear around extraction/bone procedures - implantation 23 always but 24 no info.) Only German guideline for diabetes is DGZMK+DDG (German Diabetes Society) for implantation, recommending ABD - the ambiguous survey approach deviates. Amoxicillin first, clindamycin second; guideline does not specify drug.

3.4. Prophylactic Use of ABD in Patients With Immunosuppression or Organ Transplants
No uniform approach; no positive consensus on any question. Positive TREND in: immunosuppressive disease, taking immunosuppressive medication, after organ transplantation, immunosuppressed/transplant + additional risk factors, and dental procedures involving bone, tooth extraction, implantation. Tendency to reject ABD (but no negative consensus) in: inflammation of oral mucosa, injury to gingiva/mucosa, local anesthetic injection, PA probing, postoperative suture removal. Mixed for endodontic inflammation, periodontal, dental/maxillofacial trauma. DGZMK + Working Group for Oral and Maxillofacial Medicine published an S2k guideline recommending ABD prior to dentoalveolar procedures (single 2 g amoxicillin; second choice 0.6 g clindamycin). The positive trend for bone procedures, extraction, implantation is a step in the right direction; guideline not followed for periodontal procedures and dental/maxillofacial trauma. Amoxicillin most common, clindamycin alternative.

Confidence: almost three-quarters of dentists answered "yes" they feel confident using prophylactic ABD - contrasts with the heterogeneous prescribing behavior. Year of state examination: no exam year significantly overrepresented (different generations well represented).

3.5. Limitation
537 completed surveys. ~101,344 practicing and nonpracticing dentists in Germany at start of 2023 => only slightly more than 0.5% participated. Distributed via state dental associations (not all agreed). Voluntary, neither personalized nor monitored (cannot rule out multiple responses, misrepresentation, self-selection of dentists more aware of the issue, social desirability). ABD choice limited to 10 options. Survey records self-assessment, not actual prescriptions.

4. Discussion
Generally cautious use of ABD with wide variation in type and extent - consistent with Italian, Swiss, Saudi surveys (deviation from guidelines, amoxicillin most common, mixed approach with tendency to administer ABD for procedures without guidelines, interpreted as precautionary). German dentists more aware of antibiotic stewardship, more confident, more oriented toward international guidelines. Patient groups split: endocarditis risk + immunosuppression/organ transplant (positive trends) vs joint replacement + diabetes (negative trend to negative consensus). The only positive consensus was within endocarditis (prosthetic valve), attributable to strong cross-country/society consensus. Despite AHA/DGZMK/AE recommendations, <50% would use ABD in most cases of cyanotic heart defects, congenital cyanotic heart disease, acquired valvular dysfunction, pulmonary shunts; for gingival injury, suture removal, local anesthetic injection >half use ABD rarely/never. For diabetes/joint replacement/immunosuppression the recommendations are more general -> greater discrepancy. Immunosuppression/transplant ranks second to endocarditis in compliance; explained by dentists weighing sepsis/organ-loss risk against allergy/resistance (latter is delayed and not directly attributable). Most negative-consensus questions were in diabetes (high prevalence, familiarity, education dispels fear). Implantation: no consensus in any group, but ~1/4 gave "no info" (not all place implants); excluding these often yields at least a positive trend, in line with German guidelines. Amoxicillin primary, clindamycin secondary across all groups regardless of patient type. Only 0.5% of German dentists represented. ~three-quarters feel confident despite divergent behavior (anecdotal-evidence trap).

5. Conclusion and Future Studies
The dental profession is still far from uniform use of ABD throughout Germany, but a cautious trend is emerging. RCTs on prophylactic ABD use are needed; surveys plus disease/complication frequency figures may aid decisions; dissemination of guidelines should be promoted.
