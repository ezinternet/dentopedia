1. Introduction

Zinc deficiency is common in developing countries, affecting over two billion people worldwide, and is also common in Japan. Wessells and Brown reported inadequate zinc intake prevalence in Japan of 14.7% (1990), 14.7% (1995), and 15.5% (2000) — an increasing trend. Contributing factors: rapidly growing elderly population with chronic disease/medication use, and lack of clinician awareness of zinc deficiency. The Japanese Society of Clinical Nutrition issued the Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency in 2018. Symptoms/signs of zinc deficiency vary and include taste disorders, dermatitis, hair loss, loss of appetite, susceptibility to infection, and hypogonadism.

2. Japan's Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency

2.1 Prevalence: zinc intake insufficient (below RDA) in 60-70% of both males and females aged ≥20y in Japan; significantly lower than RDA in pregnant/lactating women. Zinc deficiency commonly accompanies severe physical disabilities, liver cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis, chronic inflammatory bowel disease, type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease, cardiac insufficiency, short stature; more common in elderly and professional athletes.

2.2 Causes (4 major categories): (1) insufficient intake — meats rich in zinc, vegetables minimal; elderly eat less food/meat; rare maternal SLC30A2 gene mutations prevent zinc transfer to breast milk; (2) interference with absorption — chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, chronic IBD, excess phytic acid intake; (3) increased requirement — pregnancy/lactation, athletes (especially women); (4) excessive urinary excretion — chronic kidney disease, diabetes mellitus.

2.3 Diagnostic criteria (4 criteria in Practical Guideline): (a) one or more symptoms of zinc deficiency or low serum alkaline phosphatase (ALP, a zinc-dependent enzyme — not used as criterion in patients with liver disease/osteoporosis/CKD/diabetes since ALP can be inherently elevated); (b) ruling out other diseases with similar symptoms; (c) low serum zinc level; (d) symptom improvement with zinc administration. Serum zinc <60 μg/dL = zinc deficiency; 60-80 μg/dL = marginal deficiency; >80 μg/dL = normal. Zinc clearance test reportedly more accurate than serum zinc for marginal deficiency (cited studies: Nakamura et al. found 10 short-stature patients diagnosed via zinc clearance test improved with zinc treatment despite mean pre-treatment serum zinc of 78±6 μg/dL; Kaji et al. found mean serum zinc 75.0±12.7 μg/dL in short-stature patients diagnosed via zinc clearance test; Tomita et al. proposed 80 μg/dL cutoff). BMJ Best Practice is the only other published practical guideline (uses <60 or <70 μg/dL cutoffs); recommends oral supplementation if symptoms are typical even with normal test results given the low sensitivity of serum zinc in marginal deficiency.

2.4 Treatment: zinc-enriched foods first-line (oysters, meats, scallops, liver, and Japanese foods like tofu, rice, natto). When dietary therapy insufficient: zinc administration 50-100 mg/day (adults), 1-3 mg/kg/day (children). Patients with chronic disease and low serum zinc often improve with zinc therapy even without classic zinc-deficiency symptoms — zinc therapy recommended regardless. Adverse effects: nausea, vomiting (preventable by taking after meals), itching, and copper deficiency (associated with anemia, leukopenia) — serum zinc and copper should be checked every 3-4 months during therapy. Zinc administered for chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, IBD, CKD, diabetes, patients on chelating medications, athletes, pregnant women.

3. Zinc Deficiency and Taste Disorders

3.1 Taste perception system: water-soluble taste chemicals received by taste receptors on taste cells in taste buds (tongue/pharynx mucosa), projected to gustatory cortex via taste nerves. ~7000 taste buds in oral cavity/pharynx/larynx, concentrated in lingual papillae. Five basic tastes sensed by different receptors. Taste information transmitted via chorda tympani, large pyramidal, glossopharyngeal, and vagus nerves to gustatory cortex via medullary solitary nucleus and thalamus.

3.2 Effects in rats: zinc-deficient diet causes rats to drink bitter water they would normally avoid (indicating taste disorder). Microstructural abnormalities in taste cells (microvilli rupture, vacuolation) observed by electron microscopy. Cell differentiation from basal cells impaired, cell turnover time prolonged. Reduced expression of bitter taste receptors TAS2R40 and TAS2R107 found with zinc deficiency.

3.3 Taste disorders (human epidemiology): Japanese Society of Stomato-pharyngology 2003 patient survey found ~240,000 patients suffering taste disorders — 1.7-fold increase since 1990. Affects many elderly, more common in women (male:female ratio 2:3). Number of affected patients expected to rise with aging population. Categories: quantitative dysgeusia and qualitative dysgeusia. Types: zinc deficiency taste disorder, idiopathic taste disorder, drug-induced taste disorder, psychogenic taste disorder, systemic taste disorder. Zinc deficiency taste disorder has no clear cause/trigger other than reduced serum zinc; idiopathic taste disorder (no apparent cause) is likely also zinc-deficiency-associated.

3.4 Zinc replacement therapy: Henkin et al. (1970) first reported treating taste disorders with oral zinc — successfully treated D-penicillamine-induced drug-related taste disorder with zinc chloride. Yoshida (1991): 82% improvement in zinc deficiency/idiopathic taste disorder using 67.5mg zinc gluconate (double-blind). Sakai: 76% improvement using 87mg zinc picolinate. Heckmann: 50% improvement using 20mg zinc gluconate. Zinc gluconate/picolinate not commonly used in Japan (require individual clinic preparation) — Polaprezinc (34mg zinc, originally for gastric ulcers) is the standard Japanese prescription for taste disorders. Sakagami: 80% improvement in subjective symptoms using 150mg Polaprezinc. Efficacy is NOT immediate: double-blind Polaprezinc study showed 13.6% efficacy at week 4, rising to 47.7% at week 12, 58.8% at week 24 — treatment should continue at least 3 months. Zinc acetate hydrate (originally for Wilson's disease) has been health-insurance-covered for hypozincemia treatment in Japan since 2017, also expected effective for taste disorders.

3.5 Nutritional guidance: recommended zinc intake (Japan Ministry of Health, Labor and Welfare): 10mg/day (men), 8mg/day (women). Actual intake below standard: 8.9mg/day (men), 7.3mg/day (women) — half the Japanese population at risk of zinc-deficiency taste disorder. Facility practice: patient education using zinc-rich/zinc-absorption-promoting/zinc-absorption-inhibiting food lists.

4. Zinc Deficiency Enhances Intestinal Inflammation via Macrophage Activation

Multicenter Japanese case-control study: higher pre-study zinc doses associated with decreased odds ratio for ulcerative colitis (protective). Prospective IBD registry: zinc deficiency associated with increased risk of hospitalization, surgery, disease-related complications in IBD/Crohn's disease (CD)/UC. Authors' own data: serum zinc <80μg/dL in >75% of CD patients; zinc negatively correlated with Harvey-Bradshaw Index (HBI) clinical activity (r=-0.75, p<0.05).

Mechanism (murine models): metallothioneins (MTs, cysteine-rich zinc-binding proteins) protect against colonic mucosal inflammation — MT-I/II knockout mice show elevated inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IFN-γ, IL-17) and worsened DSS-induced colitis. TPEN (zinc chelator)-induced zinc deficiency significantly aggravated TNBS-induced colitis, increased IL-17A production and Th17 cell proportion in colonic lamina propria (not spleen/mesenteric lymph node), without directly affecting Th17 differentiation from naive CD4+ cells in vitro (suggesting non-T-cell immune involvement). TPEN administration increased M1 (Ly6C+) macrophage proportion and decreased M2 (CD206+) macrophage proportion in intestinal macrophages, increased M1/M2 ratio, and enhanced IL-23p19 expression specifically (not other cytokines). Zinc-depleted bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) showed concentration/time-dependent IL-23p19 upregulation, reversed by zinc sulfate. IFN-γ+LPS caused M1 skewing via reduced intracellular zinc. IL-17A production increased when CD4+ cells cultured with conditioned medium from zinc-depleted BMDMs; blocked by anti-IL-23p19 antibody. Nuclear IRF5 (transcription factor for IL-23p19) distribution induced by zinc deficiency. Clinically, IL-23p19 strongly upregulated in CD (not UC) colonic mucosa (Schmidt et al.); IL-23 pathway blockade (ustekinumab) effective for CD. Conclusion: zinc deficiency aggravates inflammation by activating IRF/IL-23-mediated M1 macrophages and inhibiting GATA-3/IL-4-mediated M2 macrophages.

5. Zinc and Protein Metabolism in Chronic Liver Disease

Liver cirrhosis causes defective nutrient metabolism via decreased hepatic function/portal hypertension. Protein metabolism (albumin/prothrombin synthesis, ammonia detoxification) relates to hepatic functional reserve and hepatic encephalopathy risk. Zinc activates/structurally maintains ~300 proteins/enzymes involved in growth, antioxidant effects, immune response, apoptosis, aging, carcinogenesis. Ornithine transcarbamylase (urea cycle zinc enzyme) activity decreases with zinc deficiency.

Authors' cohort (299 cirrhosis patients without HCC from 14 Japanese institutes, 2009; 235 not on oral zinc): serum zinc significantly lower in Child-Pugh class B and C vs. class A. Serum zinc positively correlated with serum albumin and branched-chain amino acid to tyrosine ratio (BTR); negatively correlated with serum ammonia. Mechanism: zinc deficiency decreases hepatic ammonia detoxification capacity, causing compensatory increase in skeletal-muscle ammonia detoxification, increased BCAA consumption by muscle, resulting in serum BCAA deficiency and decreased hepatic albumin synthesis — worsening liver function.

Zinc supplementation evidence: 12 cirrhosis patients with hyperammonemia/hypozincemia randomized to zinc acetate (150mg/day, 3 months) vs. placebo — serum zinc increased significantly, serum ammonia decreased significantly with supplementation vs. control. Reding et al.: zinc supplementation effective for hepatic encephalopathy. Marchesini et al.: zinc supplementation improved encephalopathy indicators (number connection test scores), significantly decreased blood ammonia, slightly improved Fisher ratios, improved alanine-to-urea-nitrogen metabolism.

6. Conclusions and Future Directions

Zinc deficiency prevalence in Japan is among the highest of developed countries, driven by aging population with chronic disease/medication use, compounded by low clinical awareness. Serum zinc level (a diagnostic criterion) does not accurately reflect zinc deficiency in all cases — more sensitive/accurate biomarkers needed. Taste disorder patient numbers have increased in Japan; zinc administration alleviates zinc-deficiency-induced, idiopathic, drug-induced, and systemic taste disorders, though pathophysiological mechanisms need further study, specifically regarding zinc administration's effect on these disorders. Zinc deficiency common in IBD/liver cirrhosis due to absorption interference; zinc deficiency enhances intestinal inflammation via macrophage activation (mechanism partly elucidated but not fully understood); ornithine transcarbamylase activity decline under zinc deficiency lowers hepatic ammonia detoxification, worsening liver condition. Numerous studies support zinc supplementation benefits across conditions, though effects need verification per individual disease/condition.
