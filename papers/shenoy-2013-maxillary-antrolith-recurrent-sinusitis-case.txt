Maxillary Antrolith: A Rare Cause of Recurrent Sinusitis

Authors: Shenoy VS et al.
Journal: Case Reports in Otolaryngology (2013)
DOI: 10.1155/2013/527152
PMCID: PMC3580910

Summary:
Case report of a 47-year-old woman with an antrolith causing recurrent sinusitis and oroantral fistula. Complicating history: prior Caldwell-Luc surgery (1984) for polypoid disease.

Presentation:
- Heaviness left side of face x2 months
- Loosening of upper left 2nd molar (oroantral fistula sign)
- Foul-smelling purulent nasal discharge from left nostril x1 week
- CT: opacification bilateral maxillary sinus + amorphous bone-density area in left sinus (initially thought osteoma vs malignancy)
- Diagnostic nasal endoscopy: thick purulent discharge in middle meatus

Management:
- Wide antrostomy → brownish, hard, gritty mass, freely mobile (no wall attachment) surrounded by pus and polypoid mucosa
- Too large for endoscopic removal → repeat Caldwell-Luc procedure
- Calculi (2×1 cm) removed and sent for histopathology
- Culture: Pseudomonas spp. resistant to ampicillin
- Histopathology: diagnosis of exclusion (osteoma, malignancy, rhinoscleroma, fungal concretions all excluded by decalcification + PAS staining)
- Conclusion: freely lying calcareous mass = sinolith/antrolith

Pathogenesis:
- Predisposing factors: long-standing infection, poor sinus drainage, foreign body
- In this case: bony chips from prior Caldwell-Luc surgery served as endogenous nidus
- Purulent fluid becomes concentrated → calcium phosphate + calcium carbonate precipitate → encrustation of foreign body

Background:
- Term "rhinolith" coined in 1845 for partially/completely encrusted foreign body in nose
- Only ~30 cases reported in literature up to 2005
- Most commonly involves maxillary sinus, then frontal sinus
- Rhinoliths almost always unilateral

Classification by nidus:
- Endogenous: tooth/bony fragments, blood, pus, mucus, fungi
- Exogenous: cotton, paper, snuff, dental burs, dental implants, GP points, silver points, bullets, glass, wood, grass

Radiographic features:
- Dense, irregular, well-defined mass in antrum
- Visible on panoramic, periapical, Waters' radiograph, CT
- Also seen in fungal ball (mycetoma) sinuses

Differential diagnosis:
- Supernumerary tooth, root fragment, osteoma
- Complex odontoma, cementoma, periapical condensing osteitis
- Buccal exostosis, palatine torus, impacted tooth, foreign bodies, neoplasms

Treatment:
- ESS with or without Caldwell-Luc
- Caldwell-Luc provides better exposure, ventilation, drainage for large antroliths
- Appropriate treatment of sinus infection concurrent

Key lesson:
- Prior Caldwell-Luc surgery → residual bony chips → nidus for antrolith formation
- Recommendation: thorough sinus cavity irrigation during ESS to prevent future antrolith around endogenous nidus
