Pilot Evaluation of Sealer-Based Root Canal Obturation Using Epoxy-Resin-Based and Calcium-Silicate-Based Sealers: A Randomized Clinical Trial

Authors: Minju Song, Min-Gyu Park, Sang-Won Kwak, Ruben H. Kim, Jung-Hong Ha, Hyeon-Cheol Kim
Journal: Materials (Basel). 2022;15(15):5146. Published 2022-07-25.
DOI: 10.3390/ma15155146 | PMID: 35897577 | PMCID: PMC9332451
Source: PubMed / PMC full text (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9332451/)

ABSTRACT
The purpose of this randomized controlled clinical trial was to assess the short-term clinical effectiveness of sealer-based root canal obturation using calcium-silicate-based sealer (CSBS) and epoxy-resin-based sealer (ERBS). A total of eighty patients (eighty teeth) were enrolled and seventy-two patients (seventy-two teeth) were randomly assigned into four different sealer groups: AH Plus (AH, Dentsply Sirona), ADseal (AD, Meta Biomed), CeraSeal (CS, Meta Biomed), or EndoSeal TCS (ES, Maruchi). At the first visit, root canal treatment was performed using ProTaper Next NiTi file system and passive ultrasonic irrigation was applied with 2.5% NaOCl. At the second visit, the teeth were obturated with a sealer-based obturation technique using the assigned sealer. The quality of filling obturation was evaluated with postoperative radiographs in terms of the presence of voids and sealer extrusion. The patients were recalled at 1-week, 1-month, and 3-month intervals, and pain levels were measured using visual analog scales (VAS), and clinical examination performed with percussion and palpation. Radiographs were also taken. The results were statistically compared by using the chi-square test or Fisher exact test. Among the 80 teeth included, 72 teeth received allocated intervention and 71 teeth were included in the analysis (98.6% recall). There was no significant difference among the sealers in void and sealer extrusion (p > 0.05). Postoperative pain was not indicated prominently regardless of the type of sealer. For a 3-month follow-up period, all cases were maintained successfully except for one case from AD. According to the findings, there was no significant difference between CSBS and ERBS in terms of postoperative pain or the healing process, and the variation in filling quality appears to be linked to the properties of each product.

Keywords: calcium silicate; root canal filling; sealer; sealer-based obturation

1. INTRODUCTION
Since the mid-19th century, gutta-percha (GP) has been used as a root canal filling material. GP itself cannot seal the canal completely and is always used with sealers to seal off minor gaps between the GP and canal wall and irregularities of the root canal system. Sealer has shortcomings such as microleakage, dimensional instability, and biocompatibility; therefore it is recommended to use as thin a sealer layer as possible. The warm vertical compaction / continuous wave of condensation technique using GP and epoxy-resin-based sealer (ERBS) — the gold standard for root canal sealers — is the most popular technique for increasing the amount of GP and decreasing the amount of sealer.

With the advancement in rotary NiTi instruments, the single-cone technique using a matching single cone has gained popularity owing to its simplicity, lesser technique-sensitivity, and short working time. However, several studies report the single-cone technique shows inferior filling quality to warm vertical obturation or pressured condensation techniques, presumably due to the disparity between the round tapered GP and the irregular canal shape, resulting in relatively more sealer being used.

ERBS has been regarded as the gold standard due to its resorption resistance and dimensional stability but has drawbacks including possible mutagenicity, cytotoxicity, inflammatory response, and hydrophobicity. Calcium-silicate-based sealer (CSBS) was introduced as an alternative with superior physicochemical and biologic properties: biocompatibility, biomineralization stimulation, antibacterial activity, higher binding strength to dentin, and a seal on par with ERBS.

The first commercialized CSBS, iRoot SP (Innovative Bioceramix), was developed in 2007. Various sealers followed: EndoSequence BC Sealer (Brasseler), MTA Fillapex (Angelus). CeraSeal (Meta Biomed, Cheongju, Korea) is a premixed calcium-silicate-based sealer composed of calcium silicates and zirconium oxide. EndoSeal MTA / EndoSeal TCS (Maruchi, Wonju, Korea) is marketed as a pozzolan-based MTA sealer with a syringe-delivered injectable paste, showing low void percentage and lower solubility compared to conventional CSBS. Both CeraSeal and EndoSeal are premixed, ready-to-use injectable pastes. Despite many in-vitro physicochemical comparisons, there is relatively little literature on clinical performance. The purpose of this pilot study was to assess the short-term clinical effectiveness of sealer-based root canal obturation using ERBSs and CSBSs.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1 Study design and inclusion/exclusion: Prospective clinical study at a university dental hospital (Pusan National University Dental Hospital), November 2020 to October 2021. IRB approval PNUDH-2020-045-MD; informed consent from all participants. Teeth diagnosed as asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis and/or pulp necrosis with/without chronic apical periodontitis were included. Excluded: excessive mobility, crack, internal/external resorption, calcified chamber or canals, acute periapical periodontitis or alveolar abscess, systemic disease affecting outcome (e.g., severe diabetes mellitus), intentional endodontic treatment, and C-shaped canals identified during shaping.

2.2 Sample size and randomization: Sample size determined using G*Power: significance α 0.05, power 0.8, medium effect size ω 0.4, Df 3, giving 69; initial enrollment goal 83 assuming 20% dropout. Total 80 patients (80 teeth) randomly assigned to 4 sealers (n=20 each): AH Plus (group AH; Dentsply Sirona) and ADseal (group AD; Meta Biomed) as ERBS; CeraSeal (group CS; Meta Biomed) and EndoSeal TCS (group ES; Maruchi) as CSBS. Because handling properties differ, the study could not be blinded for the endodontist.

2.3 Treatment procedure: Two-visit root canal treatment by an endodontist with >20 years experience. Visit 1: rubber dam isolation after anesthesia; access cavity; coronal flaring with OneFlare (MicroMega); glide path with PathFile #1 (Dentsply Sirona); instrumentation with ProTaper Next rotary NiTi (final apical sizes X2, X3, X4, X5 per natural canal size) with E-connect S motor; copious 2.5% NaOCl irrigation; passive ultrasonic irrigation (PUI) with UC-One; dried with paper points; calcium hydroxide paste (Calcipex II) applied; temporary restoration (Caviton). Visit 2: symptom check (only asymptomatic patients continued); isolation; medication removal; NaOCl + EDTA irrigation via PUI; dried; sealers randomly assigned and delivered via premixed syringe + master GP cone for CSBS groups; master GP cone for AD and AH groups. Master cone to working length, multiple pumping motions to improve sealer flow and remove air, coronal GP severed at orifice and compacted with hand plugger.

2.4 Clinical and radiographic evaluation: Postoperative paralleling-technique radiographs to evaluate filling quality and sealer extrusion. Voids below the canal orifice counted using two shifted-direction radiographs. Sealer extrusion counted per canal and per tooth. Recall ~1 week (7-10 days) for restoration, then 1 month and 3 months for symptoms / signs (percussion, palpation) and flare-ups. Pain measured by VAS at each visit. Radiographs taken at every visit to evaluate resolution of apical radiolucency.

2.5 Statistical analysis: Pearson chi-square test or Fisher exact test, significance 0.05. SPSS v15.0.

3. RESULTS
Among 80 patients (80 teeth) initially eligible, 72 patients (72 teeth) included; one patient (one tooth) dropped due to unavailability to follow up within 3 months (98.6% recall; 71 teeth analyzed).

Voids (per canal): ES 60% and AH 56.3% showed higher void numbers than AD 31.6% and CS 29.4%. No significant difference among sealers (p > 0.05). Voids more common in molars (50%, 23/46 teeth) than premolars (38.5%, 5/13) or anterior teeth (30.7%, 4/13).

Sealer extrusion (per tooth, per canal): AH 56.3% / 33.3% and CS 58.8% / 29.7% more frequent than ES 35% / 17.8% and AD 21.1% / 12.2%. No significant difference among sealers (p > 0.05).

Postoperative pain: All groups similar, VAS 0 to 0.06 at 1 week, no change except AH group. AH VAS 0.06 at 1 week, increased to 0.13 at 1 month and 3 months. AD showed a positive percussion response in only one case; otherwise no positive percussion/palpation at any interval.

3-month follow-up: All cases maintained successfully except one AD case with a recurring sinus tract at 1 month (originating from a cemental tear on the mesial root surface). Regression of periapical radiolucency: AD most (3/7), then ES (4/10), AH (2/6), CS (2/8). Pooled by sealer type: ERBS 38.5% (5/13) vs CSBS 33.3% (6/18) showed similar radiographic change in cases with preoperative periapical radiolucency.

4. DISCUSSION (key points)
CSBS sealer-based obturation resembles the traditional single-cone technique using a pre-fitted single GP cone, but distinct in that the sealer provides the seal as the main canal content. Void formation can act as a bacterial reservoir; affected by obturation technique, material physical properties (film thickness, flowability, wettability), and canal anatomy. Void incidence here seemed more related to root canal anatomy (higher in oval/molar canals) than to sealer type. No differences in sealer extrusion among sealers; clinical outcome is not associated with sealer extrusion per prior literature, but extruded sealer may trigger tissue reaction depending on composition — CSBS has the advantage of biocompatible, resin-free ingredients (Ricucci et al.: extruded CSBS showed no foreign body/inflammatory response). High pH of CSBS may promote hard tissue formation and interfere with osteoclastic activity, favoring healing. Postoperative pain was rare in all groups (symptomatic teeth excluded); sealer-based obturation without pressure may be linked to rare postoperative pain. Ruparel et al. reported low-concentration AH Plus evoked CGRP release unlike CSBS, a possible cause of the slight VAS increase in AH.

5. CONCLUSIONS
No significant differences in postoperative pain, void, or sealer extrusion among different CSBSs and ERBSs in 1-week, 1-month, and 3-month evaluations. Calcium-silicate-based sealer with sealer-based obturation may provide comparable clinical efficiency to epoxy-resin-based sealer. Limitations: small number of cases and short-term (3-month) evaluation; long-term studies required, given concerns about hydraulic resorption of calcium-silicate sealers.
