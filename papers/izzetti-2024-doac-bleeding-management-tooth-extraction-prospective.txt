Direct Oral Anticoagulants and Bleeding Management Following Tooth Extractions—A Prospective Cohort Study
Izzetti R, Cinquini C, Nisi M, Mattiozzi M, Marotta M, Barone A.
Dentistry Journal (Dent J Basel) 2024;12(9):279. DOI: 10.3390/dj12090279. PMID: 39329845. PMCID: PMC11430983.

1. Introduction
Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) have gained popularity due to their predictable pharmacokinetic profile and convenience compared to warfarin, eliminating routine dose titration. Dabigatran directly inhibits thrombin; apixaban, rivaroxaban, and edoxaban are factor Xa inhibitors. The anticoagulant activity poses a risk of bleeding complications in the peri-operative period after dental surgery such as tooth extractions. Different protocols exist for managing DOAC patients in the dental setting; while VKA guidelines are established (Perry et al.: check INR within 72h, no intervention <4, add local hemostatic measures + 5% tranexamic acid mouthwash 4x/day for 48h), limited evidence exists for DOACs. Douketis et al. examined 460 individuals on oral anticoagulants needing dental procedures with no significant difference between warfarin and dabigatran, though urgent procedures increased risk. Single-tooth extraction poses limited risk, supporting non-discontinuation for simple single extractions. DOACs have rapid onset and shorter half-life making reversal more challenging; vitamin K/FFP ineffective. Specific antidotes: idarucizumab (dabigatran), andexanet alfa (rivaroxaban/apixaban). Minor bleeding usually managed with local measures (pressure, topical hemostatic agents, tranexamic acid mouthwashes). Given short half-life, transient discontinuation may reduce bleeding without significantly increasing thrombosis risk. Aim: assess bleeding risk associated with tooth extractions in DOAC patients.

2. Materials and Methods
Single-center, non-randomized clinical trial, 1-week follow-up. Approved by IRB University Hospital of Pisa (Ethics committee North-West Tuscany, approval 17796/2020). STROBE-adherent.
Patients: consecutive patients referred for dental extraction 2021–2023. Inclusion: age >18, treatment with DOACs, requiring ≥1 tooth extraction, willing to consent. Exclusion: <18 years, antiplatelet/VKA therapy, congenital/acquired coagulopathies (hemophilia, factor deficiency, vWD, thrombocytopenia, cirrhosis). All prescribed: CBC, PT, PTT, INR.
Groups: at baseline assigned by cardiologist regimen (maintain vs suspend). Stratified by medication (rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban, dabigatran). Collected systemic health, dose, BMI, smoking. Vital params on intervention day.
Surgery: standardized minimally invasive technique. Local anesthesia mepivacaine 2% + epinephrine 1:100,000. Elevators/forceps. Full-thickness flap in difficult extractions. Subdivided by teeth count (≤3 vs >3; cut-off based on literature ≤3 = low risk). Socket managed with local debridement, saline rinsing, collagen sponge in alveolus. Single interrupted absorbable sutures. NO additional hemostatic measures at this stage. Antibiotics (amoxicillin 2g/die or clindamycin 1200mg/die if penicillin-allergic) for 6 days if indicated. Acetaminophen 1g for pain. Antibiotic co-dispensation to DOAC patients not considered a bleeding risk factor (unlike warfarin).
Bleeding registration:
- Intra-operative: active bleeding hindering visibility of surgical area.
- Peri-operative: oozing/active bleeding not controlled with local hemostatic measures and/or gauze compression within first 80 min, registered every 20 min. If detected, gauze with tranexamic acid applied; after 20 min compression, oxidized cellulose + additional sutures if still active; prolonged bleeding → diathermocoagulation.
- Post-operative: bleeding/oozing beyond 12h post-op, leading to hematoma of oral soft tissue requiring intervention or hospitalization.
Follow-up: wound management instructions + questionnaire during post-op week (number of bleeding episodes, management, additional pain meds). 7-day re-evaluation for complications; suture removed. Evaluated by operator not involved in surgery.
Sample size: based on Berton et al. (bleeding needing additional management in 20% of DOAC patients), ≥28 subjects required (alpha=0.05, power=0.9). Enrolled 50 to manage dropouts.
Statistics: by biostatistician not involved in surgery. Shapiro–Wilk normality; Kruskal–Wallis, Mann–Whitney, Wilcoxon signed rank; chi-squared/Fisher exact. XLStat. CRFs ≥95% complete; missing data by regression imputation.

3. Results
3.1 Patient population: 49 patients (21 females, 42.9%) receiving 136 tooth extractions completed. One excluded for non-compliance. Mean age 72.2 years (SD 8.3). 17 rivaroxaban (20mg/die n=16, 15mg/die n=1), 16 apixaban (10mg/die n=9, 5mg/die n=7), 8 edoxaban (30mg/die n=2, 60mg/die n=6), 8 dabigatran (300mg/die n=5, 220mg/die n=3). Mean DOAC duration 38 months (SD 27).
33 patients (67%) interrupted DOACs pre-surgery, mean suspension 28.48±13.04h at surgery. Suspension prescribed by cardiologist (14), GP (4), other specialists (11), 4 autonomously. All refrained from DOAC ≥24h following surgery.
Indications: atrial fibrillation (55.1%), deep vein thrombosis (24.5%), ischemic cardiomyopathy (18.3%), pulmonary embolism (16.3%), valvular dysfunction (12.2%), heart failure (8%). Hypertension 40.8%, type II diabetes 24.5%. Mean BMI 28.3±5.5 kg/m2 (overweight). 6 current smokers (12.2%), 24 former smokers (49%). Mean SBP 133 mmHg, DBP 79, HR 77.6 bpm, SatO2 96%, RR 15, temp 36.4°C, glycemia 121.4 mg/dl. Significant baseline differences in body temperature and glycemia.
Blood testing: significant difference in PT ratio for rivaroxaban vs apixaban (p=0.038) and edoxaban (p=0.03). Rivaroxaban patients had significantly higher PTT vs other DOACs (p=0.001). Mean eGFR 82.61 mL/min (mild renal impairment).

3.2 Tooth extraction features: 36 patients (73.5%) had ≤3 teeth extracted. <3 extractions per procedure showed significantly lower peri-operative bleeding rates. Post-operative bleeding differences by teeth count only at T2 and T7. Removing adjacent teeth did not influence bleeding. Surgical flap raised in 26.5%; osteotomy in 30.6% (complex extractions = 57.1%). Peri-operative bleeding higher with flap elevation (significant only at 20 min). Post-operative bleeding significantly higher at all timepoints when flap elevation required.

3.3 Intra-operative bleeding: 27 patients (55.1%) — 12 apixaban, 9 rivaroxaban, 3 edoxaban, 3 dabigatran. >3 extractions bled in 69.2%; <3 teeth in 50% (p<0.001). No difference between DOAC-suspended vs not.

3.4 Peri-operative bleeding: at 20 min in 28 patients (57.1%) — 14 apixaban, 6 edoxaban, 5 rivaroxaban, 3 dabigatran. Significant difference apixaban vs rivaroxaban at 20 min. More frequent in posterior vs anterior teeth (81% vs 64%) and complex vs simple extractions (26% vs 13%). At 40 min: 19 patients (38.8%; 7 apixaban, 6 rivaroxaban, 4 edoxaban, 2 dabigatran). At 60 min: 14 patients (28.6%; 5 rivaroxaban, 4 apixaban, 3 edoxaban, 2 dabigatran). At 80 min: 8 patients (16.3%; 5 rivaroxaban, 3 apixaban). No statistical differences in peri-operative bleeding when comparing suspended vs maintained DOAC at any timepoint.

3.5 Post-operative bleeding: peaked day 1 post-op, 23 patients (46.9%; 11 rivaroxaban, 7 apixaban, 4 edoxaban, 1 dabigatran). Day 2: 11 episodes (22.4%; 7 rivaroxaban, 3 apixaban, 1 edoxaban). Day 3: 4 episodes (8.2%). Days 4–7: only rivaroxaban patients (2 episodes at days 4 and 5, 1 at days 6 and 7), no significant differences. Significant difference at T1 and T2 between rivaroxaban and dabigatran (p=0.016 at T1, p=0.023 at T2). Trend (non-significant) for post-op bleeding in patients who did NOT discontinue DOAC at T1 and T2, while delayed bleeding (T3–T7) observed in patients who refrained from DOACs.

4. Discussion
~50% of DOAC patients experienced intra- and early peri-operative bleeding; post-op bleeding peaked at day 1. Decision to continue or temporarily stop DOACs did not significantly impact bleeding risk — similar rates regardless of interruption, provided appropriate local hemostatic measures employed. DOACs' predictable pharmacokinetics and short half-lives allow safe dental procedures without discontinuation. >3 tooth extractions: higher intra-/peri-/post-op bleeding at all timepoints (except T1), regardless of protocol. Three-teeth cut-off recognized by Lupi et al.; Labadibi et al. reported DOAC suspension could be avoided for <4 extractions with adjunctive local hemostatics. Cocero et al. suggested scheduling extractions ≥4h after last DOAC intake, avoiding extraction of contiguous premolars/molars in one session (but contiguous teeth not a risk factor in this sample). Higher bleeding with rivaroxaban may be due to prolongation of PT/PTT; apixaban is highly selective FXa inhibitor. Bleeding ~50% here vs Yagyuu et al. (10.4% post-extraction bleeding), Hiroshi et al. (1.65% dabigatran, 3.41% rivaroxaban). Patel et al.: post-op bleeding in 13%, half self-limiting; additional local hemostatic measures anticipated in 6%, DOAC modification case-by-case. Brennan et al. prospective cohort without DOAC modification: comparable bleeding (apixaban 39%, rivaroxaban 37%, dabigatran 27%); pre-extraction rivaroxaban levels ~2x higher in bleeders, 82% of rivaroxaban bleeders had taken morning dose vs 18% night before. van Diermen protocol: extractions ≥1–3h after DOAC; EHRA: reach trough (12h after dose). Heparin bridging associated with increased bleeding without reduced thromboembolism. Miller and Miller: variability in DOAC interruption 12–120h. Treatment with rivaroxaban and apixaban associated with higher bleeding risk; suspension did not influence post-op bleeding. Flap raising → increased bleeding at 20 min and T1–T7; osteotomy → higher intra-/peri-op bleeding at 20 and 40 min. Bleeding more frequent in posterior and complex extractions.
Limitations: small sample, no stratification by medication type within suspended/maintained, selection bias (heterogeneous suspension protocols), timing relative to last DOAC not accounted for, no DOAC activity assays (anti-Xa), variability in surgical procedures.

5. Conclusions
Patients treated with new oral anticoagulants show comparable rates of intra-, peri-, and post-operative bleeding. Bleeding in peri- and post-operative periods was mild and manageable with gauze compression. Suspension of anticoagulant therapy did not influence peri- or post-operative bleeding. Larger studies with better balancing required.
