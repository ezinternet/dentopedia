Title: The effects of dental hygienist autonomy on dental care utilization.
Authors: Chen J, Meyerhoefer CD, Timmons EJ
Journal: Health Economics 2024;33(8):1726-1747
DOI: 10.1002/hec.4832
PMID: 38536894

ABSTRACT (abstract-only — full text not retrieved):

Investigates the effects of regulations governing the practice autonomy of dental
hygienists on dental care use, using the 2001–2014 Medical Expenditure Panel Survey
(MEPS). Measures the strength of autonomy regulations by extending the Dental Hygiene
Professional Practice Index (DHPPI) to 2001–2014, capturing within-state regulatory
changes over time. Using a difference-in-differences framework on selected states:
relaxing supervision requirements to grant dental hygienists MODERATE autonomy increases
total dental visits via greater use of PREVENTIVE care. However, the use of dental
TREATMENT decreases when states adopt the HIGHEST level of autonomy. Both estimates
increase in magnitude in dental-care provider shortage areas. Dental visits shift to
dental hygienists in shortage areas when scope of practice is expanded, with an increase
in hygienist-performed tasks (cleanings, exams).
