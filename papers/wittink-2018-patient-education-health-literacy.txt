Patient education and health literacy.
Wittink H, Oosterhaven J.
Musculoskelet Sci Pract. 2018 Dec;38:120-127. doi: 10.1016/j.msksp.2018.06.004.
PMID: 30017902

ABSTRACT

Patient education is a relatively new science in health care; historically it was
mainly transfer of knowledge and biomedically based advice — which has been shown
to be ineffective and sometimes even counterproductive. Care has shifted from a
paternalistic "doctor knows best" model to patient-centred care, in which education
must be tailored to individual needs.

Purpose: to increase awareness of patients' health literacy levels.

Health literacy is defined as people's knowledge, motivation, and competences to
access, understand, appraise, and apply health information in order to make judgements
and decisions about health care, disease prevention, and health promotion.

Many patients have low health literacy: they have difficulty with reading, writing,
numeracy, communication, and electronic technology — all of which impede access to,
and understanding of, health-care information.

Multiple professional organizations recommend UNIVERSAL health literacy precautions
for ALL patients, regardless of literacy or education level:
  - avoid medical jargon;
  - break information into small, concrete steps;
  - LIMIT the focus of a visit to THREE key points or tasks;
  - assess comprehension via the TEACH-BACK cycle.

Printed information should be at or below a sixth-grade reading level. Visual aids
enhance understanding.
