Low health literacy and health outcomes: an updated systematic review.
Berkman ND, Sheridan SL, Donahue KE, Halpern DJ, Crotty K.
Ann Intern Med. 2011 Jul 19;155(2):97-107.
PMID: 21768583
DOI: 10.7326/0003-4819-155-2-201107190-00005

ABSTRACT (only — full text not retrieved):

Approximately 80 million Americans have limited health literacy, which puts them
at greater risk for poorer access to care and poorer health outcomes.

This is an update of a 2004 systematic review examining whether low health literacy
is related to poorer use of health care, outcomes, costs, and disparities in health
and health care, across all age groups.

Methods: Searched MEDLINE, CINAHL, PsycINFO, ERIC, and the Cochrane Library
(health literacy 2003 to 22 February 2011; numeracy 1966 to 22 February 2011).
Included studies that compared outcomes by directly measured health literacy or
numeracy. Two reviewers abstracted data and assessed study quality.

Results: 96 good- or fair-quality studies (111 articles) were included: 98 addressed
health literacy, 22 addressed numeracy, and 9 addressed both.

Low health literacy was CONSISTENTLY associated with:
- more hospitalizations
- greater use of emergency care
- lower receipt of mammography screening
- lower receipt of influenza vaccine
- poorer ability to take medications appropriately
- poorer ability to interpret labels and health messages
- among elderly persons, poorer overall health status and higher mortality rates

Poor health literacy partially explains racial disparities in some outcomes.
Conclusions about numeracy were limited by few and inconsistent studies.

Limitations: Studies were restricted to English-language articles; no MeSH heading
exists to identify these studies, complicating the search. NO evidence was found on
oral health literacy and outcomes.

Conclusion: Low health literacy is associated with poorer health outcomes and poorer
use of health care services.
