Is routine antibiotic prophylaxis warranted in dental implant surgery to prevent early implant failure? — a systematic review.
Momand P, Naimi-Akbar A, Hultin M, Lund B, Gotrick B.
BMC Oral Health. 2024 Jul 25;24(1):842.
DOI: 10.1186/s12903-024-04611-0
PMID: 39054434  PMCID: PMC11270919

[FULL TEXT retrieved from PubMed Central (PMC11270919).]

ABSTRACT
Background: Whether antibiotic prophylaxis should be administered routinely for dental implant surgery
is unresolved. Antibiotics are often given to reduce infection risk that could lead to early implant
failure. Increasing antibiotic resistance makes reducing antibiotic use important. Aim: evaluate the
efficacy of preoperative antibiotics in preventing early implant failure in overall healthy patients.
Methods: electronic search of PubMed (Medline), Web of Science, Cochrane Library up to 1 Oct 2023 for
RCTs comparing antibiotic prophylaxis with no antibiotics/placebo in healthy patients. Primary outcome:
patients with early implant failure. Risk of bias assessed; meta-analysis; GRADE certainty. RR, RD, 95% CI.
Results: After duplicate removal, 1086 abstracts screened, 17 full-text reviewed. Seven RCTs (moderate/low
risk of bias), 1859 patients, 3014 implants in meta-analysis. Early implant failure at patient level —
no statistically significant difference (RR 0.66, 95% CI 0.30-1.47) between antibiotic and placebo. Risk
difference -0.007 (95% CI -0.035-0.020), NNT 143.
Conclusion: Antibiotic prophylaxis does not seem to have a substantial effect on early implant failure;
results do not support routine antibiotic prophylaxis for dental implant surgery.

BACKGROUND
Dental implants replace teeth lost to caries, periodontal disease, or trauma. Biological complications
may be early or late. Early failure = loss of implant within first months after insertion, usually due to
lack of osseointegration; attributed to bacterial contamination during surgery, but also surgical
technique, implant characteristics (size, length, surface), surgeon experience, history of periodontitis,
smoking. Antibiotics given to reduce infection/failure; original routine was pre- and postoperative
antibiotics for all patients, questioned 25 years ago. No single placebo-controlled RCT has shown a
statistically significant association between prophylaxis and reduced early failure; underpowering is one
explanation but two RCTs had large samples (506, 473 patients). Reviews/meta-analyses pooling
non-significant results have shown statistical reduction, but statistical significance ≠ clinical
relevance — need the risk difference. Antibiotic resistance is a major global concern.
Some SRs conclude prophylaxis is indicated; others conclude routine use not warranted in healthy patients.
Previous SRs/MAs included non-placebo-controlled, non-blinded, or high-risk-of-bias studies; new RCTs in
the last two years allow a meta-analysis restricted to low-risk-of-bias placebo-controlled RCTs, plus
certainty of evidence. Aim twofold: efficacy of preoperative antibiotics in preventing early failure in
healthy patients, and certainty of evidence.

METHODS
PRISMA. Protocol registered PROSPERO CRD42021292610. Focused question (PICO): effect of antibiotic
prophylaxis vs placebo/no antibiotics in overall healthy patients undergoing dental implant surgery on
implant failure.
Inclusion: ≥20 patients; RCTs; ≥2 months follow-up.
Exclusion: mini-implants/orthodontic mini-screws; immediate implant placement at sites with apical
pathology; patients whose medical history indicated need for prophylaxis.
Three databases (PubMed, Cochrane, Web of Science) up to 1 Oct 2023, plus hand searches and
clinicaltrials.gov. Two reviewers screened titles/abstracts independently; full-text by ≥2 of 5 reviewers.
Data extraction by PM & BG, checked by others; trial authors contacted for missing data. RoB-2 used
(low/moderate/high). Studies with low/moderate risk included in meta-analysis. Random-effects (Hedges)
model; RR and RD; heterogeneity I2 and Q. Subgroups: amoxicillin-only studies; and all studies excluding
the two with immediate implant placement into extraction sockets. Stata 16 SE. GRADE for certainty.

RESULTS
Search: 1267 records; after removing 182 duplicates, 1085 titles/abstracts screened; 1069 excluded; 16
full-text reviewed; +1 from hand search. Five excluded (not randomized or lacked no-antibiotic/placebo
control). Twelve studies eligible for risk-of-bias; seven ultimately in meta-analysis.
Risk of bias: 5 high (excluded), 4 low, 3 moderate (moderate in "conflict of interest" domain).
Characteristics: six of seven were multicenter; six conducted in Europe, one in Asia; treated Jan 2006-
June 2021; all double-blinded. Five RCTs: 2 g amoxicillin 1 h preop vs placebo; one: 2 g amoxicillin 1 h
preop + 500 mg ×3 days 1-3 postop vs placebo; one: 600 mg clindamycin 1 h preop vs placebo. Three RCTs
single implants without augmentation; two included some implants into fresh extraction sockets (19.5%,
14.0%); one included some minor augmentation/sinus lift/GBR (29.9%); one only simultaneous GBR.
Outcome "early implant failure" = loss of implant within first few months after placement and before
loading with supraconstruction. Most studies refer to 3-6 month healing; integration typically 6-12 weeks.
Timing of early failure not clearly reported in any RCT. Implant stability tested at final follow-up
(3-6 months, 4 months, 3 months, or 2 months). In all, 1859 patients and 3014 implants analyzed; implant
failure in 51 patients (2.7%).

In the 7 studies: 929 received antibiotics, 930 placebo. Early failures: 20 (2.2%) antibiotic group vs
31 (3.3%) placebo group. Four RCTs lower failure with antibiotics; two lower with placebo; one equal.
Failure rates ≤6.5% in any group. Two RCTs measured PROMs (pain/QoL). No individual RCT showed a
significant difference.

Synthesis:
- Overall (7 studies): RR 0.66 (95% CI 0.30-1.47); RD -0.007 (95% CI -0.035-0.020); NNT 143 (95% CI 29-∞).
- Amoxicillin subgroup (6 studies): RR 0.60 (95% CI 0.27-1.31); RD -0.011 (95% CI -0.029-0.006).
- Excluding the two immediate-placement-in-socket studies (5 studies): RR 1.10 (95% CI 0.35-3.45);
  RD 0.002 (95% CI -0.027-0.030).
- None of the subgroup meta-analyses showed a significant difference.
Certainty: certainty that "the effect of antibiotic prophylaxis in preventing implant failure is small"
was MODERATE (GRADE).

DISCUSSION (key points)
Seven placebo-controlled double-blinded RCTs (low/moderate RoB); none individually significant. Meta-
analysis: routine prophylaxis to prevent early failure not needed. NNT 143 (CI to infinity = no effect);
routine prophylaxis would mean very many patients receive antibiotics unnecessarily. Differs from prior
reviews that included high-RoB / non-placebo RCTs. Lund et al. 2015 NNT 50 (no benefit in uncomplicated
surgery); Rodriguez Sanchez et al. 2018 NNT 67 (effective). EAO 2015 consensus: prophylaxis not recommended
for uncomplicated implant surgery; Italian Academy of Osseointegration recommended single dose in
uncomplicated cases — contradictory recommendations. Amoxicillin 2 g 1 h preop aligns with Esposito
Cochrane review; clindamycin 600 mg for penicillin allergy.
Limitations: only seven low/moderate-RoB studies; two small RCTs; design differences (follow-up timing,
chlorhexidine use as confounder, inadequate periodontal reporting, varied placement procedures, varied
smoker proportion 9.3-34%). The two RCTs with immediate post-extraction implants had higher placebo-group
failure (5.1%, 4.7%) — may underlie EAO's note of possible benefit for immediate placement into fresh
sockets. Salgado-Peralvo 2021 recommended extended prophylaxis for immediate implants (treat extracted
tooth as potentially infected) — better described as antibiotic treatment than prophylaxis.

CONCLUSION
Based on high-quality RCTs, the benefit of antibiotic prophylaxis for implant surgery is likely very
limited. Given rising antibiotic resistance, prophylaxis should be avoided in most cases of implant
surgery. Could form the foundation of clearer guidelines.
