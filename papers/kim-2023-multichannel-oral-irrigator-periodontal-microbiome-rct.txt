TITLE: Effect of a multichannel oral irrigator on periodontal health and the oral microbiome
DOI: 10.1038/s41598-023-38894-0
PMID: 37491575
PMCID: PMC10368725
JOURNAL: Scientific Reports, 2023 Jul 25; 13(1)
AUTHORS: Kim JM, Yoo SY, An JS, Woo JJ, Cho YD, Park HE (Seoul National University Dental Hospital)

ABSTRACT:
Oral biofilms or dental plaques are one of the major etiological factors for diverse oral diseases. We aimed to evaluate the effect of a multichannel oral irrigator (MCOI) on periodontal health in 29 participants randomly divided into two groups: the MCOI group and the control group. To evaluate the effect of the MCOI on periodontal health, the modified Quigley Hein Plaque Index (PI), Mühlemann-Son Sulcus Bleeding Index (SBI), bleeding on probing (BOP), and swelling were evaluated and compared before and after MCOI use for 3 days. Although PI and SBI showed statistically significant increases in the control group, the MCOI group showed no significant changes in either parameter. Moreover, the percentage of BOP was significantly lower in the MCOI group. Saliva samples were analyzed by 16s rRNA amplicon sequencing to investigate changes in the oral microbiome. Sequencing results showed that Prevotella spp. were significantly increased in the control group, whereas no significant change was detected in the MCOI group. Using the MCOI, enriched populations and functional pathways were detected in pioneer species comprising non-mutans streptococci. These findings provide evidence of the effectiveness of the MCOI in maintaining periodontal health and a healthy microbial ecology in the oral cavity.

FULL TEXT:
Introduction: Oral biofilms/dental plaques are major etiological factors for oral diseases (gingival inflammation, dental caries). Biofilm forms 1h after rinsing, matures within ~72h. Mechanical toothbrushing is gold standard for plaque removal but removes only 42-60% of total biofilm. Supplementary oral care products are needed. One-channel oral irrigators apply a pulsating stream of water to remove biofilm in hard-to-reach areas; oral irrigation reduces probing pocket depth, BOP, supragingival plaque, and pro-inflammatory cytokines. Disadvantages: must be placed at the exact location requiring irrigation, and risk of aspiration due to lack of suction (aspiration pneumonia is fatal in disabled/elderly). A multichannel oral irrigator (MCOI) (COMORAL, SMDsolutions Co., Seoul, Korea) for gingival crevice cleaning was introduced — dozens of injecting holes in the mouthpiece through which water flows simultaneously, plus a suction function synchronized with water ejection to prevent aspiration. This study evaluated MCOI effectiveness on periodontal health, with 16s rRNA amplicon and shotgun metagenomic sequencing to assess oral microbiota changes.

Methods: 30 healthy participants recruited, randomly allocated to experimental (MCOI) or control group. IRB Seoul National University Dental Hospital. Inclusion: 19-85 years, no periodontal/medical disease, ≥16 teeth (excl third molars), no laminates/crowns on evaluated teeth. Exclusion: recent antibiotics, heavy smokers, pregnancy, severe periodontal disease (>4mm probing depth), caries requiring restoration, cognitive impairment. Originally planned as RCT of 42 participants but IRB recommended reducing sample size (no preliminary study). MCOI device: machine with pumps + mouthpiece (WATERET) + connecting tube; water injection at 45° angle targeting gingival margin, delivering 500 mL tap water at 3.71 bar for 1.5 min, 60 dense streams up/down in four directions; Class I medical device. At initial visit, all 30 participants were scaled by two hygienists. 2 weeks later (first phase): PI, SBI, BOP, swelling measured + saliva collected. Then for 3 days: MCOI group used the device twice daily (morning + before bed) for 3 min at medium intensity and did NOT brush; control group did NOT brush teeth for 3 days, gargling lightly with tap water before bed. Second phase: measurements + saliva repeated. PI = modified Quigley Hein; SBI = Mühlemann-Son Sulcus Bleeding Index. Single examiner. 16s rRNA amplicon sequencing (V3-V4, MiSeq) on 29 participants; shotgun metagenome (NovaSeq) on selected samples; MetaPhlAn4/HUMAnN3 analysis. Wilcoxon signed-rank, significance p=0.05.

Results: 29 of 30 included (1 excluded due to loss during analysis). Mean age 33.7 ± 8.2 years; 16 women, 13 men.
Clinical: In control group, average PI and SBI significantly increased. In MCOI group, % BOP significantly DECREASED, and swelling also decreased (not significant). Supra-gingival calculus observed only in control group after 3 days (4 mandibular anterior teeth in 2 of 14 control participants, 14.28%); none in MCOI group.
Microbiome (16s rRNA): No significant difference in alpha diversity between groups. Significant increase in phylum Bacteroidetes (15.3 ± 6.98% vs 20.4 ± 5.19%; p=0.031) in control group, whereas MCOI group maintained a distribution similar to basal state. Prevotella spp. (opportunistic periodontal pathogens) significantly increased by 114.28% (p=0.003) in the control group, indicating possible disease progression; no significant change in Prevotella in the MCOI group. Beta diversity differed significantly between control and MCOI (PCoA ANOSIM p=0.023 and 0.006). LDA: pioneer species on tooth surface (oral commensal bacteria) significantly increased in the MCOI group (LDA score 3.06). Cariogenic mutans streptococci seldom detected in either group.
Microbiome (shotgun metagenome): MCOI group showed increased abundance of metabolic pathways (amino acid synthesis, energy metabolism, nucleotide biosynthesis) almost exclusively in gram-positive bacteria (non-mutans streptococci, Actinomyces). Fermentation pathways related to acid production enriched but no high-acidogenicity species (e.g. mutans streptococci) detected. Multiple biosynthetic pathways reduced in gram-negative bacteria in the MCOI group.

Discussion: PI increased in both groups after the trial but statistically significant only in control. MCOI may help suppress biofilm accumulation but effectiveness in decreasing plaque cannot be determined (plaque accumulated in MCOI group too). SBI significantly increased (gingivitis) only in control. BOP decreased in MCOI and increased in control — gingivitis alleviated in MCOI group, consistent with the 2001 American Academy of Periodontology statement that irrigation reduces gingival inflammation beyond toothbrushing alone. Attached supragingival biofilm on tooth surface was not removed effectively, but subgingival biofilm was removed more efficiently by MCOI (water ejected at 45° to gingival margin). Limitations: 3-day trial too short to detect precise periodontal state change (ethical reasons prevent longer); small sample (reduced from 42 by IRB); only healthy adults; preliminary study. Potential utility in disabled/elderly with low dexterity, dysphagia/aspiration risk, Alzheimer/dementia/autism patients; MCOI's suction function reduces aspiration risk. Future: longer study, additional groups (toothbrushing+MCOI, toothbrushing only, one-channel irrigator).

Conclusion: These findings provide evidence of the effectiveness of MCOI in maintaining periodontal health and a healthy microbial ecology in the oral cavity.
