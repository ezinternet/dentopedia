Title: Cost-efficiency of digital versus conventional workflow for removable complete dentures: A systematic review and meta-analysis

Authors: Muehlemann E, Pachiou A, Sáenz-Ravello G, Jung RE, Strauss FJ, Liguori MG
Journal: J Prosthodont. 2025;35(3):243-251.
DOI: 10.1111/jopr.70074
PMID: 41368723
PMCID: PMC12990028

ABSTRACT

Purpose

To compare the cost-efficiency of digitally designed and manufactured removable complete dentures (RCDs) with conventionally fabricated RCDs through a systematic review and meta-analysis.

Methods

A comprehensive electronic and manual search was performed in PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and Scopus up to February 10, 2025. Eligible studies included clinical trials and cohort studies comparing cost-related outcomes of digital and conventional RCD workflows. Primary outcomes were laboratory, clinical, and total costs; secondary outcomes included the number of treatment sessions. Meta-analyses were conducted using random-effects models. Risk of bias was evaluated using standardized tools.

Results

Four retrospective studies and one prospective study, including 184 patients, met the inclusion criteria. No statistically significant differences were observed between digital and conventional workflows in laboratory costs (mean difference [MD]: -239.77 (2025 USD); p=0.1063), clinical costs (MD: 74.39 (2025 USD); p=0.4514), total costs (MD: -357.76 (2025 USD); p=0.2577), or treatment sessions (MD: -1.47; p=0.3514). Operator experience significantly influenced clinical costs (p<0.0001) and the number of sessions (p=0.0001).

Conclusion

Within the limitations of the available evidence, digital and conventional workflows for RCD fabrication demonstrated comparable cost-efficiency. Although digital workflows may reduce the number of sessions when performed by experienced clinicians, the current evidence is insufficient to establish a clear cost-efficiency advantage.

FULL TEXT

Despite a decline in the prevalence of complete edentulism over the past decade, tooth loss persists as a significant global health issue, especially among the elderly. This irreversible condition is a disease burden for oral health, affecting mastication, aesthetics, and psychosocial well-being.

The most traditional and long-term documented treatment plan for edentulism is removable complete dentures (RCDs). RCDs have been processed for decades through conventional techniques involving physical impressions, manual procedures, and laboratory-based workflows, all of which have demonstrated long-term success. Although these methods are well-established, they necessitate multiple clinical appointments, considerable chairside time, and intricate laboratory processes. Other limitations of the conventional workflow are associated with resin shrinkage and dimensional changes, which can compromise retention, fit, and accuracy, all of which may result in discomfort, tooth movement, and even prosthesis rejection.

Recent advancements in digital dentistry have profoundly influenced the fabrication process of RCDs. Digital technologies have revolutionized restorative dentistry by incorporating workflows that integrate intraoral scanning with computer-aided design and computer-aided manufacturing (CAD-CAM). CAD-CAM-fabricated RCDs can be delivered more efficiently with fewer clinical visits while providing advantages such as digital archiving, enhanced fit, superior material properties, and improved retention. However, obstacles such as the required learning curve, dependence on technology, and reduced adaptability in complex cases persist.

The effectiveness of these prosthodontic treatments can be evaluated using various clinical measures. Chairside time and laboratory costs are key treatment-related factors in determining the feasibility of treatment protocols, while the financial aspects of a treatment plan are the most common reason influencing a patient's decision. Although digital workflows have advanced the fabrication of RCDs, their cost-efficiency compared to conventional techniques remains uncertain because no systematic reviews with quantitative analyses, such as meta-analyses, are available. Bridging this gap is essential to ensure that treatment decisions align with both clinical requirements and economic sustainability.

This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to compare digitally designed and manufactured RCDs with conventionally fabricated ones in terms of cost-efficiency. The null hypothesis was that digital workflows of RCDs would not differ in cost-efficiency from conventional techniques.

METHODS

This systematic review was conducted according to the Cochrane guidelines for conducting systematic reviews of interventions and was reported in accordance with the updated Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analysis (PRISMA 2020) guidelines. An analytical protocol was developed and registered in PROSPERO (ID: CRD42024607329). In edentulous patients receiving RCDs (P), do digitally designed and manufactured RCDs (I), compared to conventionally designed and manufactured RCDs (C), result in better cost-efficiency (O)?

A systematic electronic search was conducted on Medline (PubMed), Embase, Web of Science, and Scopus. Additionally, the references of the relevant reviews were retrieved, and the included primary studies were manually searched for additional eligible studies. A systematic search of the gray literature (OpenGrey) and of registered but unpublished trials at clinicaltrials.gov was also performed. The electronic search was conducted up to February 10, 2025, and designed and adapted to each type of database.

The inclusion criteria were as follows: randomized controlled trials, non-controlled prospective studies, observational studies, and case series. Studies directly comparing digitally fabricated RCDs with conventionally fabricated RCDs, as well as studies reporting outcomes of conventional RCDs alone, were included. Only studies published in English were considered. The exclusion criteria were case reports, in vitro studies, reviews, editorials, and conference abstracts. Additionally, studies on implant-supported or attachment-retained overdentures (including double crown-retained designs) were not deemed eligible.

In the initial stage of literature selection, two calibrated reviewers (A.P., M.G.L.) independently screened the titles and abstracts of all identified studies for eligibility, following the predefined inclusion criteria, using Rayyan Online Software (Qatar Computing Research Institute). During the second stage, the same reviewers examined the full texts of potentially eligible articles, applying the previously mentioned exclusion criteria. Any discrepancies between reviewers were resolved by consulting two co-authors (F.J.S., E.M.). Inter-examiner agreement for both stages of selection was quantified using Cohen's kappa scores. Articles that did not meet the eligibility criteria were excluded, and the reasons for exclusion were documented.

Data were collected by two independent reviewers (A.P., M.G.L.) using data extraction tables (Excel Microsoft Corporation). In case of missing data, the authors of the included studies were contacted via email to provide the missing or additional data. The following data were extracted from each eligible study: author names, year of publication, country, study design, sample size, gender, control group, test group, costs for each group (lab and clinical), total cost for each of the two groups, number of sessions for each workflow, patient satisfaction, OHRQoL, and fit accuracy.

The ROBINS-I V2 tool was used for non-randomized clinical studies to assess the risk of bias. Two independent reviewers evaluated the risk of bias of the included studies (A.P., M.G.L.). Any disagreement between reviewers was resolved by open discussion. In case no agreement could be possible, the final decision was made by another co-author (F.J.S.).

Descriptive and qualitative aspects of the included studies were summarized (study design, sample size, gender, intervention, clinical costs, laboratory costs, total cost, chairside time, and follow-up). WebPlotDigitizer (v5.2) was used to extract data from graphs. Exploratory quantitative synthesis was conducted in studies with comparable outcomes using a comparative efficiency research framework to meta-analyze economic evaluation studies following the data-harmonization process proposed by Bagepally et al., first adjusting the origin currencies from the date of analysis to 2025 using the inflation indices for every country, and then standardizing the currencies to 2025 international dollars (2025 USD, up to February 2025) using the Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) index of every country. Mean differences of laboratory, clinical, and total costs were summarized, as well as the mean difference in the number of sessions involved in every workflow as a measure of effectiveness. When necessary, preprocessing of crossover design data was conducted. Quantitative analyses summarized the mean differences in laboratory, clinical, and total costs, as well as the number of treatment sessions between digital and conventional workflows, using a random-effects model with the Hartung-Knapp adjustment. Heterogeneity was assessed using the Q test (p<0.05) and the I² statistic, while τ² and its confidence intervals were estimated with the restricted maximum-likelihood (REML) and Q-profile methods, respectively. When applicable, a subgroup analysis was performed to evaluate the effect of operator experience (students vs. specialists) on the effect estimates. Effect sizes were expressed as mean differences (MD) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). All statistical analyses were performed using the "meta" package in R (version 4.4.2, GSR), with the patient as the unit of analysis.

RESULTS

The study selection process is illustrated in the PRISMA flow diagram. An electronic literature search yielded 540 potential references (PubMed: 209, Scopus: 192, Embase: 126, Web of Science: 13). After removing duplicates, the remaining 407 studies were assessed, of which eight were deemed eligible for full-text screening (inter-reviewer agreement kappa=0.93). A hand search did not identify any additional studies. Of the eight full-text articles, three did not meet the inclusion criteria and were excluded (Miyayasu et al. 2018 — no digital workflow; Srinivasan et al. 2021 — no conventional workflow; Lo Russo et al. 2024 — many subcategories of base and teeth). Finally, five studies met the inclusion criteria and were eligible for further analysis.

All included studies were published between 2019 and 2025. Among the five studies, four were retrospective and one was prospective, and all were included for data extraction.

A total of 184 patients participated in the included studies, each receiving an RCD, fabricated either conventionally or digitally, resulting in 202 RCDs. In all studies, conventional RCDs were produced using compression molding protocols. Digital RCDs, however, were fabricated by milling in three studies, and by either milling or 3D printing in two studies. None of the included studies reported quantitative data on patient-centered effectiveness outcomes, such as OHRQoL, that could be included in the meta-analysis.

Regarding cost analysis, two studies reported costs in US dollars, while the others used Swiss francs, euros, and Japanese yen. In terms of operator experience, two studies involved dental students as the responsible clinicians, whereas the remaining three studies were conducted exclusively by prosthodontic specialists. Three retrospective and one prospective study reported on laboratory costs.

The meta-analysis for laboratory costs was performed using the mean differences reported across the included studies. The pooled effect size was -239.7 (2025 USD; 95% CI: -714.4 to 234.8), and the test for overall effect yielded t=-1.6 (n.s.), indicating no statistically significant difference in laboratory costs between the digital and conventional workflows. The heterogeneity analysis demonstrated a considerable level of variability among the studies, with an I² value of 77.7% and a significant Q-test (p=0.004). Subgroup analysis comparing students and specialists revealed no significant difference (Chi²=0.6, n.s.). One prospective and three retrospective studies assessed clinical costs.

The meta-analysis for clinical costs was conducted using the mean differences from the included studies. The pooled effect size was 74.4 (2025 USD; 95% CI: -120.0 to 268.7), with an overall effect test of t=0.7 (n.s.), indicating no statistically significant difference in clinical costs between the digital and conventional workflows. The heterogeneity analysis revealed a high level of variability across the studies, with an I² value of 97.8% and a significant Q-test (p<0.0001). Subgroup analysis between students and specialists demonstrated a significant difference (Chi²=260.9, p<0.0001), suggesting that clinical costs varied considerably depending on the operator's level of experience. One prospective and three retrospective studies evaluated total costs.

The meta-analysis for total costs was performed using the mean differences reported across the included studies. The pooled effect size was -357.7 (2025 USD; 95% CI: -1340.1 to 624.6), and the test for overall effect yielded t=-1.2 (n.s.), indicating no statistically significant difference in total costs between the digital and conventional workflows. The heterogeneity analysis revealed substantial variability among the studies, with an I² value of 73.8% and a significant Q-test (p=0.02). The subgroup analysis comparing students and specialists showed no significant difference (Chi²=1.6, n.s.), suggesting that operator experience did not markedly influence total costs.

One prospective and two retrospective studies evaluated the number of sessions required for treatment. The meta-analysis for the number of sessions was conducted using the mean differences reported across the included studies. The pooled effect size was -1.5 (95% CI: -6.7 to 3.8), with an overall effect test of t=-1.2 (n.s.), indicating no statistically significant difference in the number of sessions between digital and conventional workflows. The heterogeneity analysis demonstrated considerable variability among the studies, with an I² value of 95.1% and a significant Q-test (p<0.0001). Subgroup analysis comparing students and specialists revealed a significant difference (Chi²=14.9, p=0.0001), suggesting that the operator's level of experience influenced the number of treatment sessions required.

Among the five included studies, four were determined to have an overall moderate risk of bias, and one of them was evaluated as having a high risk of bias. The risk of bias assessment for all of the included studies was based on the ROBINS-I V2 tool for Risk of Bias In Non-randomized Studies of Interventions.

DISCUSSION

This systematic review and meta-analysis compared digitally designed and manufactured RCDs with conventionally fabricated ones in terms of cost-efficiency. Overall, the findings showed no meaningful differences in laboratory, clinical, or total costs, indicating that digital workflows are not more cost-efficient than conventional approaches.

It should be noted that the clinical costs reported in the included studies were primarily estimated based on time-related variables, rather than the actual fee charged to the patient. Therefore, these findings should be interpreted as reflecting treatment efficiency and resource utilization rather than direct patient expenses.

Laboratory and clinical costs represent key determinants in evaluating cost-efficiency between workflows. Digital manufacturing, often perceived as more expensive because of equipment acquisition and maintenance, can be balanced by potential savings from reduced manual labor and improved process efficiency. Although overall laboratory costs did not differ significantly between workflows, the meta-analysis revealed that experienced clinicians, but not students, might benefit from reduced laboratory costs. Less experienced operators, such as students, tend to require more chairside time and adjustments, contributing to increased laboratory and clinical costs. Conversely, experienced clinicians may better leverage digital technologies to streamline procedures and reduce overall costs. The findings of this review are partially in contrast with those of a previous systematic review, which reported lower laboratory and total costs associated with digital workflows. This discrepancy is likely due to differences in the type of analysis performed. The previous systematic review conducted a cost analysis, which did not account for patient outcomes. In contrast, the present study performed a meta-analysis of cost-efficiency, incorporating clinical outcomes and resulting in a more comprehensive and clinically relevant synthesis.

The pooled analysis revealed no significant differences in overall costs between digital and conventional workflows. However, a critical factor influencing cost-efficiency is the economic setting in which treatment is provided. The included studies were conducted in different countries, each with distinct healthcare systems, cost structures, and economic thresholds for cost-efficiency. Areas with greater financial power (e.g., Switzerland) have a willingness-to-pay (WTP) threshold considerably higher than others, meaning that cost-effectiveness evaluations may not be directly translatable across different settings. This variation in economic conditions is a key limitation of the current analysis, as the same treatment costs may be perceived differently depending on the local financial context.

Treatment duration and number of sessions are critical factors influencing overall treatment costs. While digital workflows are generally expected to reduce the number of appointments due to the simplification of clinical procedures and the integration of virtual design steps, rather than necessarily improved fabrication precision, the results of this review did not show significant differences compared to conventional methods. However, a visual trend toward fewer treatment sessions and shorter overall treatment duration was observed when experienced clinicians managed digital workflows. This discrepancy may stem from the learning curve associated with digital systems, as inexperienced operators may require additional sessions for adjustments or refinements, ultimately prolonging the overall treatment time. This suggests that while digital workflows may streamline clinical procedures, the difference in session reduction is not sufficient to justify the financial investment in digital equipment, particularly when considering the high initial costs associated with adopting CAD-CAM systems.

Cost-efficiency evaluates whether a solution provides the best value for the money spent. Although digital approaches have the potential to streamline clinical processes and reduce chairside time, it is important to note that the current analysis did not account for the initial investment required for digital equipment, which could further impact cost-efficiency evaluations. If the reduction in the number of sessions remains marginal, the economic justification for transitioning to digital workflows becomes even weaker. However, based on this analysis, decisions regarding the choice of workflow should not rely solely on economic factors. Clinicians should also consider non-economic factors, such as reproducibility, digital archiving, accessibility to technology, and the operator's and dental technician's expertise, when determining the most appropriate workflow for patient care.

LIMITATIONS

This systematic review presents specific limitations. First, the included studies demonstrated substantial heterogeneity in operator experience, patient demographics, fabrication protocols, and economic evaluation methods. Differences in how costs were calculated and reported limited direct comparisons and may have introduced variability in the results. Moreover, no quantitative data were available on patient-centered effectiveness outcomes, such as OHRQoL or patient satisfaction. Consequently, the present analysis primarily reflects efficiency-related aspects of cost-efficiency rather than treatment effectiveness in terms of functional or patient-reported outcomes. A further limitation is the exclusion of initial investment costs associated with digital technologies, which can substantially affect cost-efficiency analyses. Additionally, laboratory costs (independent of clinicians' qualifications) were variably reported across studies, with unclear inclusion of equipment expenses such as milling machines, possibly contributing to heterogeneity.

CONCLUSIONS

Based on the currently available studies, there is no robust evidence supporting a cost-efficiency advantage of digital over conventional workflows for RCD fabrication. Therefore, workflow selection should be guided by clinical feasibility, clinician and dental technician experience, and resource availability rather than assumptions of superior cost-efficiency.
