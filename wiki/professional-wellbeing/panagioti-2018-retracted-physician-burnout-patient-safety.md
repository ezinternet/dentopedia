## ⚠️ RETRACTION NOTICE
This paper has been **RETRACTED** by JAMA Internal Medicine (a correction was also issued). The original PDF still circulates and is widely cited; this wiki page exists explicitly to flag the retraction. **Do not cite the effect estimates as reliable evidence.** Use this page only to:
1. Avoid accidentally citing the withdrawn results in your own work.
2. Recognize the paper when it shows up in others' references.

## Summary
Panagioti et al. originally pooled 47 observational studies (42,473 physicians) and reported that physician burnout was associated with roughly 2-fold odds of unsafe care, unprofessional behavior, and lower patient satisfaction. Depersonalization was identified as the most consequential MBI subscale, and the burnout-unprofessionalism link was strongest in early-career physicians.

**This meta-analysis has since been retracted.** The original PDF still shows up frequently in literature searches and downstream citations, so the wiki keeps it as a flagged entry rather than silently dropping it. Effect estimates from this paper should not appear in any of our derived clinical or operational documents.

## What We Can NOT Use From This Paper
- The pooled OR ~2.0 for unsafe care
- The depersonalization-as-strongest-predictor claim
- The early-career amplification finding
- Any specific numerical estimate

## What This Paper Does Tell Us (Methodologically)
- High-profile retractions in burnout literature happened — be skeptical of single-study effect estimates
- Observational design cannot disambiguate burnout → bad outcomes from bad-environment → burnout
- The general direction (burnout correlates with adverse care indicators) is plausible from other sources, but quantification requires non-retracted evidence

## Methodology (Original — Now Withdrawn)
SR+MA across MEDLINE, EMBASE, PsycInfo, CINAHL through Oct 2017. Two independent reviewers. Random-effects models, formal heterogeneity (I²) and publication bias testing. Quantitative observational studies only.
