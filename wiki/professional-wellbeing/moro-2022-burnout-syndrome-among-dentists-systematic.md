## One-line Summary
MBI-only SR+MA (37 studies, 31 meta-analyzed) of dentist burnout: full-syndrome (all 3 subscales) prevalence ~13% (95% CI 0.6–21%, I²=97.1%) but emotional exhaustion (EE) alone ~28% — EE is far more common and the earliest clinical warning sign, making it the operational early-detection target.

## Summary
Moro et al. pooled 37 studies (31 meta-analyzed) using only Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) data — the deliberate exclusion of non-MBI instruments improves comparability but tightens the sample. Three numbers matter:

- **Full burnout syndrome (all 3 subscales positive): ~13%** — the headline number media usually quotes
- **Emotional exhaustion (EE) alone: ~28%** — far more common, and clinically the earliest warning sign
- **Heterogeneity I² up to 97%** — geographic and specialty differences are large enough that the 13% point estimate is not directly transferable to any specific country/setting

The practical takeaway is that "burnout prevalence" depends entirely on case definition. If you require all 3 MBI subscales positive (the strict definition), prevalence is ~13%. If you ask "what proportion of dentists report high emotional exhaustion?", it's ~28%. For early detection in a clinic, the EE subscale is the operational target.

## Key Contributions
- First MBI-only SR+MA in dentistry — eliminates instrument heterogeneity at the cost of sample size
- Separates **syndrome-level** prevalence from **subscale-level** prevalence — exposes the misleading nature of single headline numbers
- Reports both prevalence proportions and continuous MBI means, allowing comparison to other professions
- Documents that **no included study met all JBI critical appraisal criteria** — flag for citation context

## Methodology
PECO question: "What is the overall burnout syndrome prevalence in dentists according to MBI?" Six databases plus gray literature, no language/period restriction, search date 2021-01-27. MBI-only inclusion criterion. Joanna Briggs Institute critical appraisal applied — no study passed all criteria. Random-effects proportion meta-analysis in Stata 13.0.

## Results
| Outcome | Prevalence (95% CI) | I² |
|---|---|---|
| Overall burnout (all 3 subscales) | 13% (0.6–21%) | 97.1% |
| Emotional exhaustion (EE) — any | 28% (24–32%) | 20.7% |
| Depersonalization (DP) — any | 18% (8–28%) | 85.6% |
| Reduced personal accomplishment (PA) — any | 10% (8–13%) | 0% |
| High EE | 25% (19–31%) | 92.6% |
| High DP | 18% (10–26%) | 96.6% |
| Low PA | 32% (20–45%) | 97.9% |

Continuous MBI means: EE 17.90, DP 6.93, PA 34.69.

The PA subscale shows I²=0%, suggesting near-uniform low PA prevalence (~10%) across studies — depersonalization and exhaustion vary by context, but reduced accomplishment seems context-independent.

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For an owner-operator monitoring team wellbeing:
- **Watch EE first.** It's the most prevalent subscale, has the lowest heterogeneity, and is the operational entry point of burnout. A simple periodic EE-subscale check (9 items) is more useful than the full MBI.
- The headline "13% have burnout" understates risk. **A quarter to a third of dentists carry high exhaustion**, even if they don't meet the strict 3-subscale criterion.
- Apply the [[professional-wellbeing/plessas-2022-mental-health-wellbeing-interventions-dental]] framework: prevalence (this paper) tells you the burden; intervention literature (Plessas) is what's actionable, and it's underdeveloped at the systems level.

## Open Questions
- Why is PA so homogeneous (I²=0%) while EE/DP vary widely? Suggests PA may be a trait-level construct in dentistry rather than environment-driven.
- Pre-COVID data dominates — post-COVID estimates may shift (see Negucioiu 2024).
- Specialty subgroup analysis is sparse; oral surgeons, pediatric dentists, endodontists may have different risk profiles.
