## One-line Summary
Systematic review (narrative synthesis) of mental-health/wellbeing interventions in the dental sector, framed by the primary/secondary/tertiary prevention taxonomy: the primary-prevention (organizational/systems-level) cell is near-empty while evidence concentrates on individual-level secondary prevention (mindfulness, CBT, psychoeducation) — exposing a mismatch between the structural stressors dentists name and the individual-coping interventions deployed.

## Summary
Plessas et al. (UK Peninsula Dental School) systematically reviewed interventions deployed to protect mental health and wellbeing in the dental sector. The headline finding is a near-empty **primary prevention** evidence base — almost no studies test changes to workload, regulatory environment, autonomy, or workplace design, which are the stressors UK surveys repeatedly identify as the heaviest. What evidence does exist is almost entirely **secondary prevention** at the individual level: psychoeducation, mindfulness, CBT-style resilience training. Tertiary prevention (treatment, return-to-work) is also under-researched.

The authors argue this mismatch is the core problem: dentistry treats burnout as a personal coping deficit while the upstream drivers sit at the organisation and system level.

## Key Contributions
- Frames dental wellbeing evidence using the **primary / secondary / tertiary prevention** taxonomy and exposes the imbalance.
- Articulates the gap between **what UK dentists name as stressors** (regulation, NHS conditions, fear of litigation, working environment) and **what current interventions target** (individual coping).
- Recommends translation of organisational-level interventions from medicine and nursing into dentistry-specific designs.
- Frames undergraduate dental education as the natural entry point for early recognition and de-stigmatisation.

## Methodology
Systematic review following PRISMA-style search across major databases. Inclusion: any workplace-deployable intervention targeting mental health/wellbeing outcomes in dental professionals. Narrative synthesis (no meta-analysis) because of heterogeneity in intervention types, populations, and outcome measures. Quality of included studies was variable; most were small, single-arm, short follow-up.

## Results
- **Primary prevention**: a near-empty cell. Almost no organisational or systems-level intervention studies exist for dentistry.
- **Secondary prevention**: dominant category — mindfulness-based stress reduction, CBT, psychoeducation, resilience curricula. Mostly delivered to individuals already symptomatic or at-risk.
- **Tertiary prevention**: scarce. Little on burnout treatment pathways, return-to-work, or peer support after a wellbeing episode.
- Cost-effectiveness data: essentially absent.
- Outcome measures heterogeneous (MBI, PHQ-9, GAD-7, Warwick-Edinburgh, etc.), limiting comparability.

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a clinic operator, this review is a reminder that the literature mostly offers tools to help an individual cope with stressors — not tools to reduce the stressors themselves. Practical implications:
- Buying in mindfulness/EAP programs is consistent with current evidence, but treats symptoms, not causes.
- The high-leverage interventions (workload management, scheduling autonomy, supportive regulation, fair complaint handling) lack RCT evidence — judgement-based pending more research.
- Early-career staff (interns, new graduates) are the highest-yield audience for preventive education.
