## One-line Summary
Systematic review (n=12 UK studies, 11 of medium-to-high quality) of health and wellbeing in Dental Care Professionals (DCPs — hygienists, therapists, clinical dental technicians, orthodontic therapists): evidence is sparse and skewed to hygienists/therapists, with clinical dental technicians and orthodontic therapists nearly absent; determinants mirror the dentist macro/meso/micro framework (job satisfaction and career most-studied), but overall wellbeing is essentially unmeasured directly.

## Summary
Most dentist wellbeing research targets dentists. Kaki et al. fill the parallel gap for DCPs (Dental Care Professionals — hygienists, therapists, clinical dental technicians, orthodontic therapists), who outnumber dentists in any clinic. The headline finding is that only 12 UK studies exist, and they mostly look at hygienists and therapists; clinical dental technicians and orthodontic therapists are essentially absent from the literature.

Where data exist, the determinant structure mirrors what's seen for dentists: macro factors (regulation, healthcare system), meso factors (job specification, workplace), micro factors (personal, relationships, career stage). Job satisfaction and career trajectory dominate as the most-studied dimensions.

The bigger message: we don't yet have good data on the **overall health and wellbeing** of UK DCPs — just fragments on specific determinants.

## Key Contributions
- Makes the DCP workforce visible in the burnout/wellbeing literature for the first time at SR level
- Confirms determinant framework is transferable from dentist to DCP populations
- Quality-appraised 12 studies, 11 of which are 4–5 star — the evidence is at least not noisy
- Identifies clinical dental technicians and orthodontic therapists as nearly invisible in the literature

## Methodology
Seven-database systematic review limited to UK clinical DCPs. MMAT used for quality. Narrative synthesis. PRISMA flow.

## Results
- **n = 12 included studies** (11 medium-to-high quality, 1 low)
- **10 studies** on dental therapists and/or hygienists
- **1 study each** on orthodontic therapists and clinical dental technicians
- Primary explored factors: job satisfaction, professional careers
- Determinants align with Salazar 2019 framework at macro/meso/micro levels
- No coherent picture of overall wellbeing measured directly

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a clinic with 20 hygienists, this is the closest thing to direct evidence about your DCP team's wellbeing:
- Determinants you can act on (meso level): job specification clarity, workload predictability, workplace culture, relationship with dentists
- The literature won't tell you "X intervention will reduce hygienist burnout by Y%" — that data doesn't exist yet
- Job satisfaction is the most-validated proxy outcome; periodic anonymous job satisfaction surveys are a defensible proxy until burnout-specific data arrive

## Open Questions
- What's the burnout prevalence in hygienists vs. dentists? Different exposure profiles (more repetitive work, less autonomy, less litigation risk) — should produce different patterns.
- Clinical dental technicians and orthodontic therapists need basic descriptive data before intervention research is possible.
- Whether macro-level UK factors (NHS contract changes) hit DCPs differently from dentists is unstudied.
