## Summary
Long et al. is the independent replication that strengthens the Moro 2022 prevalence estimate. Different research team, different search end date (Oct 2021), different study set (15 instead of 31) — and they land on the same 13% pooled prevalence. The 95% CI is wide (6–23%) so the point estimate carries real uncertainty, but two independent SR+MAs agreeing on the headline number is the strongest evidence we have for dentist burnout magnitude.

The three subgroup findings are the real value-add over Moro 2022:

1. **Geography matters.** Europe shows the highest prevalence, Asia intermediate, Americas lowest. This is consistent with European healthcare-system pressure narratives but should not be over-interpreted from prevalence data alone.

2. **Study design biases the estimate.** Longitudinal studies (which can catch episodic burnout missed by single-time-point surveys) report higher prevalence than cross-sectional. Most published prevalence numbers come from cross-sectional surveys and therefore **underestimate** true burden.

3. **Temporal trend is downward.** Burnout prevalence in the most recent decade was significantly lower than the previous decade. (Note: search ended Oct 2021, so the full pandemic impact may not be captured — see Negucioiu 2024 for COVID-era data.)

## Key Contributions
- Independent corroboration of the ~13% headline prevalence figure
- First SR+MA to publish geographic subgroup analysis at the regional level
- Documents the design bias: cross-sectional underestimates
- Identifies a downward decade-over-decade trend (counterintuitive to the "epidemic" narrative)

## Methodology
PubMed, PsycINFO, Embase, Cochrane, Web of Science — inception through Oct 28, 2021. Random-effects model, forest plot synthesis. Subgroup analyses by geographic region, study design, and era. Standard PRISMA workflow.

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
- The 13% prevalence headline is now **convergently** supported (Moro + Long) — defensible to cite in internal wellbeing program docs.
- For South Korean (Asia) practice: expect prevalence somewhere between the European high and the Americas low — Asia falls in the middle in Long's analysis.
- If your clinic surveys EE once a year via a single-shot questionnaire, **you are likely underestimating** the true exposure (design bias). Multi-wave surveys are more sensitive.
- The downward trend is consistent with rising awareness and intervention deployment, but causation cannot be inferred from prevalence alone.

## Open Questions
- Why Europe > Americas? Hypothesized to be NHS-style regulatory pressure (UK), but unproven from prevalence data.
- The pre-/post-pandemic decade gradient inverts when COVID-era data are added — needs reanalysis.
- East Asian (Korea/Japan) subgroup not analyzed separately; mainland China data may dominate the Asia bucket.
