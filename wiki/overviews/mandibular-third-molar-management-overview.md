## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 결론: 하악 사랑니(Mandibular Third Molar, M3M) 관리는 본질적으로 **예방적 발치 (prophylactic extraction) vs 치료적 발치 (therapeutic extraction) vs 보존·관찰 (retention/monitoring)** 의사결정 문제다 — 4편 종합(Ye 2021, Sun 2026, Schmidt 2021, Al-Aroomi 2025).
> - 매복 (impaction)은 측정 가능한 병리 부담을 만든다(Ye 2021, 후향 432개): 치관주위염 (pericoronitis) 82.4%, 제2대구치 (M2M) 원심 우식 18.8%, M2M 원심 치주병변 14.8%. 이 수치가 적응증 판단의 토대.
> - 매복 형태가 병리를 예측한다(Ye 2021): 연조직·수직 매복 → 치관주위염 (p<0.0001); 근심경사·얕은(position A) → M2M 원심 우식 (p<0.0001); 근심·수평·깊은 매복 → M2M 원심 치주병변 (p<0.0001). 즉 "어떤 매복이냐"가 "무슨 병이 생기냐"를 가른다.
> - 연령 효과(Ye 2021): 청년층은 치관주위염 우세, ≥30세부터 우식·치주병변 누적(p<0.0001) → 깊은 매복은 지연 가능하나 무기한 보존은 금물.
> - 전문가 합의 적응증 3단계(Sun 2026, 중국 다기관 합의문): ① 확정적 병리 적응증(재발성 치관주위염·간극감염, 우식, M2M 치주손상, 낭종/종양, 중등도↑ 치근흡수), ② 치료적 발치(교정·악교정·TMD·방사선/항암/면역요법 전·임신 전), ③ 예방적 발치(현재 무증상이나 위험 매복) — 3단계는 **공유의사결정 (shared decision-making)** 강조, >35세·완전골매복·고위험은 관찰 관리.
> - 급성 적응증 1순위 = 치관주위염(Schmidt 2021): 일차치료는 **국소 세척·변연제거 (local irrigation/debridement)** 이며 항생제는 감염 확산·전신 증상(발열·개구장애·연하곤란)에만 한정. 그런데 치과의사 약 75%가 항생제 처방 → 치과 항생제 과처방의 핵심 원인.
> - 치관주위염 단계 치료(Schmidt 2021): 1차 = 국소요법+NSAIDs(무항생제); 재발(첫 에피소드) = 판절제술 (operculectomy); 2번째 이상 에피소드 = 발치(NICE). 중증/확산 시 메트로니다졸+아목시실린.
> - 발치 후 morbidity 저감(Al-Aroomi 2025, 11 RCT SR): **농축성장인자 (Concentrated Growth Factor, CGF)** 소켓 적용 → 통증 75%·진통제 소비 100%·방사선 골충전 80%·M2M 원심 치주지표 100% 개선. 단 부기·개구장애·치조골염 (alveolar osteitis) 효과는 불명확, 이질성으로 메타분석 불가.
> - 임상 takeaway: 매복 형태로 위험을 층화(Ye) → 3단계 적응증으로 발치/보존 결정(Sun) → 치관주위염은 국소우선·항생제 절제(Schmidt) → 발치 시 CGF로 회복 morbidity 보강(Al-Aroomi). **하치조신경 (Inferior Alveolar Nerve, IAN) 손상 위험·술식 선택**은 별도 프로토콜 참조([[overviews/third-molar-ian-risk-protocol]]).
> - 주의: 근거 위계가 섞여 있다 — Ye는 단일기관 후향(증상환자 의뢰 편향), Sun은 GRADE 미적용 전문가 합의, Al-Aroomi는 메타분석 불가 SR. 따라서 개별 임상 판단이 우선.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 4 papers framing mandibular third molar (M3M) management as a prophylactic-vs-therapeutic-vs-retention decision: impaction carries a measurable pathology burden (Ye 2021 — pericoronitis 82.4%, second-molar distal caries 18.8%, periodontal pathology 14.8%, with impaction morphology predicting which pathology occurs), a Chinese expert consensus frames a 3-tier extraction-indication framework (Sun 2026), pericoronitis (the leading acute indication) is treated locally first with antibiotics reserved for spreading/systemic infection despite ~75% of dentists over-prescribing (Schmidt 2021), and post-extraction morbidity can be modulated by Concentrated Growth Factor (CGF improves pain, analgesic use, bone fill, and distal periodontal parameters; Al-Aroomi 2025).

## 한줄요약

4편 종합 — 하악 사랑니 관리는 예방적/치료적 발치 vs 보존 결정 문제: 매복은 측정 가능한 병리 부담(치관주위염 82.4%, M2M 원심 우식 18.8%; Ye 2021)을 만들고 매복 형태가 병리를 예측하며, 전문가 합의가 3단계 발치 적응증을 제시하고(Sun 2026), 치관주위염은 국소우선·항생제 절제(치과의사 75% 과처방; Schmidt 2021), 발치 후 morbidity는 CGF로 저감(통증·골충전·치주지표 개선; Al-Aroomi 2025).

## Summary

Mandibular third molar (M3M) management is, at its core, a **prophylactic-versus-therapeutic extraction decision** layered on a third option — informed retention with surveillance. This synthesis assembles four papers that, together, supply the four inputs a clinician needs for that decision: (1) *what pathology burden impaction actually carries and which impaction patterns predict which disease* (Ye 2021), (2) *a framework for when to remove versus retain/monitor* (Sun 2026), (3) *how to manage pericoronitis — the most common acute indication — and where antibiotics do and do not belong* (Schmidt 2021), and (4) *how to reduce post-extraction morbidity once removal is chosen* (Al-Aroomi 2025). The orthogonal question of inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) injury risk and surgical technique selection (coronectomy, imaging-staged risk assessment) is covered in a companion overview rather than duplicated here.

**The pathology burden — and its morphology dependence (Ye 2021).** Ye et al. (2021), a single-center retrospective review of 432 M3Ms in 262 Shanghai patients, is the first study to assess all three major M3M-associated pathologies in one cohort using a unified classification (impaction depth, Pell & Gregory position, Winter angulation). Pericoronitis was overwhelmingly the dominant pathology (82.4%), followed by second-molar (M2M) distal caries (18.8%) and M2M distal periodontal pathology (14.8%); 64 teeth carried two or three pathologies simultaneously. The clinically pivotal finding is that **different impaction features predict different pathologies** — so the impaction pattern is itself a risk-stratification tool. Soft-tissue and vertically angulated impactions drove pericoronitis (p=0.0097 and p<0.0001). Shallow (position A) mesio-angular impactions drove M2M distal caries (p<0.0001). Deep (position B/C) mesio-angular and horizontal impactions drove M2M distal periodontal pathology including external root resorption (p<0.0001). Pathology also accumulates with age: pericoronitis dominated younger patients, but caries and periodontal lesions rose significantly in patients ≥30 (p<0.0001). The cost of indefinite retention is concrete — 10 M2Ms required extraction (5 for degree III mobility, 5 for uncurable root caries), clustering in soft-tissue, position A/B, mesio-angular/horizontal impactions at a mean patient age of 44.

**The indication framework (Sun 2026).** Sun et al. (2026), a Chinese multi-center expert consensus published in *Int J Oral Sci*, organizes extraction indications into **three tiers**: (1) *definite pathologic indicators* — recurrent pericoronitis/space infections, caries (in the M3M or the adjacent tooth), periodontal damage to the M2M, cyst/tumor, moderate-to-severe root resorption; (2) *therapeutic extraction* — for orthodontic, orthognathic, TMD, pre-radiotherapy/chemotherapy/immunotherapy, or pre-pregnancy (with deep periodontal involvement) indications; and (3) *prophylactic removal* of potentially risky but currently asymptomatic teeth (partially erupted mesial/horizontal, soft-tissue-impacted unerupted, or complete bony impaction with distal alveolar bone deficiency reaching the crown). The third tier is explicitly governed by **shared decision-making**, and the consensus favors observational management for patients over 35 with complete bony impaction and high surgical risk. The consensus epidemiology corroborates Ye: pericoronitis ~82.4% mandibular, impacted M3Ms cause 50–70% of fascial space infections, ~19.5% develop distal M2M caries, ~5% develop odontogenic cysts/tumors. Notably, the consensus explicitly leaves the optimal extraction *timing* for asymptomatic teeth unresolved — a deliberate acknowledgment that the prophylactic tier is a judgment call, not a rule.

**The leading acute indication and its antibiotic trap (Schmidt 2021).** Pericoronitis is the most common acute reason a M3M presents for care (95% of pericoronitis is the lower third molar; peak age 20–29). Schmidt et al. (2021) — a dual narrative-review-plus-systematic-review (11 studies) — establishes the evidence-based management hierarchy and exposes the practice gap. **First-line treatment is local: irrigation and debridement of the pericoronal stagnation space, plus NSAIDs for pain — not systemic antibiotics.** Antibiotics (metronidazole + amoxicillin; erythromycin if penicillin-allergic) are reserved only for *spread of infection or systemic involvement* (fever, trismus, dysphagia, lymphadenopathy, Ludwig's angina risk → hospital transfer). For prevention: operculectomy (± gingivoplasty) is preferred for a first recurrent episode, and extraction is indicated from the second episode onward (NICE) and should not be delayed once decided. Yet Schmidt's systematic review found ~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics for pericoronitis (>50% of patients receive them in cohort studies), making pericoronitis a top-4 driver of inappropriate dental antibiotic use — driven by diagnostic uncertainty, time pressure, and patient expectation rather than clinical need.

**Reducing post-extraction morbidity (Al-Aroomi 2025).** Once removal is chosen, post-extraction morbidity can be modulated with biologics. Al-Aroomi et al. (2025), a PROSPERO-registered systematic review of 11 RCTs, evaluated **Concentrated Growth Factor (CGF)** — a denser-fibrin platelet concentrate (Medifuge protocol) — placed in the M3M socket. CGF consistently improved pain (6/8 studies, 75%), analgesic consumption (2/2, 100%), radiographic bone fill (4/5, 80%), distal-M2M periodontal parameters (4/4, 100%), and soft-tissue healing (2/2, 100%). Effects on swelling (4/7, 57%), trismus (2/4, 50%), and alveolar osteitis (1/3, 33%) were inconsistent. High heterogeneity in CGF preparation and outcomes precluded meta-analysis, so the evidence is directionally favorable but not definitive. The periodontal benefit is mechanistically attractive because distal-M2M periodontal damage is exactly one of the pathologies impaction causes (Ye) and one of Sun's definite indications — CGF may help protect the very structure surgery puts at risk. Sun's consensus independently recommends CGF (alone or with bone substitute) for sockets with preoperative distal alveolar bone deficiency, converging with Al-Aroomi.

### The thesis, assembled

The four papers form a coherent decision pipeline. **Ye 2021** quantifies the pathology burden and shows impaction morphology is itself a risk-stratifier. **Sun 2026** converts that burden into a tiered indication framework that distinguishes definite, therapeutic, and prophylactic removal, with shared decision-making and surveillance for low-risk asymptomatic teeth. **Schmidt 2021** governs the most common acute presentation — local therapy first, antibiotics only for spread, extraction from the second episode — and warns against the field's antibiotic over-reflex. **Al-Aroomi 2025** addresses the back end — once you extract, CGF can reduce the morbidity, with a periodontal benefit that directly targets one of the pathologies impaction caused in the first place.

### Indication framework (synthesized)

| Tier | Trigger | Action | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Definite pathologic** | Recurrent pericoronitis / space infection; M3M or adjacent-tooth caries; M2M distal periodontal damage / root resorption; cyst or tumor | **Extract** (do not delay) | Sun 2026; Ye 2021 |
| **Therapeutic** | Orthodontic / orthognathic / TMD need; pre-radiotherapy, chemo-, or immunotherapy; pre-pregnancy with deep periodontal involvement | **Extract** as part of the broader treatment plan | Sun 2026 |
| **Prophylactic (asymptomatic, risky)** | Partially erupted mesial/horizontal; soft-tissue-impacted unerupted; complete bony impaction with distal bone deficiency to the crown | **Shared decision-making**; remove if risk profile high, otherwise monitor — esp. >35 y / high surgical risk → observe | Sun 2026 |
| **Retain + surveillance** | Asymptomatic, low-risk, deep/bony impaction; high surgical risk | **Monitor** (periodic exam/imaging) — but note caries/periodontal pathology accumulates after age 30, so retention is not indefinite | Sun 2026; Ye 2021 |

### Impaction morphology → expected pathology (risk-stratification, Ye 2021)

| Impaction feature | Predicted pathology | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Soft-tissue + **vertical** angulation | **Pericoronitis** | p=0.0097 / p<0.0001 |
| **Mesio-angular**, shallow (Pell & Gregory **position A**) | **M2M distal caries** | p<0.0001 |
| **Mesio-angular / horizontal**, deep (position B/C) | **M2M distal periodontal pathology** (incl. external root resorption) | p<0.0001 |
| Age ≥30 (any deep impaction retained) | Rising caries + periodontal lesions | p<0.0001 |

### Pericoronitis management ladder (Schmidt 2021)

| Scenario | Treatment | Antibiotics? |
|---|---|---|
| Mild/moderate pericoronitis | Local irrigation + debridement; NSAIDs | **No** |
| Recurrent (1st episode, not severe) | Operculectomy ± gingivoplasty | No |
| 2nd or subsequent episode | Extraction (don't delay once decided) | No |
| Infection spread / systemic signs (fever, trismus, dysphagia) | Local + **metronidazole + amoxicillin** (erythromycin if PCN-allergic); hospital transfer if airway threatened | **Yes — reserved** |

**Practice-gap caveat:** ~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics for pericoronitis (Schmidt 2021) — the evidence supports local therapy first; reserve antibiotics for genuine spread/systemic involvement.

### Post-extraction morbidity-reduction note (Al-Aroomi 2025)

Placing **Concentrated Growth Factor (CGF)** in the M3M socket reduces pain (75% of studies), analgesic consumption (100%), radiographic bone fill (80%), and distal-M2M periodontal parameters (100%) — the periodontal benefit directly protecting the M2M structure that impaction endangers (Ye 2021) and that Sun 2026 likewise targets with CGF for sockets with preoperative distal bone deficiency. Effects on **swelling, trismus, and alveolar osteitis are uncertain**; heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis, so treat CGF as a directionally-favorable adjunct, not an established standard.

### Evidence-weight caveat

The four papers sit at mixed evidence levels: Ye 2021 is a single-center **retrospective** review with referral (symptomatic-patient) selection bias and no community-prevalence data; Sun 2026 is an **expert consensus** without GRADE grading or resolution of asymptomatic-timing controversy; Schmidt 2021 is a narrative review + descriptive **SR** (no meta-analysis); Al-Aroomi 2025 is a **SR** that could not pool data. Individual clinical judgment — including IAN-risk assessment ([[overviews/third-molar-ian-risk-protocol]]) — governs the final decision.
