> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - **거대형태(Macrogeometry)의 역할 재정립**: 테이퍼드(tapered) vs 실린더형(cylindrical) 임플란트 형태는 1차 안정성(Primary Stability)에 영향을 미치지만, 6주 초기 치유기 연구(Mourão 2025)에서 수술 술식(surgical technique)이 임플란트 형태보다 더 강력한 결정 인자임이 확인됨.
> - **삽입 토크(Maximum Insertion Torque, MIT) vs ISQ 괴리**: 테이퍼드는 더 높은 MIT를 보였지만, 6주째 공명주파수분석(Resonance Frequency Analysis, RFA) ISQ는 실린더형이 더 높았음 — 기계적 안정성과 생물학적 안정성(Secondary Stability)이 반드시 일치하지 않음.
> - **넥 디자인(Neck Design)의 임상 효과 미입증**: 삼각형 넥(Triangular Neck, TN) vs 원형 넥(Round Neck, RN) 비교 12개월 RCT(n=36, Chrcanovic 2025)에서 변연골소실(Marginal Bone Loss, MBL), 탐침깊이(Probing Depth, PD), 협측골폭(Buccal Bone Width, BBW), 연조직 두께(Soft Tissue Thickness, STT) 모두 유의한 차이 없음.
> - **골유착 6대 전제조건(Albrektsson 1981)**: 재료·디자인·표면처리·골 상태·수술 술식·하중 조건 — 이 고전적 프레임워크는 현재도 맥락을 유지하며, 거대형태는 '디자인' 요소 하나에 불과함.
> - **나사 설계(Thread Design)의 이중성**: 테이퍼드 형태와 공격적 나사는 1차 안정성을 높이지만, 유한요소해석(Finite Element Analysis, FEA)상 crestal cortical bone 응력이 실린더형보다 높아 하중 단계에서 골소실 위험이 있음 (Hussein 2019).
> - **골질(Bone Quality)별 나사 깊이 최적화**: D3·D4 저밀도골에서 thread depth 증가 시 crestal 응력 ~40% 감소, 변위 ≥9% 감소 효과 — D2 고밀도골에서는 효과 미미 (Chang 2024).
> - **즉시식립(Immediate Implant Placement) 맥락의 한계**: 넥 디자인 RCT(Chrcanovic 2025)는 즉시식립 + 즉시 임시보철 조건에서 수행 — 비즉시식립이나 저밀도골로 결과를 외삽하기 어려움.
> - **동등성 검증 부재**: 두 RCT 모두 우월성(superiority) 설계로, 동등성(equivalence) 또는 비열등성(non-inferiority) 검정은 미수행 — 소규모 차이가 존재할 가능성을 배제할 수 없음.
> - **임상 결론**: 거대형태 선택은 골질을 먼저 평가하고 수술 술식 표준화가 우선; 저밀도골에서는 테이퍼드 + 깊은 나사 설계가 합리적 조합; 넥 디자인은 현재 증거만으로는 선택 기준이 되기 어려움.
> - **근거 수준(Evidence Level)**: 1차 논문 중 RCT 2개는 초록만 확보(abstract-only) — 절대값 데이터 부재; FEA 2개는 생체 내 적용에 외삽 한계 있음.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of six papers (2 RCTs, 1 landmark review, 1 narrative review, 2 FEAs) on implant macrogeometry: surgical technique outweighs implant shape for early stability; tapered designs raise insertion torque but not 6-week ISQ; neither triangular nor round neck design demonstrates superiority at 12 months; thread depth matters most in low-density bone (D3/D4), where deeper threads cut crestal stress ~40%.

## 한줄요약

임플란트 거대형태 6편 종합 (RCT 2, 기념비적 논문 1, 서술적 고찰 1, FEA 2): 수술 술식이 형태보다 초기 안정성에 더 큰 영향; 테이퍼드는 삽입 토크 높지만 6주 ISQ는 실린더가 우세; 넥 디자인 차이는 12개월 MBL·연조직 결과에 미반영; D3·D4 저밀도골에서 나사 깊이 증가가 crestal 응력 ~40% 절감.

## Thesis

Implant macrogeometry — taper, neck form, and thread geometry — modulates primary stability and stress distribution in bone, but its clinical impact on longer-term biological outcomes (ISQ at 6 weeks, MBL and soft tissue at 12 months) is smaller than the implant-selection literature commonly implies, with surgical technique consistently identified as the dominant variable. The stability-stress trade-off inherent to tapered designs (higher insertion torque, higher crestal stress) means that macrogeometry selection should be bone-quality-driven rather than reflexive. Neck design, at least the triangular-vs-round comparison tested to date, has failed to demonstrate superiority for any hard or soft tissue outcome at 12 months, leaving the choice currently evidence-neutral.

## Tapered vs Cylindrical — What the Evidence Shows

The debate between tapered and cylindrical macrogeometry has sharpened considerably with randomized evidence, but the picture is more nuanced than the promotional literature suggests.

**Primary stability and insertion torque.** The 2025 critical appraisal by Mourão & Dos Santos Pereira, summarizing a parallel-arm RCT (Charoenniwassakul et al., *J Clin Periodontol* 2025), confirms that tapered implants achieve significantly higher maximum insertion torque (MIT) at placement. This is mechanically intuitive: the converging taper compresses trabecular bone laterally as the implant seats, generating a press-fit that acts like a wedge. Heimes et al. (2023) attribute this to the increased contact area that a conical body achieves relative to a cylinder of identical length and apical diameter.

**The stability dissociation: torque ≠ ISQ.** Despite higher MIT, cylindrical implants showed higher ISQ and IST (percussion analysis) at six weeks in the same RCT. This dissociation matters clinically: it means that the mechanical energy stored by taper-compression at insertion does not translate into superior resonance-frequency-assessed secondary stability once early healing begins. The same torque-versus-ISQ decoupling appears with thread/body-feature geometry: Bhattacharya (Chanquini) et al. (2025), a split-mouth RCT (n=18), found that modified-macrogeometry implants with healing chambers seated at significantly *lower* insertion torque yet reached ISQ values statistically indistinguishable from conventional implants at every timepoint to 90 days, with lower early peri-implant TNF-α — reinforcing that insertion torque is a press-fit measure, not a reliable proxy for RFA-assessed stability or biological healing. The transient stability dip — a universal feature of early healing — was present in both groups, and both reached stability adequate for early loading in good-quality healed bone.

**Surgical technique as the dominant variable.** The commentary flags a critical methodological flaw: osteotomy preparation was not standardized between the tapered and cylindrical arms, meaning that the outcome differences cannot be attributed to implant macrogeometry alone. The authors explicitly conclude that surgical technique — specifically drilling under-preparation relative to implant diameter — drives primary stability more than implant shape. This is consistent with Heimes et al. (2023), who frame drilling modification as a parallel stability lever alongside macrogeometry.

**The crestal stress trade-off.** Hussein et al. (2019) provide a counterweight from FEA: across all bone types, tapered bodies generate higher peak von Mises stress at the crestal cortical bone around the implant neck compared to cylindrical bodies. This is the biomechanical cost of the taper advantage. Higher crestal stress during function is a recognized risk factor for early marginal bone loss (MBL) — which means the implant type that achieves the best initial mechanical grip may carry a higher loading-phase MBL risk, all else being equal. Chang et al. (2024) show that thread depth optimization (deeper threads in D3/D4 bone) can partially mitigate this by redistributing load away from the crest, reducing crestal cortical stress by approximately 40% in low-density bone.

**Practical synthesis.** In good-quality healed bone (D1/D2), tapered and cylindrical implants perform equivalently for clinical outcomes, and the choice should be guided primarily by drilling protocol and operator familiarity. In lower bone density (D3/D4), tapered geometry with aggressive thread depth offers a meaningful mechanical advantage during healing — but this advantage should be paired with careful loading protocol to manage the higher functional crestal stress. The Mourão (2025) commentary explicitly notes that conclusions do not extend to immediate loading, regenerated bone, or low-density bone, underscoring these as contexts where the design choice becomes more consequential.

## Neck Design — What the Evidence Shows

Implant neck (collar) design — the coronal portion of the implant body at or above the bone crest — has attracted attention as a potential lever for preserving crestal bone and maintaining peri-implant soft tissue architecture. The triangular cross-section neck, hypothesized to better distribute occlusal forces and improve soft tissue support compared to the conventional round collar, was tested prospectively in the 12-month RCT by Porta-Ferrer et al. (2025; filed under Chrcanovic-2025 in this wiki).

**RCT findings: no superiority demonstrated.** Thirty-six patients requiring immediate anterior maxillary placement were randomized to triangular neck (TN) or round neck (RN) implants, all with immediate provisionalization. At 12 months, no statistically significant between-group differences were found for any outcome: MBL, probing depth, buccal bone width (CBCT, 4 months), soft tissue thickness, bleeding on probing, or crestal width. Survival was 88.9% in both groups (two failures per group).

**Limitations of the null result.** The study was designed and powered for superiority, not equivalence or non-inferiority — meaning the null finding does not establish that the designs are equivalent; it only establishes that neither reached a statistically detectable superiority threshold. With n=18 per group, meaningful clinical differences may exist but remain undetected. The authors acknowledge this explicitly.

**The immediate placement context.** The study population was entirely anterior maxillary immediate placement with immediate provisionalization — a high-esthetic, high-complexity clinical scenario. Results may not transfer to posterior healed-bone placements or to different loading protocols.

**Clinical take.** At present, neck cross-section geometry (triangular vs round) is an evidence-neutral choice: no clinically relevant advantage has been demonstrated for either design at one year. The Albrektsson (1981) framework is instructive here — implant design (factor #2) was originally specified as "exact bone-implant fit" with threaded geometry for maximum contact; the coronal collar geometry was not a primary differentiator in the original osseointegration concept. The burden of proof for neck design innovation lies with future adequately-powered, long-term trials.

## Osseointegration Criteria (Albrektsson 6 Prerequisites)

Albrektsson et al. (1981) codified the six prerequisites for achieving and maintaining osseointegration from the Brånemark clinical series (~3000 implants, 400 patients, up to 15 years). Each maps directly onto current implant macrogeometry considerations:

1. **Implant material** — Non-alloyed titanium, tissue-tolerant and corrosion-resistant. Modern implants use titanium alloys (Ti-6Al-4V) or commercially pure titanium; the fundamental oxide-layer argument remains. Macrogeometry does not alter material.

2. **Implant design** — Defined as exact bone-implant fit; cylindrical threaded geometry was specified as maximizing contact and minimizing micromovement. In 1981, taper was not evaluated — the evidence base then supported only machined cylindrical screws. Today's tapered implants were not available to Albrektsson; the thread and fit rationale still applies, but "cylindrical" should be read as "threaded with bone-conforming geometry" rather than as excluding tapered shapes.

3. **Implant finish (surface)** — Albrektsson described an empirical preference for "somewhat rougher" finishes; this has since evolved into SLA, sandblasted/acid-etched, and anodized surfaces (Smeets 2016). Surface finish is orthogonal to macrogeometry but interacts with it: rough-surface tapered implants achieve faster secondary stability than machined cylindrical predecessors.

4. **Status of the bone** — Healthy bone essential; spongy bone corticalizes around implant over time. This is the most directly relevant factor for the tapered-vs-cylindrical decision: in D3/D4 bone, tapered geometry and deeper threads provide mechanical compensation for reduced bone stiffness (Heimes 2023, Chang 2024).

5. **Surgical technique** — "Minimal tissue violence" including constant cooling, proper drill geometry and speed. Mourão (2025) identifies surgical technique as the strongest determinant of primary stability, eclipsing macrogeometry. Albrektsson's 1981 insistence on "minimum trauma" remains the most validated non-design factor.

6. **Loading conditions** — Osseointegration occurs only under adequate initial stability; unloaded healing of 3–4 months was originally required. Mourão (2025) notes that both tapered and cylindrical implants reached early-loading-adequate stability in good bone, but explicitly excludes immediate loading, regenerated bone, and low-density bone from this conclusion — a direct echo of Albrektsson's insistence on stability before load.

## Clinical Decision Framework

### When to choose tapered vs cylindrical

| Clinical scenario | Preferred design | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| D1/D2 healed bone, standard placement | Either — technique-driven | Both achieve equivalent early-loading stability; drilling protocol > shape (Mourão 2025) |
| D3/D4 low-density bone | Tapered + deeper thread pitch | Higher contact area and press-fit advantage (Heimes 2023); thread depth cuts crestal stress ~40% (Chang 2024) |
| Immediate implant placement, anterior maxilla | Either — no neck-design evidence of superiority | Chrcanovic (2025): triangular vs round neck NS at 12 months |
| Immediate loading protocol | Insufficient evidence to favor either | Mourão (2025) excludes immediate loading from its conclusions |
| Regenerated/grafted bone | Insufficient evidence to favor either | Mourão (2025) explicitly excludes this context |
| Concern for MBL / crestal overload | Cylindrical or tapered + deeper thread | Tapered produces higher crestal von Mises stress (Hussein 2019); mitigated by thread optimization (Chang 2024) |

### Bone quality considerations (D1–D4 framework)

- **D1 (dense cortical)**: Thread design effect minimal; bone stiffness absorbs load well. Cylindrical adequate. Avoid excessive insertion torque with tapered, which can induce crestal stress.
- **D2 (thick cortical + dense cancellous)**: Thread depth optimization has negligible effect (Chang 2024). Either design works; prioritize drill sequence and torque control.
- **D3 (thin cortical + dense cancellous)**: Tapered form with deeper threads provides meaningful crestal stress reduction (~40%) and displacement control (Chang 2024). Drill under-preparation adds further primary stability.
- **D4 (minimal cortical + low-density cancellous)**: Same as D3 but more pronounced. Tapered macrogeometry + maximum thread depth optimization is the strongest mechanical compensator available without modifying site (Heimes 2023, Chang 2024). Consider also surgical under-preparation as an additional lever.

### Surgical technique prioritization

The Mourão (2025) RCT appraisal establishes that drilling protocol — specifically, degree of under-preparation relative to implant diameter — is a stronger determinant of primary stability than macrogeometry. This means the highest-yield intervention for improving stability in a given case is often optimizing the osteotomy protocol, not selecting a different implant shape. Implant design should be considered after, not instead of, technique optimization.

## Gaps & Limitations

1. **Abstract-only RCTs**: Both the Mourão (2025) critical summary and the Chrcanovic (2025) RCT are ingested as abstract-only — absolute numeric values (mean MIT, ISQ values, MBL mm) are not available for this synthesis. All findings are directional, not quantitative.

2. **Non-standardized osteotomy confounder**: The appraised RCT (Charoenniwassakul et al.) did not standardize drilling between tapered and cylindrical arms. This is the central methodological weakness: observed stability differences cannot be attributed to macrogeometry alone (Mourão 2025).

3. **FEA-to-clinic gap**: Hussein (2019) and Chang (2024) are idealized in silico models. FEA-derived stress values and design recommendations require prospective clinical validation. The ~40% crestal stress reduction from thread-depth optimization (Chang 2024) is a model prediction, not a measured in vivo MBL outcome.

4. **Short follow-up**: The Chrcanovic (2025) RCT and the appraised Mourão (2025) RCT both have ≤12 months follow-up. MBL differences, if any, may emerge later; peri-implantitis, biological width establishment, and long-term soft tissue stability are not captured.

5. **Underpowered equivalence testing**: Neither RCT was designed for equivalence or non-inferiority. The null results on neck design and macrogeometry cannot establish that the designs are interchangeable — only that no superiority was detected at these sample sizes and time points.

6. **Context specificity**: The Chrcanovic (2025) RCT was conducted entirely in anterior maxillary immediate placement with immediate provisionalization — a protocol with high esthetic pressure and specific bone anatomy. Results should not be generalized to posterior healed-bone or delayed placement protocols.

7. **Albrektsson (1981) era gap**: The 6-prerequisite framework predates tapered implants, surface treatments, and immediate loading protocols. The "cylindrical threaded" specification was a technology constraint, not a comparative conclusion.
