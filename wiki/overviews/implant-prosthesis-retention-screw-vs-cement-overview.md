> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - **핵심 명제**: 임플란트 단일 크라운 유지 방식은 나사유지 (screw-retained) · 시멘트유지 (cement-retained) · 혼합 나사시멘트유지 (Screw-and-Cement-Retained Prosthesis, SCRP)의 3분 결정이며, 어느 하나도 모든 조건을 만족하지 못하는 절충(trade-off)이다.
> - **나사유지**의 강점은 회수성 (retrievability)과 점막하 잔류시멘트 (residual cement) 없음(임플란트주위염 위험 회피)이지만, 나사접근홀 (Screw Access Hole, SAH)이 협측/심미부에 노출되면 적용이 어렵다.
> - **시멘트유지**의 강점은 수동적합 (passive fit) 허용폭, 교합면 온전성, 심미성이지만 점막하 잔류시멘트가 임플란트주위염 (peri-implantitis)의 주요 유발 인자다.
> - **SCRP**는 어버트먼트에 크라운을 시멘트 접착 후 나사로 함께 제거 — 양쪽 장점을 결합하나, 좁고 협측 경사진 치조골에서는 SAH 위치를 못 잡아 적용 불가 사례가 많다 (Park 2022).
> - **Park 2022 (후향적 CBCT, 47명)**: SCRP 적용 가능성은 상악 제1소구치 **52%**, 제2소구치 **78.8%** — 보철축-치조골축 각도차 (Ridge Angle, RA)가 유일한 독립 예측인자(OR=1.885, 정확도 93.1%). 즉 SCRP는 항상 가능한 것이 아니라 해부학적 조건부 선택지다.
> - **Kim 2023 (전향적 다기관 RCT, 64개, 12개월)**: 신개념 탈착형 볼-스프링 유지 보철 (Ball-and-Spring-Retained Prosthesis, BSRP) vs SCRP — 양군 100% 생존, 변연골흡수 (Marginal Bone Loss, MBL)·탐침깊이 차이 없음. 합병증 패턴만 갈림 — BSRP는 어버트먼트 나사 풀림↑, SCRP는 나사 파절↑.
> - **Lee 2021 (FEA)**: 시멘트유지 vs 신형 시멘트리스 나사유지 (cementless screw-retained) — 시멘트리스 쪽이 임플란트 응력 ~60%(수직)·골 응력 ~71%(수직) 낮음. 시멘트유지는 임플란트 경부(neck)에 응력 집중.
> - **Lee 2025 (FEA)**: SCRP vs 시멘트리스 나사유지(SRP) vs 신형 스크류리스 후크유지 (Hook-Retained Prosthesis, HRP) — SCRP가 임플란트 응력 최고(수직 32.91 MPa), HRP(21.33)·SRP(21.92)는 더 낮고 골 응력은 3군 동등. 시멘트와 나사를 모두 줄인 설계가 생역학적으로 유리.
> - **Leung 2022 (서술적 종설)**: 시멘트유지를 택했을 때의 luting 재료 선택 backbone — 고강도 세라믹(지르코니아)엔 레진/CaAl-GI 계열, 주조금속 포스트코어엔 인산아연, 가철성 보철 전엔 비유게놀 임시시멘트. "모든 조건을 만족하는 단일 시멘트는 없다."
> - **임상 종합**: 가능하면 회수성·잔류시멘트 회피 위해 나사계열(나사유지/SCRP) 우선, 단 SAH가 심미·교합을 해치거나 RA가 커 SCRP 불가 시 시멘트유지로 전환하고 이때 잔류시멘트 통제·적정 luting 재료를 선택. 신형 시멘트리스 나사유지·후크유지·BSRP는 FEA·단기 RCT 근거가 쌓이는 차세대 옵션.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 5 papers framing implant single-crown retention as a screw- vs cement- vs hybrid SCRP decision: screw-based modes give retrievability and avoid subgingival residual cement but need a non-esthetic screw-access hole; cement-retained gives passive-fit tolerance and esthetics but risks residual cement; SCRP combines both yet is anatomically feasible only ~52–79% of the time in maxillary premolars (Park 2022), with comparable clinical survival to detachable BSRP (Kim 2023) and FEA showing cementless screw-/hook-retained designs distribute stress more favorably than cement-retained or SCRP (Lee 2021, Lee 2025); Leung 2022 guides luting-agent choice when cementing.

## Summary

The retention of an implant-supported single crown is fundamentally a three-way decision — **screw-retained**, **cement-retained**, or the **hybrid screw-and-cement-retained prosthesis (SCRP)** — and the literature we hold frames it as a trade-off in which no single mode wins on every axis.

**Screw-retained** prostheses offer two decisive advantages: full *retrievability* (the crown can be removed for repair, hygiene, or component access) and the *absence of subgingival cement*, which removes residual-cement as a peri-implantitis trigger. The cost is a screw-access hole (SAH) on the occlusal/lingual surface and a need for the implant axis to emerge through a non-esthetic, non-functional position.

**Cement-retained** prostheses tolerate *non-ideal implant angulation* (the cement gap absorbs the discrepancy, giving passive-fit latitude), preserve the occlusal table and esthetics (no access hole), but carry the well-known risk of *residual subgingival cement* driving peri-implant inflammation.

**SCRP** is designed to capture both worlds — the crown is cemented to the abutment extra-orally (or with a controlled, retrievable cement seat) and the assembly is then secured to the fixture by an abutment screw, so it is both retrievable and passively fitting. But SCRP is not always geometrically possible.

Park et al. (2022), in a retrospective CBCT virtual-implantation study of 47 Korean patients (58 implants), quantified exactly this limit: SCRP was feasible in only **52% of maxillary first premolars** and **78.8% of second premolars**, because the screw-access channel must exit between the buccal and palatal cusps and the narrow, buccally inclined premolar ridge frequently prevents it. The single independent predictor of being forced into cement retention was the angle difference between the prosthesis axis and the alveolar ridge axis — the **ridge angle (RA)** — where a 1° increase raised the odds of needing CRP by 1.885-fold (93.1% predictive accuracy). This establishes that SCRP is an *anatomically conditional* option, identifiable at planning from CBCT, not a default.

Kim et al. (2023), in a prospective two-center RCT (64 implants, 12 months), compared SCRP against a novel detachable **ball-and-spring-retained prosthesis (BSRP)** that removes the crown without disturbing the abutment. Both groups reached **100% survival** with no significant difference in marginal bone loss (MBL) or probing depth; the only divergence was the complication signature — BSRP showed abutment-screw loosening (11 events) while SCRP showed screw fractures (2 events). Clinically, then, the retention mode is chosen on retrievability, esthetics, and complication tolerance rather than on survival, which is comparable across modes at short term.

Two FEA studies probe the biomechanics. Lee et al. (2021) compared conventional cement-retained zirconia crowns against a novel **cementless screw-retained** ("screw-in-screw" base-abutment + titanium-link) design on a mandibular premolar: the cementless design produced **~60% lower implant stress** and **~71% lower bone stress** under 100 N vertical load (26.3 vs 65.3 MPa implant; 9.97 vs 34.04 MPa bone), with the cement-retained stress concentrating at the implant *neck*. Lee et al. (2025) extended this to three systems — SCRP vs cementless screw-retained (SRP) vs a novel screwless **hook-retained prosthesis (HRP)** — and found SCRP carried the **highest implant fixture stress** (32.91 MPa vertical, 262.24 MPa oblique), while HRP (21.33 / 187.50 MPa) and SRP (21.92 / 187.64 MPa) were lower and nearly identical, with peri-implant *bone* stress essentially equal across all three. The biomechanical message: removing the cement layer and minimizing the screw-mediated load path tends to lower fixture stress without compromising bone loading.

Finally, Leung et al. (2022), a narrative review of seven luting-cement families, governs the *cement-retained* branch of the decision: it classifies cements by chemistry, bonding mechanism, and indication, and its thesis — *no single cement satisfies every ideal requirement* — means luting-agent selection is itself a property-driven trade-off (e.g., hybrid CaAl/GI or resin cement for high-strength ceramics like zirconia, zinc phosphate for cast-metal post-cores, non-eugenol provisionals before composite, light/dual-cured resin for thin veneers).

### Comparison table — retention modes

| Axis | Screw-retained (incl. cementless SRP / hook HRP) | Cement-retained (CRP) | SCRP (hybrid) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Retrievability | High — remove via screw | Low — usually destructive | High — remove via screw |
| Residual subgingival cement risk | None | Present (peri-implantitis driver) | Minimal (controlled/extra-oral cementing) |
| Passive-fit tolerance / angulation latitude | Low — axis must emerge correctly | High — cement gap absorbs | Moderate–high |
| Esthetics / occlusal table | SAH may compromise buccal/incisal | Intact, no access hole | SAH present but planned palatal/central |
| Anatomic feasibility (max. premolar) | — | Always | **52–78.8%** (Park 2022; RA-dependent) |
| Implant fixture stress (FEA) | Lowest (Lee 2021 cementless 26.3 MPa; Lee 2025 SRP 21.92 / HRP 21.33 MPa vert.) | Highest in single-crown cement model (Lee 2021: 65.3 MPa vert.) | Highest among 3 in Lee 2025 (32.91 MPa vert.) |
| Survival (12 mo) | — | — | 100%, = BSRP (Kim 2023) |
| Main complication | Screw loosening / SAH chipping | Cement excess, debonding | Screw fracture (Kim 2023) |
| Luting-agent dependence | None (or cylinder-only in HRP) | High — see Leung 2022 | Low–moderate (controlled cement) |

### Retention-mode selection framework

1. **Default toward a screw-based mode (screw-retained or SCRP)** when retrievability and avoidance of subgingival cement matter — i.e., most posterior single crowns, and any case where future component access or hygiene is a priority. Screw-based modes also carry the FEA-favored stress profile (Lee 2021, Lee 2025).
2. **Check SCRP feasibility at planning from CBCT** (Park 2022): measure the ridge angle (RA) and confirm the screw channel can exit between the cusps. In the maxillary first-premolar region especially, expect ~half of cases to be geometrically *unsuitable* for SCRP.
3. **Switch to cement-retained (CRP)** when the screw access hole would compromise esthetics (anterior/buccal emergence) or occlusal contacts, or when implant angulation is non-ideal and only a cement gap can absorb it (Park 2022's high-RA cases).
4. **When cementing, select the luting agent by restoration and substrate** (Leung 2022): resin or hybrid CaAl/GI for zirconia/lithium-disilicate, zinc phosphate for cast-metal cores, non-eugenol provisional cement before composite, light/dual-cured resin for thin (<2 mm) ceramics — and control residual cement meticulously.
5. **Consider next-generation cement-/screw-minimizing designs** where available — cementless screw-retained (SRP), screwless hook-retained (HRP), or detachable BSRP — which carry favorable FEA stress (Lee 2021, Lee 2025) and equivalent short-term survival to SCRP (Kim 2023), pending long-term clinical confirmation.
