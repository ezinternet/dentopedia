## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 결론: C형 근관(C-shaped Canal) 유병률은 ①치아종류 ②지역/인종 ③성별의 3개 독립 구배로 설명되며, Yousefi 2025 SR+MA(CBCT 101편)가 정량적 척추(spine)다.
> - 치아종류 구배: 하악 제2대구치(Mandibular 2nd Molar)가 17.3%로 최다, 상악 제1대구치(Maxillary 1st Molar)가 0.8%로 최소. 하악 제1대구치는 바닥(rare-event tail).
> - 지역 구배: 아시아 우세 — 한국 31.3~45.5%, 중국 ≤41.27%로 최고치, 이스라엘 4.6%·이란 2%로 최저. 약 10배 차이 → 인구별 사전확률(population prior)로 의심도를 조정해야 함.
> - 성별 구배: 하악 제2대구치에서 여성 우세(여 23.6% vs 남 16.7%) — 이라크(p=0.001)·멕시코(OR 1.78, p=0.004) 두 대륙에서 재현. 소표본·저유병 코호트에서는 사라짐(검정력 문제).
> - 좌우 차이는 유의하지 않음(R 21.2% vs L 23.1%) → 한쪽에서 C형 발견 시 반대측(contralateral)도 예상하라는 임상 휴리스틱.
> - 분류: 대구치 우세 형태는 Fan C2형(semicolon), 소구치 우세 형태는 C3형(분리된 2근관). Shemesh 2017은 63%에서 근관 형태가 치근을 따라 변함 → 입구(orifice)의 Melton 분류만으로는 불충분, Fan 단면/CBCT 필요.
> - 소구치 예외: 대구치는 2번이 우세하지만 소구치는 1번(Mandibular 1st Premolar)이 우세(C형 소구치 증례의 83.3%). 단, 소구치 C형의 절대 빈도는 낮음(Yu 2012: 1.1%/0.6%).
> - 병인: Hertwig 상피초(HERS)의 협측/설측 융합 실패로 발생 — 유전적(외상 아님), 아시아 우세·높은 양측성(70~81%)과 일치.
> - 위험 핵심: radicular-groove 부위 얇은 벽(최소 0.26 mm)이 strip-perforation(천공) 위험을 만듦 → 보수적 성형, 오목부(concavity)의 과도한 측방 기구조작보다 underfill을 수용.
> - 임상 takeaway: 치료 전 CBCT(놓친 근관 1개당 치근단 병변 odds 약 3.66배↑), 현미경 시야, 초음파 활성 NaOCl+17% EDTA로 isthmus 화학 소독, 열가소성/연속파 충전.
> - 본 컬렉션 한계: 모두 해부/유병률 연구 — C형 vs 비-C형 대구치의 장기 치아생존 outcome 데이터 없음.

## One-line Summary

Across 12 papers (1 SR+MA of 101 CBCT studies, 7 population CBCT cross-sections, 1 narrative review, 1 cross-sectional morphology study, 2 case series), C-shaped canal prevalence is governed by a steep tooth-type gradient (mandibular 2nd molar 17.3% highest → maxillary 1st molar 0.8% lowest), an Asian-predominant geographic gradient, and a consistent female predominance; Fan C2 is the dominant molar configuration, the thin buccal/lingual radicular-groove wall (down to 0.26 mm) drives strip-perforation risk, and management hinges on CBCT, microscope visualization, ultrasonic isthmus disinfection, and thermoplasticized obturation.

## Thesis

**The quantitative spine of this topic is the Yousefi 2025 SR+MA** ([[endodontics/anatomy/yousefi-2025-cshaped-canal-prevalence-cbct-sr-ma]]), which pooled 101 CBCT studies (screened from 5,548) and established three orthogonal gradients that every individual population study can be read against:

1. **Tooth-type gradient** — mandibular second molar carries the highest prevalence (**17.3%**) of any tooth; the maxillary first molar the lowest (**0.8%**). The mandibular first molar sits near the floor.
2. **Sex gradient** — in the mandibular second molar, C-shaped canals are female-predominant (**23.6% vs 16.7%** male).
3. **Geographic/ethnic gradient** — the **Asian continent** is highest; side (right vs left) is *not* significantly different (21.2% vs 23.1%), which underwrites the clinical heuristic that finding a C-shaped canal on one side should make you anticipate it contralaterally.

The individual population CBCT studies in this collection mostly **corroborate** the spine and, where they deviate, the deviation is explained by population, tooth choice, or counting method rather than by contradicting the underlying biology:

- **Corroborating the ~17% mandibular-2nd-molar anchor**: Iraqi 17.4% ([[endodontics/anatomy/abdalrahman-2022-cshaped-canal-second-molars-iraqi-cbct]]) sits almost exactly on the pooled estimate; Mexican 14.1% ([[endodontics/anatomy/armenta-2024-cshaped-canal-radix-mandibular-molars-mexican-cbct]]) and Indian mandibular 18.9% ([[endodontics/anatomy/singh-2022-cshaped-canal-second-molars-indian-cbct]]) bracket it.
- **Corroborating the female predominance**: independently confirmed in the Iraqi cohort (23% vs 10.4%, p=0.001, Abdalrahman 2022) and the Mexican cohort (27.3% vs 13.3%, p=0.004, OR 1.78, Armenta 2024) — two separate continents reproducing the SR+MA sex effect.
- **Deviating low (non-Asian, Middle East/Mediterranean)**: Israeli mandibular 2nd molar **4.6%** ([[endodontics/anatomy/shemesh-2017-cshaped-canals-cbct-mandibular-molars]]) and Iranian **2%** ([[endodontics/anatomy/shekarian-2023-cshaped-canals-three-rooted-mandibular-iranian]]) are far below the pooled 17.3% — consistent with the Asian-high / non-Asian-low gradient.
- **Deviating high (counting method)**: the Indian overall 22% (Singh 2022) is inflated by **per-patient (not per-tooth)** reporting and Melton-based classification, a caveat that explains the apparent excess without contradicting the spine.

The two **case reports** then operationalize the anatomy at the chairside: Biswas 2023 ([[endodontics/anatomy/biswas-2023-see-through-c-case-series]]) shows microscope-guided management of C1 and C2 mandibular second molars, and Wei 2025 ([[endodontics/anatomy/wei-2025-cshaped-mandibular-first-molar-case]]) documents the *rare tail* of the distribution — a bilateral C-shaped **first** molar with the first-ever reported four separate canals in a single fused C-shaped root. The narrative review (Fernandes 2014, [[endodontics/anatomy/fernandes-2014-cshaped-root-canal-configuration-review]]) supplies the etiology and classification scaffolding, and the morphology study (Song 2008, [[endodontics/anatomy/song-2008-cross-sectional-morphology-and-minimum-canal]]) supplies the safety-margin physics — the **thin radicular-groove wall** that turns this from an epidemiology curiosity into a perforation-risk problem.

## Tooth-Type Gradient: 2nd Molar ≫ 1st Molar; the Premolar Exception

The single most reproducible finding across the collection is the **mandibular second-molar dominance** for C-shaped canals, with the first molar near the floor:

- Yousefi 2025 SR+MA: mand 2nd molar **17.3%** vs maxillary 1st molar 0.8%.
- Shemesh 2017: 2nd 4.6% vs 1st **0.16%** (≈29× gradient).
- Armenta 2024: 2nd 14.1% vs 1st **0.2%** (≈70× gradient).
- Shekarian 2023: 2nd/3rd 2% vs 1st 1.7% (gradient flattens in this low-prevalence Iranian cohort).

The **mandibular first molar is the rare-event tail** — Wei 2025's bilateral first-molar case is notable precisely *because* first-molar C-shapes are uncommon (its compiled review puts first-molar prevalence at 0.16–24.33%, with most populations <1%: Portuguese 0.6%, Israeli 0.16%, Mexican 0.2%, Thai 0.39%). When it does occur it can be anatomically extreme (four separate canals in one fused C-shaped root).

**The premolar exception flips the tooth-position rule.** In molars, the second molar dominates; in premolars, the **first** premolar dominates. Brea 2021 found that of all C-shaped premolar cases, **83.3% were first premolars** vs 16.7% second premolars, with C3 (two separate canals) the leading first-premolar configuration (59.1%). Yu 2012, however, found premolar C-shapes rare overall in western Chinese (first PM 1.1%, second PM 0.6%) — so the *first-premolar-over-second-premolar* pattern (Brea) and the *low absolute premolar rate* (Yu) coexist: the mandibular first premolar is the premolar most likely to be C-shaped, but premolar C-shapes are uncommon relative to mandibular second molars. The clinical lesson is that the mandibular first premolar, not the second, is the premolar to watch.

## Geographic / Ethnic Gradient: Asian Highest

The SR+MA's "Asian continent highest" conclusion is mirrored cleanly by the population studies when ranked:

- **High (East Asian)** — Korean 31.3–45.5% and Chinese ≤41.27% (Fernandes 2014 review compilation); Song 2008 cites 30–50% in Asian populations; Wei 2025's review cites second-molar figures up to ~44.5–48.7%.
- **Intermediate (South Asian / Latin American)** — Indian mandibular 18.9% (Singh 2022); Mexican 14.1% (Armenta 2024); Iraqi 17.4% (Abdalrahman 2022) sits intermediate-to-high.
- **Low (Mediterranean / Middle Eastern non-East-Asian)** — Israeli 4.6% (Shemesh 2017); Iranian 2% (Shekarian 2023).

This ~10-fold spread across populations (2% Iranian → 45% Korean) is the strongest argument in the collection that **population-specific priors should drive pre-treatment suspicion** — and why a single global number is misleading. Armenta 2024 itself tabulates the global heterogeneity it sits within (overall 13.9%, China 44.0%, Brazil 6.8%, Mexico ~14.2%).

## Sex Effect: Female-Predominant

Three independent lines confirm female predominance for mandibular C-shaped canals, on three different continents:

- **Yousefi 2025 SR+MA** (pooled): female 23.6% vs male 16.7%.
- **Abdalrahman 2022** (Iraq): female 23% vs male 10.4%, **p=0.001**.
- **Armenta 2024** (Mexico): female 27.3% vs male 13.3%, **p=0.004, OR 1.78**.

Note that some single-population studies report no significant sex effect (Shemesh 2017, Shekarian 2023, Brea 2021 premolars), and the older Fernandes 2014 narrative review even stated "no gender correlation." The discrepancy is a power/tooth-type issue: the sex effect is detectable at the pooled mandibular-second-molar level (Yousefi) and in adequately powered single cohorts (Abdalrahman, Armenta), but washes out in smaller or low-prevalence samples. **The current best evidence (the SR+MA + two confirmatory RCT-sized cross-sections) supports a real female predominance in mandibular second molars.**

## Classification Systems: Melton vs Fan

Fernandes 2014 is the conceptual anchor for classification, cataloguing the competing systems:

- **Cooke & Cox (1979)** — first description.
- **Melton (orifice-level, C1–C3)** — C1 continuous "C", C2 semicolon, C3 two/three separate canals. Used by Singh 2022 (continuous/Cat 1 dominant at 52.7%) and Biswas 2023 (C1, C2 cases).
- **Fan et al. (2004, cross-sectional, C1–C5)** — refines Melton by describing how the configuration *changes down the root*. Used by Shekarian 2023, Abdalrahman 2022 (modified Melton C1–C5; C2 dominant 56.3%), Armenta 2024 (C2 dominant 39.3%), Brea 2021 (extended C1–C6 for premolars), and Wei 2025 (C3c).

The practical takeaway from combining the systems with Shemesh 2017's finding that **configuration changes along the root in 63% of cases**: orifice-level Melton typing alone is insufficient — the Fan cross-sectional view (or CBCT) is needed because the C1 orifice you see at the chamber may become C2/C3 apically. **Dominant molar configuration across the CBCT studies is Fan C2** (Abdalrahman 56.3%, Armenta 39.3%), i.e. the "semicolon" with a partially separated canal — while in **premolars the dominant config is C3** (Brea, two separate canals).

## Etiology

Fernandes 2014: the C-shape arises when **Hertwig's epithelial root sheath (HERS) fails to fuse** on the buccal or lingual root surface, leaving a longitudinal radicular groove and a fused conical/square root. Buccal fusion failure → "C" closed toward lingual; lingual fusion failure → "C" closed toward buccal. This is now regarded as **genetic, not traumatic**, which is consistent with both the strong racial predilection (Asian predominance) and the high bilateral occurrence (70–81% in the review; mandibular cases predominantly bilateral in Abdalrahman 2022).

## Clinical Management Implications

The danger of C-shaped anatomy is not just "extra canals" but **thin dentin over the radicular groove**, plus an isthmus/fin network that conventional instrumentation cannot reach.

**1. Perforation risk from thin walls (Song 2008 → Biswas/Wei).** Song 2008 located the minimum dentin thickness at the buccal concavity / central groove of the "C", where over-instrumentation can strip-perforate without warning; Fernandes 2014 quotes a minimum measured wall of **0.26 mm** (thinner on the lingual). The clinical consequences appear directly in the case reports: Biswas 2023 lost the apical constriction mid-treatment in a thin-walled C-shaped canal and salvaged it with an **orthograde MTA apical plug**; Wei 2025 explicitly invokes the strip-perforation/fracture risk. **Rule: shape conservatively; accept underfill over aggressive lateral instrumentation in the concavity.**

**2. CBCT before treatment.** Standard radiographs underestimate the system (Song 2008). The SR+MA, both case reports, and Shemesh 2017 all converge on CBCT as the morphology gold standard — Wei 2025 quantifies the stakes: ≥1 missed canal ≈ **3.66× higher periapical-lesion odds**. Use population priors to decide when CBCT is justified: an Asian patient's mandibular second molar warrants a far higher index of suspicion than an Israeli/Iranian first molar.

**3. Anticipate asymmetry along the root and the heuristic of contralateral symmetry.** Shemesh 2017: 55% unilateral and config changes along the root in 63% — so the orifice does not predict the apex, and one side does not guarantee the other within a tooth. Yet at the *patient* level, Yousefi 2025's non-significant side difference and Abdalrahman's predominantly bilateral mandibular pattern support **expecting a contralateral C-shaped canal** when one is found. (These are not contradictory: bilateral *patient*-level symmetry coexists with intra-tooth *along-root* variability.)

**4. Isthmus disinfection — ultrasonics + chemistry.** The fins and isthmuses between the C's limbs are mechanically inaccessible, so disinfection is chemical: both case reports used **NaOCl + 17% EDTA with ultrasonic/sonic activation** (Biswas: 5.25% NaOCl; Wei: 3% NaOCl), and Fernandes 2014's management section calls for small-file exploration (#8/10/15) plus ultrasonic/sonic-assisted debridement of fins. Microscope ("see-through C", Biswas) for direct visualization of multiple/atypical orifices.

**5. Obturation.** Thermoplasticized / continuous-wave techniques to fill the irregular connected lumen three-dimensionally (Biswas: Thermafil; Song 2008: continuous-wave over lateral condensation; Fernandes 2014: modified obturation for 3-D fill).

## Open Questions / Gaps

- **No PDRN/biologic or outcome-survival data** in this collection — every study is anatomical/prevalence or short case follow-up (1-year). Long-term tooth-survival outcomes of C-shaped vs non-C-shaped molars are not covered by the papers we hold.
- **Counting-method heterogeneity** (per-tooth vs per-patient, Melton vs Fan) is the dominant source of between-study variance and is not fully resolved even by the SR+MA — direct cross-study prevalence comparison should be made cautiously.
- **Premolar evidence is thin and discordant** (Yu's 1.1% vs Brea's 19.2% overall) — premolar C-shaped epidemiology is less settled than the molar story.
