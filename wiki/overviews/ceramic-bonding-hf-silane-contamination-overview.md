## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 핵심 명제: 9편 인 비트로(in vitro) 연구 종합 — 실리카 기반 세라믹 접착 성공은 4개 결정의 연쇄(불산 (Hydrofluoric Acid, HF) 농도 → 실란 → 오염 제거 → 시멘트 선택)에 달려 있다.
> - 축 1 — HF 농도: 장석계(feldspathic) 세라믹은 ≥3% HF 필요(1%는 노화 후 14.5→10.2 MPa로 열화), 10%가 5%보다 우수(Moura 2020, p=0.02), 산부식 시간(60-120초)·HF 가열은 무의미.
> - 리튬 디실리케이트 (Lithium Disilicate, LDS): 가열한 HF(70°C)가 미세인장결합강도 (μTBS)를 ~18→~29 MPa로 선택적으로 향상(Leyva del Rio 2021) — 장석계에는 이득 없음(결정 미세구조 차이).
> - 축 2 — 실란: 대부분의 유니버설 접착제는 분해된 실란(실록산 형태)을 함유해 반응성이 낮음 — 별도 사전가수분해 실란 프라이머가 화학적으로 우수(Dimitriadi 2019, NMR로 입증, Calibra Silane만 활성 실라놀 단량체 함유).
> - 별도 실란 단계는 대부분의 유니버설 접착제에서 필수(Single Bond·All Bond Universal은 p<0.001로 향상)이나 FuturaBond는 예외 — Scotchbond Universal 단독은 열순환 시 50-70% 탈락(Sagen 2025).
> - 실란만으로는 불충분: HF 처리한 LDS에 실란만 적용 시 무처리 대조군과 차이 없음(20.17 vs 18.36 MPa) — 실란 후 레진 접착제 레이어 추가가 결정적(45.49 MPa로 최고, Simasetha 2021).
> - 축 3 — 트라이인(try-in) 오염 회복: 타액+실리콘 페이스트 오염 후 Monobond Etch&Prime (MEP) 또는 5% HF 재처리만 결합강도 회복 — 물·인산·에탄올·Ivoclean은 모두 불충분(Del Bianco 2025), Ivoclean은 마케팅과 달리 최악의 활성 세정제.
> - 축 4 — 시멘트: 예열 복합레진(68°C, 10분)이 통상 레진 시멘트·유동성 복합레진보다 우수(Grangeiro 2024) — 유동성 복합레진은 최저 성능에 세라믹 파절 위험까지 증가하므로 합착재로 비권장.
> - 임상 프로토콜 [claude 해석]: ① HF 산부식(장석계 5-10%/60초, LDS 9%/20초) → ② 전용 실란 프라이머 → ③ 레진 접착제 레이어 → ④ 예열 복합레진 합착.
> - 구강내 세라믹 칩 수리: 전용 실란+접착제(BISCO 등) 필수, 표면처리(HF vs 다이아몬드 디스크)는 접착제 선택보다 부차적 — 구강내 HF 독성 위험을 고려하면 다이아몬드 디스크 거칠게+전용 실란+접착제가 선호된다.
> - 전 근거가 in vitro이므로 임상 외삽에 주의하며, 제조사 지침을 대체하지 않는다.

## One-line Summary

Nine in vitro studies establish that successful silica-based ceramic bonding depends on a chain of four decisions — HF concentration ≥3% (≥ 10% optimal for feldspathic), a dedicated prehydrolyzed silane primer followed by an adhesive layer, Monobond Etch&Prime or HF re-etch after try-in contamination, and preheated composite (68°C) as the highest-performing luting agent.

## 한줄요약

9편의 인 비트로 연구 종합: 실리카 기반 세라믹 접착 성공은 4가지 결정 — HF 농도 ≥3%(장석계는 10% 최적), 별도 사전가수분해 실란+접착제 레이어, 트라이인 오염 후 Monobond Etch&Prime 또는 HF 재처리, 예열 복합레진(68°C) 사용 — 의 연쇄에 달려 있다.

## Summary

핵심 명제 (Core Propositions)

**1. A minimum of 3% HF is required for durable feldspathic ceramic bond; 10% is optimal.**
Venturini et al. (2015) showed that immediate bond strength is statistically equivalent across 1–10% HF (14.2–15.7 MPa), but after 230-day aging + 12,000 thermocycles, only the 1% group degraded significantly (14.5 → 10.2 MPa). All groups ≥3% remained stable. Moura et al. (2020) confirmed in a 3-way factorial design (90-day storage) that HF concentration was the only significant factor — 10% HF outperformed 5% HF for Vita Mark II feldspathic ceramic (p=0.02). *(Evidence: in-vitro × 2)*

**2. Heated HF (70°C) selectively enhances bond strength for lithium disilicate, but not for feldspathic ceramic.**
Leyva del Rio et al. (2021) used 9% HF at room temperature vs. 70°C on both ceramic types. Heated HF elevated μTBS significantly only for lithium disilicate (L70: 29.11 ± 8.26 MPa vs. LC: 17.87 ± 6.96 MPa; p<0.001). Feldspathic ceramic showed no benefit (F70: 18.24 vs. FC: 19.94 MPa; p>0.05). The differential response is driven by the crystalline microstructure of LDS that responds more dramatically to thermally enhanced acid dissolution. *(Evidence: in-vitro)*

**3. Most universal adhesives contain degraded silane (siloxane forms) with reduced ceramic reactivity — a dedicated prehydrolyzed silane primer is chemically superior.**
Dimitriadi et al. (2019) used 13C-NMR to characterize five commercial silane products. Only Calibra Silane contained active silanol monomers; Scotchbond Universal and Monobond Plus contained silanol-siloxane adducts; Kerr Silane Primer contained siloxane polymers with the lowest reactivity. ATR-FTIR confirmed ceramic surface chemical bonding (Si-O peaks) only for Calibra Silane. Weibull SBS analysis ranked Calibra Silane highest in characteristic life on both polished and HF-etched lithium disilicate surfaces. HF etching partially compensated for inferior silane chemistry by micromechanical retention, but did not eliminate the gap on polished surfaces. *(Evidence: in-vitro, NMR + FTIR)*

**4. Separate silane application is necessary even with silane-containing universal adhesives — but product-dependent exceptions exist.**
Kheirkhah et al. (2021) showed that adding separate Bis-Silane significantly increased SBS with Single Bond Universal and All Bond Universal on feldspathic porcelain after 5,000 thermocycles (p<0.001 for both), but not with FuturaBond Universal (p>0.05). Sagen et al. (2025) confirmed the broader pattern: when repairing feldspathic porcelain chips, Scotchbond Universal (with silane) caused 50–70% debonding during thermal cycling, while a separate silane primer + adhesive protocol (BISCO) produced dramatically superior and durable results regardless of surface treatment (HF etching or diamond disc roughening). The inferiority of universal adhesives is attributed to silane monomer hydrolysis at the product's acidic all-in-one pH. *(Evidence: in-vitro × 2)*

**5. Adding an adhesive layer after silane priming is critical — silane alone is insufficient for optimal LDS bond strength.**
Simasetha et al. (2021) showed that Kerr Silane Primer alone on HF-etched (4.5%, 20 s) lithium disilicate was statistically no better than the untreated control (KS: 20.17 MPa vs. Control: 18.36 MPa; p>0.05). The addition of a resin adhesive (Adper Scotchbond Multi-purpose) after a pure γ-MPS experimental silane elevated SBS to 45.49 MPa — the study maximum. This demonstrates that the silane layer creates a chemical bridge but the adhesive resin layer is needed to build mechanical bulk and integrate the silanized surface with the luting cement. *(Evidence: in-vitro)*

**6. After try-in contamination (saliva + silicone paste), only self-etching ceramic primer (Monobond Etch&Prime) or HF re-etching restores bond strength — water, phosphoric acid, ethanol, and Ivoclean are all insufficient.**
Del Bianco et al. (2025) contaminated IPS e.max CAD with fresh saliva and silicone try-in paste, then applied seven cleaning protocols. Only MEP (Monobond Etch&Prime, self-etching primer) and 5% HF re-etching achieved high SBS (statistically equivalent to each other; p>0.05). Water rinse, 37% phosphoric acid, 70% ethanol, and Ivoclean cleaning paste all yielded significantly lower bond strength (p<0.001). SEM confirmed that no method achieved complete contaminant removal, but MEP's combined etching-and-priming chemistry overcame residual contamination. Ivoclean performed worst among active cleaning agents — notably poor given its marketing positioning. *(Evidence: in-vitro)*

**7. Preheated composite (68°C) is the superior luting strategy for silica-based ceramics — outperforming conventional resin cement and flowable composite.**
Grangeiro et al. (2024) compared three luting strategies for Vita Mark II feldspathic ceramic and Vita Enamic PICN. After standard HF + Monobond N silane protocol, preheated composite (Filtek P60, 68°C/10 min) achieved the highest μSBS at baseline and after 5,000 thermal cycles for both materials. Flowable composite consistently performed worst and was associated with increased ceramic fracture risk (low-modulus luting agent). The mechanism: preheating reduces viscosity, enabling composite to flow into HF-created microporosity before cure — mimicking low-viscosity resin cement while retaining full filler content. *(Evidence: in-vitro)*

## Results

### 축 1 — HF 산 농도 및 조건 (HF Acid Concentration and Conditions)

The foundation of silica-based ceramic bonding is adequate HF etching. Three studies address the concentration and conditioning variables.

| Material | HF Protocol | Key Outcome | Source |
|----------|------------|-------------|--------|
| Vita Mark II (feldspathic) | 1% HF, 60 s | Bond strength degrades significantly after aging (14.5 → 10.2 MPa) | Venturini 2015 |
| Vita Mark II (feldspathic) | 3–10% HF, 60 s | Bond strength stable after 230 d + 12,000 thermocycles (14.2–15.7 MPa) | Venturini 2015 |
| Vita Mark II (feldspathic) | 5% HF, 60 or 120 s | Not significantly different from each other | Moura 2020 |
| Vita Mark II (feldspathic) | 10% HF, 60 s | Higher SBS than 5% HF (only significant factor, p=0.02) | Moura 2020 |
| Feldspathic ceramic | 9% HF, RT, 60 s | μTBS: 19.94 ± 4.14 MPa | Leyva del Rio 2021 |
| Feldspathic ceramic | 9% HF, 70°C, 60 s | μTBS: 18.24 ± 5.29 MPa (no benefit from heating) | Leyva del Rio 2021 |
| Lithium disilicate | 9% HF, RT, 20 s | μTBS: 17.87 ± 6.96 MPa | Leyva del Rio 2021 |
| Lithium disilicate | 9% HF, 70°C, 20 s | μTBS: 29.11 ± 8.26 MPa (**highest across all groups**) | Leyva del Rio 2021 |

**Key synthesis conclusions:**

- **Feldspathic ceramic**: Use ≥3% HF; 10% preferred when available. Etching time (60–120 s) does not significantly affect outcome. Heating HF provides no benefit.
- **Lithium disilicate**: Standard room-temperature HF etching achieves 17–20 MPa range; heated HF (70°C) uniquely elevates this to ~29 MPa. Ceramic-specific etching time (20 s, not 60 s) is appropriate.
- **Phosphoric acid neutralization post-HF**: Does not impair bond strength (Moura 2020, p>0.05) — clinicians can safely use PA + NaHCO₃ for HF waste management without compromising bond quality.

### 축 2 — 실란 처리 (Silane Application)

This is the most nuanced axis and the source of the greatest clinical misunderstanding. Four studies converge on a consistent hierarchy.

**The silane chemistry problem (Dimitriadi 2019)**

The premise that universal adhesives containing silane are equivalent to dedicated silane primers is chemically unfounded:

| Product | Silane Form (NMR) | FTIR Bond to Ceramic | Bond Reliability (Weibull) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Calibra Silane (dedicated) | Silanol monomers (active) | Confirmed (Si-O peaks) | Highest |
| Monobond Plus | Silanol-siloxane adducts | Partial | Intermediate |
| Scotchbond Universal | Silanol-siloxane adducts | Partial | Intermediate |
| G-Multi Primer | Methoxylated-siloxane | Absent | Lower |
| Kerr Silane Primer | Siloxane polymers | Absent | Lowest |

HF etching compensates via micromechanical retention, partially neutralizing silane quality differences — but this compensation is incomplete, particularly on polished surfaces (e.g., intraoral repairs without surface roughening access).

**The "separate silane" question (Kheirkhah 2021, Sagen 2025)**

Both studies confirm that for most universal adhesive products, a separate silane step is necessary:

- Single Bond Universal + separate silane: SBS significantly higher (Kheirkhah 2021, p<0.001)
- All Bond Universal + separate silane: SBS significantly higher (Kheirkhah 2021, p<0.001)
- FuturaBond Universal + separate silane: No additional benefit (Kheirkhah 2021, p>0.05) — exception, not the rule
- Scotchbond Universal: 50–70% debonding during thermocycling when used without separate silane (Sagen 2025)

**The adhesive layer requirement (Simasetha 2021)**

Silane alone is not sufficient. The critical hierarchy for lithium disilicate bond strength (Simasetha 2021):

1. No silane (Control): 18.36 MPa
2. Commercial silane alone (KS): 20.17 MPa — not significantly different from control
3. Silane + resin adhesive (EXP/ADP): 45.49 MPa — highest

The adhesive resin layer integrates the silanized ceramic surface with the cement matrix, building the mechanical bulk needed for clinical durability.

**Clinical rule:** For ceramic bonding (both feldspathic and LDS), use: **(1) dedicated prehydrolyzed silane primer → (2) resin adhesive layer → (3) resin cement**. Do not rely on silane-in-adhesive alone unless the specific product (e.g., FuturaBond) has been tested and validated.

### 축 3 — 오염 제거 (Contamination Cleaning)

Try-in is an unavoidable clinical step that introduces dual contamination: saliva (proteinaceous pellicle, bacteria) and silicone try-in paste (oily silicone residues). Del Bianco et al. (2025) systematically tested seven approaches on IPS e.max CAD.

| Cleaning Method | SEM Findings | Bond Strength Outcome |
|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Water rinse | Residual contamination | Insufficient (lowest) |
| 37% Phosphoric acid | Residual contamination | Insufficient |
| 70% Ethanol | Residual contamination | Insufficient |
| Ivoclean cleaning paste | Residual contaminants confirmed | Insufficient (worst active agent) |
| 5% HF re-etch (no prior HF) | No complete removal | **Sufficient** (high SBS) |
| Monobond Etch&Prime — MEP (with prior HF) | No complete removal | **Sufficient** (high SBS) |
| Monobond Etch&Prime — MEP (without prior HF) | No complete removal | **Sufficient** (high SBS) |

The three high-performing groups (MEP with or without prior HF; HF re-etch) were statistically equivalent (p>0.05). All cleaning-only groups were significantly inferior (p<0.001).

**Mechanism**: MEP's acid component (20 s rubbing) simultaneously etches surface contaminants and re-exposes ceramic microstructure; the silane component then bonds to the freshly exposed glass phase. This works because the acid-etching action does not require the pre-cleaned baseline that simple surfactant washes depend on.

**Key clinical insight**: Ivoclean — a cleaning paste marketed specifically for this scenario — performed no better than ethanol or water and showed residual silicone contaminants under SEM. The product's marketing claims are not supported by these data.

### 축 4 — 대안 시멘트 (Alternative Luting Agents)

Grangeiro et al. (2024) challenged the default assumption that conventional resin cement is the gold standard for silica-based ceramic adhesive cementation.

| Luting Agent | Feldspathic Baseline μSBS | Feldspathic Post-Aging μSBS | PICN Baseline | PICN Post-Aging |
|--------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Preheated composite 68°C | Highest | Highest | Highest | Highest |
| Conventional resin cement | Intermediate | Intermediate | Intermediate | Intermediate |
| Flowable composite | Lowest | Lowest | Lowest | Lowest |

All groups declined significantly after 5,000 thermal cycles, but rank order was preserved across time points and materials.

**Mechanism**: Heating composite to 68°C for 10 minutes reduces viscosity sufficiently to flow into HF-created microporosity before polymerization — achieving the penetration advantage of low-viscosity resin cement while retaining full filler content and modulus. The resulting interface has both micromechanical interlocking and the mechanical resilience of a high-filler composite.

**Flowable composite as a luting agent is discouraged**: Low modulus increases stress concentration at the ceramic-cement interface under occlusal load, raising ceramic fracture risk beyond the bond strength concern.

## Clinical Protocol Summary [claude 해석]

*This step-by-step protocol is a working clinical hypothesis derived from synthesis of the 9 papers. It does not replace individual manufacturer instructions. All evidence is in vitro.*

### Standard Cementation Protocol (Feldspathic or Lithium Disilicate)

**Step 1 — HF Etching**
- Feldspathic ceramic: 5–10% HF, 60 seconds, room temperature
  - 10% preferred over 5% for higher bond strength (Moura 2020)
  - 1% is insufficient for long-term durability (Venturini 2015)
  - Heating HF adds no benefit for feldspathic (Leyva del Rio 2021)
- Lithium disilicate: 9% HF, 20 seconds
  - Heated HF (70°C) significantly improves bond (~29 MPa vs ~18 MPa) if equipment available (Leyva del Rio 2021)
- Post-HF: Rinse thoroughly. Optional: 37% phosphoric acid + NaHCO₃ neutralization for HF waste management — does NOT impair bond strength (Moura 2020)

**Step 2 — Dedicated Silane Primer**
- Use a prehydrolyzed silane primer (e.g., Calibra Silane or equivalent with confirmed silanol monomer form)
- Do NOT rely on silane incorporated in universal adhesives (except FuturaBond, which was tested and validated — but even this is product-specific)
- Apply silane according to manufacturer instructions; allow solvent evaporation

**Step 3 — Resin Adhesive Layer**
- Apply a resin adhesive after silane priming — this step is critical
- Silane alone provides no significant bond advantage over untreated control for LDS (Simasetha 2021)
- Light-cure the adhesive layer

**Step 4 — Luting Agent Selection**
- Preheated composite (68°C, 10 min preheating) > conventional resin cement > flowable composite (Grangeiro 2024)
- Flowable composite should NOT be used as a luting agent for silica-based ceramics
- Standard dual-cure resin cement is acceptable and clinically proven; preheated composite is an evidence-supported upgrade

### Contamination Recovery Protocol (After Try-In)

If restoration has been tried in (saliva + try-in paste contamination):

- **Do NOT use**: Water rinse, phosphoric acid, ethanol, or Ivoclean — all insufficient after combined contamination (Del Bianco 2025)
- **Use either**:
  - Option A: Monobond Etch&Prime (MEP) — 20 s active rubbing, rinse, dry → proceed directly to cement (MEP contains silane; no additional silane needed)
  - Option B: 5% HF re-etch 20 s → rinse → dry → then standard silane + adhesive protocol
- Both options yield equivalent bond strength (Del Bianco 2025)
- Option A (MEP) is preferred intraorally where HF application is hazardous; Option B is appropriate in the laboratory

### Intraoral Ceramic Chip Repair Protocol

- Dedicated silane + adhesive (e.g., BISCO Intraoral Repair Kit) is mandatory — universal adhesive alone caused 50–70% specimen debonding during thermal cycling (Sagen 2025)
- Surface treatment (HF etching vs. diamond disc roughening) is secondary to bonding agent selection — both surface treatments performed equivalently when the correct bonding protocol was used (Sagen 2025)
- Since intraoral HF application carries tissue toxicity risk with no demonstrated bond advantage over roughening in this context, diamond disc roughening + dedicated silane + adhesive is the preferred intraoral repair approach

## Note on Alammar 2022 (Zirconia Contrast — Non-HF Pathway)

All nine studies in this overview address **silica-based ceramics** (feldspathic, lithium disilicate, PICN) where HF etching is the foundation. Zirconia (Y-TZP) is chemically inert and cannot be HF-etched; its durable bonding pathway is fundamentally different. [[prosthetic-materials/alammar-2022-zirconia-bonding-durability-clinical-outcomes-sr]] (SR, 24 clinical studies) establishes that the optimal clinical protocol for zirconia-to-tooth/implant bonding is **airborne-particle abrasion (APA) or tribochemical silica coating (TBS APA) + MDP monomer-based primer + MDP or phosphoric ester monomer-based resin cement** — and, importantly, that skipping surface pretreatment did not affect clinical outcomes for certain zirconia restoration types on most abutment surfaces, suggesting that MDP primer chemistry alone can compensate in select scenarios. This directly contrasts with the silica-ceramic axis: here, surface pretreatment (HF etching) is non-negotiable for durable bond strength, while cement type is relatively secondary — for zirconia, the primer chemistry (MDP) is the critical variable, not a surface-acid step.

**Why MDP works on zirconia, and the updated evidence (2023–2025).** The mechanism is now resolved: [[resin-bonding/nagaoka-2017-mdp-zirconia-chemical-interaction-nmr]] used NMR to show 10-MDP bonds zirconia via both ionic (deprotonated P–O⁻ ↔ Zr) and hydrogen bonding, concentration-dependent down to ~1 ppb. Recent meta-analyses refine the protocol: [[resin-bonding/su-2023-mdp-resin-zirconia-bonding-durability-sr-ma]] (72-study MA) confirms MDP-zirconia bonds decline with aging (MD 5.58) and that **MDP + an additional surface treatment beats MDP alone** (MD 10.17); the network MA [[resin-bonding/li-2024-zirconia-surface-treatments-monomers-nma]] (77 studies) ranks fine-particle (25–53 µm) air abrasion + 10-MDP cement highest while gas plasma and silane-alone are ineffective. On cement class, [[resin-bonding/borouziniat-2024-self-adhesive-vs-etch-resin-cement-zirconia-sr-ma]] (SR+MA) found self-adhesive ≈ self-etch cement for zirconia (total-etch inferior). For post-try-in saliva contamination, [[resin-bonding/musharbash-2025-cleaning-agents-contaminated-zirconia]] found air-particle abrasion most effective with Ivoclean/Katana Cleaner/Zirclean as viable chemical alternatives (cf. the silica-ceramic cleaning axis above).

## Note on Dhawan 2026 (Tangential Paper)

[[resin-bonding/dhawan-2026-bonding-total-self-etch-fiberpost]] addresses adhesive system selection (total-etch vs. self-etch vs. self-adhesive) for glass fiber post cementation to root dentin — a fundamentally different substrate (dentin, not ceramic) with different bonding mechanisms. Self-etch adhesives achieved the highest push-out bond strength (131.03 N vs. 115.25 N vs. 75.04 N for TE and SA respectively; p=0.001). This paper's findings are NOT transferable to ceramic bonding contexts covered in this overview. It shares the resin-bonding category but addresses tooth-adhesive-post interfaces, not ceramic-silane-cement interfaces. See the paper's own page for clinical implications in endodontic prosthodontics.
