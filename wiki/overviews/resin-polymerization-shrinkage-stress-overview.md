> - **핵심 메시지**: 직접 복합레진의 변연 실패(marginal failure)·법랑질 균열·이차우식의 뿌리는 **중합수축응력 (polymerization shrinkage stress)** 이다. 학계는 이를 세 갈래 — ①단량체 화학(monomer chemistry), ②충전·경화 전략(placement/cure), ③구조 보강(structural reinforcement) — 으로 공격한다.
> - **① 단량체 화학 (저수축, low-shrinkage)**: Bis-GMA/TEGDMA 기질에 폴리우레탄 디메타크릴레이트 (Polyurethane Dimethacrylate, PU-MA) 20 wt% + 글래스 플레이크 충전으로 부피수축 1.35% 달성, 노화 후 물성 안정 (Feng 2024); **티올-엔 단계성장중합 (thiol-ene step-growth)** 으로 gel point를 늦춰 수축응력↓ + 불소화(fluorination)로 세균부착↓ → 미세누출↓·이차우식↓ (He 2026).
> - **② 충전·경화 전략 (벌크필, bulk-fill)**: 3초 초고조사(>3000 mW/cm²)는 수축 strain은 약간↓이나 stress는 약간↑ — **network mobility 감소로 flow 기반 응력완화가 막히기 때문**; AFCT (addition-fragmentation chain transfer) 첨가제는 고조사에서도 응력을 낮춘다 (Algamaiah 2021).
> - **벌크필 기전**: 벌크필 종류 간 수축은 대체로 비슷하고 **충전율 (filler wt%)** 이 수축·파괴인성 (fracture toughness, K_IC)을 좌우; sonication·예열(preheating)은 흐름성만 개선하고 수축·인성 손해 없음 (Yang 2022).
> - **③ 구조 보강 (단섬유강화, SFRC)**: 심하게 undermine된 대형 MOD에서 중합수축응력이 직접수복의 **67–83%에 >3 mm 법랑질 균열**을 유발; **단섬유강화복합재 (Short-Fiber-Reinforced Composite, SFRC) base + CAD/CAM 인레이(최저 V-factor)** 는 균열 거의 0·최고 생존율·복구 가능(reparable) 실패 (Magne 2023).
> - **측정법 caveat**: 동일 재료라도 photoelastic 법(6.4–13.4 MPa)이 contraction-force 법(1.2–4.8 MPa)보다 **수 배 높은** 수축응력 값을 준다 — 두 방법은 강하게 상관(r=0.8)하므로 순위는 보존되나 **논문 간 절대 MPa 비교 금지** (Szczesio-Wlodarczyk 2024).
> - **결정적 통찰 (dark-cure)**: 수축응력의 대부분은 vitrification 도중·이후(이른바 "dark-cure")에 발생 → soft-start/느린 경화는 임상적 전환율에서 응력을 충분히 완화하지 못한다 (Algamaiah 2021, Magne 2023).
> - **자가접착 플로어블 (self-adhesive flowable)**: 통상형 플로어블이 미세경도 최고이고, 자가접착형은 일단계 적용 대가로 물성이 낮으며 세균부착은 표면조도가 아닌 **레진 매트릭스·필러 성분**에 좌우 (Yeloglu-Akbuar 2026).
> - **공통 분모**: 5개 기전 모두 수축응력을 **단량체(분자)·경화(network)·구조(V-factor)** 어느 한 단계에서 줄이는 것 — 어느 한 갈래도 단독으로 응력을 0으로 만들지 못한다.
> - **임상 결론**: 대형 와동일수록 ①저수축 재료 또는 ②인레이(낮은 V-factor)로 수축 부피를 줄이고, ③SFRC base로 균열을 분산하며, 경화 protocol 조정만으로 응력 문제를 해결했다고 착각하지 말 것 — 실패가 일어나더라도 **복구 가능한 양상**으로 설계하는 것이 핵심.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 7 composite-resin bench/animal studies framing polymerization shrinkage stress as the root cause of marginal failure, attacked on three fronts — monomer chemistry (PUDMA 1.35% volumetric shrinkage [Feng 2024]; fluorinated thiol-ene step-growth lowering stress + microleakage [He 2026]), placement/cure strategy (ultra-rapid high-irradiance trades strain↓ for stress↑ but AFCT lowers stress [Algamaiah 2021]; filler wt% governs bulk-fill shrinkage and toughness [Yang 2022]), and structural reinforcement (SFRC base + CAD/CAM inlay cuts shrinkage-induced enamel cracks from 67–83% to ~0% via the lowest V-factor [Magne 2023]) — with a measurement-method caveat (photoelastic 6.4–13.4 MPa vs contraction-force 1.2–4.8 MPa, r=0.8 [Szczesio-Wlodarczyk 2024]) and the self-adhesive flowable class characterized as composition-driven [Yeloglu-Akbuar 2026].

## Summary

Direct composite restorations fail at the margin: microleakage, recurrent (secondary) caries, post-operative sensitivity, cuspal deflection, and — in large cavities — enamel cracking. The common upstream cause is **polymerization shrinkage stress**: as methacrylate monomers convert to polymer, the material contracts (~1.5–3% by volume); once the material is bonded and vitrified, that contraction can no longer be relieved by flow and instead loads the tooth–restoration interface. This overview synthesizes seven 2021–2026 studies that attack shrinkage stress on three complementary fronts, plus a measurement-validity paper that explains why the field's stress numbers are not directly comparable across studies.

The recurring mechanistic insight (Algamaiah 2021; Magne 2023) is that **most stress develops late** — during and after vitrification, in the so-called "dark-cure" period — which is why simply slowing the cure (soft-start) does not solve the problem at clinically relevant conversion. The field therefore intervenes earlier (the monomer itself), differently (network architecture), or structurally (reducing the volume of shrinking material and reinforcing the residual tooth).

## Thesis — Three Fronts Against Shrinkage Stress

Polymerization shrinkage stress is the root cause of direct-composite marginal failure. The 7 papers map onto three attack fronts, with one methodological paper framing how stress is even measured.

### Front 1 — Monomer chemistry (low-shrinkage)

The highest-leverage intervention is the resin matrix itself, because shrinkage originates at the monomer-to-polymer conversion. Two material-development studies exemplify distinct chemistries:

- **Polyurethane dimethacrylate (PUDMA).** **Feng 2024** added a polyurethane dimethacrylate oligomer (PU-MA, 20 wt%) to a Bis-GMA/TEGDMA base with 75 wt% glass-flake/borosilicate fillers, reaching a low **volumetric shrinkage of 1.35%** while *retaining* flexural strength and hardness after aging (60-day water immersion + 5000 thermocycles) better than commercial controls. The trade-off: higher filler loading raised water sorption/solubility and reduced depth of cure.
- **Fluorinated thiol-ene step-growth.** **He 2026** used **thiol-ene step-growth polymerization**, which delays the gel point and thereby lowers shrinkage stress, *combined* with surface fluorination to reduce bacterial adhesion (low surface free energy). The single material (DST-1) showed significantly lower microleakage in Class II restorations, reduced streptococcal adhesion, good wear/polish, and no adverse systemic/tissue response in Wistar rats — at the cost of modestly reduced tensile strength. He 2026 explicitly connects the causal chain: **low shrinkage stress → better marginal sealing → lower secondary-caries risk.**

Both confirm matrix modification as the dominant lever, but neither is a commercial restorative yet (experimental, hand-fabricated; load-limited).

### Front 2 — Placement & cure strategy (bulk-fill kinetics)

Bulk-fill composites and high-irradiance LCUs change *how* the material is placed and cured. The trade-offs are subtle:

- **Ultra-rapid high-irradiance curing.** **Algamaiah 2021** found that 3 s at ~3000–3200 mW/cm² slightly *lowers* shrinkage strain but slightly *raises* shrinkage stress — because reduced network mobility limits flow-based stress relaxation. Crucially, an **AFCT (addition-fragmentation chain transfer) agent** in one material lowered stress even under high-irradiance curing, showing network-rearrangement chemistry can be layered onto fast curing.
- **Bulk-fill kinetics & toughness.** **Yang 2022** measured four bulk-fills with viscosity-reduction features (sonication, pre-heating) and found shrinkage broadly comparable across types, with **filler content (wt%) the dominant correlate of both shrinkage and fracture toughness (K_IC)**. Sonication (SonicFill 3) and pre-heating (Viscalor) improved flowability *without* a shrinkage or toughness penalty — and pre-heating duration (30 s vs 3 min) had no effect.

The placement/cure front buys handling and depth-of-cure convenience but cannot eliminate stress; it can only avoid making it worse (and AFCT can modestly reduce it).

### Front 3 — Structural reinforcement (SFRC + reduced V-factor)

When the cavity is large, no monomer or cure tweak fully protects the residual tooth, so the answer becomes *structural*. **Magne 2023** is the clinical-consequence bookend: in 36 maxillary molars with severely undermined cusps and large MOD slots, shrinkage stress produced **>3 mm enamel cracks in 83% of direct controls and 67% of direct + SFRC base groups, but essentially 0% in CAD/CAM inlays over an SFRC base.** The inlay wins by minimizing the **V-factor** (volume of shrinking material via a thin luting layer), and it also gives the best fatigue survival and the most *reparable* failures (the direct control failed 100% catastrophically). Notably, the flowable SFRC (everX Flow, −2.58% shrinkage) only modestly reduced cracking versus the original everX Posterior (−1.52%) — material shrinkage still matters even within "reinforcing" bases.

### The measurement-method caveat (why the numbers don't line up)

**Szczesio-Wlodarczyk 2024** explains why stress values across these studies should not be compared at face value: on the same 10 commercial composites, the **photoelastic method gave 6.4–13.4 MPa** while the **contraction-force method gave 1.2–4.8 MPa** — a several-fold difference for identical materials. The two correlate strongly (r=0.8), so material *rankings* transfer, but absolute MPa values are method-specific. This is essential context for reading any cross-study stress comparison (and explains why this overview emphasizes *direction* and *ranking* over absolute magnitudes).

### Adjacent class — self-adhesive flowables

**Yeloglu-Akbuar 2026** characterized self-adhesive flowables (one-step, no separate adhesive) against a conventional flowable: the conventional flowable (Filtek Ultimate Flowable) had the highest microhardness, and bacterial adhesion was **composition-dependent, not predicted by surface roughness alone**. Self-adhesive flowables trade mechanical/handling simplicity for lower hardness and are load-limited — relevant because flowables are frequently proposed as low-stress "stress-breaker" liners, but their property profile (and biofilm affinity) is driven by matrix/filler composition, not by being flowable per se.

## Clinical Decision Framework

| Clinical situation | Shrinkage-stress concern | Evidence-based response |
|---|---|---|
| Small–moderate cavity, good handling | Modest; manageable | Standard or bulk-fill composite; filler wt% drives properties (Yang 2022) |
| Want fast cure (high-irradiance LCU) | Strain↓ but **stress↑** | Accept fast cure only if material has stress-relaxing chemistry (AFCT); don't assume soft-start relaxes dark-cure stress (Algamaiah 2021) |
| High secondary-caries risk | Microleakage → recurrent caries | Prefer low-stress + low-adhesion chemistry where available (thiol-ene/fluorinated, He 2026); for flowables, choose by composition not roughness (Yeloglu-Akbuar 2026) |
| Large MOD, undermined cusps | **High; enamel-crack risk** | Reduce V-factor — SFRC base **and** CAD/CAM inlay; SFRC base alone only partly protects (Magne 2023) |
| Choosing a low-shrinkage material | Want lowest volumetric shrinkage | Matrix modification (PUDMA 1.35%, Feng 2024) is the highest-leverage front, but verify aging stability and depth of cure |
| Comparing published stress values | Numbers may not be comparable | Confirm measurement method before comparing MPa across papers (photoelastic ≫ contraction-force; Szczesio-Wlodarczyk 2024) |

**Bottom line:** there is no single fix for shrinkage stress. Reduce it at the monomer (Feng, He), avoid amplifying it at the cure (Algamaiah, Yang), and — in large cavities — reduce the shrinking volume and reinforce structurally (Magne). Read stress numbers by *direction and ranking*, not absolute MPa (Szczesio-Wlodarczyk), and design restorations so that when they do fail, they fail repairably.
