> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - **총의치 수명**: 전통 총의치(Complete Denture, CD)의 가중평균 수명은 10.1년(상악 10.3년, 하악 8.6년); 실패율은 2년 5% → 10년 이상 41%로 증가 (Taylor 2021). 하악이 더 빠르게 실패하는 이유는 잔류치조제 흡수(Residual Ridge Resorption, RRR)가 더 심하기 때문.
> - **디지털 vs 전통 제작**: 밀링(Milling) CAD/CAM 의치는 굴곡강도·표면조도에서 우수하고, 유지력도 전통보다 높다(p=0.015). 3D 프린팅은 적합 정밀도에서 우수하지만 유지력이 전통보다 낮을 수 있다(Avelino 2024). 환자 만족도·OHRQoL은 세 방법 모두 동등. 디지털 워크플로우는 의자 시간 205–233분 절감, 비용도 낮으나 보더 몰딩 인상(Border-Molded Impression)과 시적 의치 단계 생략 시 유지력 상실(37.5%)·재제작(31.3%) 위험 증가.
> - **간소화 vs 전통 제작**: 간소화 기법(2–4회 내원)은 전통 기법(5–6회) 대비 임상 시간 77분·비용 $86 절감, 6개월 이내 환자 만족도 동등(Sanjeevan 2021). 10년 장기 추적에서도 만족도 차이 없음.
> - **고정성 vs 가철성 임플란트 보철**: 임플란트 지지 고정성 완전의치(Implant-supported Fixed Complete Denture, IFCD, g=1.68)와 임플란트 피개의치(Implant-Supported Overdenture, IOD, g=1.26)는 모두 큰 치료 효과를 보이며 전체 환자보고결과(dPRO)에서 유의한 차이 없음(p=.165). 의치 안정성만 IFCD가 유의하게 우수(ES 차이 2.37, p=.032) (Abou-Ayash 2023). 장기 데이터(≥5년, 상악 4임플란트): IFCD가 임플란트(98.5%)·보철물(99.9%) 생존율 높으나 변연골 흡수(Marginal Bone Loss, MBL)는 더 많음(−1.20 vs −0.27 mm) (Lan 2025).
> - **임플란트 수**: 하악 단일 임플란트 IOD 5년 생존율 91.93%·10년 84.62% — 저비용 최소침습 옵션으로 유효(Padmanabhan 2020). **단일(1-IOD) vs 2개(2-IOD) 직접 비교 17편 RCT SR+MA: 임플란트 생존율은 5년까지 동등하나, 의치 파절·재제작·메탈하우징 재부착 같은 보철 합병증은 1-IOD에서 약 2배 잦음(5Y RR 2.10/2.57/2.31); 리라이닝·O-ring 교체는 차이 없음 → 구조강도 부족이 원인이므로 1-IOD 의치상에 금속 보강 권고(FEA: 하우징 인장응력 −61.8%) (Koyama 2025).** 하악 2임플란트 IOD는 단일보다 dPRO가 유의하게 높음(ES 차이 0.73, p<.001). 상악은 최소 4임플란트 필요; 상악 4임플란트 + 금속 보강 + 전체 구개 피개가 보철 실패 최저(8.33%) (Tayari 2024).
> - **어태치먼트 시스템**: 바(Bar)·텔레스코픽(Telescopic) vs 볼(Ball)·LOCATOR — 스플린트 여부는 MBL·합병증·생존율에 유의한 차이 없음(Leao 2018, Prasad 2024). 보철 합병증 순위(적은 순): LOCATOR < Telescopic < Bar < Ball < 마그넷(Magnet); 마그넷은 7.4배 높은 보철 합병증 → 일상 사용 비권고. 4임플란트 바·텔레스코픽만 2임플란트 LOCATOR보다 MBL 유의하게 낮음(Bi 2022). **바(splinted) 선택 시 재료도 결정 변수 — CAD-CAM 밀링 티타늄 bar가 가장 근거가 탄탄(임플란트 생존 ~100%, bar 합병증 7.4%, MBL 0.324 mm, OHIP/만족도 최상); PEEK/BioHPP 폴리머 bar는 임플란트주위염 신호 → 회피 또는 감시 (Chappuis-Chocano 2023).**
> - **의치 접착제**: 크림(Cream)·파우더(Powder) 접착제는 유지력(Kapur 지수), 교합력(Bite Force)을 유의하게 향상(중등도 근거); 스트립·쿠션은 유의한 효과 없음(Koay 2024). 전체적으로 접착제는 유지력 SMD 1.34, 교합력 SMD 0.98, 저작 기능 SMD 0.72 향상(Shu 2021).
> - **유지관리**: 기계적(칫솔)+화학적(알칼리 과산화물) 병용 세정이 단독 방법보다 생물막 제거에 우수(Schmutzler 2021). 마이크로웨이브 소독은 nystatin 국소 항진균 치료와 동등한 의치성 구내염(Denture Stomatitis, DS) 치료 효과(저확실성 근거)(Sousa 2021).
> - **수직교합고경(OVD) 결정**: 단일 방법은 없으며 사전·사후 발치 다중 기법 병용이 표준(Alhajj 2017, Fayad 2025). 안면·손가락 인체계측(Anthropometric)이 표준 OVD와 높은 상관(엄지 길이 r≈0.63)이지만 이질성(I²=99%)이 매우 높아 보조 수단에 그침(Goyal 2026). OVD 과다(+5mm)는 부정적 심리 지표 상승, 과소(−3mm)는 교합력 감소(Matsuda 2014).
> - **핵심 임상 결정 원칙**: 안정성 선호 → IFCD; 비용·가역성 선호 → IOD(2임플란트 하악/4임플란트 상악); 최소 비용 → 단일 임플란트 하악 IOD + 지연 부하; 어태치먼트 → LOCATOR 최소 합병증, 마그넷 회피; 디지털 의치 → 보더 몰딩 인상 + 시적 의치 단계 유지.

## 1. Digital vs Conventional Complete Denture Fabrication

### The Evidence Base

Four SR+MAs provide converging evidence that digitally fabricated complete dentures (Digital Complete Denture, DCD) are clinically equivalent to or better than conventional dentures across most outcomes.

**Mechanical and physical properties (Srinivasan 2021, 73 studies):**
- Milled CAD/CAM dentures are mechanically superior: significantly higher flexural strength, flexural modulus, yield strength, and toughness vs both conventional and 3D-printed (all p < 0.0001)
- 3D-printed dentures show superior trueness of fit vs conventional (p = 0.039) but inferior esthetics (p < 0.0001) and color stability
- Overall CAD/CAM retention is significantly better than conventional (p = 0.015)
- CAD/CAM workflows require less chairside time (p = 0.037) and lower overall cost (p < 0.0001)
- Digital record preservation: a clinically important advantage for geriatric patients with limited follow-up access

**Patient-centered outcomes (Jafarpour 2024, 11 studies; Avelino 2024, 16 studies):**
- Patient satisfaction and OHRQoL show no significant difference between any fabrication method (conventional vs milled vs 3D-printed)
- Milled CDs achieved significantly higher clinician satisfaction (ES = 1.42; I² = 0%) vs conventional — 3D-printed did not
- Milled CDs show fewer post-insertion adjustment visits vs conventional; 3D-printed show no difference from conventional
- Conventional CDs outperform 3D-printed in retention (p = .02), but not milled (p = .20)

**Head-to-head: 3D-printed vs milled (Khorasani 2024, 4 studies):**
- No significant difference in retention (SMD = 0.165, p = 0.343) or patient satisfaction (SMD = −0.595, p = 0.236)
- High heterogeneity (I² = 59–86%) limits conclusions; only 4 eligible clinical studies exist
- Directional trend: 3D printing slightly better retention numerically; milling slightly better satisfaction numerically

### Critical Procedural Points (Thu 2024, 39 studies)

A systematic review of which procedures are actually used in digital denture fabrication revealed:
- **Border-molded impression remains essential**: produces 14.5–16.1 N maxillary base retention vs only 6.2–6.6 N with intraoral scanning — a statistically significant ~60% retention deficit from IOS
- **Trial denture placement is underused but critical**: only 13/39 studies performed it; omission leads to esthetic/occlusal complications and extra unscheduled visits
- Despite workflow improvements, digital dentures still carry substantial complication rates: up to 37.5% retention loss, 31.3% denture remakes, 66.7% pressure sores
- Clinical time savings: 205–233 minutes vs conventional; 2–4 visits most common

### Simplified Conventional Techniques (Sanjeevan 2021, 19 RCTs)

Simplified complete denture techniques (2–4 appointments, stock tray, no facebow, simplified jaw records) save a mean of 77 minutes of clinical time (MD = −77.34, 95% CI −122 to −32) and $86 per case (MD = −85.89) with no difference in patient satisfaction (SMD = 0.02) or OHRQoL at 6 months or beyond. One study (Kawai et al.) showed equivalence maintained at 10-year follow-up. This evidence supports simplified techniques in public health and resource-limited settings without compromising patient outcomes.

### Fabrication Method Decision Summary

| Method | Mechanical strength | Fit accuracy | Retention | Patient satisfaction | Clinician satisfaction | Cost | Visits |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional | Moderate | Reference | Reference | Equivalent | Reference | Higher | 5–6 |
| Simplified conventional | Moderate | Similar | Similar | Equivalent | — | Lower (~$86) | 2–4 |
| CAD/CAM milled | **Superior** | Good | **Better** | Equivalent | **Superior** | Lower | 2–4 |
| 3D-printed | Lower strength | **Best** | Slightly lower | Equivalent | Equivalent | Lower | 2–4 |

## 2. Implant-Retained vs Implant-Supported: Treatment Effects Comparison

Abou-Ayash et al. (2023) is the key SR+MA (28 prospective studies, n = 1457) quantifying treatment effects on dental patient-reported outcomes (dPROs) for implant-supported fixed complete dentures (IFCDs) vs implant overdentures (IODs):

| Outcome | IFCD (Hedges' g) | IOD (Hedges' g) | Between-group p |
|---|---|---|---|
| Overall dPRO | 1.68 (1.15–2.20) | 1.26 (0.99–1.52) | .165 (NS) |
| Denture stability | — | — | .032 (**IFCD superior**, ES diff 2.37) |
| Mandibular 2 vs 1 implant | — | 0.73 advantage for 2 implants | <.001 |
| Attachment type (balls vs bars) | — | NS | NS |

Both prosthesis types achieve large (g > 0.8) positive treatment effects, qualifying as clinically meaningful improvements from the edentulous/conventional denture baseline. The only outcome where IFCD significantly outperforms IOD is **denture stability** — the decisive factor for patient counseling. Attachment type (ball vs bar) and follow-up duration did not moderate outcomes, suggesting PRO-based attachment choice is not evidence-justified.

**Long-term (≥5 years, maxillary 4-implant, Lan 2025, 16 studies):**

| Outcome | IOD | IFR (IFCD) |
|---|---|---|
| Implant survival | 94.5% | **98.5%** |
| Prosthesis survival | 85.0% | **99.9%** |
| Marginal bone loss (5y) | **−0.27 mm** | −1.20 mm |
| Main complication | Retention loss (0.34/pt) | Acrylic tooth fracture (0.09/pt) |

IFCDs offer superior implant and prosthesis survival but at the cost of greater peri-implant bone loss. IODs preserve bone but carry higher maintenance burden, primarily retention loss. Both are clinically predictable at medium-term.

## 3. Overdenture: Implant Number — 1 vs 2 vs 4

### Single Implant (Padmanabhan 2020, 17 studies)

Single implant-retained mandibular overdentures (SIROs) are a validated, cost-effective option:
- 5-year implant survival: 91.93%
- 10-year implant survival: 84.62%
- Loading protocol hierarchy (survival): Delayed > Immediate > Early (differences NS)
- Ball abutment fracture rate: 10.8 per 100 prosthesis-years; Locator maintenance rate: 16.84 per 100 prosthesis-years
- Prosthetic failure (base fractures) ranges 36.3%–72% — the dominant limitation
- Indicated for: financial constraints, systemic health limitations, minimal surgical risk

**Direct 1-IOD vs 2-IOD head-to-head (Koyama 2025, 17 RCTs, follow-up 12–60 mo):**
The strongest direct comparison cleanly dissociates survival from maintenance burden:
- **Implant survival is identical** at 1Y / 3Y / 5Y, including across loading-protocol subgroups — there is no survival penalty for the single implant.
- **Prosthetic complications run ~2× higher with 1-IOD**: denture fracture (5Y RR 2.10, 1.21–3.64, p=0.008), new-denture fabrication (5Y RR 2.57, 1.11–5.94, p=0.03), metal-housing reattachment (5Y RR 2.31, 1.03–5.18, p=0.04).
- **Relining and O-ring replacement do NOT differ** — the authors read this as evidence the extra fractures stem from reduced *structural strength* under the single-implant fulcrum, not greater denture mobility. Cited FEA: 1-IOD shows ~50% greater total deformation; a **metal framework cuts implant-housing tensile stress by 61.8%** → metal reinforcement is recommended for 1-IOD bases.
- This **updates and partly overturns** an earlier SR (Ahmed et al.) that had reported *better* implant survival for 1-IOD; once post-2017 RCTs are added, that survival advantage disappears.
- Cost: 1-IOD ≈ 75% of 2-IOD, but long-term complications + framework reinforcement narrow the gap.

### Two Implants — Mandibular Minimum Standard (McGill/York Consensus)

- PRO advantage over single implant: ES = 0.73 (p < 0.001) — clinically meaningful (Abou-Ayash 2023)
- Implant failure rate: 0%–2.5% (vs 0%–8.9% for single); note Koyama 2025 (RCT-only) finds *implant survival equivalent* between 1- and 2-IOD — the 2-IOD advantage is in prosthetic durability, not implant survival
- Delayed loading is optimal; immediate loading must use metal reinforcement
- The two-implant mandibular overdenture remains the evidence-based minimum standard for patients prioritizing retention/support or unable to attend frequent maintenance; 1-IOD suits lower initial cost / lower invasiveness when frequent maintenance is feasible (Koyama 2025)

### Four Implants — Mandibular and Maxillary

- 4-implant overdentures: implant survival rate near 100% in delayed loading (Bi 2022)
- 4-bar and 4-telescopic overdentures show significantly less MBL than 2-locator overdentures — the only significant peri-implant difference across attachment combinations
- Maxillary arch requires minimum 4 implants for IOD; 2-implant maxillary IOD carries high implant failure (Tayari 2024)
- 6-implant maxillary IOD: lowest implant failure but higher prosthetic maintenance burden

### Failure Risk Factors (Tayari 2024, 20 studies, 2018–2023)

Independent risk factors for implant-supported overdenture failure:

| Risk Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Maxillary arch | Avg failure ~5% vs mandibular ~3.3%; Type III/IV bone |
| Insufficient implant number | Fewer implants → higher per-implant load |
| Immediate loading | All failures in one trial occurred within first 12 weeks |
| Poor systemic health (diabetes, smoking, bisphosphonates, head/neck irradiation) | Elevated implant failure |
| Absence of metal framework | Maxillary partial coverage without metal: 60%–70% prosthetic failure; with metal + total palatal: 8.33% |
| Locator attachment (maxillary) | Higher peri-implantitis incidence than bar in some studies |
| Natural dentition antagonist (maxillary IOD only) | Elevates maxillary IOD failure risk |

## 4. Attachment Systems — Ball vs Bar vs Magnetic vs Locator

### Splinted vs Unsplinted (Leao 2018, 9 studies; Prasad 2024, 25 RCTs)

The most actionable finding is **equivalence**: splinted (bar) and unsplinted (ball, locator) overdenture attachments show no significant difference in:
- Marginal bone loss: MD −0.11 mm (95% CI −0.37 to 0.14, P = .39) — far below 1.5 mm biologic failure threshold
- Prosthetic complications: RR 1.26 (P = .31)
- Implant survival: RR 0.37% (P = .14)
- Biological complications (splinted vs unsplinted): P = .902

This finding removes a key rationale for mandatory bar use. Attachment choice can be individualized based on cost, hygiene access, inter-implant angulation, and patient preference without compromising clinical outcomes.

### Prosthetic Complication Hierarchy (Prasad 2024, 25 RCTs — best evidence)

From fewest to most prosthetic complications:
**LOCATOR < Telescopic/Conus < Bar < Ball < Magnet**

Magnets carry 7.4× higher prosthetic complications vs all other attachment systems — the strongest evidence against routine magnet use in current literature.

### System-Specific Profiles

| Attachment | Pros | Cons | Best indication |
|---|---|---|---|
| LOCATOR | Fewest prosthetic complications (RCT evidence); simple maintenance | Moderate maintenance frequency | Default unsplinted choice; widely indicated |
| Ball/Stud | Low complication vs locator (RR 0.55, Sutariya 2021); handles non-parallel implants | Higher fracture rate | Non-parallel implants; limited inter-arch space |
| Bar (splinted) | Best retention (Sutariya 2021); lower MBL with 4 implants; CAD-CAM milled titanium ~100% ISR, low complications (Chappuis-Chocano 2023) | Technical complexity; cost; hygiene access; polymer (PEEK/BioHPP) bars carry peri-implantitis signal | Maximum retention priority; 4-implant designs; prefer milled titanium bar material |
| Telescopic/Conus | Best patient satisfaction + mucosal health (qualitative); low peri-implant tissue reaction | Higher initial cost; laboratory complexity | Patient satisfaction priority; good bone support |
| Magnet | — | 7.4× prosthetic complications; demagnetization; wear | Not recommended for routine use |

### CAD-CAM Milled Bars — Bar Material Selection (Chappuis-Chocano 2023, 10 studies, SR+MA)

When a bar (splinted) design is chosen, the bar can now be CAD-CAM milled rather than conventionally soldered, and the bar *material* becomes its own decision. This SR+MA (10 studies: 1 RCT, 1 pseudorandomized, 6 prospective, 2 retrospective; follow-up 3 mo–4 yr) stratifies milled-bar outcomes by material:

| Bar material | Implant survival | Complications / signal |
|---|---|---|
| Milled titanium | ~100% (9 studies, 100% ISR) | **Best-evidenced**: pooled bar-level complications 7.4%, biological 2.8%, MBL 0.324 mm; best OHIP/satisfaction |
| Zirconia | ~100% | Promising; bone loss ≈ titanium; no peri-implantitis; under-evidenced |
| PEEK / BioHPP (polymer) | ~100% | **Peri-implantitis signal** (none with Ti/zirconia) — possibly from hygiene-limiting design + microbial accumulation on polymer; needs vigilance |

Key points:
- Milled titanium vs conventional soldered gold bar: **RR 0.638 (95% CI 0.108–3.755, p=0.619)** — no significant complication difference, but milled bars avoid the soldered-extension fracture mode.
- MBL pooled 0.324 mm but with very high heterogeneity (I²=97.5%) — interpret cautiously; bone loss was independent of bar metal (zirconia ≈ titanium ≈ conventional).
- Overall GRADE very low to moderate; PEEK/BioHPP/zirconia need long-term RCTs.
- **Clinical steer**: milled titanium is the default evidence-based bar material (maxilla and mandible) up to 4 years; reserve polymer bars for cases warranting peri-implantitis surveillance.

### Peri-implant Health by Attachment + Number (Bi 2022, 28 studies, Bayesian NMA)

- Overall implant survival rate: 98.4% (43 failures in 2,666 implants)
- Only statistically significant MBL difference: 4-bar < 2-locator; 4-telescopic < 2-locator
- 4-locator ranked best for probing depth (PPD)
- 2-magnet and 2-bar ranked worst for plaque index
- All other comparisons across MBL, PPD, plaque index, bleeding index, gingival index: non-significant

## 5. Denture Adhesives — Evidence for Retention and Function

### Efficacy vs Placebo/No Adhesive (Shu 2021, 39 RCTs/CCTs)

Denture adhesives (DAs) provide statistically and clinically significant improvements:

| Outcome | SMD | 95% CI | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Denture retention | **1.34** | 0.89–1.79 | <0.001 |
| Maximum bite force | **0.98** | 0.50–1.47 | <0.001 |
| Masticatory performance | **0.72** | 0.23–1.22 | 0.004 |

The retention effect (SMD 1.34) is in the large range, confirming DAs as a conservative management tool for declining retention and function. Clinical indications: xerostomia, compromised ridge anatomy, frail/handicapped patients, and the CD adaptation period.

### Type Comparison — Which DA is Best? (Koay 2024, 17 RCTs, NMA)

First network meta-analysis directly comparing adhesive types:

| Adhesive type | Bite force | Mandibular retention | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cream | Effective (incisal RR 7.63; premolar/molar RR 33.66) | Effective (RR 1.27) | Moderate GRADE |
| Powder | Effective (premolar/molar RR 36.00) | **Best** (RR 1.54) | Moderate GRADE |
| Strip | NS for any outcome | NS | Low |
| Cushion | NS for any outcome | NS | Low |

SUCRA rankings: Powder > Cream for mandibular Kapur's retention; Cream > others for incisal bite force; Powder ≈ Cream for premolar/molar bite force.

**Clinical recommendation**: Cream and powder adhesives are the evidence-based first-line options. Strip and cushion adhesives lack significant support in any outcome category. No adhesive type achieved significance for OHRQoL measures.

## 6. Maintenance — Cleaning Protocols and Stomatitis Management

### Cleaning Protocols for Removable Dental Prostheses (Schmutzler 2021, 44 prospective studies)

**Gold standard**: Combined mechanical + chemical cleaning outperforms either approach alone.

| Modality | Efficacy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brushing alone | Moderate | Technique-dependent; adequate daily maintenance but insufficient for thorough disinfection |
| Alkaline peroxide (effervescent tablets) | Good | Overnight soaking; effective against Candida; standard first-line chemical |
| Sodium hypochlorite | High antimicrobial | Effective at dilute concentrations; high concentration may affect PMMA color stability |
| Ricinus communis | Comparable to NaOCl | Evidence in some studies |
| Microwave disinfection | Supported | Clinically effective; especially for denture stomatitis |
| Ultrasonic cleaning | Supported | Effective; reduces manual effort |

**Recommended two-step daily protocol**: mechanical brushing (all surfaces) + overnight chemical soaking in alkaline peroxide tablet solution.

Evidence on material property effects (color stability, surface roughness, dimensional stability) of cleaning agents on PMMA remains limited — a recognized clinical research gap, especially for newer 3D-printed resin types.

### Denture Stomatitis (Sousa 2021, 3 RCTs)

Microwave disinfection of complete dentures is as effective as topical nystatin for treating denture stomatitis (Candida albicans):
- Candida on denture surface: equivalent (p > .05)
- Clinical signs of stomatitis: equivalent (p > .05)
- One advantage for microwave: palatal Candida reduction at 90 days (SMD 0.47, 95% CI 0.02–0.91, p < .05)
- GRADE certainty: **low** (only 3 RCTs)
- Clinical implication: microwave disinfection is a viable drug-free alternative when antifungal therapy is contraindicated or drug resistance is a concern

**Microwave protocol**: place denture in water-filled microwave-safe container; 650W for 3 minutes (most common protocol in included studies). Do NOT microwave metal-reinforced prostheses.

## 7. Longevity — Survival and Complication Rates

### Conventional Complete Dentures (Taylor 2021, 42 studies)

| Arch | Weighted mean longevity | SD |
|---|---|---|
| Maxillary | 10.3 years | 3.8 |
| Mandibular | 8.6 years | 2.6 |
| Overall | 10.1 years | 4.0 |

**Pooled failure proportions over time:**

| Follow-up | Failure proportion | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|
| ≤2 years | 5% | 0–10% |
| 5–6 years | 12% | 8–16% |
| ≥10 years | **41%** | 28–53% |

Maxillary dentures consistently outlast mandibular due to less severe residual ridge resorption. Denture age is the dominant failure predictor — proactive replacement counseling after 10 years of service is evidence-supported. Note: this dataset predates widespread CAD/CAM adoption; digital denture longevity data remain unavailable.

### Implant-Supported Overdentures

| Design | Implant survival | Prosthesis survival | Time frame |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mandibular 1-implant IOD | 91.93% | ~64–72% (base fracture issue) | 5 years |
| Mandibular 2-implant IOD (bar) | ~97.5–100% | ~87.3% | Per study range |
| Maxillary 4-implant IOD | 94.5% | 85.0% | ≥5 years |
| Maxillary 4-implant IFR | **98.5%** | **99.9%** | ≥5 years |

Implant-supported designs far exceed conventional denture longevity benchmarks, particularly for prosthesis survival in fixed designs. The prosthesis survival deficit of IODs is driven primarily by retention loss (attachment wear) rather than catastrophic failure.

## 8. Occlusal Vertical Dimension (OVD) — Determination for Complete Denture Patients

OVD determination is a foundational step in complete denture fabrication; errors in OVD are immediately harmful to patients.

### Why OVD Accuracy Matters (Matsuda 2014)

In 21 complete-denture wearers, duplicate dentures at altered VDO demonstrated:
- +5 mm (increased OVD) → significant rise in EEG negative-mood indicator after chewing (p < .05)
- −3 mm (decreased OVD) → significant decrease in occlusal force (p < .05)
- Errors in either direction cause measurable physiological and psychological sequelae

**Signs of excessive (increased) OVD**: muscle fatigue, speech difficulty with clicking teeth, accelerated ridge resorption, prosthetic instability, stretched/strained lower facial appearance.
**Signs of reduced (decreased) OVD**: impaired mastication, TMD risk, aged/overclosed face — shortened lower facial third, deepened mentolabial fold, drooping commissures, increased mentalis strain, loss of lip support.

### Why Rest Position Alone is Unreliable (Sheppard 1975)

Cephalometric study (n = 50 complete-denture wearers) showed:
- Facial soft tissue masks true mandibular movement — facial measurements misrepresent skeletal position
- Edentulous rest position is variable and shifts on denture insertion (usually increases)
- Edentulous rest position is not a reliable sole basis for OVD determination

### Method Classification and Evidence (Alhajj 2017, Fayad 2025)

**Pre-extraction methods** (when available): intraoral measurement, profile tracing, cephalometric records, existing denture transfer — capture natural OVD before tooth loss.

**Post-extraction methods** (primary clinical tools):
- **Freeway space method**: physiologic rest position − freeway space (2–4 mm) — most commonly used
- **Phonetic methods**: sibilant "s"/"ch" closest speaking space ≈ 1–2 mm; vowel "O" ≈ 5.5 mm / "E" ≈ 7.5 mm clearance
- **Facial esthetic/proportions**: facial thirds, lip support, lip-teeth relationship — subjective
- **Craniofacial landmark measurements**: Willis gauge (subnasion→gnathion), calipers, Knebelman craniometer — population-dependent
- **Digital methods**: 3D facial scanning, CBCT, virtual articulators, AI prediction — emerging precision tools

**No single method is universally superior** — all are empirical. Clinical standard: combine at least two methods (typically freeway space + phonetics + esthetic evaluation).

### Anthropometric Methods (Goyal 2026, 13 cross-sectional studies, n = 3275)

First meta-analytic ranking of facial/finger measurement proxies for OVD:
- **Strongest overall**: thumb length (r = 0.63 common-effects; I² = 99%)
- **Strongest in men**: distal outer canthus to rima oris distance (r ≈ 0.57–0.58; I² = 86%)
- **Strongest in women**: little-finger length (r ≈ 0.46; I² = 94%)

The very high heterogeneity (I² up to 99%) across all anthropometric methods means no single proxy is dependable across populations. Anthropometric measurements serve as corroborating tools only, not as standalone OVD determinants.

## 9. Clinical Decision Guide

### Fabrication Method

```
Is the patient edentulous and needs a new complete denture?
│
├── Resource-limited / public health setting → Simplified conventional (2–4 visits, stock tray)
│   └── Equivalent satisfaction; saves 77 min + $86 per case
│
├── Standard care → CAD/CAM milled
│   └── Superior fit, strength, clinician satisfaction; requires same visits as simplified digital
│
└── Cost-sensitive but wants digital → 3D-printed
    └── Use only if border-molded impression + trial denture steps retained
        (omission → 37.5% retention loss risk)
```

### Implant Planning for Overdentures

```
Arch and implant number:
│
├── MANDIBULAR
│   ├── Minimum viable → 1 implant (delayed loading, LOCATOR/ball)
│   │   └── 5y ISR 91.93%; implant survival = 2-IOD (Koyama 2025) but ~2× prosthetic
│   │       complications (fracture/remake/housing) → REINFORCE base with metal framework
│   │       (FEA: −61.8% housing tensile stress); cost ≈ 75% of 2-IOD; needs frequent maintenance
│   ├── Standard of care → 2 implants (delayed loading)
│   │   └── Significant PRO advantage over 1 implant (ES 0.73, p<.001); fewer prosthetic
│   │       complications than 1-IOD though implant survival equivalent (Koyama 2025)
│   └── Maximum peri-implant bone preservation → 4 implants + bar/telescopic
│       └── Only combination showing significant MBL advantage vs 2-locator
│
└── MAXILLARY
    ├── Minimum → 4 implants (lower bone density Type III/IV)
    ├── Fixed vs removable → IOD better MBL; IFR better survival
    └── Metal framework + total palatal coverage MANDATORY if IOD
        (partial coverage without metal = 60–70% prosthetic failure)
```

### Attachment Selection for Mandibular IOD

```
Priorities:
├── Lowest maintenance burden → LOCATOR (fewest prosthetic complications)
├── Non-parallel implants / limited inter-arch → Ball/stud
├── Maximum retention → Bar (prefer CAD-CAM milled titanium; avoid polymer PEEK/BioHPP bars — peri-implantitis signal)
├── Best patient satisfaction + mucosal health → Telescopic/Conus
└── Avoid → Magnet (7.4× prosthetic complications)

Splinting: bar (splinted) vs others (unsplinted) = equivalent
biological and implant outcomes (P = .902) — do NOT mandate bar
for biological reasons; use clinical/cost criteria
```

### Fixed vs Removable Decision

```
Patient priority:
├── Stability is primary complaint → IFCD
│   └── Only outcome where IFCD significantly outperforms IOD (p=.032)
├── Cost / reversibility / bone preservation → IOD
│   └── Both achieve large PRO treatment effects (g>1.0)
│   └── IOD: less MBL long-term (−0.27 vs −1.20 mm at 5y)
└── Both options: no overall PRO superiority of fixed over removable
    (p=.165) — patient counseling should focus on stability, not assumption
    that "fixed = better experience"
```

### Maintenance Scheduling

- **Daily**: mechanical brushing (all surfaces) + chemical soak (alkaline peroxide overnight)
- **Denture stomatitis present**: microwave disinfection (650W, 3 min) OR nystatin topical — equivalent efficacy
- **Metal-reinforced prosthesis**: no microwave; chemical soak only
- **Denture age**: counsel on replacement at 8–10 years (mandibular sooner); failure probability exceeds 40% at 10+ years
- **Denture adhesive**: cream or powder if retention inadequate; strip/cushion lack evidence
- **Recall interval**: for IOD patients with peri-implant disease history, 3–4 month intervals; long-term maxillary IOD carries 20%–38.5% peri-implantitis rate at 10 years
