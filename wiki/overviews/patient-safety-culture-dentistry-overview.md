> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - **핵심 명제**: 치과는 환자안전문화(Patient Safety Culture)에서 의료(medicine)에 뒤처지지만, 그 격차를 좁히는 측정 가능하고 개선 가능한 핵심 지렛대는 팀워크(Teamwork)다. 측정→팀워크 투자→인적요인 설계로 따라잡는다는 것이 이 종합의 결론.
> - **격차의 크기 (Yansane 2020)**: 미국 4개 치과기관 단면조사(수정 MOSPSC, n=656, 응답률 40.6%)에서 의료기관이 안전문화 10개 영역 중 **9개**, 전반적 질 항목 6/6, 환자안전·질 이슈 8/9에서 치과를 앞섰다 — 즉 거의 전 영역에서 치과가 뒤처졌다.
> - **치과의 상대적 강점**: 그 와중에 치과가 가장 강했던 영역은 조직학습(Organizational Learning) **85%**, 팀워크(Teamwork) **79%** — 따라잡기의 발판이 바로 여기에 있다.
> - **근거가 적용되는 현장 (Bailey 2021)**: 치과 진료의 약 **95%가 일차의료(primary care, 개원가)** 에서 이뤄지지만, 환자안전 근거 대부분은 2차 진료·학계에서 생산됐다 — 근거가 만들어지지 않은 곳에 적용되고 있는 불일치.
> - **인적요인 (Human Factors)**: 오류는 개인의 잘못이 아니라 시스템·환경·팀 상호작용에서 발생한다 — 비난(blame)이 아니라 시스템 설계로 접근.
> - **제2의 피해자 (Second Victim)**: 유해사건에 연루된 임상의·팀원 자신도 고통·죄책감·자신감 상실로 피해를 입으며, 비처벌적 지지가 안전문화의 일부다(징벌은 보고를 억제).
> - **팀워크→성과 (Schmutz 2019, 일반 의료 근거 차용)**: 급성기 의료팀 31편(1390팀) SR+MA에서 팀워크↔임상성과 **r=0.28(≈OR 2.8, 중간 크기)**, 팀 구성·크기·과제·환자 현실성 조절변수 모두 비유의 — 일상 진료에서도 효과가 일관(위기 상황 한정 아님).
> - **팀워크→안전 (Manser 2009, 일반 의료 근거 차용)**: 사건분석·종사자 인식·관찰연구 세 갈래가 팀워크를 진료 질·안전의 결정인자로 확립하고, 팀워크/리더십→직원 웰빙→안전한 진료 능력의 사슬을 제시.
> - **중요한 단서**: 팀워크 기둥(Schmutz·Manser)은 수술실·중환자실·응급실 등 **일반 의료 근거를 치과에 유추 적용**한 것 — 치과 고유 추정치가 아니라 방향성 앵커로 봐야 한다. 치과 안전문화 연구 자체는 아직 미성숙(주로 2차 진료·학계).
> - **임상·운영 takeaway**: ① MOSPSC로 우리 치과의 안전문화를 측정하고, ② 근거가 가장 강하고 개선 가능한 팀워크에 투자하며, ③ 인적요인 관점으로 워크플로를 설계하고, ④ 제2의 피해자를 지지하며, ⑤ 과잉규제(over-regulation)는 개방적 보고·학습을 억제하므로 경계한다.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 4 papers arguing that dentistry trails medicine on patient safety culture (medicine beat dentistry on 9/10 MOSPSC dimensions; dentistry strongest in organizational learning 85% and teamwork 79%), that ~95% of dental care happens in primary care where the safety evidence was not generated, and that teamwork — the dental safety culture's relative strength and a measurable, improvable driver of clinical performance (r=0.28, OR≈2.8) and safety in general healthcare — is the most actionable lever to close the gap, alongside human-factors design, second-victim support, and restraint on over-regulation.

## 한줄요약

치과는 안전문화 10개 영역 중 9개에서 의료에 뒤처지고(조직학습 85%·팀워크 79%가 그나마 강점), 진료의 95%가 근거가 생산되지 않은 일차의료에서 이뤄진다 — 격차를 좁히는 측정·개선 가능한 핵심 지렛대는 팀워크(일반 의료 근거 r=0.28·OR 2.8 차용)이며, 인적요인 설계·제2의 피해자 지지·과잉규제 경계가 이를 뒷받침한다고 본 4편 종합.

## Thesis

A patient safety culture is the shared set of values, attitudes, and behaviours that make safe care the default rather than the exception. Two propositions organize the dental evidence:

1. **Dentistry lags medicine, and the gap is measurable.** Yansane (2020), using the AHRQ Medical Office Survey on Patient Safety Culture (MOSPSC) modified for dentistry across 4 US dental institutions (n=656, 40.6% response), found medical institutions outperformed dental on **9 of 10** safety-culture dimensions, on **6 of 6** overall-quality items, and on **8 of 9** patient-safety/quality issues. The gap is not a vague impression — it is benchmarkable with a validated instrument.

2. **Teamwork is the dental safety culture's relative strength *and* its most improvable lever.** In the same survey, dental clinics scored highest on **organizational learning (85%)** and **teamwork (79%)** — the two dimensions where dentistry is least behind. Teamwork is also where the strongest causal evidence sits: in general healthcare it correlates with clinical performance at **r=0.28 (≈OR 2.8)** (Schmutz 2019) and is established as a determinant of patient safety through three convergent evidence streams (Manser 2009). The strategic implication: build on the strength you already have.

Underpinning both is **where the work happens**. Bailey (2021) notes that **~95% of dental care is delivered in primary care**, yet most dental patient-safety research originates from secondary care and academia. The evidence is being applied in a setting where it was largely not generated — so the practical toolkit (human factors, second-victim support, a non-punitive culture, restraint on over-regulation) matters as much as the metrics.

**Caveat carried throughout this page:** the *teamwork pillar* (Schmutz, Manser) is **general-healthcare evidence extrapolated to dentistry**, drawn from acute/dynamic domains (surgery, anaesthesia, ICU, ED, obstetrics). The dental clinic is a smaller, lower-acuity team; treat the magnitudes (r=0.28, OR 2.8) as directional anchors, not transplanted dental estimates. The dental literature itself does not supply these effect sizes.

## Evidence Map

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Yansane 2020 — Assessing Patient Safety Culture in Dentistry | Cross-sectional survey (MOSPSC modified for dentistry), 4 US dental institutions | 656 / 1,615 (40.6%) | Medicine beat dentistry on **9/10** safety-culture dimensions, 6/6 quality items, 8/9 safety/quality issues; dental strongest in **organizational learning 85%**, **teamwork 79%** | cross-sectional (dental-specific) |
| Bailey 2021 — Developing a Patient Safety Culture in Primary Dental Care | Narrative review / perspective (Prim Dent J) | — | **~95%** of dental care is in primary care, but safety evidence comes from secondary care/academia; toolkit = human factors, second-victim support, non-punitive culture, caution vs over-regulation | narrative-review (dental-specific) |
| Schmutz 2019 — How effective is teamwork really? | SR + meta-analysis (PRISMA, REML), acute care | 31 studies / 1390 teams | Teamwork ↔ clinical performance **r=0.28 (95% CI 0.20–0.35), OR≈2.8**; all 6 moderators non-significant (effect holds in routine care, not just crises) | sr+ma — **general healthcare, cross-applied** |
| Manser 2009 — Teamwork and patient safety in dynamic domains | Narrative review (OR/ICU/ED/trauma) | — | Teamwork is a determinant of safety via 3 streams: critical incidents, provider perceptions↔well-being, observed communication/coordination/leadership | narrative-review — **general healthcare, cross-applied** |

The two dental-specific papers (Yansane, Bailey) supply the *state of dental safety culture* and the *how-to*. The two general-healthcare papers (Schmutz, Manser) supply the *quantitative and conceptual rationale for the teamwork pillar*, extrapolated to dentistry by analogy — the dental evidence base does not yet contain its own equivalents.

## Clinical Decision Points

How a dental practice actually builds and measures a safety culture:

1. **Measure first — use MOSPSC.** Administering the dentistry-modified Medical Office Survey on Patient Safety Culture is the concrete, validated way to baseline your clinic's safety culture and identify weak dimensions (Yansane 2020). Repeat it to track movement. You cannot improve what you do not measure, and safety culture is heterogeneous across academic, private, and medical settings — your clinic's profile is not assumed.

2. **Invest in teamwork — the evidence-strongest, most improvable dimension.** It is already dentistry's relative strength (79%, Yansane) and the dimension with the best causal support (r=0.28, OR≈2.8, Schmutz; three-stream determinant of safety, Manser). Because the effect holds in **routine** care and across team configurations (moderators non-significant), team training should not be confined to emergency/crisis scenarios — embed communication, coordination, and leadership into everyday workflow (handoffs, briefings, shared mental models).

3. **Design for human factors, not blame.** Treat errors as emerging from system, environment, and team interactions rather than individual fault (Bailey 2021). Redesign the work system — checklists, standardized handoffs, environmental ergonomics — so the safe action is the easy action.

4. **Support the second victim.** The clinician or team member involved in an adverse event is themselves harmed (distress, guilt, eroded confidence). A supportive, non-punitive response is part of the safety culture, not separate from it — and punitive responses suppress the open reporting the whole system depends on (Bailey 2021). This intersects directly with staff well-being, which Manser ties to the capacity to deliver safe care.

5. **Avoid over-regulation.** Excessive prescriptive regulation can be counterproductive, suppressing the open reporting and learning a genuine safety culture requires (Bailey 2021). Aim for a learning culture, not a compliance-checklist culture.

## Gaps & Future Research

- **Dental safety-culture research is immature.** Most of it comes from secondary care and academia, while ~95% of care is delivered in primary/private practice (Bailey 2021). The single dental benchmark here (Yansane) is cross-sectional, US-only, n=656 at 40.6% response, and heavily academic-dental — generalizability to community/private dental practice and to other health systems is unestablished.
- **The teamwork pillar is extrapolated, not dental-specific.** The r=0.28 / OR 2.8 effect and the three-stream determinant model come from acute-care medicine (Schmutz, Manser), and Schmutz explicitly cautions his results may not generalize to long-term or low-acuity care. There is **no dental-specific quantification** of the teamwork→performance or teamwork→safety relationship. A dental teamwork–outcome study is the obvious missing piece.
- **Outcome-level evidence is thin even in the borrowed base.** In Schmutz, the teamwork↔hard-patient-outcome correlation (r=0.13, k=4) is weaker and rests on only 4 studies; most evidence is process-level (r=0.30).
- **No validated dental team-performance instrument.** Manser's stated future challenge — validated team-performance assessment tools and integrative models — remains open, and is doubly open for dentistry, which has not yet developed its own.
