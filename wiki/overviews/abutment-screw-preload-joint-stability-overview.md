## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 핵심 명제: 나사 풀림 (Screw Loosening, ASL)은 임플란트 보철에서 가장 흔한 기계적 합병증(5년 ~10.4%, 10년 ~20.8%; 발생률 5–15%)이고, 그 상류 원인은 전하중 (Preload) 손실 — 체결 시 생기는 축방향 압착력의 소실이다. 가해진 토크 중 실제 전하중으로 전환되는 비율은 ~8–10%에 불과하다 (Lee 2025).
> - 두 가지 전하중 손실 기전: ① 세틀링 (Settling/Embedment Relaxation) — 무하중에서도 접촉면 미세조도가 초기 체결로 붕괴하며 2–10% 전하중이 빠진다 (Pardal-Peláez 2017), ② 동적 피로 (Cyclic Fatigue) — 저작하중 반복으로 추가 손실, 전체 피로 후 제거 토크 손실은 16.1–39% 범위 (Pardal-Peláez 2017).
> - 마찰 (Friction)이 핵심 레버 (Bulaqi 2015, FEA): 나사산이 거칠수록(마찰계수↑) 세틀링↑·잔류 전하중↓. 재조임 (Retightening)은 세틀링을 줄이되 전하중은 거의 회복 못 하며, 그 이득은 마찰이 높을수록 커진다.
> - 재조임 프로토콜: 초기 체결 후 ~10분(또는 3–10분) 뒤 재토크 — 세틀링으로 빠진 전하중을 회복하는 표준 권고 (Nithyapriya 2018, Vinhas 2022). 다회 재조임이 단회보다 높은 제거 토크값 (Removal Torque Value, RTV)을 준다 (Vinhas 2022).
> - 연결부 형태 (Connection Design) 논쟁: SR 레벨에서는 내부연결·특히 모스 테이퍼 (Morse Taper)가 풀림에 가장 강함 (Pardal-Peláez 2017). 그러나 Vinhas 2022(180 임플란트)는 외부 vs 내부 연결이 전하중 유지에 차이 없음을 보여 — 기하학보다 절차(재조임)가 결정적임을 시사.
> - 세틀링은 테이퍼가 급할수록 커진다 (Kim 2020): 외부 버트조인트 0.8 µm < 내부 육각 ~10–11 µm < 내부 8° 모스 테이퍼 옥타곤 19–25 µm. 같은 세틀링이 지대주 유형에 따라 정반대 결과 — 2-piece(나사고정)는 전하중 손실, 1-piece(마찰고정)는 오히려 제거 토크 증가.
> - 나사 설계 (Screw Design) 자체가 풀림 저항을 좌우 (Lee 2025): 오스템 KSIII(머리경 축소·나사산 narrow·길이↑)는 모든 토크에서 토크 손실 <25%(토크 비의존), TSIII(기존형)는 36.5–51.8% 손실(~2.5–3.0배). 약 35 Ncm가 가장 안정적 전하중.
> - 모스 테이퍼 침하 (Abutment Subsidence): 나사 없는 순수 테이퍼락(Bicon형)은 하중으로 지대주가 계속 가라앉아 후기 교합 저하를 일으킨다 (Ren 2024). 침하는 잠금깊이↑(r=−0.807)로 줄고 시팅 하중↑(r=0.645)으로 증가 → 시팅 시 ≥5회 가압 + 최종 교합조정 지연 권고.
> - 체결 토크 (Tightening Torque) 권고: 제조사 지정값을 보정된 토크렌치로 — 더 높이지 말 것. Vélez 2020에서 변연 부적합은 30 Ncm에서 최저이나 20→30 Ncm 사이 유의차 없음; 과토크는 항복 초과 시 전하중 손실·나사 파절. 시스템별 ~20–35 Ncm(Astra류 35 Ncm).
> - 전하중 보존 6대 레버 (Nithyapriya 2018): 재료(금 > Ti합금 > 표면처리 Ti > 순 Ti), 내부연결, 윤활(saliva·Vaseline·Ti 나노입자), 보정 토크기, 10분 후 재토크, 접촉면적 ↑·지대주 칼라 길이 ↓.
> - 임상 결론: 나사 풀림 방지는 단일 제품이 아니라 절차 — 보정 토크렌치로 제조사 지정 토크 → ~10분 대기 → 재토크 → 3–6개월 리콜 시 재확인. 후방 대구치 등 고하중부에서는 세틀링이 교합도 낮출 수 있어 전달 후 교합 재확인 필수.
> - 재조임 타이밍 재논쟁: Varvara 2020(내·외부 육각 80개, 35 Ncm)은 초기 조임 후 **2분** 재조임이 5·10분·비재조임보다 전하중 손실을 가장 적게 했고(내부 육각 < 외부 육각, 모든 시점), 전통적 10분 권고가 오히려 늦다고 봄 — 세틀링이 빠르게 일어나므로 2분 시점이 회복 여지가 더 큼.
> - 세틀링 plateau는 2회째에 도달 (Saleh-Saber 2017, 내부 연결 4종): 30 Ncm를 1→2회 재조임 때만 유의한 추가 안착, 3–5회는 무변화. 팔각형(octagon) 연결이 안착 최소(4–18 µm), 내부 육각형이 최대(12–35 µm), 모스 6°·11°는 중간 — 인상부터 최종 보철까지 단계마다 안착이 누적돼 부동적합(passive fit)을 깰 수 있음.
> - 토크렌치 정확도 (Torque-Limiting Device, TLD): 기계식 렌치 3종 모두 설정값보다 **11–29.3% 낮게** 전달(Marenzi 2026) — 그래도 25 Ncm 설정 시 전 제조사에서 미세간극(Microgap) 無. 즉 under-delivery를 감안해 제조사 지정값으로 정확히 설정·정기 교정이 핵심.
> - 마찰 코팅의 양면성: 탄소(DLC) 코팅 나사는 25 Ncm 10회 반복 조임 시 전하중이 329.9→253.7 N으로 감소, 마찰계수 0.33→0.44로 증가 — 나사 머리 마찰이 주범, 코팅 나사 **재사용 금지** (Sagheb 2023). 토크의 ~50%는 머리 마찰, ~10%만 전하중化.
> - 나사 코팅재 비교 (Coelho 2024/2025): **PTFE 테이프는 윤활제처럼 작용해 전하중을 낮춤**(28.88 vs 30.41 N·cm) → 풀림 방지에 역효과; GapSeal 실리콘 젤은 전하중 유지+제거토크 증가. 단 30만 사이클 하중 후엔 PTFE 파편이 나사산에 끼여 RTV가 역전 최고(31.50 Ncm)가 됨 — 무하중 GapSeal, 구강 모사 후 PTFE.
> - 어버트먼트 오염 (Abutment Contamination): 내부 육각 연결부에 석고 오염 시 부분(3/6면)만으로도 초기 전하중 유의 감소, 완전(6/6면) 오염은 반복하중 후 전하중 손실까지 가속(Sun 2026, n=90) — 시팅 전 연결부 청결이 전하중 형성의 전제조건. 실제 265개 맞춤 어버트먼트 중 상당수가 오염 관찰됨.
> - 맞춤 vs 기성 어버트먼트 (Selvi 2025, 200만 사이클): CAD-CAM 맞춤(custom)은 기성(stock)보다 기저 RTV가 낮으나 200만 사이클 후 전하중 유지는 동등 — 둘 다 기능 하중에 적합. 전하중 손실의 대부분이 5만–100만 사이클에 집중되므로 **초기(삽입 후 수주 내) 재조임** 권고.
> - 연결부 기하 심화 (Microgap·Cone Angle): 모든 연결부에 하중 전부터 마이크로갭(0.15–9 µm)이 존재하고 하중 시 최대 40.5 µm로 확대(Angermair 2024, 방사광); 소원추각(<12°) friction-fit은 지대주 내측 변위로 임플란트 외벽을 변형시키나 대원추각(~45°) butt-joint는 그렇지 않음 — 변연골 응력 전달 차이의 기전적 단서.
> - 어버트먼트 형태와 골 응력 (Aalaei 2017, FEA): 세그먼트형(분리형) 어버트먼트는 각도 하중 시 변연골 응력을 1/4로 낮추되(31 vs 126 MPa) 나사 응력은 약간 높임(430 vs 375 MPa) — 골 보호와 나사 보호 사이의 trade-off. 각도(비축성) 하중이 임상 주벡터이므로 골 보호 쪽이 유리.

## One-line Summary

Hub synthesis of 18 prosthetic-materials papers on the implant abutment screw joint: screw loosening — the most common mechanical complication (~10.4% at 5y, ~20.8% at 10y) — is driven by preload loss, which has two mechanisms (settling/embedment relaxation, 2–10% even unloaded; plus cyclic fatigue, 16.1–39% total removal-torque loss). Friction/thread roughness is the master lever (Bulaqi 2015 FEA; Sagheb 2023 showing carbon-coated screws lose preload 329.9→253.7 N over 10 reuses as head friction climbs), retightening counters settling with the optimal interval contested (10 min per Nithyapriya 2018/Vinhas 2022 vs 2 min per Varvara 2020), settling plateaus at the 2nd retorque and scales with connection geometry (Kim 2020: 0.8→25.6 µm; Saleh-Saber 2017: octagon least, internal hex most), connection geometry's loosening effect is contested (SR-level Morse-taper superiority vs Vinhas 2022 finding no design effect), screw geometry itself governs loosening (Lee 2025: KSIII <25% vs TSIII 36.5–51.8% loss, ~35 Ncm optimum), and screwless pure taper-lock joints subside under load causing late occlusal drift (Ren 2024). Adjuncts/details: torque wrenches under-deliver 11–29.3% yet abolish microgap at 25 Ncm (Marenzi 2026); PTFE tape lowers preload (avoid) while GapSeal preserves it (Coelho 2024) — though cyclic loading reverses PTFE to highest RTV (Coelho 2025); abutment contamination drops preload even partially (Sun 2026); custom and stock abutments are fatigue-equivalent with greatest loss before 1M cycles (Selvi 2025); microgaps pre-exist in all connections and expand to 40.5 µm under load (Angermair 2024); and segmented abutments cut peri-implant bone stress 4× at slight screw-stress cost (Aalaei 2017). Bottom line: loosening control is procedural — manufacturer-specified torque with a calibrated wrench, clean connection, retorque after a short delay, re-verify at recall.

## 한줄요약

임플란트 어버트먼트 나사 체결부에 관한 18편 종합. 나사 풀림(최다 기계적 합병증; 5년 ~10.4%, 10년 ~20.8%)의 원인은 전하중 손실이며, 기전은 세틀링(무하중에서도 2–10%)과 동적 피로(총 제거토크 손실 16.1–39%) 둘이다. 마찰·나사산 조도가 핵심 레버(Bulaqi 2015; Sagheb 2023 — 탄소코팅 나사 10회 재사용 시 머리 마찰↑로 전하중 329.9→253.7 N 감소, 재사용 금지), 재조임이 세틀링을 보상하되 최적 시점은 논쟁적(10분: Nithyapriya 2018·Vinhas 2022 vs 2분: Varvara 2020), 세틀링은 2회째 재조임에서 plateau에 도달하고 연결부 형태에 따라 다르며(Kim 2020: 0.8→25.6 µm; Saleh-Saber 2017: 팔각 최소·내부육각 최대), 연결부 형태의 풀림 효과는 논쟁적(SR은 모스테이퍼 우위 vs Vinhas는 차이 없음), 나사 설계 자체가 풀림을 좌우하며(Lee 2025: KSIII <25% vs TSIII 36.5–51.8%, ~35 Ncm 최적), 나사 없는 순수 테이퍼락은 하중으로 침하해 후기 교합 저하를 일으킨다(Ren 2024). 보조·세부: 토크렌치는 설정값보다 11–29.3% 낮게 전달하나 25 Ncm에서 미세간극 無(Marenzi 2026); PTFE 테이프는 전하중을 낮춰 회피, GapSeal은 유지(Coelho 2024) — 단 사이클 하중 후 PTFE가 RTV 최고로 역전(Coelho 2025); 어버트먼트 오염은 부분만으로도 전하중 감소(Sun 2026); 맞춤·기성 어버트먼트는 피로 동등, 손실은 100만 사이클 이전에 집중(Selvi 2025); 마이크로갭은 모든 연결부에 하중 전부터 존재해 하중 시 40.5 µm까지 확대(Angermair 2024); 세그먼트형 어버트먼트는 변연골 응력을 4배 낮춤(Aalaei 2017). 결론: 풀림 관리는 절차 — 보정 토크렌치로 제조사 지정 토크 → 연결부 청결 → 단시간 뒤 재토크 → 리콜 재확인.

## Summary

The abutment screw is the single most failure-prone point in a screw-retained implant restoration. Across these papers a coherent thesis emerges: **screw loosening is a preload-loss problem, preload loss has two mechanisms (settling and fatigue), and the dominant clinical control is procedural — calibrated torque plus a delayed retorque, on a clean connection — far more than product choice (connection or screw brand).**

**Preload, and why so little of the torque becomes clamping force.** Preload is the axial clamping force generated when the abutment screw is tightened, holding the abutment against the implant. Only ~8–10% of the applied tightening torque actually converts to preload — the rest is consumed by friction at the threads and the screw-head seat (Lee 2025). Sagheb 2023 quantified this friction split directly: ~50% of the tightening torque is consumed by screw-*head* friction and only ~10% generates preload. This is why friction is not a nuisance variable but the *governing* variable: anything that changes the coefficient of friction changes how much preload a given torque delivers and how much of it survives. Sagheb 2023 also showed the friction is not fixed — repeatedly tightening a carbon-(DLC)-coated screw to 25 Ncm ten times drove preload down monotonically (329.9 → 253.7 N) as the coefficient of friction *rose* (0.33 → 0.44), the loss localizing to the screw head rather than the threads; coated screws should therefore be single-use.

**Mechanism 1 — the settling effect (embedment relaxation).** Even with no occlusal load, micro-roughness on the two mating surfaces collapses under the initial torquing, the joint "settles," and clamping force bleeds off — costing roughly **2–10% of preload** (Pardal-Peláez 2017; Varvara 2020 brackets it slightly wider at 2–15%). Bulaqi 2015 (FEA) supplies the mechanism: rougher thread surfaces (higher coefficient of friction) increase the settling effect and lower retained torque/preload, while smoother/low-friction interfaces preserve more preload. Kim 2020 measured settling directly as axial displacement under a single 700 N load and showed it scales with interface taper — flat external butt-joints barely move (0.8 µm) while steep 8° Morse-taper octagons settle most (up to 25.6 µm). Saleh-Saber 2017 measured settling as cumulative axial displacement across four *internal* connections through every prosthetic stage (impression coping → resin pattern → metal frame → final abutment) and found connection geometry dominates: internal octagon settled least (4–18 µm), internal hexagon most (12–35 µm), with Morse hex 6°/11° intermediate. Critically, Saleh-Saber 2017 also showed settling **plateaus at the 2nd retorque** — repeating 30 Ncm produced significant additional displacement from the 1st to 2nd application but nothing further on the 3rd–5th — the mechanical basis for "retorque once, then stop." A confounder that masquerades as settling is interface debris: Sun 2026 (n=90, internal hex) showed that contaminating the connection surfaces with dental stone reduced *initial* preload formation even when only 3 of 6 hexagonal walls were fouled (partial ≈ extensive for the initial deficit), and extensive (6/6) contamination additionally accelerated fatigue-driven preload loss — with SEM line-angle wear as the damage mechanism. Their audit of 265 custom abutments confirmed visible connection contamination is a real clinical occurrence, not a hypothetical, making connection cleanliness a precondition for preload formation.

**Mechanism 2 — dynamic fatigue.** Under cyclic masticatory loading, preload erodes further. Pardal-Peláez 2017 (SR of 10 in-vitro cyclic-loading studies) brackets total post-fatigue removal-torque loss at **16.1–39%**, and Vinhas 2022 confirmed that cyclic loading (500 N × 1000 cycles) significantly reduced removal torque for *every* connection tested. Lee 2025, under ISO 14801 fatigue (150 N, 14 Hz, 100,000 cycles, 30° off-axis), showed how much of that loss is attributable to the screw design itself. Two longer-cycle studies refine *when* the loss happens and whether abutment fabrication matters. Selvi 2025 ran custom (CAD-CAM) vs stock conical-hex abutments to **2 million cycles** (ISO 14801) and found that although stock abutments start with higher removal torque, the two are **mechanically equivalent at clinical loading durations** — and, decisively, the greatest preload loss occurs in the **50,000–1,000,000-cycle window**, i.e. the early post-insertion period, which is the strongest mechanistic argument for an *early* clinical retightening visit. Post-fatigue retorque restored removal torque in both groups, confirming the loss is reversible. Sun 2026 adds that the fatigue loss is not uniform across joints: heavily contaminated internal-hex connections lost significantly more removal torque (ΔRTV) after 240,000 cycles than clean or partially contaminated ones — so debris that survives seating turns an ordinary fatigue loss into an accelerated one.

**The retightening lever.** Because settling happens quickly and predictably, a **delayed retorque after the initial tightening** re-seats the joint and recovers the settling-related preload — the most consistently recommended single procedure across this set (Nithyapriya 2018, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Vinhas 2022). The nuance from Bulaqi 2015: retightening reduces the settling effect but raises preload only *insignificantly*, and its benefit grows at high friction — i.e. retorque mainly counters settling rather than restoring lost clamping force. Vinhas 2022 adds that *multiple* re-tightenings give higher removal-torque values than a single tightening (in all but one of its groups).

**The optimal interval is itself contested.** The classic recommendation is ~10 minutes (Nithyapriya 2018; the Dixon 1995/Bakaeen 2002 lineage), but **Varvara 2020** (80 implants, internal vs external hex, 35 Ncm, retorque at 2/5/10 min vs none) found retightening at **2 minutes** minimized preload loss significantly better than 5 or 10 minutes — reasoning that by 10 minutes much of the settling has already occurred, leaving less preload to recover. Varvara 2020 also confirmed internal hexagon retained more preload than external hexagon at every matched interval. Saleh-Saber 2017 constrains how *many* retorques help: a second 30 Ncm application added significant settling correction but a third through fifth did not — so the practical protocol is a single delayed retorque (the exact delay still debated between 2 and 10 minutes), not repeated re-tightening.

**The connection-design debate (the key tension in this body).** At the systematic-review level, the internal connection — and especially the Morse taper — best resists cyclic-loading-induced loosening (Pardal-Peláez 2017), because internal/conical joints transfer load deeper and reduce micromovement, whereas in external joints axial load acts directly on the screw. Vélez 2020 reinforces a related fit advantage: the 11° Morse-taper joint achieved the lowest marginal misfit (~0.6 µm). **But Vinhas 2022 directly challenges geometric determinism** — across 180 implants in 4 designs (2 external, 2 internal), connection design did *not* significantly influence preload maintenance; cyclic loading degraded all of them, and retightening helped all of them. The reconciliation: connection geometry modulates the *fit and settling profile*, but the *procedural* controls (calibrated torque, delayed retorque) dominate preload retention regardless of geometry.

**Microgap is not a geometry that geometry abolishes.** Angermair 2024 used synchrotron phase-contrast imaging (0.1 µm resolution) on four systems and found that **every** connection — three small-angle (<12°) conical friction-fits and one ~45° butt-joint — carried a microgap *before any load was applied* (0.15–9 µm), expanding to **40.5 µm** under load. The cone angle dictated the *displacement mode*: small-angle conical joints seated deeper under load and deformed the outer implant wall, whereas the large-angle butt-joint distributed force without that wall deformation — a mechanistic link between connection design and crestal-bone stress. Marenzi 2026 found this gap also depends on delivered torque: at 25 Ncm all three tested Morse-taper systems (5°/3°/8°) showed *zero* microgap by SEM, but at 20 Ncm the smallest- and largest-angle tapers gapped more than the 5° — so taper angle and torque magnitude interact at the threshold. The settling counterpart of this is Saleh-Saber 2017's ranking — octagon < Morse 6°/11° < internal hexagon — confirming that internal geometries are *not* interchangeable for dimensional stability even when they all "settle."

**Abutment architecture as a bone-protection lever (distinct from loosening).** Aalaei 2017 (3D FEA, mandibular molar, Straumann tissue-level) compared a **segmented** (two-piece abutment + coping) vs **non-segmented** (one-piece) screw-retained abutment and found a clean trade-off: under the clinically dominant 45° angular load the segmented design cut peri-implant bone stress nearly fourfold (31 vs 126 MPa; micro-strain 2400 vs 9400 µɛ) at the cost of slightly higher abutment-screw stress (430 vs 375 MPa). The two-piece junction permits micro-motion that dissipates lateral force before it reaches crestal bone — so the segmented abutment trades a little screw-stress headroom for substantial bone protection, the favorable exchange in off-axis-loaded sites.

**Screw design as an independent lever.** Lee 2025 dissociated torque magnitude from screw geometry in two Osstem systems: the modified **KSIII** screw (reduced head diameter, narrower thread profile, longer nominal length) held torque loss **<25% at every torque level (torque-insensitive)**, while the conventional **TSIII** lost **36.5–51.8% (~2.5–3.0× more)** and was strongly torque-dependent. **~35 Ncm gave the most stable preload**, and 40 Ncm was not protective. Pardal-Peláez 2017 echoes that screw factors matter — conical-head screws loosen less than flat-head, larger screw-joint diameter favors torque maintenance, and material ranks gold/Ti-alloy favorably; Nithyapriya 2018 ranks preload by material as **gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti**.

**Screw coatings — a double-edged lever.** Two same-group studies dissect the popular chairside habit of coating the screw. Coelho 2024 (n=45, external hex, static) found **PTFE (Teflon) tape acts as a lubricant and *reduces* preload** (28.88 vs 30.41 N·cm control) while collapsing removal torque (16.15 vs 26.59 N·cm) — i.e. it undermines the very clamping force it is meant to protect — whereas **GapSeal silicone gel preserved preload and *raised* removal torque** (28.67 vs 26.59 N·cm), the only material to improve both. Coelho 2025 then added 300,000-cycle ISO cyclic loading and uncovered a **reversal**: without loading PTFE still gave the lowest removal torque (15.30 Ncm), but *after* cyclic loading PTFE flipped to the **highest** removal torque (31.50 Ncm vs ~26–27 for control/GapSeal) — SEM showed PTFE fragments lodged in the thread valleys, mechanically interlocking. PTFE nonetheless lowered preload in *both* conditions, and micro-CT confirmed neither coating created a microgap. The honest read: GapSeal is the safer static choice (preserves preload), PTFE's anti-loosening benefit only materializes after function and at the cost of preload — so neither replaces correct torque, and PTFE's reputation as a simple "thread-locker" is not supported by its preload effect.

**Tightening torque — reach the spec, don't exceed it (but expect under-delivery).** Vélez 2020 showed marginal misfit falls as insertion torque rises and is lowest at the manufacturer-recommended 30 Ncm, but with **no significant gain from 20→30 Ncm** — under-torquing risks loosening/fracture/microleakage, over-torquing past yield causes preload loss and screw fracture. The transferable rule is system-specific: tighten to the *manufacturer's* value (internal-conical systems span ~20–35 Ncm; Astra-type ~35 Ncm) with a calibrated wrench, retorque after a short delay, do not maximize. The wrench itself is a hidden error source: Marenzi 2026 tested three manufacturers' mechanical torque-limiting wrenches and found **all under-delivered by 11–29.3%** relative to their set value (wrench A consistently the most accurate). Reassuringly, at a 25 Ncm setting every wrench still abolished the microgap by SEM despite under-delivery — but the finding underscores that wrenches must be calibrated and periodically re-verified, and that the *set* value already builds in a margin that over-tightening would erase.

**The screwless edge case — taper-lock subsidence.** Pure Morse-taper (taper-lock) joints such as Bicon have **no retention screw**: the abutment is held by friction/cold welding and keeps *subsiding* into the implant under load, lowering occlusal contacts over time (Ren 2024). Subsidence is reduced most by greater locking depth (r=−0.807) and increases with seating load (r=0.645), while pull-apart locking force rises with seating load (r=0.963). The clinical protocol differs from screw-retained joints: **seat the abutment ≥5 times, provisionalize with only preliminary occlusal adjustment, and defer final occlusal adjustment until the joint has settled.**

## Preload-Loss Mechanism Map (settling vs fatigue)

| Mechanism | When it occurs | Magnitude | Master lever | Key evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Settling / embedment relaxation** | Immediately, even unloaded; micro-roughness collapses on initial torquing | ~2–10% (Varvara 2020: 2–15%) preload loss | Surface finish / coefficient of friction; **delayed retorque (2 min Varvara vs 10 min classic)** | Pardal-Peláez 2017; Bulaqi 2015 (FEA mechanism); Nithyapriya 2018; Varvara 2020 |
| **Settling as axial displacement** | Under static load (single 700 N) or across prosthetic stages | 0.8 µm (external) → 25.6 µm (8° Morse-taper octagon); internal: octagon 4–18 µm < hex 12–35 µm | Interface taper steepness; connection geometry; abutment type (1-piece vs 2-piece) | Kim 2020; Saleh-Saber 2017 (plateau at 2nd retorque) |
| **Friction-coating drift (screw reuse)** | Across repeated tightenings of a coated screw | Preload 329.9 → 253.7 N over 10 reuses; COF 0.33 → 0.44 (head friction) | Single-use coated screws; head-friction control | Sagheb 2023 |
| **Contamination-impaired preload formation** | At seating, if connection surfaces are fouled | Partial (3/6) ≈ extensive (6/6) initial preload deficit; extensive accelerates fatigue ΔRTV | Connection cleanliness before seating | Sun 2026 |
| **Dynamic (cyclic) fatigue** | During masticatory function | 16.1–39% total removal-torque loss; greatest loss in 50k–1M-cycle window | Screw design + connection load path; **early** retorque | Pardal-Peláez 2017 (SR); Vinhas 2022; Lee 2025; Selvi 2025 (custom = stock; early window) |
| **Coating × loading reversal** | PTFE-coated screw, before vs after cyclic loading | RTV 15.3 Ncm (no load) → 31.5 Ncm (after 300k cycles); preload lowered throughout | Loading state; coating choice (GapSeal static, PTFE post-load) | Coelho 2024 (static); Coelho 2025 (cyclic reversal) |
| **Taper-lock subsidence (screwless)** | Progressive, post-delivery, under load | reduced by locking depth (r=−0.807); raised by load (r=0.645) | Locking depth; seating-load protocol (≥5×) | Ren 2024 |

Note on the friction paradox: lower friction delivers *higher* preload for a given torque (Bulaqi 2015), but Kim 2020 shows the *opposite preload sign* by abutment type — under settling, two-piece (screw-joint/tension) abutments **lose** removal torque while one-piece (friction-retained) abutments **gain** it. The same settling phenomenon, read through tension vs friction retention, produces opposite clinical signs.

**The honest read:** SR-level evidence (Pardal-Peláez 2017) and fit data (Vélez 2020) favor the internal/Morse-taper joint, but the most direct head-to-head preload-maintenance experiment (Vinhas 2022, 180 implants) found connection design *did not* govern preload retention. Treat connection geometry as a modulator of fit and settling, not as a substitute for the retorque protocol.

## Tightening & Retightening Protocol (screw-retained joints)

1. **Clean and inspect the connection before seating.** Connection-surface contamination (stone, polishing residue, blood) impairs initial preload formation even when only partial, and accelerates fatigue-driven loss when extensive — verified prevalent on real custom abutments (Sun 2026). Inspect and clean the internal connection before tightening.
2. **Use a calibrated torque device — and re-verify it.** A calibrated dynamometric/digital torque wrench is mandatory — uncalibrated tightening causes implant-abutment misfit (Nithyapriya 2018, Vélez 2020). Mechanical torque-limiting wrenches systematically under-deliver by 11–29.3% (Marenzi 2026), so periodic re-calibration matters even though a 25 Ncm setting still abolished microgap in that study.
3. **Tighten to the manufacturer's specified value — do not exceed it.** Internal-conical systems span ~20–35 Ncm (Astra-type ~35 Ncm); fit improvement plateaus (no significant 20→30 Ncm gain) and over-torque past yield causes preload loss and screw fracture (Vélez 2020). Lee 2025 found ~35 Ncm gave the most stable preload for Osstem systems and 40 Ncm was not protective. Higher set torque also closes the microgap up to a threshold (zero gap at 25 Ncm across tapers, Marenzi 2026).
4. **Retorque once after a short delay, before loading.** This re-seats the joint and recovers settling-related preload (Nithyapriya 2018, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Vinhas 2022). The optimal interval is debated — classic guidance is ~10 min, but Varvara 2020 found 2 min minimized loss best. Settling correction plateaus after the 2nd application, so a single delayed retorque suffices — repeated re-tightening adds little (Saleh-Saber 2017), though Vinhas 2022 found multiple re-tightenings marginally higher.
5. **Schedule an early recheck.** Most preload loss happens in the early post-insertion / 50k–1M-cycle window regardless of custom vs stock abutment (Selvi 2025) — an early retightening visit (within weeks) targets the highest-risk period; post-fatigue retorque restores removal torque.
6. **Reduce friction where possible, but use coatings cautiously.** Smooth/low-friction interfaces and lubrication (saliva, Vaseline, Ti nanoparticles) preserve more preload and regulate settling (Bulaqi 2015, Nithyapriya 2018). Avoid PTFE tape as a thread-lock — it lowers preload (Coelho 2024) even though cyclic loading can later raise its removal torque (Coelho 2025); GapSeal preserves preload statically. Do not reuse carbon/DLC-coated screws — repeated tightening raises head friction and bleeds preload (Sagheb 2023).
7. **Prefer favorable screw factors.** Gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti for preload; conical-head over flat-head; larger screw-joint diameter; the KSIII-type modified geometry resists loosening across torque levels (Lee 2025, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Nithyapriya 2018).
8. **Re-verify at recall.** Settling-driven preload loss can also lower occlusion after delivery in load-heavy molar sites (Kim 2020) — re-check torque and occlusion at 3–6 month recalls. In off-axis-loaded sites, a segmented abutment design markedly lowers crestal bone stress (Aalaei 2017).

## Abutment Subsidence (screwless taper-lock)

For pure Morse-taper / taper-lock systems (no retention screw), the failure mode is not screw loosening but **progressive abutment subsidence** into the implant under occlusal load, producing late occlusal drift (Ren 2024):

- **Locking depth is the dominant determinant** — deeper locking reduces subsidence most (r=−0.807) and also raises retention (r=0.607).
- **Seating load raises both retention and subsidence** — locking force rises with load (r=0.963) but so does sinking (r=0.645), forcing a seating protocol rather than a one-shot torque.
- **Thicker implant outer wall** resists elastic deformation → less subsidence.
- **Protocol:** seat the abutment **≥5 times**, restore provisionally with only preliminary occlusal adjustment, and defer final restoration + occlusal adjustment until the joint has settled in-mouth.
