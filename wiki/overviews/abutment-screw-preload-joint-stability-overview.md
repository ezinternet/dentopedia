## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 핵심 명제: 나사 풀림 (Screw Loosening, ASL)은 임플란트 보철에서 가장 흔한 기계적 합병증(5년 ~10.4%, 10년 ~20.8%; 발생률 5–15%)이고, 그 상류 원인은 전하중 (Preload) 손실 — 체결 시 생기는 축방향 압착력의 소실이다. 가해진 토크 중 실제 전하중으로 전환되는 비율은 ~8–10%에 불과하다 (Lee 2025).
> - 두 가지 전하중 손실 기전: ① 세틀링 (Settling/Embedment Relaxation) — 무하중에서도 접촉면 미세조도가 초기 체결로 붕괴하며 2–10% 전하중이 빠진다 (Pardal-Peláez 2017), ② 동적 피로 (Cyclic Fatigue) — 저작하중 반복으로 추가 손실, 전체 피로 후 제거 토크 손실은 16.1–39% 범위 (Pardal-Peláez 2017).
> - 마찰 (Friction)이 핵심 레버 (Bulaqi 2015, FEA): 나사산이 거칠수록(마찰계수↑) 세틀링↑·잔류 전하중↓. 재조임 (Retightening)은 세틀링을 줄이되 전하중은 거의 회복 못 하며, 그 이득은 마찰이 높을수록 커진다.
> - 재조임 프로토콜: 초기 체결 후 ~10분(또는 3–10분) 뒤 재토크 — 세틀링으로 빠진 전하중을 회복하는 표준 권고 (Nithyapriya 2018, Vinhas 2022). 다회 재조임이 단회보다 높은 제거 토크값 (Removal Torque Value, RTV)을 준다 (Vinhas 2022).
> - 연결부 형태 (Connection Design) 논쟁: SR 레벨에서는 내부연결·특히 모스 테이퍼 (Morse Taper)가 풀림에 가장 강함 (Pardal-Peláez 2017). 그러나 Vinhas 2022(180 임플란트)는 외부 vs 내부 연결이 전하중 유지에 차이 없음을 보여 — 기하학보다 절차(재조임)가 결정적임을 시사.
> - 세틀링은 테이퍼가 급할수록 커진다 (Kim 2020): 외부 버트조인트 0.8 µm < 내부 육각 ~10–11 µm < 내부 8° 모스 테이퍼 옥타곤 19–25 µm. 같은 세틀링이 지대주 유형에 따라 정반대 결과 — 2-piece(나사고정)는 전하중 손실, 1-piece(마찰고정)는 오히려 제거 토크 증가.
> - 나사 설계 (Screw Design) 자체가 풀림 저항을 좌우 (Lee 2025): 오스템 KSIII(머리경 축소·나사산 narrow·길이↑)는 모든 토크에서 토크 손실 <25%(토크 비의존), TSIII(기존형)는 36.5–51.8% 손실(~2.5–3.0배). 약 35 Ncm가 가장 안정적 전하중.
> - 모스 테이퍼 침하 (Abutment Subsidence): 나사 없는 순수 테이퍼락(Bicon형)은 하중으로 지대주가 계속 가라앉아 후기 교합 저하를 일으킨다 (Ren 2024). 침하는 잠금깊이↑(r=−0.807)로 줄고 시팅 하중↑(r=0.645)으로 증가 → 시팅 시 ≥5회 가압 + 최종 교합조정 지연 권고.
> - 체결 토크 (Tightening Torque) 권고: 제조사 지정값을 보정된 토크렌치로 — 더 높이지 말 것. Vélez 2020에서 변연 부적합은 30 Ncm에서 최저이나 20→30 Ncm 사이 유의차 없음; 과토크는 항복 초과 시 전하중 손실·나사 파절. 시스템별 ~20–35 Ncm(Astra류 35 Ncm).
> - 전하중 보존 6대 레버 (Nithyapriya 2018): 재료(금 > Ti합금 > 표면처리 Ti > 순 Ti), 내부연결, 윤활(saliva·Vaseline·Ti 나노입자), 보정 토크기, 10분 후 재토크, 접촉면적 ↑·지대주 칼라 길이 ↓.
> - 임상 결론: 나사 풀림 방지는 단일 제품이 아니라 절차 — 보정 토크렌치로 제조사 지정 토크 → ~10분 대기 → 재토크 → 3–6개월 리콜 시 재확인. 후방 대구치 등 고하중부에서는 세틀링이 교합도 낮출 수 있어 전달 후 교합 재확인 필수.

## One-line Summary

Hub synthesis of 8 prosthetic-materials papers on the implant abutment screw joint: screw loosening — the most common mechanical complication (~10.4% at 5y, ~20.8% at 10y) — is driven by preload loss, which has two mechanisms (settling/embedment relaxation, 2–10% even unloaded; plus cyclic fatigue, 16.1–39% total removal-torque loss). Friction/thread roughness is the master lever (Bulaqi 2015 FEA), retightening ~10 min after initial torque counters settling (Nithyapriya 2018, Vinhas 2022), connection geometry is contested (SR-level Morse-taper superiority vs Vinhas 2022 finding no design effect), settling scales with taper steepness (Kim 2020: 0.8→25.6 µm), screw geometry itself governs loosening (Lee 2025: KSIII <25% vs TSIII 36.5–51.8% loss, ~35 Ncm optimum), and screwless pure taper-lock joints subside under load causing late occlusal drift (Ren 2024). Bottom line: loosening control is procedural — manufacturer-specified torque with a calibrated wrench, retorque after a short delay, re-verify at recall.

## 한줄요약

임플란트 어버트먼트 나사 체결부에 관한 8편 종합. 나사 풀림(최다 기계적 합병증; 5년 ~10.4%, 10년 ~20.8%)의 원인은 전하중 손실이며, 기전은 세틀링(무하중에서도 2–10%)과 동적 피로(총 제거토크 손실 16.1–39%) 둘이다. 마찰·나사산 조도가 핵심 레버(Bulaqi 2015), 초기 체결 ~10분 뒤 재조임이 세틀링을 보상(Nithyapriya 2018, Vinhas 2022), 연결부 형태는 논쟁적(SR은 모스테이퍼 우위 vs Vinhas는 차이 없음), 세틀링은 테이퍼가 급할수록 커지고(Kim 2020: 0.8→25.6 µm), 나사 설계 자체가 풀림을 좌우하며(Lee 2025: KSIII <25% vs TSIII 36.5–51.8%, ~35 Ncm 최적), 나사 없는 순수 테이퍼락은 하중으로 침하해 후기 교합 저하를 일으킨다(Ren 2024). 결론: 풀림 관리는 절차 — 보정 토크렌치로 제조사 지정 토크 → 단시간 뒤 재토크 → 리콜 재확인.

## Summary

The abutment screw is the single most failure-prone point in a screw-retained implant restoration. Across these eight papers a coherent thesis emerges: **screw loosening is a preload-loss problem, preload loss has two mechanisms (settling and fatigue), and the dominant clinical control is procedural — calibrated torque plus a delayed retorque — far more than product choice (connection or screw brand).**

**Preload, and why so little of the torque becomes clamping force.** Preload is the axial clamping force generated when the abutment screw is tightened, holding the abutment against the implant. Only ~8–10% of the applied tightening torque actually converts to preload — the rest is consumed by friction at the threads and the screw-head seat (Lee 2025). This is why friction is not a nuisance variable but the *governing* variable: anything that changes the coefficient of friction changes how much preload a given torque delivers and how much of it survives.

**Mechanism 1 — the settling effect (embedment relaxation).** Even with no occlusal load, micro-roughness on the two mating surfaces collapses under the initial torquing, the joint "settles," and clamping force bleeds off — costing roughly **2–10% of preload** (Pardal-Peláez 2017). Bulaqi 2015 (FEA) supplies the mechanism: rougher thread surfaces (higher coefficient of friction) increase the settling effect and lower retained torque/preload, while smoother/low-friction interfaces preserve more preload. Kim 2020 measured settling directly as axial displacement under a single 700 N load and showed it scales with interface taper — flat external butt-joints barely move (0.8 µm) while steep 8° Morse-taper octagons settle most (up to 25.6 µm).

**Mechanism 2 — dynamic fatigue.** Under cyclic masticatory loading, preload erodes further. Pardal-Peláez 2017 (SR of 10 in-vitro cyclic-loading studies) brackets total post-fatigue removal-torque loss at **16.1–39%**, and Vinhas 2022 confirmed that cyclic loading (500 N × 1000 cycles) significantly reduced removal torque for *every* connection tested. Lee 2025, under ISO 14801 fatigue (150 N, 14 Hz, 100,000 cycles, 30° off-axis), showed how much of that loss is attributable to the screw design itself.

**The retightening lever.** Because settling happens quickly and predictably, a **retorque ~10 minutes (range 3–10 min) after the initial tightening** re-seats the joint and recovers the settling-related preload — the most consistently recommended single procedure across this set (Nithyapriya 2018, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Vinhas 2022). The nuance from Bulaqi 2015: retightening reduces the settling effect but raises preload only *insignificantly*, and its benefit grows at high friction — i.e. retorque mainly counters settling rather than restoring lost clamping force. Vinhas 2022 adds that *multiple* re-tightenings give higher removal-torque values than a single tightening (in all but one of its groups).

**The connection-design debate (the key tension in this body).** At the systematic-review level, the internal connection — and especially the Morse taper — best resists cyclic-loading-induced loosening (Pardal-Peláez 2017), because internal/conical joints transfer load deeper and reduce micromovement, whereas in external joints axial load acts directly on the screw. Vélez 2020 reinforces a related fit advantage: the 11° Morse-taper joint achieved the lowest marginal misfit (~0.6 µm). **But Vinhas 2022 directly challenges geometric determinism** — across 180 implants in 4 designs (2 external, 2 internal), connection design did *not* significantly influence preload maintenance; cyclic loading degraded all of them, and retightening helped all of them. The reconciliation: connection geometry modulates the *fit and settling profile*, but the *procedural* controls (calibrated torque, delayed retorque) dominate preload retention regardless of geometry.

**Screw design as an independent lever.** Lee 2025 dissociated torque magnitude from screw geometry in two Osstem systems: the modified **KSIII** screw (reduced head diameter, narrower thread profile, longer nominal length) held torque loss **<25% at every torque level (torque-insensitive)**, while the conventional **TSIII** lost **36.5–51.8% (~2.5–3.0× more)** and was strongly torque-dependent. **~35 Ncm gave the most stable preload**, and 40 Ncm was not protective. Pardal-Peláez 2017 echoes that screw factors matter — conical-head screws loosen less than flat-head, larger screw-joint diameter favors torque maintenance, and material ranks gold/Ti-alloy favorably; Nithyapriya 2018 ranks preload by material as **gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti**.

**Tightening torque — reach the spec, don't exceed it.** Vélez 2020 showed marginal misfit falls as insertion torque rises and is lowest at the manufacturer-recommended 30 Ncm, but with **no significant gain from 20→30 Ncm** — under-torquing risks loosening/fracture/microleakage, over-torquing past yield causes preload loss and screw fracture. The transferable rule is system-specific: tighten to the *manufacturer's* value (internal-conical systems span ~20–35 Ncm; Astra-type ~35 Ncm) with a calibrated wrench, retorque after a short delay, do not maximize.

**The screwless edge case — taper-lock subsidence.** Pure Morse-taper (taper-lock) joints such as Bicon have **no retention screw**: the abutment is held by friction/cold welding and keeps *subsiding* into the implant under load, lowering occlusal contacts over time (Ren 2024). Subsidence is reduced most by greater locking depth (r=−0.807) and increases with seating load (r=0.645), while pull-apart locking force rises with seating load (r=0.963). The clinical protocol differs from screw-retained joints: **seat the abutment ≥5 times, provisionalize with only preliminary occlusal adjustment, and defer final occlusal adjustment until the joint has settled.**

## Preload-Loss Mechanism Map (settling vs fatigue)

| Mechanism | When it occurs | Magnitude | Master lever | Key evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Settling / embedment relaxation** | Immediately, even unloaded; micro-roughness collapses on initial torquing | ~2–10% preload loss | Surface finish / coefficient of friction; **retorque ~10 min** | Pardal-Peláez 2017; Bulaqi 2015 (FEA mechanism); Nithyapriya 2018 |
| **Settling as axial displacement** | Under static load (single 700 N) | 0.8 µm (external) → 25.6 µm (8° Morse-taper octagon) | Interface taper steepness; abutment type (1-piece vs 2-piece) | Kim 2020 |
| **Dynamic (cyclic) fatigue** | During masticatory function | 16.1–39% total removal-torque loss | Screw design + connection load path; pre-load retorque | Pardal-Peláez 2017 (SR); Vinhas 2022; Lee 2025 |
| **Taper-lock subsidence (screwless)** | Progressive, post-delivery, under load | reduced by locking depth (r=−0.807); raised by load (r=0.645) | Locking depth; seating-load protocol (≥5×) | Ren 2024 |

Note on the friction paradox: lower friction delivers *higher* preload for a given torque (Bulaqi 2015), but Kim 2020 shows the *opposite preload sign* by abutment type — under settling, two-piece (screw-joint/tension) abutments **lose** removal torque while one-piece (friction-retained) abutments **gain** it. The same settling phenomenon, read through tension vs friction retention, produces opposite clinical signs.

## Connection-Design Comparison Matrix

| Connection | Settling magnitude (Kim 2020) | Marginal fit (Vélez 2020) | Loosening resistance (SR view, Pardal-Peláez 2017) | Preload maintenance (Vinhas 2022) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **External hex** | Lowest (butt-joint 0.8 µm) | Higher misfit; torque-sensitive; angulation worsens fit | Lower — axial load acts directly on screw | No significant difference vs internal | Suggested for multi-unit prostheses (Pardal-Peláez) |
| **Internal hex** | Intermediate (~10–11 µm at 11°) | — | Higher than external | No significant difference vs external | Higher preload than external hex per Nithyapriya 2018 |
| **Internal Morse taper (screw-retained)** | Highest settling at steep 8° octagon (19–25.6 µm), but best fit | Lowest misfit (~0.6 µm); 20→30 Ncm NS; taper masks angulation | **Best** loosening resistance; load transferred deeper, less micromovement | No significant difference vs others | Suggested for single-tooth prostheses (Pardal-Peláez) |
| **Pure Morse taper / taper-lock (screwless)** | Subsides progressively under load | n/a (no screw) | Friction/cold-weld retention; pull-apart force ↑ with seating load | n/a (no screw preload) | Late occlusal drift; needs ≥5× seating + delayed final occlusion (Ren 2024) |

**The honest read:** SR-level evidence (Pardal-Peláez 2017) and fit data (Vélez 2020) favor the internal/Morse-taper joint, but the most direct head-to-head preload-maintenance experiment (Vinhas 2022, 180 implants) found connection design *did not* govern preload retention. Treat connection geometry as a modulator of fit and settling, not as a substitute for the retorque protocol.

## Tightening & Retightening Protocol (screw-retained joints)

1. **Use a calibrated torque device.** A calibrated dynamometric/digital torque wrench is mandatory — uncalibrated tightening causes implant-abutment misfit (Nithyapriya 2018, Vélez 2020).
2. **Tighten to the manufacturer's specified value — do not exceed it.** Internal-conical systems span ~20–35 Ncm (Astra-type ~35 Ncm); fit improvement plateaus (no significant 20→30 Ncm gain) and over-torque past yield causes preload loss and screw fracture (Vélez 2020). Lee 2025 found ~35 Ncm gave the most stable preload for Osstem systems and 40 Ncm was not protective.
3. **Retorque ~10 minutes (3–10 min) after initial tightening, before loading.** This re-seats the joint and recovers settling-related preload (Nithyapriya 2018, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Vinhas 2022). Multiple re-tightenings outperform a single tightening (Vinhas 2022).
4. **Reduce friction where possible.** Smooth/low-friction interfaces and lubrication (saliva, Vaseline, Ti nanoparticles) preserve more preload and regulate settling (Bulaqi 2015, Nithyapriya 2018).
5. **Prefer favorable screw factors.** Gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti for preload; conical-head over flat-head; larger screw-joint diameter; the KSIII-type modified geometry resists loosening across torque levels (Lee 2025, Pardal-Peláez 2017, Nithyapriya 2018).
6. **Re-verify at recall.** Settling-driven preload loss can also lower occlusion after delivery in load-heavy molar sites (Kim 2020) — re-check torque and occlusion at 3–6 month recalls.

## Abutment Subsidence (screwless taper-lock)

For pure Morse-taper / taper-lock systems (no retention screw), the failure mode is not screw loosening but **progressive abutment subsidence** into the implant under occlusal load, producing late occlusal drift (Ren 2024):

- **Locking depth is the dominant determinant** — deeper locking reduces subsidence most (r=−0.807) and also raises retention (r=0.607).
- **Seating load raises both retention and subsidence** — locking force rises with load (r=0.963) but so does sinking (r=0.645), forcing a seating protocol rather than a one-shot torque.
- **Thicker implant outer wall** resists elastic deformation → less subsidence.
- **Protocol:** seat the abutment **≥5 times**, restore provisionally with only preliminary occlusal adjustment, and defer final restoration + occlusal adjustment until the joint has settled in-mouth.
