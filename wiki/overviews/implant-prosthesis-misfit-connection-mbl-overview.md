> - **결론(bottom line)**: 수동적합 (passive fit)은 오래된 도그마지만, 부적합 (misfit)이 임플란트주위 변연골소실 (marginal bone loss, MBL)을 직접 일으킨다는 근거는 생각보다 약하고 미묘하다 — 부적합의 1차 임상결과는 **골 소실이 아니라 나사 합병증(screw-related complication)**이다.
> - **Katsoulis 2017 체계적 문헌고찰 (systematic review, SR)** (생체내 10편 = 인체 5 + 동물 5): 부적합 → 나사 풀림·파절 위험↑은 확인됐으나, **골 소실 연관성은 확인 불가**, 허용 가능 부적합 임계값 (tolerable misfit threshold)도 미정. 정의·측정법이 연구마다 제각각인 게 핵심 문제.
> - **측정법(assessment)은 비표준화**: 선형 왜곡 (linear distortion), 수직 갭 (vertical gap), 변형률 게이지 (strain gauge), 나사 회전력 (screw torque) — 4가지가 혼재해 메타분석 (meta-analysis) 자체가 불가능했다.
> - **Jokstad 2014** (n=30 무치악, 평균 19년, 3D 스캐너): 임상 평균 부적합 **150 μm**인데도 임플란트 생존율 96.7% — 장기적으로 상당한 부적합을 **생물학적으로 견뎠다(long-term tolerance)**. 골 소실과의 상관은 약함(R²=0.04, P=.29).
> - **Jokstad의 진짜 신호**: 나사 합병증을 겪은 보철군의 부적합이 유의하게 더 컸다(169 vs 134 μm, P=.005) — 즉 부적합의 위험은 **역학적(나사)** 경로로 발현.
> - **Couso-Queiruga 2025** (n=505 임플란트, 10.6년): 부적합 ↔ 골 소실을 **최초로 정량화**. 갭 임계값 **≥0.1 mm**, 용량-반응 = 갭 0.1 mm당 변연골소실 (distance to bone, DIB) +0.08 mm.
> - **단, Couso-Queiruga의 반전**: 다변량 모델에서 갭 크기(GapDIM)는 유의성을 잃고(P=.28), **흡연(+0.36 mm)·치주염 병력(+0.16 mm)**이 독립적으로 더 강한 예측인자. → 부적합은 순수 역학보다 **생물학적 경로(갭 → biofilm 저류 → 염증 → 골 소실)**로 작용할 가능성.
> - **연결부 설계(connection)는 부적합과 독립적인 별개 변수**: Kim 2022(n=374, 동일 설계·연결부만 상이)에서 외부연결 (external hex)이 1년 변연골소실↑(1.23 vs 0.72 mm, P<.001)이나 5년엔 수렴, 생존율은 차이 없음(93.3 vs 90.7%). 초기 골 적응 후 수렴하는 패턴.
> - **임상 보호인자**: Kim 2022에서 나사-시멘트 혼합유지 보철 (Screw-and-Cement-Retained Prosthesis, SCRP)이 변연골소실 감소와 독립 연관(P=.027) — 잉여 시멘트 완전 제거 가능 때문. 위험인자는 중증 흡연·긴 임플란트·브릿지.
> - **종합 임상 프레임워크**: ① 가능한 최소 부적합을 목표로 하되 ㎛ 단위 완벽주의에 집착할 필요는 없다(150 μm도 19년 생존). ② ≥0.1 mm 가시적 변연 갭은 재제작·수정 대상. ③ 골 소실의 진짜 동인은 흡연·치주염 같은 **환자 위험인자**이므로 부적합 통제와 함께 이 인자들을 우선 관리. ④ 연결부는 내부 원추형 (internal conical)이 초기 변연골소실에 유리하나 장기 생존은 동등 — 부적합 관리가 연결부 선택보다 비중이 크지 않다.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 4 papers (1 SR + 3 long-term/quantitative cohorts): implant-prosthesis marginal misfit reliably drives screw-related complications but its link to peri-implant bone loss is weak and largely biologically (biofilm) mediated — a ≥0.1 mm gap marks a quantifiable threshold (each 0.1 mm → +0.08 mm bone loss; Couso-Queiruga 2025), yet 150 μm misfit is tolerated for ~19 years (Jokstad 2014) and patient factors (smoking, periodontitis) dominate bone loss; abutment-connection type (external vs internal conical) affects early MBL independently but converges by 5 years with equivalent survival (Kim 2022).

## Summary

Passive fit — seating a fixed implant prosthesis without inducing static strain in the framework, components, or peri-implant bone — has been a prosthodontic dogma since the Brånemark era. The implicit assumption was that any marginal misfit transmits load to bone and provokes peri-implant bone loss. The four papers synthesized here, read together, substantially **nuance** that assumption: misfit's most reliable consequence is **mechanical (screw complications)**, its biological (bone-loss) effect is small, threshold-dependent, and likely **biofilm-mediated rather than purely biomechanical**, and connection design is a **separate, independent** contributor to early marginal bone loss.

### The misfit-and-bone-loss question is genuinely nuanced

**Katsoulis 2017** (SR + Foundation for Oral Rehabilitation 2016 consensus; 2,626 records → 10 in vivo studies, 5 human + 5 animal) is the definitional anchor. Its three findings frame the whole topic: (1) misfit of screw-retained implant-supported fixed dentures **does** raise screw-loosening/fracture risk; (2) a **biological sequela — bone loss — could not be confirmed**; (3) a maximum tolerable misfit threshold **could not be defined**. Crucially, Katsoulis 2017 attributes the inability to answer the bone-loss question partly to **methodological heterogeneity**: misfit was assessed four incompatible ways across studies — linear distortion, vertical gap, strain gauges, and screw torque — making meta-analysis impossible. The clinical bottom line was conservative: aim for the least misfit achievable, but gaps and static strains do not appear to harm initial osseointegration or long-term peri-implant bone stability; they raise screw complications.

**Jokstad 2014** (retrospective, n=30 edentulous mandibles, average 19 years, range 12–32) supplies the longest-follow-up clinical misfit data and the strongest evidence for **long-term tolerance**. Using a then-novel 3D method (intraoral scan of implant positions + desktop scan of the detached FDP intaglio, matched in metrological software), it quantified a mean clinical misfit of **150 μm** (range 95–232). Despite that, implant survival was **96.7%** over ~19 years and the correlation between misfit and marginal bone loss was **weak and non-significant (R²=0.04, P=.29)**. The signal that *was* significant matched Katsoulis 2017: prostheses with screw complications had significantly higher misfit than those without (**169 vs 134 μm, P=.005**), giving an empirical screw-complication concern threshold around **>134 μm**. So Jokstad 2014 reinforces that misfit's consequence is mechanical, not biological — and that substantial misfit is survivable long-term.

**Couso-Queiruga 2025** (retrospective, n=301 patients / 505 Straumann tissue-level implants, 10.6 years, University of Bern) is the paper that finally **quantifies** the bone-loss association Katsoulis 2017 could not confirm — and in doing so reveals why it had been elusive. Radiographic gap analysis showed a monotonic, statistically significant univariate relationship: distance-to-bone (DIB) of **3.22 mm (no gap) → 3.36 mm (gap >0–<0.1 mm) → 3.45 mm (gap ≥0.1 mm)**, with **≥0.1 mm** as the threshold and a **dose-response of +0.08 mm bone loss per 0.1 mm gap** (P=.03). But the decisive twist is in the multivariate model: **gap dimension (GapDIM) lost significance (P=.28)**, while **smoking (+0.36 mm, P=.002)** and **history of periodontitis (+0.16 mm, P=.001)** remained strong independent predictors. The authors interpret this as misfit acting through a **biological pathway** — the gap is a biofilm reservoir that drives inflammation and bone loss — rather than direct biomechanical overload. (One scope caveat: tissue-level implants place the gap supracrestally, so bone-level implants, with the gap nearer the crest, may show a larger effect.)

So the misfit→bone-loss story across these three: **associated and dose-dependent in isolation, but small in magnitude, biologically (not mechanically) mediated, and subordinate to patient risk factors.** This is exactly the "nuanced, less catastrophic than dogma assumed" picture — passive fit matters, but perfect ㎛-level fit is neither achievable nor the dominant determinant of peri-implant bone health.

### Connection design is a separate, independent lever

**Kim 2022** (retrospective 5-year cohort, n=374 implants / 175 patients, Gangneung-Wonju National University, Korea) isolates a different variable: implant–abutment **connection type**. Its design strength is a near-matched pair — Sola (external hex, butt joint) vs Luna (internal 11° conical hex), Shinhung — identical in design and SLA surface, differing **only** in connection geometry, minimizing confounding. Findings: (1) **no survival difference** (external 93.3% vs internal 90.7%, P=.353); (2) **external connections lost significantly more bone at 1 year** (1.23 vs 0.72 mm, P<.001), but this **converged by 5 years** (1.20 vs 1.00 mm, P=.137) — an early-adaptation pattern consistent with microgap/microleakage being a transient early-loading phenomenon at the external butt joint. The internal conical connection's tighter seal reduces microgap and early crestal remodeling, but does not buy a long-term survival advantage. Kim 2022 also surfaces a clinical lever orthogonal to misfit: **SCRP (screw-and-cement-retained prosthesis) was independently associated with less MBL (P=.027)** — attributed to extraoral cementation allowing complete excess-cement removal — while **heavy smoking (P=.001), longer implants (P=.018), and bridge prostheses (P=.004)** raised MBL. The smoking finding independently echoes Couso-Queiruga 2025.

### What level of misfit matters · how it's measured · the connection contribution

|---|---|---|
| **What misfit causes screw complications?** | Mechanical risk rises with misfit; empirical concern band **>134 μm** (complication group 169 vs 134 μm) | Jokstad 2014; Katsoulis 2017 |
| **What misfit affects bone?** | Visible radiographic gap **≥0.1 mm** = quantitative threshold; +0.08 mm bone loss per 0.1 mm gap (univariate) | Couso-Queiruga 2025 |
| **Is the bone effect mechanical or biological?** | Likely **biological** — gap loses significance in multivariate; biofilm/inflammation pathway dominates | Couso-Queiruga 2025 |
| **Can substantial misfit be tolerated long-term?** | Yes — **150 μm mean misfit, 96.7% survival at ~19 yr**, weak/non-significant bone-loss correlation | Jokstad 2014 |
| **Is there a defined maximum tolerable misfit?** | **No** consensus threshold; aim for least achievable | Katsoulis 2017 |
| **How is misfit measured?** | 4 non-standardized methods (linear distortion, vertical gap, strain gauge, screw torque); 3D STL scanning emerging; radiographic gap for in-situ | Katsoulis 2017 (methods); Jokstad 2014 (3D); Couso-Queiruga 2025 (radiographic) |

### Clinical decision framework

1. **Aim for the least misfit achievable, but do not chase perfection.** Passive fit remains the goal, yet 150 μm misfit survived ~19 years (Jokstad 2014) and no consensus maximum exists (Katsoulis 2017). Micron-level obsession is not evidence-supported; **gross misfit avoidance** is.
2. **Use ≥0.1 mm visible radiographic gap as an actionable trigger.** A gap at or above this threshold is associated with measurable bone loss (Couso-Queiruga 2025) and warrants framework correction or remake rather than acceptance.
3. **Treat misfit's primary risk as mechanical (screw complications).** Misfit >134 μm clusters with screw loosening/fracture (Jokstad 2014; Katsoulis 2017) — manage with verified seating, correct preload/torque, and screw maintenance recall.
4. **Recognize the bone-loss pathway is biological, so control biology first.** Because gap dimension loses independent significance once smoking and periodontitis are accounted for (Couso-Queiruga 2025; smoking reconfirmed in Kim 2022), **smoking cessation and periodontal control outrank ㎛-level fit refinement** for protecting bone.
5. **Connection choice: prefer internal conical for early crestal preservation, but expect long-term parity.** External-hex butt joints show more 1-year MBL that converges by 5 years with equal survival (Kim 2022). Connection type is a real but limited and self-limiting variable — not a substitute for misfit and patient-factor control.
6. **Favor cement-removal-friendly retention (SCRP) where indicated.** SCRP was independently protective against MBL (Kim 2022), plausibly by eliminating retained subgingival cement — a controllable biological insult in the same inflammatory pathway as a misfit gap.

**Net thesis:** passive fit is a durable principle, not an absolute. The dominant, manageable consequence of misfit is screw complication; its bone effect is real but small, threshold-gated (≥0.1 mm gap), and mediated through biofilm/inflammation, which is why **patient risk factors (smoking, periodontitis) and excess-cement control matter more than the last micron of fit**. Connection design contributes independently to early bone remodeling but converges over time. The clinician's leverage is greatest on biology and gross fit, not on perfectionist passivity.
