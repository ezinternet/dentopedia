## One-line Summary

Dental zirconia has evolved from opaque 3Y-TZP cores (900–1200 MPa, LTD-susceptible) to composition-gradient multilayer restorations spanning all esthetic indications; 5-year survival is comparable to metal-ceramic (97.6% veneered, 91–100% monolithic), but intraoral LTD confirmed from 6 months with grain pull-out nanoparticle concern at 5 years, and CAD/CAM milling defects — not intrinsic blank flaws — are the dominant fracture initiators in as-sintered clinical restorations.

## 한줄요약

치과용 지르코니아는 불투명 3Y-TZP 코어(900–1200 MPa, LTD 취약)에서 조성경사 다층 블록까지 진화했으며, 5년 생존율은 금속도재관과 유사하나(베니어 97.6%, 모놀리식 91–100%), 구강 내 LTD가 6개월부터 확인되고 5년에 나노입자 유출이 우려되며, CAD/CAM 밀링 결함이 임상 파절의 주된 기원이다.

## Summary

### 핵심 명제

1. **Ceramic strength hierarchy**: Polycrystalline zirconia (Y-TZP, 800–1500 MPa) anchors the top of the dental ceramic strength ladder — more than double lithium disilicate (300–500 MPa) and an order of magnitude above feldspathic porcelain (70–90 MPa) (Warreth 2020).

2. **Generational taxonomy — strength vs. translucency trade-off**: Each increase in yttria content from 3Y-TZP through 4Y-PSZ to 5Y-PSZ gains translucency (>50% cubic phase at 5Y) at the direct cost of flexural strength (900–1200 MPa → 700–900 MPa → 600–700 MPa) and fracture toughness, because cubic phase cannot undergo transformation toughening (Cesar 2024).

3. **Transformation toughening is the central mechanical asset — and the LTD liability**: Stress at a crack tip drives tetragonal → monoclinic (t→m) phase transformation (~4% volume expansion) that closes the crack and yields fracture toughness KIc of 5–10 MPa·m^0.5 — the highest among dental ceramics. The same phase susceptibility makes 3Y-TZP vulnerable to moisture-driven LTD at body temperature (Chopra 2024).

4. **CAD/CAM milling defects dominate as-sintered fracture risk**: In a seven-material in vitro study (3Y, 4Y, 5Y), as-sintered specimens fail from extrinsic milling gouges and edge chips (lower mean strength, higher Weibull modulus); polishing shifts failure to intrinsic subsurface pores and grain clusters (higher mean, lower Weibull modulus). Neither surface state is clearly superior in reliability (Kwon 2024).

5. **Veneered zirconia 5-year survival equals metal-ceramic overall, but material fracture rate is 10× higher**: SR of 35 studies (n=5,275 implant-supported crowns) shows 5-year survival of 97.6% for zirconia-ceramic vs. 98.3% for metal-ceramic (NS), yet material fracture is 2.1% vs. 0.2% (p=0.001). Chipping rates were nearly identical at 2.8% vs. 2.9% (Pjetursson 2018).

6. **Monolithic zirconia eliminates chipping but faces a short evidence base**: The first SR+MA on monolithic zirconia (MZ) CAD/CAM single crowns (9 studies, n=1,657) reports survival of 91–100%, but mean follow-up is only 1.07 years and heterogeneity is very high (I²=93.74%); bruxism is a documented risk factor with marginal integrity dropping to 31.60% in one bruxism cohort (Leitão 2022).

7. **Intraoral LTD is real, starts at 6 months, and progresses significantly through 5 years**: The Koenig prospective cohort (n=101 posterior 3Y-TZP monolithic prostheses) confirmed LTD onset at 6 months with localized non-uniform monoclinic clusters; at 5 years, LTD progression was statistically significant but remained at the extreme surface (ISO 25% Vm threshold not exceeded); fracture rate was 4.5% and whether LTD contributed is unresolved; glazing offers no protection at any timepoint (Koenig 2021, Koenig 2024).

8. **Grain pull-out from occlusal tribological stress may release 3Y-TZP nanoparticles**: The 5-year Koenig follow-up identified, via FIB-SEM, that tribological grain pull-out in occlusal areas differs mechanistically from classical nucleation-growth LTD, and raises a new concern of sub-micron 3Y-TZP nanoparticle release into the body — biocompatibility is unresolved and requires dedicated toxicological study (Koenig 2024).

9. **Intraoral ceramic repair is a viable minimally invasive alternative to replacement**: When fracture exposes a veneered zirconia core, the evidence-based protocol is airborne abrasion (50 µm Al₂O₃) on the zirconia core + HF etching (9.6%, 60 sec) of remaining veneering ceramic + silane + MDP-containing adhesive + incremental composite resin. For monolithic zirconia, sandblasting + MDP adhesive (no HF, no glass phase). Bond strength is the critical determinant of repair longevity (Alqarawi 2022).

## Results

### 축 1 — 세대 분류: 3Y → 5Y TZP (Strength vs. Translucency Trade-off)

Cesar et al. (2024) established the definitive generational taxonomy after screening 5,102 articles (Web of Science, 2008–2023):

| Generation | Composition | Cubic phase | Flexural strength | Translucency | Clinical role |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1st gen | 3Y-TZP (high alumina ~0.25 wt%) | <5% | 900–1200 MPa | Low (opaque) | Framework/core only; veneered |
| 2nd gen | Low-alumina 3Y-TZP | <5% | 900–1100 MPa | Improved | Monolithic posterior crowns/FPDs |
| 3rd gen | 4Y-PSZ | >30% | 700–900 MPa | High | Monolithic anterior and posterior |
| 4th gen | 5Y-PSZ | >50% | 600–700 MPa | Very high | Monolithic esthetic restorations |
| Ultra | >5 mol% Y | ~60% | 500–600 MPa | Maximum | High-esthetic, limited load areas |
| Current standard | Composition-gradient multilayer | Varies by layer | 3Y base + 5Y occlusal/incisal | Layered gradient | Full-contour, all indications |

**The alumina/yttria trade-off is the engine of every generation**: alumina (~0.25 wt%) improved sinterability and grain boundary strength in 1st gen, but reduces light transmittance. Reducing alumina and increasing yttria stabilizes more cubic phase (larger grains → less light scattering → more translucent), but cubic phase cannot transform under stress → no transformation toughening → lower KIc. Composition-gradient multilayer blocks resolve this by placing tough 3Y-TZP at the structural gingival zone and translucent 5Y-PSZ at the incisal/occlusal zone; inter-layer flaw problems of earlier versions have been resolved by newer powder compaction technology (Cesar 2024).

High-speed sintering (30–90 min vs. ~8 h standard) and 3D-printed zirconia remain under development and are not yet clinically reliable (Cesar 2024).

Warreth (2020) provides the broader ceramic hierarchy context: zirconia at 800–1500 MPa sits above all other dental ceramic classes, enabling its use in high-load posterior positions and multi-unit frameworks where glass-based ceramics would fail.

### 축 2 — 기계적 특성: Transformation Toughening, LTD, Fatigue

Chopra et al. (2024) synthesized zirconia's mechanical performance across prosthodontic and implant applications:

**Core property profile (3Y-TZP):**

| Property | Value | Clinical implication |
|---|---|---|
| Flexural strength | 900–1200 MPa | Withstands posterior occlusal loads |
| Fracture toughness (KIc) | 5–10 MPa·m^0.5 | Highest among dental ceramics; resists crack propagation |
| Hardness | ~1200 HV | High wear resistance; risk of opposing enamel wear on unpolished surfaces |
| Elastic modulus | ~200 GPa | Stiffer than bone (100–130 GPa); stress shielding risk in implants |
| LTD susceptibility | Higher with lower yttria | 3Y-TZP most susceptible; 4Y/5Y less so (more cubic phase) |

**Transformation toughening dual nature**: The t→m phase transformation (~4% volume expansion) that gives 3Y-TZP exceptional KIc is driven by the same metastable tetragonal phase susceptibility that makes it vulnerable to LTD. Moisture-driven t→m transformation at 100–400°C (or in body fluids at 37°C over years) progressively converts the surface to monoclinic phase, reduces integrity, and may initiate microcracking. Thus the mechanism that is zirconia's greatest strength at short-to-medium term is its primary long-term liability (Chopra 2024).

**Cyclic fatigue**: Under cyclic occlusal loads in a wet environment, subcritical crack growth occurs from existing defects — clinically relevant for long-span bridges and implant-supported prostheses. 4Y/5Y-PSZ shows lower transformation toughening and therefore potentially different fatigue behavior compared to 3Y-TZP.

**Wear**: Monolithic zirconia shows low wear itself but may cause higher opposing enamel wear than polished feldspathic porcelain on unpolished or re-ground surfaces.

### 축 3 — 결함 분석: CAD/CAM Processing Defects and Failure Origin

Kwon et al. (2024) characterized fracture origins in 7 commercial CAD/CAM zirconia materials (3Y, 4Y, 5Y; n=168 specimens total) using four-point bend testing and FE-SEM + EDS fractography:

**Two-population flaw model:**

| Surface state | Dominant fracture origin | Strength outcome | Weibull modulus |
|---|---|---|---|
| As-sintered (AS) | Extrinsic: milling gouges, edge chips, surface scratches | Lower mean | Higher (more consistent) |
| High-polished (HP) | Intrinsic: subsurface pores, large grain clusters, EDS-identified foreign inclusions (Al, Si, Ca, Fe), corner flaws | Higher mean | Generally lower (more variable) |

**Clinical implications of the polishing paradox**: Polishing removes surface milling defects (increasing mean strength) but exposes random intrinsic flaws from the blank and sintering process (decreasing Weibull modulus — i.e., worse reliability). Clinical zirconia restorations delivered glazed or lightly polished retain milling-introduced defects as active fracture initiators.

**5Y-PSZ heterogeneity**: Two of three 5Y-PSZ materials showed no significant strength change with polishing (milling damage possibly self-heals during high-temperature sintering or is less severe in higher-yttria compositions); one 5Y material showed significant strength increase but reliability decrease after polishing — manufacturer-dependent blank quality matters (Kwon 2024).

**EDS-identified contaminants** at fracture origins (aluminum, silicon, calcium, iron) suggest powder contamination or bur wear debris entering the blank — a clinically opaque manufacturing variable with direct fracture risk implications.

### 축 4 — 임상 생존율: Veneered Zirconia vs. Metal-Ceramic (SR), and Monolithic Zirconia (SR+MA)

**Pjetursson 2018 — Veneered zirconia vs. metal-ceramic (SR, 35 studies, n=5,275 implant-supported crowns):**

| Outcome | Veneered zirconia-ceramic | Metal-ceramic | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5-year survival | 97.6% (95% CI 94.3–99.0) | 98.3% (95% CI 96.8–99.1) | NS |
| Complication-free | 83.8% | 86.7% | — |
| Chipping | 2.8% | 2.9% | NS |
| Material fracture | 2.1% | 0.2% | p=0.001 |
| Aesthetic complications | Fewer | More | — |
| Biological complications | Similar | Similar | — |

The 10× higher material fracture rate in veneered zirconia (2.1% vs. 0.2%, p=0.001) is the dominant technical risk signal. No monolithic zirconia studies met the ≥3-year threshold at the time of this review's literature search (through November 2016), making Pjetursson 2018 specific to bi-layered constructions only (Pjetursson 2018).

**Leitão 2022 — Monolithic zirconia CAD/CAM single crowns (SR+MA, 9 studies, n=1,657):**

| Parameter | Finding |
|---|---|
| Survival range | 91%–100% |
| Mean follow-up | 1.07 years (range 0.3–2.1 yr) |
| Chipping | None (by definition — monolithic, no veneer) |
| BOP mean | 29.12% |
| Bruxism subgroup — marginal integrity | 31.60% |
| Fractures requiring replacement | Reported in 3 of 9 studies |
| Heterogeneity I² | 93.74% |
| SR vs. cementation type (p) | 0.730 (NS) |

Monolithic zirconia eliminates the veneer chipping complication entirely and achieves acceptable short-term survival, but the evidence base is constrained by very short follow-up (mean 1.07 years) and high heterogeneity. Bruxism is the standout patient-level risk factor. The key clinical gap this paper opens: long-term survival beyond 2 years, and the role of LTD in explaining fractures — addressed separately by the Koenig cohort (Leitão 2022).

### 축 5 — 저온분해 (LTD): Clinical Documentation, Onset, Progression, Nanoparticle Concern

The Koenig prospective cohort (NCT02150226, n=101 posterior 3Y-TZP monolithic elements, Liège, Belgium) provides the only long-term clinical LTD dataset with quantitative Raman spectroscopy:

**Koenig 2021 (2-year results):**
- LTD onset confirmed at **6 months** post-insertion via ex vivo Raman spectroscopy (2,640 measurement points/timepoint)
- Two mechanistically distinct LTD subtypes:
  - **Axial areas**: Classical nucleation-growth t→m transformation, progressing surface-inward
  - **Occlusal areas**: Tribological grain pull-out — distinct mechanism; standard Raman quantification underestimates severity in this zone
- LTD progression is non-uniform (localized clusters), not a uniform surface transformation
- ISO 25% monoclinic volume fraction (Vm) threshold not exceeded at 2 years
- **Glazing offers no protection** against LTD
- No prosthetic failures attributable to LTD at 2 years

**Koenig 2024 (5-year results, same cohort, adds FIB-SEM):**
- LTD progression from baseline to 5 years is **statistically significant**
- Transformation remains confined to the extreme surface in non-uniform clusters — ISO threshold apparently not exceeded
- **New key finding**: Tribological grain pull-out may release **sub-micron 3Y-TZP nanoparticles** into the oral cavity and body. Biocompatibility is unknown and requires dedicated toxicological follow-up
- **Fracture rate at 5 years: 4.5%** — whether LTD contributed to these fractures is not established
- Glazing remains non-protective (consistent with 2-year data)

**Clinical significance**: LTD in functioning 3Y-TZP restorations is real and progressive — not merely a laboratory artifact from autoclave testing. The ISO threshold not being exceeded at 5 years is partially reassuring for current clinical performance, but the nanoparticle release pathway and its long-term biological consequences are genuinely unresolved. The 4.5% fracture rate at 5 years (Koenig 2024) is substantially higher than the Leitão 2022 SR+MA upper bound of 0–9% (across all 9 studies), and the LTD contribution cannot be excluded.

### 축 6 — 파절 수리: Intraoral Ceramic Repair

Alqarawi et al. (2022) reviewed 21 studies (17 in vitro, 3 case reports, 1 prospective clinical) from 2017–2022. Ceramic chipping is the most frequent technical complication in fixed prosthodontics (12.7% at 3 years per prior SR data cited in the review). Intraoral repair avoids the risks of abutment damage and excessive tooth reduction from crown removal.

**Surface conditioning protocol by ceramic type:**

| Substrate | Protocol | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Feldspathic / Leucite (veneering ceramic) | HF etching + silane + composite | HF + silane > HF + universal adhesive (bond strength) |
| Lithium disilicate | HF + silane; grit blasting + rubber dam | MDP primer as alternative |
| Zirconia-reinforced lithium silicate | HF ± laser (Nd:YAG, Er:YAG) | Laser combination may be superior |
| Veneered zirconia — veneer portion | HF (9.6%, 60 sec) + silane | Applied to glass-containing veneering ceramic |
| Veneered zirconia — exposed core | Airborne abrasion (50 µm Al₂O₃) + MDP-containing adhesive | No glass phase in zirconia core → HF ineffective |
| Monolithic zirconia | Sandblasting + MDP adhesive | No HF; silane alone insufficient |

**Standard step-by-step protocol for veneered zirconia repair (Alqarawi 2022):**
1. Airborne abrasion (50 µm Al₂O₃) on exposed zirconia core surface
2. HF acid etching (9.6%) of remaining veneering ceramic — 60 seconds
3. Rinse and dry
4. Silane application to entire repair area; dry/heat-activate
5. MDP-containing adhesive resin application
6. Composite resin application in increments; light cure each increment

The primary failure mode is inadequate bond strength from technique deviation or contamination. Repair is positioned as a viable temporary-to-medium-term option that preserves tooth structure and avoids abutment damage — not as equivalent to a new restoration (Alqarawi 2022).

## Clinical Decision Threads

**Thread 1 — Material selection for monolithic posterior crowns (high-load zones)**

Warreth (2020) and Cesar (2024) jointly support monolithic 3Y-TZP or low-alumina 3Y-TZP (2nd gen) for posterior single crowns in bruxism-free patients needing maximum strength. However, the LTD signal from Koenig (2021, 2024) introduces a long-term surveillance requirement: 3Y-TZP restorations should be monitored for surface changes, and nanoparticle concerns from grain pull-out in occlusal areas have not been resolved toxicologically. For esthetic posterior-anterior cases, 4Y-PSZ or multilayer (3Y base + 5Y top) represents the current market standard (Cesar 2024) and avoids the worst LTD susceptibility while retaining adequate strength (700–900 MPa).

**Thread 2 — Veneered vs. monolithic construction for implant-supported crowns**

Pjetursson (2018) shows that veneered zirconia-ceramic achieves 5-year survival equivalent to metal-ceramic (97.6% vs. 98.3%), but at the cost of a 10× higher material fracture rate (2.1% vs. 0.2%, p=0.001). Leitão (2022) confirms that monolithic zirconia eliminates the chipping problem entirely and achieves 91–100% short-term survival, but the mean follow-up is only 1.07 years and bruxism dramatically worsens marginal integrity (31.60%). The practical decision rule: for non-bruxers, monolithic zirconia reduces the dominant complication of veneered systems (chipping); for bruxers, both veneered and monolithic carry elevated risk, and the choice should be accompanied by occlusal protection and close surveillance.

**Thread 3 — Surface finishing decisions at chairside delivery**

Kwon (2024) reveals that as-sintered zirconia retains milling defects as active fracture initiators, while polishing removes them but introduces random intrinsic flaws with lower Weibull modulus (less consistent reliability). Neither state is clearly optimal. The practical implication: any chairside adjustment that re-introduces grinding marks without subsequent polishing or glazing creates a fresh stress concentration. When occlusal adjustment is necessary, complete the adjustment then re-polish or re-glaze before delivery. The polishing paradox is material-grade-specific — 5Y-PSZ materials respond differently from 3Y-TZP to polishing (Kwon 2024).

**Thread 4 — Managing the fracture/chipping complication**

When veneered zirconia chips or fractures, intraoral repair is the first-line option before deciding on crown replacement. The two-substrate protocol (HF + silane on the glass-containing veneer; sandblasting + MDP adhesive on the exposed zirconia core) is the evidence-based approach (Alqarawi 2022). For monolithic zirconia fractures, no HF is used — sandblasting + MDP adhesive is the protocol. Repair is appropriate as a minimally invasive bridge to definitive replacement when the extent of fracture and remaining bond area are favorable. Glazing of the repair site is not protective against LTD (Koenig 2021, Koenig 2024), so it should not be offered as an LTD mitigation strategy.
