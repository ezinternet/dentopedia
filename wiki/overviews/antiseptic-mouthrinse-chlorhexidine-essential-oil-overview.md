## 한국어 핵심요약

> [!summary] 한국어 핵심요약
> - 핵심 명제: 항균 가글은 **기계적 위생의 보조이지 대체가 아니며**, 치은염·치태에는 효과적이나 부착소실(부착수준, CAL/치주낭깊이, PD)을 개선하지 못한다. 약물 선택은 "효능"보다 **부작용·사용기간·미생물 영향**으로 갈린다. 7편 종합(NMA 1 + Cochrane SR+MA 1 + SR 1 + RCT 4, 치주학 + 구강미생물학 교차).
> - **효능 순위 (치은염 감소, 네트워크 메타분석/NMA)**: 에센셜오일 (Essential Oil, EO) ≥ 클로르헥시딘 (Chlorhexidine, CHX) ≥0.10% ≈ triclosan-copolymer > 세틸피리디늄 (CPC) >0.05% (Figuero 2019, 53 RCT, n=8,457). EO가 수치상 최상위.
> - **치태 억제 챔피언은 CHX**: Cochrane SR+MA에서 CHX 가글 치태 표준화평균차 (Standardized Mean Difference, SMD) **−1.45** (대폭), 치은염 GI −0.21 (통계적 유의하나 임상적 효과는 경미) (James 2017, 51 RCT).
> - **CHX 농도 논쟁**: Figuero는 유효 임계값 **≥0.10%** 제시; James(Cochrane)는 "어느 농도(0.05/0.12/0.2%)가 더 낫다는 근거 없다"; Escribano RCT는 **0.05% CHX+CPC**도 치태·출혈(BoP)·타액 세균(F. nucleatum·P. intermedia)을 유의 감소시킴을 실증. → 표준 0.12%, 저농도도 임상 유효.
> - **CHX의 아킬레스건 = 부작용**: 치아 착색 SMD **+1.07** (4주↑ 일관), 미각이상·점막 자극·작열감 (James). 따라서 **장기 사용 부적합** → 단기·고필요 상황에 한정.
> - **수술 후 적응증**: 치주·임플란트 수술 후 기계적 위생이 어려운 기간에 CHX 가글이 1주 치태 29–86%·출혈 최대 73% 감소; **0.12% + 항착색시스템(ADS)** 이 효능/부작용 비율 최적 (Solderer 2019, 11편). 단 PD 추가 개선은 없음.
> - **EO의 강점 = 착색 없음 + 미생물 친화적**: Listerine형 EO는 CHX와 효능 유사하면서 착색 부작용이 없어 **장기 가정 사용 대안**. 12주 매일 사용에도 구인두 마이크로바이오타 유의 변화 없음(안전) (Plummer 2022); 반면 CHX는 7일 사용만으로도 다양성·질산환원균 감소 보고.
> - **EO의 미생물 "reset" (단, 이해상충 주의)**: spike-in 절대정량 메타게놈 RCT에서 EO 가글이 4주 만에 이상증식(dysbiosis) 치태를 건강 조성으로 전환, 치은염 ≥37% 감소 — 단 Johnson & Johnson 후원 연구로 해석 주의 (Min 2024). 기전은 광범위 살균 후 건강 상재균 우선 재정착.
> - **비치과적 보조변수**: 흡연자는 NSPT 후 가글의 항진균·치주 효과가 상쇄됨 (Basudan 2023); 허브 가글(miswak)은 항염은 CHX와 동등하나 Candida 억제는 CHX가 우월.
> - **공통 한계 — 구조 개선 없음**: 모든 가글은 치태·염증을 줄일 뿐 PD/CAL을 회복시키지 못한다 (Escribano, Solderer). 가글은 **기계적 위생이 불충분한 환자·기간의 chemo-preventive 보조**일 뿐.
> - **임상 결정 ladder**: 장기 가정 가글 → EO(효능≈CHX, 무착색, 미생물 친화); 단기·수술후·NSPT 보조·SPC 불순응 → 0.12% CHX(+ADS), 보통 2–4주 한정; 착색 우려·심미 환자 → EO 또는 허브 대안; 흡연자 → 가글 효과 기대치 하향.
> - **확신도**: 효능·착색(축 1·4) 근거 강함(Cochrane + NMA); 미생물 reset(축 5)은 RCT지만 산업후원·단일연구 → 중간.

## One-line Summary

Synthesis of 7 papers (1 network meta-analysis, 1 Cochrane SR+MA, 1 SR, 4 RCTs; periodontics + oral-microbiology) on antiseptic mouthrinses for chemical plaque control. Mouthrinses are adjuncts to — never substitutes for — mechanical hygiene, and reduce plaque/gingivitis but not pocket depth or attachment level. For gingivitis, essential oils (EO) rank at least as high as chlorhexidine (CHX) ≥0.10% ≈ triclosan (Figuero 2019 NMA, 53 RCTs), while CHX is the strongest plaque suppressor (Cochrane SMD −1.45; James 2017). CHX's defining liability is tooth staining (SMD +1.07), making it a short-term/high-need agent (post-surgery, NSPT adjunct, non-compliant SPC) best used at 0.12% with antidiscoloration additives. EO matches CHX efficacy without staining and is microbiome-friendly (12-week microbiota-neutral; Plummer 2022) and may even "reset" dysbiotic plaque (Min 2024, industry-funded), making it the preferred long-term home rinse. Smoking blunts mouthrinse benefit (Basudan 2023).

## Summary

Chemical plaque control with antiseptic mouthrinses is one of the most common adjuncts in periodontal and preventive practice. This overview integrates the highest-quality comparative evidence — a network meta-analysis ranking agents head-to-head (Figuero 2019), a Cochrane SR+MA on chlorhexidine (James 2017), a post-surgical SR (Solderer 2019), and four RCTs spanning low-concentration CHX (Escribano 2010), herbal comparison (Basudan 2023), and microbiome effects (Min 2024; Plummer 2022) — into a single decision framework. Two agents dominate the evidence: **chlorhexidine (CHX)** and **essential oils (EO, e.g. LISTERINE-type thymol/eucalyptol/menthol/methyl salicylate)**.

### Axis 1 — Comparative efficacy for gingivitis

Figuero 2019's NMA (53 RCTs, n=8,457; 6-month home-use studies) ranks essential oils highest for gingival index reduction, with CHX ≥0.10% and triclosan-copolymer statistically indistinguishable and CPC >0.05% also effective. The clinically important message is that **EO matches CHX efficacy for gingivitis** while avoiding CHX's staining — making EO a reasonable long-term alternative.

### Axis 2 — Plaque suppression: CHX is the strongest

For raw antiplaque power, CHX remains the benchmark. The Cochrane SR+MA (James 2017, 51 RCTs, n=5,345) reports a **large plaque reduction (SMD −1.45)** at 4–6 weeks, sustained at 6 months, with a smaller, clinically modest gingivitis reduction (GI −0.21). CHX's plaque effect exceeds what EO/CPC achieve — but plaque suppression alone is not the clinical endpoint that matters most.

### Axis 3 — Concentration: 0.12% standard, but lower works

Three lines of evidence converge on a nuanced position. Figuero sets an efficacy threshold at **CHX ≥0.10%**. James (Cochrane) concludes there is **no evidence that one concentration (0.05/0.12/0.2%) is more effective than another**. Escribano 2010 (double-blind RCT, n=47, non-compliant SPC periodontitis) demonstrates that even **0.05% CHX + 0.05% CPC** significantly reduces plaque, bleeding, and salivary/subgingival pathogens (*F. nucleatum*, *P. intermedia*) vs placebo. Practically: 0.12% is the standard, 0.2% offers no proven efficacy advantage but more side effects, and low-concentration formulas are a legitimate option for sensitive or long-term use.

### Axis 4 — Side effects: CHX's defining constraint

CHX's Achilles heel is its side-effect profile: **tooth staining (SMD +1.07**, consistent beyond 4 weeks), taste disturbance, mucosal irritation/desquamation, and burning sensation (James 2017). This is precisely why CHX is **not** a long-term agent. Solderer 2019 shows that **antidiscoloration systems (ADS)** (ascorbic acid, sodium bisulfate) added to 0.12% CHX reduce staining while preserving efficacy — the best benefit-to-harm ratio.

### Axis 5 — Microbiome effects: EO is gentler, and may "reset"

The microbiology axis separates the two agents further. Plummer 2022 (OMEGA substudy RCT, n=153) found **12 weeks of daily alcohol-free EO (Listerine Zero) caused no significant shift** in the oropharyngeal microbiota (PERMANOVA p=0.413, no FDR-significant taxa) — contrasted against published CHX data where just 7 days causes diversity loss and nitrate-reducing-bacteria depletion. Min 2024 (RCT, n=153, spike-in absolute-quantification metagenomics) goes further: alcohol-containing EO **shifted dysbiotic plaque toward healthy composition within 4 weeks** ("microbiome reset") while reducing gingivitis ≥37%, via non-selective broad-spectrum bactericidal action followed by preferential repopulation of health-associated commensals. **Caveat**: Min is Johnson & Johnson-funded (strong COI), so the reset claim, while mechanistically plausible, is a single industry-sponsored result.

### Axis 6 — Indication-specific use and modifiers

- **Post-surgery** (Solderer 2019): CHX is a chemo-preventive tool during the window when mechanical hygiene is impaired — 1-week plaque reduction 29–86%, bleeding up to 73%; use **0.12% + ADS**. No additional PD improvement.
- **NSPT adjunct** (Basudan 2023): 0.12% CHX reduces periodontal inflammation in non-smokers; **smoking abolishes the benefit** (no periodontal or antifungal effect). Herbal *Salvadora persica* (miswak) equals CHX for anti-inflammation but CHX is superior for *Candida* suppression.
- **Non-compliant SPC** (Escribano 2010): low-concentration CHX controls plaque and pathogens when mechanical hygiene alone is insufficient.
- **Home gingivitis control** (Figuero/James): EO or CHX; EO favored for long-term because of staining.

### The shared limitation — no structural gain

Every rinse in this synthesis controls plaque and inflammation but **does not improve pocket depth or clinical attachment level** (Escribano, Solderer). Mouthrinses are adjuncts for periods or patients where mechanical control is inadequate — they never replace scaling/root planing or address attachment loss.

### Clinical decision ladder

1. **Long-term home rinse** → essential oils (efficacy ≈ CHX, no staining, microbiome-friendly).
2. **Short-term / high-need** (post-surgery, NSPT adjunct, non-compliant SPC) → 0.12% CHX (+ ADS), typically 2–4 weeks.
3. **Esthetic concern / staining-averse** → EO or herbal alternative; if CHX needed, add ADS.
4. **Smokers** → lower expectations for any rinse; reinforce smoking cessation and mechanical control.

## Key Contributions

- Synthesizes the two dominant antiseptic rinses (CHX vs EO) across 6 axes — efficacy ranking, plaque suppression, concentration, side effects, microbiome, and indication-specific use
- Reconciles the apparent CHX-vs-EO tension: CHX wins on raw plaque suppression (SMD −1.45) but EO matches it on gingivitis (NMA top-ranked) while avoiding staining and microbiome disruption
- Settles the concentration question pragmatically: 0.12% standard, no proven advantage of 0.2%, 0.05% clinically effective (Escribano) — consistent with Cochrane's "no concentration superior"
- Surfaces the microbiome dimension as a differentiator (EO microbiota-neutral/reset vs CHX disruptive) and flags the industry-funding caveat on the reset claim
- Anchors the universal limitation: rinses reduce plaque/inflammation but never improve PD/CAL — adjunct, not therapy
- Translates to an indication-driven decision ladder (long-term EO vs short-term/high-need CHX+ADS) with smoking as an effect modifier
