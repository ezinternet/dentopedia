## One-line Summary

Narrative review of *Streptococcus sanguinis* biofilm formation mechanisms and antagonistic interactions with cariogenic and periodontopathic pathogens, highlighting this commensal's dual role in oral health protection and infective endocarditis risk.

## Summary

*Streptococcus sanguinis* is a health-associated commensal bacterium that serves as a pioneering colonizer of the oral cavity, arriving in infants at a median age of 9 months and occupying tooth surfaces, mucosa, and saliva. Despite its abundance and health-association, research has overwhelmingly focused on pathogens rather than commensals. This 2018 narrative review synthesizes the molecular biology of *S. sanguinis* biofilm formation, its competitive interactions with caries and periodontal pathogens, and its paradoxical role as a cause of infective endocarditis.

**Biofilm formation** proceeds in two stages. Initial attachment is mediated by fimbriae (PilA/B/C) binding to salivary components on the hydroxyapatite pellicle, and by adhesins SsaB (binds saliva-coated hydroxyapatite) and SrpA (binds human sialoglycans). Biofilm maturation depends on glucan synthesis by GtfP (the only functional glucosyltransferase in *S. sanguinis* SK36) and eDNA release driven by H₂O₂ from the pyruvate oxidase SpxB. L-arginine, CiaR/BrpT transcriptional regulators, and nucleotide biosynthesis genes also modulate biofilm architecture.

**Against *S. mutans***: *S. sanguinis* produces H₂O₂ via SpxB, suppressing *S. mutans* growth. *S. mutans* counter-attacks with mutacins (I and IV) and thrives under acidic, nutrient-rich (high sugar) conditions that suppress SpxB. *S. sanguinis* uses the arginine deiminase system (ADS) to neutralize acid, and can inactivate the competence-stimulating peptide (CSP) that triggers *S. mutans* mutacin production. Exogenous L-arginine selectively reduces *S. mutans* biomass more than *S. sanguinis* in dual-species biofilms; arginine + fluoride combination further suppresses cariogenic activity.

**Against periodontal pathogens**: 16S rRNA sequencing consistently associates *S. sanguinis* with periodontal health and depleted abundance in diseased sites. However, the picture is ambiguous mechanistically: *P. gingivalis* fimbriae bind to *S. sanguinis* surface GAPDH, potentially using the commensal as a scaffold. *S. sanguinis* suppresses *P. gingivalis* mfa1 short fimbriae expression. *F. nucleatum* RadD/Aid1 adhesins mediate coaggregation with *S. sanguinis*, and coaggregation partially shields *F. nucleatum* from H₂O₂ killing. *A. actinomycetemcomitans* soft-tissue colonization is reduced by *S. sanguinis* under flow conditions.

**Infective endocarditis**: *S. sanguinis* is among the top three causes of endocarditis alongside staphylococci and enterococci. Despite this, biofilm formation ability does NOT correlate with endocarditis virulence in animal models — four of eight biofilm-defective *S. sanguinis* mutants retained full virulence. Sucrose-dependent glucan production is also unlikely on heart valves given normal blood sucrose is nearly absent. The cardiac "vegetation" (platelet-fibrin nodule) resembles but does not strictly qualify as a biofilm per definition.

## Key Contributions

- Synthesizes molecular details of *S. sanguinis* biofilm initiation (PilA, SsaB, SrpA) and maturation (GtfP glucan, eDNA, SpxB H₂O₂)
- Defines SpxB-mediated H₂O₂ as the primary ecological weapon of *S. sanguinis* against *S. mutans*
- Documents L-arginine ecological caries management: selectively enriches *S. sanguinis*, depletes *S. mutans*, synergistic with fluoride
- Identifies ADS as *S. sanguinis* pH-buffering mechanism against cariogenic acid
- Highlights paradox: *S. sanguinis* is a health marker but also an attachment scaffold for *P. gingivalis* and *F. nucleatum*
- Disentangles endocarditis from oral biofilm biology — cardiac virulence is biofilm-independent
- Argues for research parity between commensals and pathogens; dysbiosis understanding requires both

## Methodology

- Narrative review of peer-reviewed literature on *S. sanguinis* SK36 (reference strain with fully sequenced 2.39 Mbp genome)
- Evidence sources: in vitro co-culture antagonism assays, sHA adhesion assays, dual-species biofilm formation, 16S rRNA sequencing epidemiology, FISH spatial mapping (Welch et al.), rabbit infective endocarditis animal model
- Key analytical tool: signature-tagged mutagenesis library (800 mutants) screened for biofilm defects and endocarditis virulence

## Results

| Interaction | Mechanism | Net outcome |
|---|---|---|
| *S. sanguinis* vs *S. mutans* | SpxB H₂O₂; ADS pH buffering; CSP inactivation | *S. sanguinis* suppresses *S. mutans*; H₂O₂ effect abolished in high-sugar/low-pH |
| L-arginine treatment | Reduces GtfP-glucan in *S. mutans*; less effect on *S. sanguinis* | Ecological shift toward *S. sanguinis*, anti-caries potential |
| *S. sanguinis* vs *P. gingivalis* | *P. gingivalis* binds *S. sanguinis* GAPDH; *S. sanguinis* suppresses mfa1 | Ambiguous: commensal may facilitate pathogen attachment |
| *S. sanguinis* vs *F. nucleatum* | RadD/Aid1 coaggregation; H₂O₂ attenuated by coaggregation | *F. nucleatum* partially escapes killing via *S. sanguinis* coaggregation |
| *S. sanguinis* vs *A. actinomycetemcomitans* | Competitive exclusion from soft-tissue surfaces (flow model) | *S. sanguinis* protective in flow conditions |
| Infective endocarditis | Biofilm defects do not predict virulence; glucan absent without sucrose | Endocarditis virulence is biofilm-independent |
