## One-line Summary
Narrative review of oral streptococcal taxonomy, virulence factors, and immunomodulatory roles, showing how these pioneer colonisers maintain homeostasis in health but serve as accessory pathogens and systemic infection sources in disease.

## Summary
Bloch et al. review the full spectrum of oral streptococcal biology across eight phylogenetic groups. Streptococci dominate all oral niches and act as pioneer colonisers enabling later microbial succession. Their immunomodulatory role is complex: 30% of plaque-derived isolates suppress IL-8 in epithelial cells; S. mitis induces protective hBD-2 while simultaneously damping neutrophil activity via IL-10, PD-L1, and PGE2. As accessory pathogens, S. gordonii substantially elevates the pathogenicity of P. gingivalis and A. actinomycetemcomitans; in germ-free mice, P. gingivalis cannot cause periodontal disease without the commensal streptococcal core. Cariogenic potential of mutans streptococci (S. mutans, S. sobrinus) derives from three traits: copious glucan EPS synthesis from sucrose, acidogenicity, and acid tolerance. Dissemination into the bloodstream causes infective endocarditis (notably S. mitis, S. oralis, S. gordonii), bacteraemia, and brain haemorrhage.

## Key Contributions
- Eight-group phylogenetic classification; mitis group is largest (20 oral members) and includes core oral microbiota.
- 30% of plaque streptococcal isolates downregulate IL-8; quantified anti-inflammatory prevalence.
- Dual accessory-pathogen role: streptococci scaffold periodontal pathogen pathogenicity without being primary pathogens themselves.
- AgI/II adhesin family: broad-spectrum receptor binding (fibronectin, collagen, laminin, fibrinogen, platelets).
- Systemic disease: S. mitis/oralis/gordonii as causative agents of infective endocarditis after bacteraemia.

## Methodology
Narrative (non-systematic) review. Synthesises in vitro, germ-free mouse, and clinical literature on streptococcal taxonomy, ecology, adhesion, immunomodulation, and disease associations.

## Results
Streptococcus is dominant genus in supra-/subgingival plaque and soft tissues. S. mitis and S. oralis are core oral microbiota. 30% of isolates show anti-IL-8 activity. P. gingivalis fails to induce periodontal disease in germ-free mice without commensal streptococcal core. AgI/II binds α5β1 integrin, fibrinogen, and platelets. CovR represses virulence and modulates complement resistance in S. mutans.
