Narrative review of the oral microbiome from community assembly and multi-omics methods to the polymicrobial networks underlying caries, periodontitis, and systemic dissemination.

## Summary
Sedghi et al. present a comprehensive review of the oral microbiome from initial neonatal colonisation through adult dysbiosis. Pioneer streptococci (S. mitis, S. sanguinis, S. gordonii, S. salivarius) establish early communities before tooth eruption; successional colonisation driven by bridging organisms leads to complex polymicrobial dental plaque. The review evaluates culture-independent research platforms — 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing, shotgun metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, metabolomics, and single-cell sequencing — explaining the limitations of each. Oral disease (caries, periodontitis) is attributed to network-level dysbiosis rather than single pathogens, with diet (Westernised sugar-rich diet) and oral hygiene as major selective pressures. Oral pathobionts are detected in distant organs (lung, heart, brain, placenta), establishing the oral microbiome as a systemic reservoir. Over 250 species have been cultivated from the oral cavity.

## Key Contributions
- Successional colonisation model: pioneer streptococci → bridging species → late anaerobes.
- Multi-omics framework for oral microbiome research with platform-specific strengths/limitations.
- Polymicrobial disease model — dysbiotic community networks drive caries and periodontitis.
- Diet and oral hygiene as dominant environmental selective pressures on microbiome composition.
- Oral-systemic disease axis: pathobionts disseminate to cardiovascular and other distant sites.

## Methodology
Narrative (non-systematic) review. Synthesises published experimental (germ-free mouse, in vitro biofilm), epidemiological, and multi-omics literature. No defined population/intervention/control.

## Results
>250 cultivated oral species documented. Pioneer colonisers bind mucosal surfaces before tooth eruption. Three dominant NGS platforms: Illumina MiSeq (short-read), PacBio, Oxford Nanopore (long-read). Germ-free mouse studies confirm causal microbiota role in caries and periodontal bone loss. Oral bacteria detected in intestine, lung, heart, placenta, and brain. Arginine supplementation and targeted probiotics (anti-S. mutans, alkalinity-promoting) identified as microbiome modulation strategies.
