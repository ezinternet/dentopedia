## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Folia Microbiologica 2025) cataloguing oral dysbiosis mechanisms across caries, periodontitis, halitosis, and candidiasis, then surveying emerging microbiota-based therapies: probiotics, postbiotics, predatory bacteria (Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus), bacteriophages, and oral microbiota transplantation.

## Summary

The oral microbiome is the second-most diverse microbial community in the human body (>1,100 taxa in the HOMD), spanning bacteria, fungi, viruses, archaea, and protozoa across all seven dominant phyla (Firmicutes, Proteobacteria, Actinobacteria, Bacteroidetes, Fusobacteria, Spirochaetes, TM7). Disruption of this ecosystem — dysbiosis — underlies dental caries, periodontal disease, halitosis, and oral candidiasis.

The review frames dysbiosis not as simple pathogen invasion but as ecological collapse: caries develops through Takahashi & Nyvad's three-stage progression (dynamic stable → acidogenic → aciduric) driven by S. mutans acid production and "rotund" biofilm architecture. Periodontitis is orchestrated by the red complex (P. gingivalis / T. forsythia / T. denticola), where P. gingivalis acts as a keystone pathogen — subverting complement (C3/C5 cleavage by gingipains), blunting TLR4 signaling, and suppressing IL-8 chemotaxis at low abundance to enable permissive dysbiosis. Halitosis arises from proteolytic VSC production (H₂S, methyl mercaptan) by Gram-negative anaerobes in tongue coating and periodontal pockets. Oral candidiasis reflects Candida–bacterial (particularly Candida–Streptococcus mutans) synergism through ALS3/glucan-mediated co-adhesion.

Emerging therapies reviewed include:
- **Probiotics**: S. salivarius K12/M18 and Lactobacillus spp. reduce plaque/bleeding indices, modulate cytokines (↓IL-1β, IL-8; ↑IL-10, IgA), and reduce S. mutans in caries trials.
- **Postbiotics**: Heat-killed bacterial preparations (L. paracasei ET-22) show similar efficacy to live probiotics for halitosis and plaque, with improved safety/stability.
- **Predatory bacteria (BALOs)**: B. bacteriovorus selectively invades Gram-negative periplasm and self-limits once prey is depleted — targeting P. gingivalis and T. forsythia without harming Gram-positive commensals or eukaryotic cells.
- **Bacteriophages**: Lytic phages against S. mutans (SMHBZ8, ɸAPCM01), E. faecalis (PEf771, HEf13), F. nucleatum (FNU1), and S. gordonii (ΦSG005) demonstrated in vitro and some in vivo efficacy.
- **Oral microbiota transplantation (OMT)**: Analogous to FMT; shown in a canine periodontitis model, a pediatric oncology mucositis prevention pilot (maternal saliva; no adverse effects), and a halitosis rat model.

## Key Contributions

1. **Red complex virulence mechanistic synthesis** — P. gingivalis gingipains cleave C3/C5/LL-37 and inhibit neutrophil chemotaxis; T. denticola dentilisin disrupts epithelial tight junctions; succinate → SUCNR1 → macrophage IL-1β → bone resorption cascade.

2. **Three-stage caries dysbiosis model** (Takahashi & Nyvad) — Health (non-mutans streptococci + Actinomyces) → acidogenic (frequency of sugar exposure tips adaptation) → aciduric (mutans streptococci + lactobacilli dominant; pH chronically low).

3. **Probiotic clinical evidence summary** — S. salivarius M18 and K12 reduce gingival/plaque indices; probiotic mouthwash ≈ chlorhexidine for chronic gingivitis; probiotic + SRP adjunct → immunological modulation (β-defensins upregulation, cytokine regulation).

4. **Postbiotics definition and evidence** — ISAPP 2019 definition; heat-killed L. paracasei ET-22 reduces T. denticola, F. nucleatum, P. gingivalis and VSC generation as effectively as live bacteria.

5. **Predatory bacteria (BALOs) mechanism** — periplasmic invasion (B. bacteriovorus) vs epibiotic attachment (Micavibrio); self-limiting population dynamics; delivery candidates: gels, rinses, slow-release devices in gingival sulcus.

6. **Bacteriophage precision targeting** — SMHBZ8 prevents caries in murine model; PEf771 superior to 10 antibiotics in root canal infection model; HEf13 (burst size 352) effective in ex vivo dentin; ΦSG005 encodes AcrIIA5-like Cas9 inhibitor for anti-CRISPR defense.

7. **OMT concept and early evidence** — pediatric oncology pilot (Goloshchapov et al.): maternal saliva OMT reduced chemotherapy-induced mucositis severity with no adverse effects; halitosis rat model validated microbial transfer concept.

8. **Microbial metabolite landscape** — SCFAs (dual roles: mucosal protection vs cytotoxic at high concentrations), VSCs, polyamines (spermidine/putrescine, linked to tumorigenesis), indole compounds (AhR activation → mucosal immunity), nitrate–nitrite–NO pathway (oral bacteria → cardioprotective nitric oxide; disrupted by antimicrobial mouthwash).

## Methodology

- **Study type**: Narrative review; no systematic search protocol, no PRISMA.
- **Scope**: Oral microbiome ecology → dysbiosis pathogenesis → clinical evidence for novel therapies (emphasis on last 5 years of probiotic/postbiotic trials).
- **Evidence types covered**: In vitro, animal, and clinical (RCTs, case series, pilot studies); quality not formally appraised.
- **Special focus**: Bacteriophage catalogue (organism-level detail — lytic cycles, burst sizes, genomic safety assessment).

## Results

### Probiotic clinical outcomes (selected)
| Condition | Probiotic | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Gingivitis (children + elderly) | Probiotic mouthwash | Reduced S. mutans; superior to xylitol mouthwash in children |
| Chronic gingivitis | Probiotic mouthwash | ≈ chlorhexidine for plaque and bleeding scores |
| Gingivitis (young adults) | S. salivarius M18 / K12 | Reduced gingival and plaque indices |
| Periodontitis + SRP | Lactobacillus spp. | ↓IL-1β, IL-8; ↑IL-10, salivary IgA; ↓bleeding on probing |
| Caries (children with ECC) | L. salivarius SD1 | ↑ antimicrobial peptides; ↓ S. mutans |
| Halitosis | L. salivarius G60 ± inulin | Reduced halitosis and tongue coating |
| Peri-implant mucositis | Probiotics | Reduced bleeding scores |

### Bacteriophage evidence
| Phage | Target | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| SMHBZ8 | S. mutans | Prevents caries in murine model; no macrophage toxicity |
| ɸAPCM01 | S. mutans | Burst size ~44; ↓ biofilm metabolic activity |
| PEf771 | E. faecalis | Superior to 10 antibiotics in root canal and rat model |
| HEf13 | E. faecalis | Burst size 352; effective in ex vivo dentin |
| FNU1 | F. nucleatum | Lyses cells + disrupts biofilms |
| ΦSG005 | S. gordonii | Encodes AcrIIA5-like anti-CRISPR protein |

### Limitations
- BALOs cannot target Gram-positive bacteria (e.g., S. mutans in caries).
- Phage host range narrow; phage resistance can evolve.
- OMT: no human periodontal RCT; donor screening and standardization unresolved.
- All novel therapies: inter-individual microbiome variability complicates outcomes; long-term safety data lacking.
