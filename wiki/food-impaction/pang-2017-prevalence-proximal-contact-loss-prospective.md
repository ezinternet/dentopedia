## Summary
This 7-year prospective study followed 150 patients restored with 234 posterior implant-fixed prostheses (IFPs) supported by 384 implants, recruited 2009–2014 and followed to mid-2016. Proximal contact tightness was recorded with calibrated aluminum strips, with proximal contact loss (PCL) defined as a tightness gap exceeding 50 μm. Among 299 proximal contacts, 179 (59.9%) developed PCL. Kaplan–Meier/log-rank analysis identified adjacent-tooth bone level, adjacent-tooth root configuration, proximal-contact position, and jaw position as significant; Cox proportional-hazards regression retained proximal-contact position, adjacent-tooth bone level, and jaw position. The authors conclude that lower alveolar bone support of adjacent teeth, maxillary IFP location, and the mesial site are associated with higher PCL incidence, framing PCL as a multifactorial prosthetic complication.

## Key Contributions
- Large 7-year prospective dataset with a high observed PCL rate (59.9%).
- Time-dependent cumulative PCL modeled by Kaplan–Meier and Cox regression.
- Introduces adjacent-tooth bone support and root configuration as quantified risk factors.

## Methodology
Prospective cohort. 150 patients recruited Jan 2009–Dec 2014, followed to June 2016. 234 posterior IFPs on 384 implants; 299 proximal contacts. Contact tightness measured with aluminum strips at regular intervals; PCL defined as tightness >50 μm. Analysis by log-rank (Kaplan–Meier) and Cox proportional-hazards regression.

## Results
- PCL in 179/299 contacts (59.9%).
- KM/log-rank significant (P<0.05): adjacent-tooth bone level, adjacent-tooth root configuration, proximal-contact position, jaw position.
- Cox regression significant: proximal-contact position (mesial), adjacent-tooth bone level, jaw position.
- Higher PCL associated with lower adjacent-tooth bone support, maxillary IFPs, and mesial site.
