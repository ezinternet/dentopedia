## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 6 observational studies in adolescents and young adults (age 10–26) finding self-reported halitosis prevalence of 23.1–77.5% (mean 44.7%) with consistent associations to anxiety, depression, social isolation, and reduced self-esteem measured by OHIP-14 and SCL-9-R.

## Summary

This systematic review (PRISMA) is the first to focus specifically on the psychosocial and emotional burden of halitosis in adolescents and young adults. Searching four databases (Scopus, Scholar, Web of Science, ProQuest) through November 2022, the authors retrieved 593 records and ultimately included 6 observational studies after NHLBI quality assessment. All included studies were cross-sectional or cohort designs from Brazil and India primarily, covering participants aged 10–26 years.

Self-reported halitosis prevalence varied widely from 23.1% to 77.5% (mean 44.7%), reflecting methodological heterogeneity in measurement (organoleptic tests vs. self-report questionnaires). Despite this heterogeneity, all studies converged on a consistent psychosocial profile: young people with halitosis experienced elevated anxiety and depressive symptoms (documented via SCL-9-R), worse oral health-related quality of life (OHIP-14), social isolation, reduced self-esteem, and stigma-related difficulties. The psychological burden was present even in pseudo-halitosis and halitophobia cases where no objective malodor was measurable.

The authors conclude that halitosis management in young populations requires a dual-track approach addressing both the oral health etiology and the psychological sequelae, rather than focusing solely on malodor elimination.

## Key Contributions

- First SR specifically on psychosocial impact of halitosis in adolescents/young adults (gap in literature)
- Documents wide prevalence heterogeneity (23.1–77.5%) and its measurement-methodology drivers
- Shows psychological burden extends to pseudo-halitosis and halitophobia — objective malodor not required for emotional harm
- Highlights OHIP-14 and SCL-9-R as most commonly used validated instruments in this context
- Advocates integrated medical + psychological management paradigm for halitosis in youth

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (PRISMA); no meta-analysis (heterogeneity precluded pooling)
- **Databases**: Scopus, Google Scholar, Web of Science, ProQuest Central (up to November 2022)
- **Included studies**: n=6, all observational (cross-sectional/cohort), ages 10–26, English language
- **Exclusion**: Reviews, case reports, age >26, no social/emotional outcome measured
- **Quality tool**: NHLBI Observational Cohort/Cross-Sectional Quality Assessment (14-item)
- **Outcome measures**: Self-reported halitosis prevalence; OHIP-14 (OHRQoL); SCL-9-R (anxiety/depression); social isolation measures; self-esteem scales

## Results

| Outcome | Finding |
|---|---|
| Self-reported halitosis prevalence | 23.1%–77.5% (mean 44.7%) across 6 studies |
| Anxiety & depression | Elevated in halitosis group (SCL-9-R, non-standardized Qs) |
| OHRQoL (OHIP-14) | Lower scores in adolescents/young adults with halitosis |
| Social behavior | Social isolation, avoidance of close-contact interactions |
| Self-esteem | Reduced; negative body image |
| Stigma | Social discrimination and stigma reported |
| Pseudo-halitosis/halitophobia | Psychological burden present even without objective malodor |

Halitosis etiology in included populations: incorrect tongue brushing (primary), reduced salivary flow, periodontal disease, caries, orthodontic brackets, debris accumulation.
