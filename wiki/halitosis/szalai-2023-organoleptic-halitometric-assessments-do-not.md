## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 76 studies (n=14,635) shows Halimeter, OralChroma, and bench GC correlate only moderately with the gold-standard organoleptic test (r=0.65–0.76), and no device can currently replace sensory halitosis assessment.

## Summary

This SR+MA (PROSPERO CRD42022320024) evaluated whether common device-supported halitometers can replace the subjective organoleptic test (OLT) for diagnosing intra-oral halitosis. Seventy-six studies involving 14,635 adult patients were included. Pooled Spearman correlation coefficients were: Halimeter 0.65 (I²=95%), OralChroma 0.69 (I²=12%), and bench gas chromatography (GC) 0.76 (I²=0%). Despite all correlations being statistically significant, none exceeded the threshold for strong clinical agreement. SROC analysis confirmed no device was significantly superior to another. The authors conclude that better halitometers must be developed as alternatives to OLT, particularly given safety concerns during infectious disease outbreaks (e.g., COVID-19) that make close-range sensory examinations hazardous.

## Key Contributions

1. First meta-analysis to pool Spearman correlations across all major halitometer device classes versus OLT in intra-oral halitosis.
2. Demonstrates Halimeter (r=0.65) and OralChroma (r=0.69) do **not** correlate well with OLT — below clinically acceptable thresholds.
3. Bench GC shows the highest correlation (r=0.76) but is impractical for routine clinical use.
4. High heterogeneity for Halimeter (I²=95%) vs. near-zero for GC (I²=0%) reveals Halimeter's consistency problem across clinical contexts.
5. SROC analysis confirms no device achieves clearly superior diagnostic AUC.
6. Highlights urgent need for validated contactless halitosis measurement tools, especially post-COVID-19.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review + random-effects meta-analysis
- **Databases**: MEDLINE, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, CENTRAL (searched March 23, 2022)
- **Included**: Adult clinical studies reporting Spearman r between VSC device and OLT; intra-oral halitosis only; systemic disease patients excluded
- **Index tests**: Electrochemical sensors (Halimeter), portable GC (OralChroma), bench GC, eNose
- **Reference standard**: Organoleptic test (OLT), scored 0–5
- **Primary outcome**: Spearman correlation coefficient (device vs. OLT)
- **Secondary outcomes**: Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV; SROC curves
- **Risk of bias**: QUADAS-2 and QUADAS-C
- **Inter-rater agreement**: Cohen's Kappa
- **Reporting**: PRISMA 2020

## Results

| Device | Pooled Spearman r | 95% CI | I² |
|---|---|---|---|
| Halimeter (electrochemical) | 0.65 | [0.53–0.74] | 95% |
| OralChroma (portable GC) | 0.69 | [0.63–0.74] | 12% |
| Gas chromatograph (bench GC) | 0.76 | [0.67–0.83] | 0% |

- All r values statistically significant (p<.01) but none indicate strong clinical substitutability for OLT
- Halimeter: high heterogeneity limits clinical generalizability; OralChroma: most consistent evidence
- GC: best correlation and consistency but expensive and impractical for routine clinics
- SROC: no device shows significantly superior AUC; no single halitometer recommended over others
- **Clinical bottom line**: Current halitometers are insufficient replacements for OLT; new devices or hybrid approaches needed
