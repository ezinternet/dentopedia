## Summary

This systematic review (PRISMA; PROSPERO CRD42019129337) is the first to comprehensively catalog **medications that cause extra-oral halitosis** — halitosis arising from systemic drug effects rather than intraoral factors. Searching 5 databases through February 2019, the authors screened 2,943 records and included 34 clinical trials/cohorts covering 30,736 patients, in which 1,932 cases of drug-related halitosis (6.2%) were recorded. Risk of bias was low in 18 studies, unclear in 10, and high in 6; inter-rater agreement was excellent (Cohen's kappa = 0.94).

The 34 included studies span 10 pharmacological groups. The most striking finding is that **cysteamine** (aminothiol group, used for cystinosis/Huntington's disease) caused halitosis in up to 100% of patients at doses ≥15 mg/kg. Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, glycopyrrolate) caused halitosis in 4.5–15.5% of patients, likely via xerostomia. Organosulfur substances act through direct production of volatile sulfur compounds (VSCs). The mechanism for most other groups is less characterized but likely involves xerostomia, altered microbiome, or metabolite production.

Routes of administration varied widely — including oral, IV, intranasal, topical, subcutaneous, and eye drops — confirming that even non-oral routes can produce systemic halitosis via bloodborne delivery of volatile compounds to the lungs.

## Key Contributions

1. **First systematic review** focused exclusively on drug-induced extra-oral halitosis — filling a gap noted in prior literature (previous reviews identified only ~9 compounds).
2. **10-group drug taxonomy**: acid reducers, aminothiols, anticholinergics, antidepressants, antifungals, antihistamines/steroids, antispasmodics, chemotherapeutic agents, dietary supplements, organosulfur substances.
3. **Quantitative incidence data** for key groups: cysteamine up to 100%; oxybutynin 15.5%; glycopyrrolate 4.5%; ranitidine 0.9%.
4. **Clinical decision support**: knowledge of these medications enables clinicians to screen drug history when evaluating halitosis patients, and informs prescribing decisions.

## Methodology

- **Design:** Systematic review, PRISMA-compliant
- **Databases:** PubMed, Scopus, ScienceDirect, Ovid, Cochrane Library (through February 2019)
- **Study types included:** Clinical trials, cohort studies
- **Key inclusion requirement:** Halitosis arising from systemic drug effect (not local oral effect); reported as individual side effect with numeric data; patients halitosis-free at baseline
- **Key exclusion:** Cross-sectional designs; combined genuine/pseudohalitosis records; missing dosage data
- **Records:** 2,943 after deduplication → 208 full-text → 34 included
- **RoB:** Cochrane tool; 18 low / 10 unclear / 6 high; kappa = 0.94
- **Halitosis assessment:** 11 studies objective (organoleptic/molecular exhaled air analysis), 21 subjective (patient complaint), 1 combined

## Results

**Overall:** 1,932/30,736 patients (6.2%) experienced drug-related halitosis across 34 studies.

| Drug Group | Representative Drugs | Halitosis Incidence |
|---|---|---|
| Aminothiols | Cysteamine (≥15 mg/kg or 0.9–1.2 g/m²) | Up to 100% |
| Anticholinergics | Oxybutynin 5–15 mg/day | 15.5% |
| Anticholinergics | Glycopyrrolate 40–175 μg/day | 4.5% |
| Acid reducers | Ranitidine 150 mg/day | 0.9% (1/110) |
| Organosulfur substances | Garlic/DMSO derivatives | Variable; direct VSC production |
| Antidepressants | Multiple | Reported; xerostomia mechanism |
| Chemotherapeutic agents | Multiple oncology drugs | Reported; mucosal/microbiome disruption |
| Antifungals, antihistamines/steroids, antispasmodics, dietary supplements | Various | Reported |

**Mechanisms:**
- **Xerostomia pathway:** Anticholinergics, antidepressants, antihistamines reduce salivary flow → decreased VSC clearance and increased anaerobic bacterial activity
- **Direct volatile metabolite production:** Aminothiols (cysteamine → sulfur metabolites), organosulfur compounds (allyl mercaptan from garlic)
- **Mucosal disruption:** Chemotherapeutic agents alter oral/GI mucosa and microbiome
- **Bloodborne/pulmonary route:** IV and non-oral routes still produce halitosis via pulmonary excretion of volatile drug metabolites
