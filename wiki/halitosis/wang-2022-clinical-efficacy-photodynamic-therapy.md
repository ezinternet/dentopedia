## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 8 RCTs (n=345): PDT and PDT+tongue scraper significantly reduce H2S immediately post-treatment vs. tongue scraper alone, but this advantage disappears at all follow-up time points (7–90 days).

## Summary

Wang et al. (2022) conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis to evaluate the clinical efficacy of photodynamic therapy (PDT) for halitosis. Eight RCTs involving 345 patients were identified from five databases (PubMed, Cochrane Library, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus; inception to August 2022). The primary comparator was tongue scraper (TS). Outcomes measured volatile sulfur compound (VSC) concentrations — H2S, CH3SCH3, and CH3SH — at immediate, 7-, 14-, 30-, and 90-day time points.

The meta-analysis demonstrated that PDT (MD = −34.49, 95% CI [−66.34, −2.64], p=0.03) and PDT+TS (MD = −67.72, 95% CI [−101.17, −34.28], p<0.001) were significantly more effective than TS alone in reducing H2S immediately after treatment. However, no significant differences were found between any groups at subsequent follow-up time points. PDT showed no significant advantage over TS for CH3SCH3 or CH3SH at any time point.

The authors conclude that PDT is a beneficial and promising short-term treatment for halitosis, with an acute bactericidal effect mediated by reactive oxygen species (ROS) targeting VSC-producing bacteria on the tongue dorsum. The absence of sustained benefit suggests recolonization occurs, and PDT may be best used as an adjunct to ongoing mechanical hygiene rather than as a standalone therapy.

## Key Contributions

1. Provides meta-analytic evidence that PDT produces a statistically significant immediate reduction in H2S versus tongue scraper alone
2. Shows PDT+TS combination achieves approximately double the immediate H2S reduction of PDT alone
3. Establishes that PDT's benefit is acute — not sustained beyond immediate post-treatment measurement
4. Finds no PDT benefit for DMS (CH3SCH3) or methanethiol (CH3SH), suggesting H2S-specific photochemical sensitivity
5. Synthesizes the most rigorous (RCT-only) evidence available through 2022 for PDT in halitosis management

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review + meta-analysis
- **Databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus (inception–August 10, 2022)
- **Included studies**: 8 RCTs, n=345
- **Intervention**: PDT (photosensitizer + light activation, typically methylene blue + LED) ± tongue scraper
- **Comparator**: Tongue scraper alone
- **Primary outcome**: VSC concentration (H2S, CH3SCH3, CH3SH) measured by gas chromatography or Halimeter
- **Time points**: Immediate post-treatment, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days, 90 days
- **Effect measure**: Mean difference (MD) with 95% CI
- **PDT parameters**: Varied across trials — methylene blue photosensitizer most common; LED (red spectrum) light source; tongue dorsum application

## Results

### H2S — Immediate post-treatment
| Comparison | MD | 95% CI | P |
|---|---|---|---|
| PDT vs. TS | −34.49 | [−66.34, −2.64] | 0.03 |
| PDT+TS vs. TS | −67.72 | [−101.17, −34.28] | <0.001 |

### H2S — Follow-up (7, 14, 30, 90 days)
- No significant differences among TS, PDT, and PDT+TS at any follow-up interval
- Immediate benefit does not persist; likely due to bacterial recolonization of tongue dorsum

### CH3SCH3 and CH3SH
- No significant differences between PDT and TS at any time point
- PDT's photochemical activity may preferentially target H2S-producing species

### Clinical takeaway
- PDT is effective for acute halitosis reduction (patient-facing appointments, short-term esthetic/social events)
- Not superior to mechanical tongue cleaning for long-term halitosis control
- PDT+TS synergism suggests combination protocols are preferable when PDT is used
