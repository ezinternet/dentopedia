## One-line Summary

Systematic review (Oral Diseases 2023, 7 pp) finds bidirectional links between halitosis and chemosensory disorders: halitosis patients exhibit impaired olfactory/gustatory function, while anosmia/hyposmia impairs self-perception of oral malodor.

## Summary

This short-communication systematic review from Aarhus University (Schertel Cassiano et al., published online March 2021, print Oral Diseases March 2023, Vol 29 Issue 2, pp 369–375) is the first SR to specifically examine the association between halitosis and chemosensory disorders — encompassing both olfactory (smell) and gustatory (taste) function.

The review, conducted using JBI (Joanna Briggs Institute) methodology for etiology/risk systematic reviews, identifies evidence that:

1. **Halitosis patients show impaired chemosensory function**: chronic halitosis is associated with reduced olfactory sensitivity (Altundag et al., 2015), and altered tongue-film biochemistry — driven by the same anaerobic bacteria responsible for VSC production — disrupts taste receptor homeostasis.

2. **Chemosensory impairment affects halitosis self-perception**: patients with anosmia, hyposmia, or olfactory desensitization (prolonged adaptation to their own odor) may fail to perceive their own malodor, introducing systematic self-reporting bias in prevalence studies.

3. **Shared etiological pathways**: volatile sulfur compounds (H₂S, CH₃SH), microbial metabolites (indole, skatole), and salivary hypofunction (xerostomia) represent converging mechanisms linking oral malodor production with chemosensory dysfunction. Non-oral systemic causes of halitosis (e.g., metabolic, respiratory) additionally affect chemosensation via central or peripheral pathways.

4. **Bidirectional clinical implication**: clinicians assessing halitosis should screen for co-existing smell/taste complaints; conversely, patients presenting with chemosensory disturbances should be assessed for halitosis.

The review is presented in short-communication format (7 pages, ~14 references), limiting full GRADE or meta-analytic synthesis.

## Key Contributions

- **First systematic review** specifically on the halitosis–chemosensory disorder association
- Establishes mechanistic framework: VSCs and oral microbial metabolites → olfactory receptor disruption / taste bud microenvironment alteration
- Identifies self-perception bias as a confounder in halitosis prevalence and patient-reported outcome studies
- Connects xerostomia (dry mouth, reduced saliva volume) as a shared intermediary: reduced salivary flow impairs both taste stimulus transport and creates halitosis-permissive anaerobic environment
- Calls for standardized, simultaneous measurement of VSC levels and validated chemosensory tests in future research

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (short communication), JBI methodology for etiology/risk reviews
- **Databases**: Not explicitly listed (consistent with PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science for this Aarhus research group)
- **Population**: Adults with halitosis (objective or self-reported) and/or chemosensory disorders
- **Outcomes**: Olfactory function, gustatory function, self-perceived halitosis, clinical measurements of oral malodor (VSC, organoleptic)
- **Synthesis**: Narrative — meta-analysis not performed due to study heterogeneity
- **Quality assessment**: JBI critical appraisal tools

## Results

| Finding | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Olfactory impairment in halitosis | Altundag et al. (2015): chronic halitosis patients had reduced olfactory scores vs. controls |
| Taste disruption mechanism | Tongue biofilm biochemistry (anaerobic metabolites) alters taste bud homeostasis (Feng et al., 2014, 2018) |
| Salivary pathway | Xerostomia reduces chemosensory stimulus delivery; halitosis-associated dry mouth compounds taste loss |
| Self-perception failure | Olfactory desensitization (Stuck et al., 2014) and anosmia → underestimation of own malodor |
| Non-oral etiologies | Systemic/metabolic causes of halitosis (Preti et al., 1992) independently alter chemosensation |
| NHANES data | 13–23% of US adults report smell or taste alterations (Rawal et al., 2016) — high population burden relevant to halitosis assessment |
