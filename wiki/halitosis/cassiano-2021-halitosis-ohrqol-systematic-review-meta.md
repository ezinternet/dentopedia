## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis investigated whether halitosis is associated with impaired oral-health-related quality of life (OHRQoL). Electronic searches through June 2021 identified 13 observational studies for qualitative synthesis, of which 10 cross-sectional studies (n=2,692) were pooled in meta-analysis. The pooled standardized mean difference (SMD) was 0.51 (95% CI 0.27–0.75), indicating a moderate and statistically significant association between halitosis and worse OHRQoL. Subgroup analyses showed the association was significant only in adults, not in children or adolescents. Neither OHRQoL instrument type, halitosis measurement method, nor cultural background significantly influenced the pooled estimate. Meta-regression revealed that methodological quality (risk of bias) explained approximately 20% of between-study heterogeneity, with higher-risk-of-bias studies overestimating the effect. The authors conclude that halitosis is associated with impaired OHRQoL, particularly in adult populations, though causality cannot be determined from cross-sectional evidence.

## Key Contributions

1. **First pooled effect size** for halitosis–OHRQoL association: SMD 0.51 (moderate effect, Cohen's conventions)
2. **Age-stratified finding**: association is significant in adults, not demonstrated in children/adolescents
3. **Method-agnostic result**: effect holds regardless of OHRQoL instrument (OHIP-14, HALT, OMQ-QoL, GOHAI) or halitosis assessment method (organoleptic, VSC, self-report)
4. **Cross-cultural consistency**: no significant cultural background effect on pooled SMD
5. **Risk of bias moderates heterogeneity**: ~20% of between-study variance attributable to methodological quality

|---|---|---|---|

| By cultural background | No difference | — | No |

**Heterogeneity**: Substantial I² observed across studies; methodological quality (risk of bias) explained ~20% of variance. Studies with high risk of bias overestimated the magnitude of association.

**Practical interpretation**: A moderate effect size (SMD ~0.5) in a cross-sectional SR+MA means halitosis-affected individuals score meaningfully worse on OHRQoL instruments, with real functional and psychosocial implications (difficulty eating, social embarrassment, avoidance behaviors, reduced self-confidence).

**Limitation**: All 10 meta-analyzed studies were cross-sectional — no causal direction can be inferred. Whether halitosis causes worse OHRQoL, or whether patients with lower OHRQoL perceive/report more halitosis, cannot be determined from this evidence.
