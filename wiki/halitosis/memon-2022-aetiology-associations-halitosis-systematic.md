## One-line Summary

Systematic review (19 studies, 2014–2020): 80–90% of halitosis is intra-oral (coated tongue, periodontal disease, poor hygiene); 10–20% is extra-oral (ENT, GI, systemic disease).

## 한줄요약

## Summary

This systematic review synthesizes empirical evidence (2014–2020) on the causes and associations of halitosis across 19 studies: 12 cross-sectional, 2 randomized controlled trials, and 5 case-control studies. The central finding confirms the established 80–90% intra-oral origin of halitosis, with coated tongue identified as the single most consistently reported factor across all study designs, followed by periodontal disease and poor oral hygiene. Extra-oral causes — accounting for 10–20% — span ENT conditions (adenoid hypertrophy, allergic rhinitis), gastrointestinal (*H. pylori* infection), systemic infections (viral hepatitis B), neurological disorders (Parkinson's disease), and iatrogenic causes (fixed orthodontic appliances). VSC chemistry distinguishes the sources: methyl mercaptan and hydrogen sulphide predominate in intra-oral halitosis, while dimethyl sulphide is a marker of extra-oral origin.

## Key Contributions

1. **Structured aetiological taxonomy**: physiologic vs. pathologic (intra-oral vs. extra-oral) with contemporary 2014–2020 evidence.
2. **Tongue coating confirmed as primary factor**: consistently identified regardless of study design.
3. **VSC chemistry as source discriminator**: dimethyl sulphide elevation signals extra-oral origin; H₂S + CH₃SH signal intra-oral.
4. **Extra-oral map**: ENT, GI/hepatic, neurological, and iatrogenic causes compiled across study designs.
5. **Detection method summary**: organoleptic (gold standard, 0–5 scale), Halimeter®, and gas chromatography.

## Methodology

- **Databases**: MEDLINE (PubMed), Google Scholar, Wiley Online Library
- **Search terms**: 'halitosis', 'bad-breath', 'oral malodour causes and aetiology'
- **Inclusion window**: January 2014 – December 2020; English-language; empirical study designs (cross-sectional, RCT, case-control) reporting aetiologic factors
- **Screening flow**: 1490 initial → 474 (date filter + deduplication) → 44 (abstract screen) → 19 (final)
- **Study types included**: 12 cross-sectional, 2 RCTs, 5 case-control
- **No meta-analysis**: heterogeneity in study design and outcome measures precluded quantitative synthesis
- **Limitation**: no formal risk-of-bias tool applied

## Results

### Intra-oral aetiological factors (80–90% of halitosis cases)

| Factor | Reported in |
|---|---|
| Coated tongue | All 3 study design categories |
| Periodontal disease | Cross-sectional + case-control; *P. gingivalis*, *T. forsythia*, *T. denticola*, *Prevotella* spp. |
| Poor oral hygiene | Cross-sectional studies |
| Higher saliva viscosity | Cross-sectional (Komori et al. 2016) |

### Extra-oral aetiological factors (10–20% of halitosis cases)

| Category | Specific condition |
|---|---|
| ENT | Adenoid hypertrophy, allergic rhinitis |
| Gastrointestinal | *Helicobacter pylori* infection |
| Hepatic/infectious | Viral hepatitis B |
| Neurological | Parkinson's disease |
| Iatrogenic | Fixed orthodontic appliances |
| Psychiatric | Depression (reduces oral hygiene motivation) |

### VSC chemistry by source
- **Methyl mercaptan (CH₃SH) + hydrogen sulphide (H₂S)** → intra-oral halitosis
- **Dimethyl sulphide ((CH₃)₂S)** → extra-oral halitosis (elevated in systemic conditions)

### Detection methods
- **Organoleptic scoring** (0–5 scale): gold standard — trained examiner direct assessment
- **Halimeter®**: portable VSC measurement (total VSC concentration)
- **Gas chromatography (GC)**: breath, saliva, tongue debris — most precise, specifies individual VSC compounds
