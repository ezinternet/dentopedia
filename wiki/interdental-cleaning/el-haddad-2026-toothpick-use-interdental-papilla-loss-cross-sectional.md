## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional study (n=69, ages 20–29; 87% toothpick users) — habitual wooden-toothpick interdental use is associated with significantly reduced interdental papilla presence (PPI, P<.05) in maxillary anterior teeth ("black triangles"), worse with the vertical up/down technique, frequency >3×/day, and >3 years' duration; alveolar bone level and proximal contact length were unaffected (abstract-only).

## Summary

Wooden toothpicks are one of the most widely used interdental-cleaning tools, but their effect on the **interdental papilla** in the esthetic anterior zone is rarely quantified. This cross-sectional study (n=69, ages 20–29) deliberately enrolled only manual toothbrushers who used **toothpicks exclusively** for interdental cleaning (no floss or interdental brushes), isolating the toothpick's effect. Using the **Papilla Presence Index (PPI)** plus clinical measurement of papilla height, proximal contact length, and alveolar bone level, it compared toothpick users with nonusers.

**87% of participants used toothpicks**, and users had **significantly less papilla presence** than nonusers (P<.05). The damage was technique- and dose-dependent: the **vertical "up/down" insertion** technique caused greater papilla loss than the horizontal "in/out" method, and loss was more severe with **frequency >3×/day** and **duration >3 years**. Importantly, **alveolar bone level and proximal contact length did not differ** — the injury is confined to the soft-tissue papilla, producing the esthetic "black triangle." As a cross-sectional study with a modest sample, it shows association, not causation, but supports counselling patients toward gentler interdental-cleaning alternatives.

> **Naming caution.** This paper is about the **wooden toothpick used as an interdental cleaner** — a *harm*. It is unrelated to the **Watanabe "toothpick method" of toothbrushing** (a beneficial toothbrush technique), even though both use the word "toothpick." See Related Papers.

## Key Contributions

- Documents a real harm of a near-universal habit (87% prevalence) — **toothpick interdental use ↔ interdental papilla loss / black triangles** in the maxillary anterior.
- Pinpoints **modifiable risk factors**: vertical technique, high frequency (>3×/day), long duration (>3 years).
- Demonstrates **soft-tissue–specific** injury (no bone-level or contact-length change), clarifying the mechanism is gingival/papillary trauma rather than hard-tissue change.
- Provides a clinical message: advise **safer interdental aids** and gentler technique to preserve anterior papilla esthetics.

## Methodology

- **Design**: cross-sectional observational (questionnaire + clinical examination).
- **Sample**: n=69, ages 20–29; all manual toothbrushers using **toothpicks as their sole interdental aid** (floss/interdental-brush users excluded) to isolate the toothpick effect.
- **Index**: Papilla Presence Index (PPI) for papilla integrity; clinical measures of papilla height, proximal contact length, alveolar bone level.
- **Exposure data**: structured questionnaire — frequency, duration, technique (vertical "up/down" vs horizontal "in/out").
- **Comparison**: toothpick users vs nonusers.

## Results

- **Prevalence**: 87% reported toothpick use.
- **Papilla presence**: significantly reduced in users vs nonusers (**P<.05**).
- **Technique**: vertical "up/down" → greater papilla loss than horizontal "in/out".
- **Dose-response**: frequency **>3×/day** and duration **>3 years** → significantly more severe papilla loss.
- **Hard tissue**: **no** significant difference in alveolar bone level or proximal contact length between groups.
- **Conclusion**: frequent/prolonged toothpick use (particularly vertical) is associated with significant interdental papilla loss ("black triangles"); clinicians should advise safer interdental-cleaning alternatives. Cross-sectional design + modest sample → causality cannot be established.
