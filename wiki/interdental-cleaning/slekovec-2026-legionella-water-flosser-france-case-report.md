## One-line Summary

Case report (n=1, France 2026): a water flosser filled with nonsterile home tap water harbored *Legionella pneumophila* serogroup 1 (Lp1) biofilm (300 CFU/L) and was the whole-genome-identical (sequence type 42) source of fatal-risk nosocomial Legionnaires' disease in a 48-yo myeloma/auto-HSCT patient — first literature case linking an oral irrigator to *Legionella* infection, a low-incidence but serious infection-control signal for immunocompromised users.

## Summary

This is the first reported case of Legionnaires' disease transmitted by a water flosser (oral irrigator). A 48-year-old man with IgG-Kappa myeloma, admitted for autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (auto-HSCT), developed nosocomial Legionnaires' disease. Standard hospital water sources were exhaustively negative, but his personal water flosser — brought from home, used with tap water, and not captured in the admission inventory — yielded *Legionella pneumophila* serogroup 1 (Lp1). Whole-genome sequencing showed the patient's clinical isolate and the device isolate were **identical (sequence type 42)**, establishing the device as the source. The patient survived a 62-day hospitalization (47 days in the medical ICU). The clinical takeaway is a safety/infection-control one: water flossers filled with nonsterile water can accumulate *Legionella* biofilm in the reservoir/tubing and aerosolize it through the pressurized oral jet, posing a serious — though low-incidence — risk to immunocompromised patients. Manufacturers generally do not require sterile water but recommend **regular cleaning and disinfection** of the reservoir; in hospital and immunocompromised settings, patient-owned aerosol-generating devices warrant caution and inventory capture.

## Key Contributions

- **Safety signal, not efficacy**: this paper adds the adverse-event counterweight to the interdental-cleaning device literature, which is otherwise dominated by plaque/gingivitis efficacy RCTs.
- **Genomically confirmed source attribution**: identical whole genomes (ST42) for patient and water-flosser Lp1 isolates, with all hospital water sources negative — a clean molecular link.
- **Mechanism**: nonsterile tap water + no reservoir cleaning → biofilm; pressurized jet → inhalable aerosol → infection in immunocompromised host.
- **Practical takeaway**: regular cleaning/disinfection of the water flosser reservoir; in hospitals, capture patient-owned devices at admission and control their use; healthcare workers should be aware of devices brought in from outside.
- **Framing**: explicitly low-incidence (no prior literature cases) but important given the growing water-flosser market and growing immunocompromised population.

## Methodology

- **Single case report** with environmental source investigation and molecular linkage.
- **Clinical confirmation**: *Legionella*-positive blood culture (day 10), positive BinaxNOW Lp1 urinary antigen, PCR-positive bronchoalveolar lavage, culture-positive endotracheal aspirate.
- **Environmental sampling** (NF T 90-431): sink/shower with and without anti-*Legionella* filter, toilet bowl, and the water flosser (tank filled with sterile water, device run, jet water cultured on GVPC, MALDI-TOF identification).
- **Whole-genome sequencing**: both Lp1 isolates (patient + device) via Oxford Nanopore; Flye assembly; sequence type via legsta — used to link patient and device isolates.

## Results

- **Patient**: 48-yo man, IgG-Kappa myeloma, peripheral auto-HSCT (August 2024, Besançon University Hospital). Fever day 10 with *Legionella* bacteremia; respiratory deterioration day 16–17; MICU transfer.
- **Hospital sources all negative**: filtered and unfiltered sink/shower water, toilet bowl, and all 10 routine 2024 ward-network *Legionella* tests were negative.
- **Water flosser positive**: Lp1 *and* *Legionella* serogroup 2–14 recovered at **300 CFU/L** from the device jet; reservoir showed fouling (nonsterile water, no cleaning).
- **Molecular link**: patient and device Lp1 isolates had **identical genomes, sequence type 42** (known but non-major clone) → common origin, most likely the patient's home water network.
- **Course**: ARDS, mechanical ventilation, dialysis, cardiorespiratory arrest (no sequelae), multi-organ failure with acute renal failure, extremity necrosis, sacral pressure ulcer, malnutrition, amyotrophy; **62-day hospitalization including 47 days in MICU**; survived. No further hospital cases afterward.
- **Context**: hospital-acquired Legionnaires' disease carries ~3× the mortality of community-acquired (cited 8.6% vs 28.6%), concentrating the risk in immunocompromised inpatients.
