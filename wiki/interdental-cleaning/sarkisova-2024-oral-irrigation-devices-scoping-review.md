## One-line Summary

Scoping review (JBI / PRISMA-ScR; 275 sources, 1962–2023, 185 clinical trials + 15 SRs) mapping the whole oral-irrigation-device evidence base: oral irrigators consistently reduce periodontal/peri-implant inflammation (likely via microbiota modulation), are safe and well-accepted, but plaque-removal evidence is mixed and caries/PROM/pediatric/cost/environmental data are sparse.

## Summary

This is the first broad scoping review of oral irrigation devices (OIs), charting 275 sources accumulated since OIs entered the market in 1962. Rather than answering a single efficacy question, it maps the *breadth* of the evidence and names the gaps. The headline finding: OIs reliably reduce periodontal and peri-implant inflammation across populations and irrigant solutions, but their effect on plaque removal is inconsistent. The authors propose the anti-inflammatory benefit comes not from fully removing biofilm but from modulating the oral microbiota away from dysbiosis (fewer late-colonizer/anaerobic pathogens, more early colonizers/aerobes). OIs are generally safe and well-accepted within manufacturer pressure limits, with a transient-bacteremia caution for infective-endocarditis-risk patients. The review situates the category's narrower trials (water-flosser-vs-floss, vs interdental brush, orthodontic use) inside one map and flags where evidence is thin: caries prevention, validated PROMs, pediatric/special-needs populations, peri-implantitis, cost, and environmental impact.

## Key Contributions

- **First broad map of the OI literature** (275 sources, 1962–2023), as opposed to prior narrow or multi-aid reviews.
- **Inflammation yes, plaque unclear** — a clean separation of the two outcome domains, with the inflammation benefit robust and the plaque benefit mixed.
- **Mechanistic hypothesis**: microbiota modulation, not biofilm elimination — supported by in vitro biofilm-disruption-without-removal and clinical microbiome-shift data.
- **Quantified safety envelope**: 70–90 psi safe for non-ulcerated attached gingiva, 50–70 psi for ulcerated/non-keratinized tissue; bacteremia caution.
- **Explicit gap list** to steer future trials (caries, PROMs, pediatrics/special needs, peri-implantitis, cost/environment, irrigator-vs-air-flosser).

## Methodology

- **Framework**: Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) scoping-review methodology; reported per PRISMA-ScR. JBI population–concept–context: concept = "oral irrigation devices"; population/context deliberately unrestricted. By design, **no risk-of-bias / quality assessment** — the goal is mapping breadth, so findings must be read cautiously.
- **Databases (4)**: CINAHL, Dentistry & Oral Sciences Source, MEDLINE, Scopus. **Gray literature (8)**: Cochrane Library, Cochrane Oral Health Trials Register, Google, Google Scholar, ISRCTN, OpenGray, ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global, WHO ICTRP; plus Scopus citation searching.
- **Dates**: main search 12 Jan 2022 (no date limiter); follow-up 23–24 Dec 2023. English-language only.
- **Screening (PRISMA flow)**: 1769 records → title/abstract (≥75% reviewer-agreement pilot) → 418 full-text → 174 excluded → 244 database + 31 gray/citation = **275 included**.
- **Key scope decision**: **air flossers excluded** (pressurized air + water droplets — technologically distinct from pressurized-water OIs).
- **What was mapped**: device types/brands, pressure/safety, bacteremia, effects on dental materials, subgingival penetration depth, periodontal/peri-implant inflammation, biofilm/plaque, calculus, staining, oral microbiome, inflammation markers, GCF, endodontic use, patient acceptance, and non-oral-hygiene applications — grouped by methodology then by theme, irrigant, and population.

## Results

**Corpus (n=275)**: 185 clinical trials, 30 in vitro, 15 systematic reviews, 13 animal, 12 observational, 9 descriptive, 11 non-hygiene applications. Geography skewed to North America (148), then Western Europe (49) and Asia (47). Mostly adults — only 6 child and 26 adolescent studies (15 adolescent trials in fixed orthodontics). 69 sources declared industry funding/affiliation.

**Device types / mechanisms**: pulsating devices more effective than non-pulsating (the decompression phase drives efficacy); faucet-attached units can exert higher pressure but are harder to attach (some users quit); **Waterpik is the most-studied brand** (~139–156 sources). Subgingival tips deliver to 64–100% of pocket depth vs 44–71% for supragingival tips — OIs are the only home-care aid that can reach the base of deep pockets, a unique drug-delivery property for chlorhexidine/antimicrobials. Specialized devices produce ozonated or magnetized water.

**Clinical outcomes**:
- *Inflammation (138 sources)* — **consistent** reductions in bleeding, edema, erythema, probing depth, and clinical attachment loss, irrespective of solution, method, or population; less inflammation vs brushing alone or vs floss/air-flosser/interdental brush in 119 trials + 13 SRs.
- *Plaque/biofilm (164 sources)* — **mixed**: in vitro biofilm disruption is consistent, but clinical trials split (≈23 no reduction vs ≈31 improvement over brushing±floss); most SRs found no plaque benefit or could not pool for heterogeneity, only 2 SRs (Allen; AlMoharib) showed improvement. Consistent benefit in special-needs groups.
- *Microbiome (58 sources)* — shift away from dysbiosis (fewer anaerobes/spirochetes/motile rods, more health-associated early colonizers/aerobes), supporting the proposed mechanism.
- *Safety (19 sources)* — 70–90 psi safe (attached gingiva), 50–70 psi (ulcerated tissue); excessive pressure risks soft-tissue trauma and particulate penetration.
- *Bacteremia (10)* — risk present; caution in infective-endocarditis-risk patients.
- *Materials (6)* — generally safe; microhybrid composite damage at 100 psi, bulk-fill at 63 psi, poorly-margined inlays.
- *Staining (12)* — chlorhexidine raises staining; lower concentration + subgingival delivery mitigates while keeping efficacy.
- *Acceptance (21)* — generally high satisfaction/compliance, but validated PROM tools rarely used; some find it messy/time-consuming.

**Gaps the review identifies**: (1) caries prevention — *no* studies; possibly needs solutions like electrolyzed water that cut biofilm or raise plaque pH (water irrigation does not affect plaque pH); (2) validated PROMs; (3) pediatric and special-needs feasibility trials; (4) peri-implantitis — only 1 of 9 implant trials; (5) mechanism via modern molecular techniques (few periodontitis microbiome studies); (6) cost and environmental-impact comparisons; (7) irrigator-vs-air-flosser head-to-heads; (8) sufficiently powered, long-duration trials with standardized devices/indices.
