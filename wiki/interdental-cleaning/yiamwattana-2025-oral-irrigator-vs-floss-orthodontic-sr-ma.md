## One-line Summary

SR+MA (4 studies eligible, 3 meta-analysed) — adding an oral irrigator to toothbrushing did NOT outperform dental floss for plaque-index (SMD −0.88, 95% CI −2.25 to 0.50, p=0.10, I²=98%) or bleeding-index (SMD −0.90, 95% CI −2.78 to 0.98, p=0.35) reduction in fixed-orthodontic patients; very high heterogeneity, weak evidence.

## Summary

> [!note] Abstract-only ingest — full text not retrieved (no PMC open access). All numbers below are extracted verbatim from the PubMed abstract; risk-of-bias, study weights, and GRADE detail are not captured.

Patients with fixed orthodontic appliances are at high risk of plaque accumulation, and the best interdental adjunct to toothbrushing is debated. Oral irrigators (OI, water flossers) have been proposed as an easier alternative to dental floss (DF), but head-to-head studies have conflicted. This systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO **CRD42023395736**), searching multiple databases up to **May 7, 2024**, pooled standardised mean differences in plaque index (PI) and bleeding index (BI) using a random-effects model (DerSimonian–Laird). Of **975 records**, **4 articles** were eligible and **3 were meta-analysed**. Adding an OI to toothbrushing did **not** outperform DF for either PI or BI reduction, and heterogeneity was extreme. The authors note OI still lowered PI and BI from baseline — it is a useful hygiene aid, just not superior to floss.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA we hold that directly compares **oral irrigator vs dental floss** (both as adjuncts to toothbrushing) in **fixed-orthodontic** patients.
- Quantifies the comparison as SMD for PI and BI; both crossed the null.
- Clinical message: OI is **non-superior** to floss in orthodontic patients, but is itself an effective adjunct (reduces PI/BI from baseline) — so patient preference / compliance can drive the choice.

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review + meta-analysis; PROSPERO CRD42023395736.
- **Search**: comprehensive multi-database search up to **May 7, 2024**.
- **Population**: fixed-orthodontic-appliance patients.
- **Comparison**: oral irrigator (OI) vs dental floss (DF), each combined with toothbrushing.
- **Outcomes**: plaque index (PI) and bleeding index (BI), baseline → study end.
- **Analysis**: standardised mean difference (SMD), random-effects model, DerSimonian–Laird estimator, 95% CI.
- **Flow**: 975 records → **4 eligible** → **3 meta-analysed**.

## Results

| Outcome | Pooled SMD (OI vs DF) | 95% CI | p | Heterogeneity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plaque index (PI) | −0.88 | −2.25 to 0.50 | 0.10 (NS) | I² = 98% |
| Bleeding index (BI) | −0.90 | −2.78 to 0.98 | 0.35 (NS) | — |

- **No significant difference** between OI and DF for PI or BI reduction at study end.
- **Very high heterogeneity** (PI I²=98%) → pooled PI estimate is unreliable; certainty of evidence is weak.
- Both CIs span a wide range crossing the null (large negative to small positive).
- Secondary observation: OI lowered initial PI and BI **from baseline** — effective hygiene adjunct, not a floss-beater.

**Bottom line**: incorporating an oral irrigator into regular toothbrushing does **not outperform** dental floss for plaque and bleeding control in orthodontic patients. Given only 4 studies, extreme heterogeneity, and wide CIs, the evidence is weak — choice between OI and DF can reasonably follow patient compliance and preference rather than a measured efficacy advantage.
