## One-line Summary

Comparative life-cycle assessment (LCA, not a clinical trial) of 8 interdental cleaning aids used daily for 5 years across 16 environmental impact categories: single-use floss picks had the largest footprint (worst in 13/16 categories), while bamboo interdental brushes had the smallest footprint in 5 categories including carbon (climate change).

## 한줄요약

치간 청소 도구 8종을 1인이 5년간 매일 사용하는 시나리오로 16개 환경영향 범주에 걸쳐 비교한 전과정평가(Life Cycle Assessment, LCA; 임상시험 아님). 일회용 플로스픽(floss pick)이 16개 중 13개 범주에서 환경발자국이 가장 컸고, 대나무 치간칫솔(bamboo IDB)이 탄소(기후변화) 포함 5개 범주에서 가장 작았다 — 임상적 필요·비용과 함께 환경영향도 고려해 제품을 권고하라.

## Summary

This is a **comparative life-cycle assessment (LCA), not a clinical trial** — it models the environmental footprint of interdental cleaning aids rather than measuring clinical efficacy. The authors (Eastman Dental Institute, UCL, with Dublin Dental University Hospital) compared **8 products — 4 floss + 4 interdental brushes (IDBs)** — under a single functional unit: **one person using the aid every day for 5 years** to prevent/manage periodontal disease. Footprints were quantified across **16 environmental impact categories** (climate change in kg CO₂e, ozone depletion, water use, eutrophication, ecotoxicity, etc.) using OpenLCA + Ecoinvent under EU PEF methodology.

The headline finding: **single-use floss picks are the worst environmental choice**, with the largest footprint in **13 of 16** categories — driven mainly by the polypropylene plastic handle (49% of the floss pick's carbon footprint). There was **no single "best" product**: the bamboo IDB led in the most categories (5, including carbon), followed by replaceable-head IDBs (4), regular floss (3), sponge floss (3), and bamboo floss (1).

**Clinical bottom line**: clinicians should weigh **environmental impact alongside clinical need and cost** when recommending interdental aids. Where floss is indicated, regular/sponge/bamboo floss are greener than floss picks; where IDBs are indicated, weekly **reusable brushes (bamboo handle or reusable plastic handle with replaceable heads) beat single-use IDB picks**. Even so, every aid's environmental burden is far smaller than that of established periodontal disease, so the planetary benefit of preventing disease likely outweighs the aids' footprint.

## Key Contributions

- **First study** to quantify and rank the environmental footprint of different interdental cleaning aids (prior dental LCA work covered only toothbrushes).
- Shows **carbon footprinting alone is insufficient** — a full 16-category LCA changes the picture, and no product wins across all categories.
- Adds a **green-dentistry / planetary-health axis** to interdental product selection, complementing efficacy and adherence evidence.
- Provides directly actionable guidance: avoid single-use picks; prefer reusable/refillable aids.

## Methodology

- **Design**: attributional comparative LCA, physical allocation by mass; OpenLCA v1.8 + Ecoinvent v3.7; ISO + EU Product Environmental Footprint (PEF) methodology.
- **Functional unit**: one individual using the aid **daily for 5 years** (chosen to match a prior toothbrush LCA).
- **8 products** (best-sellers on Amazon UK, brands anonymized): **floss** — regular (nylon roll + plastic dispenser), sponge, floss picks (single-use, plastic handle), bamboo (glass jar); **IDBs** — regular (plastic handle, weekly), IDB picks (single-use), replaceable-head (reusable handle + heads, weekly), bamboo (bamboo handle, weekly).
- **System boundaries (cradle-to-grave)**: raw materials (dismantled, weighed to 0.01 g, 5-yr quantity calculated), manufacture, transport (6/8 made in Europe, 2 in China), consumer use (UK, unheated tap water), disposal (UK recycling). Retail excluded as equal across products.
- **Primary outcome**: LCIA across **16 impact categories** (ecosystem quality, human health, planetary resource use).
- **Secondary outcomes**: normalized results, DALYs, contribution analysis.
- **Note**: LCA yields descriptive results only — **no p-values or confidence intervals**.

## Results

- **Floss picks = worst overall** — largest footprint in **13/16** categories; IDB picks second worst.
- **Smallest footprint by number of categories won** (no single product won all):
  - **Bamboo IDB — 5 categories** (climate change, freshwater eutrophication, ionizing radiation, fossil use, mineral/metal use)
  - **Replaceable-head IDB — 4** (acidification, marine eutrophication, terrestrial eutrophication, photochemical ozone creation)
  - **Regular floss — 3** (non-carcinogenic effects, respiratory inorganics, land use)
  - **Sponge floss — 3** (freshwater ecotoxicity, carcinogenic effects, ozone layer depletion)
  - **Bamboo floss — 1** (water use)
- **Carbon (climate change, kg CO₂e / 5 yr)**: floss picks **11.42** → bamboo IDB **1.31**; bamboo IDB ~48% less CO₂ than a regular IDB. (A plastic toothbrush over 5 yr = 25.6 kg CO₂e — more than any interdental aid here.)
- **DALYs** (scaled to UK adult population ~52.9M over 5 yr): floss pick **753** (highest), IDB picks **412**, bamboo floss **125** (lowest); regular floss 75% lower DALY than floss picks, regular IDBs 57% lower than IDB picks. DALYs driven by climate change (44–99%) + carcinogenic effects (0–64%).
- **Contribution analysis**: floss-pick plastic handle = 49% of its carbon footprint; bamboo IDB handle = just 5%.
- **Caveat**: bamboo is not unambiguously green (water/fertilizer use, biodiversity loss); recycled plastic may ultimately outperform bamboo, as seen for toothbrushes.
