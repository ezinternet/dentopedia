## Summary
This systematic review asked whether occlusal stress is a mechanism in NCCL formation, searching PubMed, ISI Web of Science, and EMBASE for "abfraction." Of 372 publications identified, 69 were ultimately included (31 clinical and 38 laboratory studies). The majority — 56/69 (81%) — found an association between occlusal stress and NCCLs, and 24 of the 38 laboratory studies (largely finite element analyses) demonstrated stress concentration at the cervical region. Crucially, however, no clinical study showed that NCCLs were caused by stress alone. The review thus represents the most abfraction-favorable synthesis, but its conclusion is driven heavily by laboratory/FEA evidence rather than clinical demonstration of causation. Its single-keyword search strategy and failure to separate true abfraction lesions from purely erosive/abrasive ones were later criticized by Dioguardi 2024, making this paper the "pro-abfraction" pole against the null clinical SRs of Senna 2012 and Silva 2013.

## Key Contributions
- Most abfraction-supportive SR (81% of publications report an association).
- Separates laboratory (FEA stress-concentration) from clinical evidence and shows the support is lab-weighted.
- States plainly that no clinical study proves stress-alone causation.

## Methodology
SR; keyword "abfraction" across three databases, English only; 372 identified → 69 included (31 clinical, 38 laboratory). No meta-analysis. (Abstract-only ingest.)

## Results
56/69 (81%) found an occlusal stress-NCCL association; 23 reported stress/occlusal factors associated with NCCLs; 24/38 lab studies found cervical stress concentration. No clinical study established stress as sole cause.
