## One-line Summary
Narrative review arguing abfraction theory is unproven and that asymptomatic NCCLs should be monitored (>=6 months) rather than restored or occlusally adjusted to prevent progression.

## 한줄요약

## Summary
Focused on abfraction as a subtype of NCCL, this University of Florida review concludes that despite decades of finite-element and photoelastic modeling, clinical studies have failed to confirm a strong association between occlusal loading and cervical lesions — the abfraction theory remains unproven. The authors adopt Grippo's pathodynamic schema and reframe NCCL management around personalized risk-factor identification before any intervention. Their central clinical message is conservative: occlusal adjustment and restoration are not indicated to prevent further tooth loss or halt progression. Asymptomatic, vital, functional teeth with abfraction lesions should be monitored for at least six months before any invasive procedure; restoration is justified only to replace form/function, relieve hypersensitivity, or address esthetics, and a combined restorative-surgical approach may be used when gingival recession coexists.

## Key Contributions
- Evidence-based rejection of occlusal adjustment/restoration as progression-prevention measures.
- Explicit >=6-month monitoring threshold for asymptomatic lesions.
- Personalized-dentistry framing: identify and address patient risk factors first.
- OCT evidence that dentin demineralization drives early formation while occlusal stress contributes to progression.

## Methodology
Narrative review of abfraction etiology, diagnosis, and treatment; reproduces Grippo's pathodynamic mechanism schema. No systematic search, no quality grading.

## Results
Reports weak/inconsistent NCCL-occlusion association across clinical studies. Prevalence greater in incisors and premolars than canines/molars; mandibular premolars more affected than maxillary. Concludes abfraction theory unproven and management should default to monitoring.
