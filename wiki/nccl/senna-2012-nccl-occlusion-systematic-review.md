## One-line Summary
Systematic review of 28 clinical studies (3 prospective, 25 cross-sectional) finding that heterogeneity and bias prevent any conclusion about occlusion's role in NCCL aetiology; meta-analysis was not possible.

## Summary
Senna and colleagues systematically reviewed clinical studies addressing whether occlusion contributes to NCCL aetiology. From 286 MEDLINE articles, 28 met inclusion (3 prospective, 25 cross-sectional). The review found extreme heterogeneity in study design, NCCL diagnostic criteria, methods of analysis, and the occlusal variables examined, alongside substantial methodological bias (notably non-blinded examiners). This heterogeneity produced a wide range of reported prevalences and "masked any conclusion about the role of occlusion in NCCL aetiology," and precluded meta-analysis. The paper is an early, frequently cited demonstration that clinical evidence for the abfraction/occlusion hypothesis is methodologically weak rather than clearly positive or negative — a key data point in the unresolved occlusion controversy.

## Key Contributions
- First clinical-study SR focused on the NCCL-occlusion link.
- Identifies heterogeneity and examiner bias as the core reasons the question remains unanswered.
- Establishes that no meta-analysis is feasible with the existing evidence base.

## Methodology
SR of clinical studies; MEDLINE search (286 retrieved → 28 included). Full-text appraisal of clinical investigations evaluating occlusal aspects of NCCL aetiology. No meta-analysis. (Ingested from abstract + references; body text not obtained.)

## Results
3 prospective and 25 cross-sectional studies included. Extreme heterogeneity in design, diagnostic criteria, and occlusal analysis; substantial bias. No quantitative pooling; conclusion about occlusion's role not possible.
