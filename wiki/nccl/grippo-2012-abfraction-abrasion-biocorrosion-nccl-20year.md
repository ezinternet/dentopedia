## One-line Summary
Classic narrative review proposing "biocorrosion" terminology and the stress-friction-biocorrosion pathodynamic schema that frames NCCLs as inherently multifactorial.

## Summary
Grippo, Simring, and Coleman revisit two decades of NCCL research and argue that neither toothbrush-dentifrice abrasion nor acid "erosion" alone explains these lesions. They propose replacing "erosion" with "biocorrosion," which encompasses chemical, biochemical, and electrochemical degradation by endogenous and exogenous acids, proteolytic agents, and piezoelectric effects on dentin. Their Revised Schema of Pathodynamic Mechanisms treats each NCCL as the product of a case-specific interplay of stress (abfraction), friction (wear), and biocorrosion, shaped by modifying factors such as saliva, tongue action, tooth form, microstructure, mobility, and positional prominence. The paper explicitly warns against labeling all NCCLs "abfractions." This schema underpins most subsequent etiologic writing on NCCLs, including Goodacre 2022 and Nascimento 2016.

## Key Contributions
- Introduced "biocorrosion" as the umbrella term for chemical/biochemical/electrochemical tooth degradation.
- Formalized the three-mechanism interaction schema reproduced in later reviews.
- Rejected single-mechanism attribution of NCCLs; designation must reflect the individual case's mechanism mix.
- Enumerated modifying factors that alter lesion expression.

## Methodology
Narrative synthesis of ~20 years of laboratory and clinical literature; no systematic search or quantitative pooling. Note: archived PDF is a Wiley webpage capture containing abstract, clinical significance, and the full reference list; body text not included.

## Results
Conceptual framework only — no pooled quantitative outcomes. Clinical significance statement: comprehensive medical/dental history plus the schema allows the dentist to identify and treat the etiology of root-surface lesions and institute prevention targeted at causative and modifying factors.
