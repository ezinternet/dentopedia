## Summary
This University of Foggia narrative review surveys the competing theories of NCCL etiopathogenesis and how each acts on the tooth's mineralized structures. It positions erosion (acidic chemical dissolution) and abrasion (repeated mechanical trauma, chiefly tooth brushing) as the two widely accepted mechanisms, and abfraction — destruction driven by anomalous, non-axial occlusal loads concentrating stress at the cervical region — as the disputed third. The authors trace the historical classification of these lesions from G.V. Black (1908) forward and argue that neither erosion nor abrasion alone can explain every NCCL, so a multifactorial framework combining chemical, mechanical, and (controversially) occlusal-stress contributions is required. The paper is the conceptual companion to the same group's 2024 scoping review, which subjects the abfraction theory specifically to systematic appraisal.

## Key Contributions
- Organized comparison of NCCL etiologic theories and their structural impact on enamel/dentin.
- Reaffirms erosion + abrasion as accepted, abfraction as contested.
- Provides historical lineage of NCCL classification.

## Methodology
Narrative literature review (non-systematic); conceptual synthesis of erosion, abrasion, and abfraction theories. No risk-of-bias assessment or pooling.

## Results
No quantitative pooling. Qualitative conclusion: multifactorial etiology required; single mechanisms insufficient; abfraction's occlusal-load role unresolved.
