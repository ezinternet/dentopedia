## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis is the first to directly pool a head-to-head comparison of **one-stage versus two-stage ridge split** procedures for horizontal alveolar ridge augmentation in adults. In the ridge-split technique a narrow alveolar crest is longitudinally sectioned and expanded buccolingually to create width for implant placement and/or grafting. The two approaches differ in timing: the **one-stage** protocol places the implant in the same session as the expansion, whereas the **two-stage** protocol expands first and defers implant placement to a second surgery after healing.

Following PRISMA, the authors searched PubMed, Google Scholar, and EBSCO Host (January 2000 – December 2021) for prospective and follow-up studies reporting outcomes as mean ± SD. Eleven studies met eligibility for qualitative synthesis; only three were suitable for meta-analysis. Risk of bias was appraised with the Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (studies) and the JBI checklist (case reports). The pooled standardized mean difference (random-effects model) favoured the one-stage approach by an average of roughly 0.89, leading the authors to conclude that one-stage ridge split is superior to two-stage on comparative analysis. The signal is weakened by the small meta-analysable base, uniformly moderate-to-high risk of bias, poor heterogeneity, and funnel-plot asymmetry indicating possible publication bias.

This page **refines** the staging decision underlying the staged (two-stage) technique anchored by Enislidis 2006: a contemporary comparative SR tilts the timing choice toward one-stage, without overturning the standalone validity of the staged greenstick technique as a documented option for difficult ridges.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA to **directly compare one-stage vs two-stage ridge split** timing for horizontal ridge augmentation.
- Quantitative direction-of-effect estimate: pooled **SMD favours one-stage (~0.89)** under a random-effects model.
- Structured RoB appraisal (NOS + JBI) of the small ridge-split comparative evidence base, transparently flagging that all 11 studies were moderate-to-high RoB.

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review + meta-analysis, PRISMA-reported.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Google Scholar, EBSCO Host.
- **Window**: January 2000 – December 2021.
- **Inclusion**: prospective + follow-up studies comparing one-stage vs two-stage ridge split, outcomes as mean ± SD.
- **RoB tools**: Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (studies); Joanna Briggs Institute checklist (case reports).
- **Synthesis**: standardized mean difference (SMD), random-effects model, p < 0.05 significance.
- **Yield**: 11 studies qualitative; 3 studies meta-analysed.
- abstract-only — full text not retrieved (PMC returned an empty body); content built from the structured abstract.

## Results

- **All 11 included studies: moderate-to-high risk of bias.**
- **Pooled SMD favoured one-stage** ridge split — on average ~0.89 toward one-stage versus two-stage.
- **Heterogeneity** mostly poor across pooled outcomes.
- **Funnel-plot asymmetry** → possible publication bias / small-study effects.
- **Conclusion**: one-stage ridge split is superior to two-stage on comparative analysis (low-certainty evidence given RoB, heterogeneity, and bias signals).
