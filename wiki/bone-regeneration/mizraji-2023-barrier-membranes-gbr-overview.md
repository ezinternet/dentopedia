## Summary

The most recent authoritative overview of GBR barrier membranes in the top-tier review journal Periodontology 2000. The review covers all major membrane categories and incorporates updated meta-analyses of clinical outcomes.

**Core principle**: All barrier membranes serve to exclude fast-proliferating epithelial and connective tissue from the regeneration space, allowing slower-growing osteogenic cells to populate the defect. The membrane material determines:
1. Space maintenance capacity
2. Resorption profile (resorbable vs non-resorbable)
3. Biologic/bacteria compatibility and tissue response
4. Second-surgery requirement

## Key Contributions

- Tier-1 journal (Periodontology 2000) synthesis of all membrane categories
- Includes updated meta-analyses of bone gain and complication rates by membrane type
- Provides clinical selection framework: defect size × space maintenance need × biologic priority
- Distinguishes ribose vs glutaraldehyde cross-linking within the cross-linked collagen subcategory
- Covers emerging alternatives: amnion/chorion, acellular dermal matrix, pericardium membranes

## Methodology

Narrative review with meta-analysis data incorporated. Coverage:

**Non-resorbable membranes:**
| Membrane | Key property | Clinical note |
|---|---|---|
| e-PTFE | Expanded, microporous (Gore-Tex) | Original GBR membrane; now largely discontinued (pore = bacteria harbor) |
| d-PTFE | Dense, non-porous, bacteria-resistant | Open healing possible; gold standard non-resorbable; requires removal |
| Titanium mesh | Maximum space maintenance | Highest VBG potential; highest exposure risk; mandates 2nd surgery |
| Ti-reinforced PTFE | PTFE + embedded Ti wire | Balance: PTFE bacteria resistance + Ti space maintenance; requires removal |

**Resorbable membranes:**
| Membrane | Resorption timeline | Key note |
|---|---|---|
| Native collagen | 4–6 weeks | Fastest; most common; inadequate for large VRA |
| Cross-linked collagen (ribose, e.g. OSSIXPlus) | 6–24 weeks | Collagenase-resistant; open healing capable; ~30% higher exposure vs native |
| Cross-linked collagen (glutaraldehyde, e.g. BioMend Extend) | 6–9 weeks | Cytotoxicity concern with residual glutaraldehyde |
| PLGA/PLA synthetic | Variable | Limited clinical uptake |
| Pericardium / amnion / chorion | Variable | Emerging; especially used in socket sealing and soft tissue |

## Results

Space maintenance hierarchy (from best to least):
**Ti mesh > Ti-PTFE > d-PTFE > cross-linked collagen > native collagen ≈ synthetic**

Exposure risk hierarchy:
**Ti mesh (7–66%) > cross-linked collagen (~30% higher than native) > native collagen > d-PTFE (~15%, open-healing tolerable)**

Second surgery required:
- Non-resorbable (d-PTFE, Ti mesh, Ti-PTFE, e-PTFE): YES
- Resorbable (all collagen types, PLGA, pericardium): NO

Clinical indication mapping:
- **Large vertical defects**: non-resorbable preferred (Ti mesh / Ti-PTFE / d-PTFE)
- **Lateral/combination defects**: both viable
- **Ridge preservation / socket sealing**: resorbable adequate; XL collagen for open healing
