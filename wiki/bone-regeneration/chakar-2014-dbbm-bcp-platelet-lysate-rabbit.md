## Summary

Chakar et al. (2014) conducted a randomized rabbit femoral condyle study to compare healing in critical-size defects filled with DBBM or BCP, with or without autologous platelet lysate (APL). Twelve male New Zealand rabbits were split into two groups of six: DBBM ± APL and BCP ± APL, with bilateral femoral defects providing n=6 per subgroup. Animals were sacrificed at 6 weeks.

Histomorphometric analysis showed that **BCP (60% HA / 40% β-TCP) produced significantly greater new bone formation and underwent significantly more resorption** than DBBM. This is consistent with BCP's designed resorbability through its β-TCP component. DBBM, while osteoconductive, was largely non-resorbed at 6 weeks — consistent with its known very slow resorption kinetics.

Critically, the addition of APL (prepared by freeze-thaw platelet lysis) provided **no measurable benefit** to new bone formation when combined with either material. Despite confirmed in vitro mitogenic activity (100–200% cell proliferation enhancement), the growth factor effect did not translate in vivo. This is an important negative finding for the platelet concentrate literature — platelet-derived growth factors alone, without an appropriate scaffold interaction, may not enhance bone regeneration in standardized animal models.

## Key Contributions

- Quantified the BCP vs DBBM performance gap in a standardized critical-size defect model: +70% new bone, −60% residual material with BCP.
- First randomized comparison of APL with two contrasting bone substitutes — demonstrated APL adds no benefit to either.
- Highlighted the dissociation between in vitro growth factor activity and in vivo bone formation efficacy.
- Provided pre-PRF-era baseline for platelet concentrate + bone substitute interaction data.

## Methodology

- Animal: 12 male New Zealand rabbits, 4 months, bilateral femoral condyle critical-size defects
- Groups: (1) DBBM vs DBBM+APL (n=6 rabbits, 2 defects/rabbit); (2) BCP vs BCP+APL (n=6 rabbits, 2 defects/rabbit)
- APL: Freeze-thaw platelet lysate from autologous blood; ~10⁶ platelets/µL; biological activity confirmed by in vitro proliferation assay
- Healing: 6 weeks
- Assessment: Non-demineralized histology + histomorphometry (new bone %, residual material %)

## Results

| Comparison | New Bone | Residual Material |
|---|---|---|
| BCP vs DBBM | +70% (p<0.001) | −60% (p<0.001) |
| DBBM+APL vs DBBM | No significant difference | No significant difference |
| BCP+APL vs BCP | No significant difference | No significant difference |

New bone observed around particles of all groups — confirmed osteoconductive scaffold function for both materials. DBBM remains largely non-resorbed at 6 weeks; BCP actively resorbs (β-TCP component degraded).
