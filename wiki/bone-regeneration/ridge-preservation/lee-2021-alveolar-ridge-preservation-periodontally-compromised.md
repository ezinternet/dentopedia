## One-line Summary

Retrospective cohort (418 periodontally compromised extraction sites, 287 patients): ARP significantly reduced implant-placement infeasibility (0.8% vs 4.7% no-ARP) and reduced the need/severity of bone augmentation at implant placement.

## Summary

This single-center retrospective cohort from Seoul National University addresses a gap in the alveolar ridge preservation (ARP) literature: most ARP evidence comes from intact, non-infected sockets, but the teeth most commonly extracted in practice are those lost to periodontal or combined endodontic–periodontal disease — i.e., *infected* sockets. Lee et al. asked whether ARP (defined as immediate guided bone regeneration with a resorbable membrane immediately after extraction, healing by secondary intention) improves the **feasibility of subsequent implant placement** in these compromised sites.

Across 418 sites (171 no-ARP, 247 ARP) in 287 patients, ARP cut implant-placement **infeasibility from 4.7% to 0.8%**. Beyond simply enabling placement, ARP **attenuated the severity of bone augmentation** required at implant surgery: absence of ARP was an independent predictor of greater horizontal, vertical, and total augmentation need on multivariable logistic regression. The authors' linked prior cohort established that ARP in infected sockets — with thorough debridement and systemic antibiotics — is safe (2.7% infection, 0.7% reinfection requiring biomaterial removal). The clinical message: in periodontally compromised sockets, ARP is a defensible, often beneficial intervention rather than over-treatment.

## Key Contributions

- Evaluates ARP specifically in **periodontally/endodontically compromised (infected) sockets**, the real-world majority of extractions, rather than intact sockets.
- Uses **implant-placement feasibility** (binary) as the primary endpoint — a clinically meaningful outcome distinct from millimeter dimensional change.
- Demonstrates ARP **reduces the severity** of bone augmentation needed at implant placement (horizontal / vertical / total), not just whether placement is possible.
- Multivariable logistic regression isolates "no ARP" as an independent predictor of augmentation need alongside location and sex.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Retrospective chart review (STROBE), IRB-approved, single center (SNUDH). Extractions 2011–2015, implants placed 2011–2018.
- **Case definition** (periodontally compromised socket):
  - *Periodontal*: mobility >2°, deepest probing depth >8 mm, apically narrowing marginal bone loss.
  - *Combined endo–perio*: radiographic periapical lesion >4 mm + negative electric pulp test + the above periodontal features.
- **Groups**: ARP = bone graft immediately post-extraction (resorbable membrane, no flap, secondary-intention healing; Bio-Oss and other materials); patients self-selected after being offered ARP vs no-ARP.
- **Outcomes**: primary — feasibility of implant placement (yes/no); secondary — horizontal/vertical/sinus-floor/total bone augmentation at implant surgery, and implant failure before loading.
- **Analysis**: multivariable logistic regression; sinus elevation analyzed only for maxillary posterior; P < 0.05.
- **Flow**: 10,060 screened → 574 eligible sites → 418 sites implanted (171 no-ARP, 247 ARP).
- **Limitations**: self-selected non-randomized groups, no CBCT at extraction (pre-extraction defect severity unmatched — major confounder), exclusion of non-implanted sites, outcomes only to before-loading.

## Results

- **Implant-placement infeasibility**: ARP **0.8%** vs no-ARP **4.7%** (significantly lower with ARP).
- **Horizontal and vertical bone augmentation**: significantly predicted by **location** and **no ARP**.
- **Total bone augmentation**: significantly predicted by **sex, location, and no ARP**.
- **Safety (linked prior cohort)**: 2.7% infection, 0.7% reinfection requiring removal of grafted biomaterial.
- **Conclusion**: ARP in periodontally compromised sites may improve implant-placement feasibility and attenuate the severity of bone augmentation at implant placement.
