## One-line Summary

Rat model (n=17, 3 age groups) demonstrating that senescent (18-month-old) rats show significantly impaired alveolar socket healing — reduced bone volume, trabecular thickness, empty sockets with inflammatory infiltrate — versus middle-aged (10M) animals which showed the best regenerative response.

## Summary

This study from Jeonbuk National University (전북대학교) quantified the age-dependent effects on alveolar bone healing following mandibular tooth extraction in male Sprague-Dawley rats across three age groups: 2M (young), 10M (middle-aged), and 18M (senescent). At 2 weeks post-extraction, the senescent (18M) group exhibited significantly reduced bone volume and trabecular thickness compared to younger groups. Serum bone turnover markers showed the highest baseline metabolic activity (osteocalcin and CTX-I) in younger rats, with a distinct post-extraction TRAP5b response pattern. Paradoxically, the 10M group demonstrated the most favorable healing with maximal alkaline phosphatase (ALP) expression filling the socket. The 18M group showed compromised healing characterized by empty sockets and inflammatory cell accumulation. The study concludes that aging significantly deteriorates the biological healing capacity of alveolar bone, emphasizing the need for age-specific considerations in dental rehabilitation planning.

## Key Contributions

1. **Age-stratified socket healing model**: Establishes 3-tier experimental aging model (2M/10M/18M) to map socket healing trajectory across lifespan
2. **Middle-aged peak**: Demonstrates peak regenerative capacity at 10 months — not youth — with maximal ALP socket filling
3. **Senescent failure mechanisms**: Empty sockets + inflammatory cell accumulation as histological hallmarks of age-related healing failure
4. **Bone turnover marker profiles**: Age-differential TRAP5b and osteocalcin/CTX-I patterns provide mechanistic insight into impaired osteoclast-osteoblast coupling in aging

## Methodology

- **Model**: Male Sprague-Dawley rat mandibular tooth extraction
- **Groups**: 2M (n=6), 10M (n=6), 18M (n=5)
- **Timepoint**: 2 weeks post-extraction
- **Outcomes**: Micro-CT (BV/TV, Tb.Th), serum markers (osteocalcin, CTX-I, ALP, TRAP5b), histology, immunohistochemistry

## Results

| Parameter | 2M | 10M | 18M |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bone volume | High | Most favorable | Significantly reduced |
| Trabecular thickness | High | Favorable | Significantly reduced |
| ALP socket filling | High | Maximal | Compromised |
| Socket histology | Regenerating | Best healing | Empty + inflammatory cells |
| Baseline bone turnover | Highest | Intermediate | Lowest |

Key finding: The 18M senescent group showed the worst outcomes on all metrics. Notably, the middle-aged 10M group outperformed the young 2M group on socket filling (ALP expression), suggesting that peak bone regenerative response to extraction may occur in middle age before senescent decline.

## Clinical Relevance

- **Geriatric implant planning**: Age-related impairment in socket healing supports rationale for ridge preservation in elderly patients
- **Inflammatory mediator targeting**: Inflammatory cell accumulation in aged sockets suggests anti-inflammatory strategies may benefit older patients
- **Bone turnover monitoring**: TRAP5b and osteocalcin markers may serve as screening tools for healing prognosis in aging patients
- **Age-specific rehabilitation**: Clinical protocols may need adjustment for elderly patients — longer healing times, grafting, or biologic augmentation
