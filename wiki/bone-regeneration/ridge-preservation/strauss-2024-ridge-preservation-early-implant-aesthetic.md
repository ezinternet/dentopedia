## One-line Summary

RCT secondary analysis (n=75, 3 groups 1:1:1; 55 datasets at 1y) of early implant placement (8-wk delayed) in the anterior maxilla: ARP (DBBM-C + collagen matrix or palatal graft) cut the need for additional GBR at placement from 68.4% (no ARP) to ~30%, but simultaneous GBR at placement — not ARP alone — was the dominant driver of restored buccal convexity at 1 year (72.7% with GBR vs 36.0% ARP-only).

## Summary

This is a radiographic-and-profilometric secondary analysis of a three-arm parallel RCT in the aesthetic zone. Seventy-five patients with a failing single anterior-maxillary tooth were randomized 1:1:1 to alveolar ridge preservation (ARP) with DBBM containing 10% collagen (DBBM-C) sealed either by a collagen matrix (ARP-CM) or a palatal graft (ARP-PG), versus unassisted socket healing (control). Eight weeks later, **early (type-2) implant placement was done in every patient**, with additional GBR performed at placement when the residual ridge required it.

The headline is a two-part message. First, ARP works as intended for the *next* surgical step: it roughly halved the proportion of sites needing additional GBR at implant placement (≈30% with ARP vs 68.4% without). Second — and this is the clinically important caveat — once you look at the 1-year facial contour, the strongest determinant of restored **buccal convexity** is not whether ARP was done but whether **additional GBR was performed at implant placement**. Sites that received ARP *and* GBR reached convexity in 72.7%, versus only 36.0% for ARP without GBR. Residual buccal bone height and additional GBR were the significant predictors in adjusted models. Profilometric (surface-scan) outcomes tended to agree with the CBCT picture.

Bottom line: ARP attenuates ridge changes and reduces the GBR burden, but early placement *with simultaneous GBR* yields the best contour regardless of whether ARP preceded it. ARP should be framed as reducing — not eliminating — the need for hard-tissue augmentation in the aesthetic zone.

## Key Contributions

- Disentangles **ARP at extraction** from **additional GBR at implant placement**, showing the latter dominates 1-year buccal contour.
- Quantifies ARP's effect on downstream surgery: additional-GBR need 31.6% (ARP-CM) / 29.4% (ARP-PG) vs 68.4% (control).
- Pairs CBCT (radiographic) with profilometric (STL surface-scan) outcomes on the same patients, with concordant findings.
- Anchors the analysis in **early/type-2 timing** (8 weeks), filling a gap between immediate- and late-placement ARP evidence.

## Methodology

- **Design**: parallel-group RCT, 1:1:1, n=75; this paper = radiographic + profilometric secondary analysis.
- **Groups**: (a) ARP-CM = DBBM-C + collagen matrix; (b) ARP-PG = DBBM-C + free palatal graft; (c) control = unassisted healing.
- **Timing**: tooth extraction → 8 weeks → early implant placement in all arms; additional GBR at placement as indicated.
- **Outcomes**: CBCT ridge dimensions and profilometric contour change at 8 weeks (pre-implant) and 1-year post-loading; presence of buccal convexity; adjusted regression for predictors.
- **Analyzed**: 55/70 datasets at 1y (ARP-CM 19; ARP-PG 17; control 19).

## Results

| Outcome | ARP-CM | ARP-PG | Control |
|---|---|---|---|
| Additional GBR needed at placement | 31.6% | 29.4% | 68.4% |

- **Buccal convexity at 1y** — ARP without additional GBR: 36.0% (9/25); ARP **with** additional GBR: 72.7% (8/11), p = 0.042.
- **Significant predictors (adjusted)**: residual buccal bone height and additional GBR at placement (p < 0.05).
- **Profilometry**: tendency to agree with radiographic outcomes.
- **Conclusion**: early placement + ARP attenuates 1-year changes, but early placement **with simultaneous GBR** consistently yields superior contour regardless of ARP.
