## One-line Summary

Mouse subcutaneous model + in vitro: 40 wt% L-serine incorporation into bovine collagen scaffolds significantly slows degradation (2.70 vs 0.37 mm² residual area, p<0.05) without chemical crosslinking, demonstrating amino acid-assisted tuning of GBR membrane resorption.

## Summary

This study evaluated whether incorporating L-serine — a polar amino acid with a hydroxyl side chain capable of non-covalent hydrogen bonding — could modulate collagen scaffold degradation without chemical crosslinking. Collagen scaffolds (Type I bovine skin; 2% w/v) were prepared with 0–40 wt% L-serine and characterized structurally (XRD, FT-IR, circular dichroism, SEM). In vivo degradation was assessed in a subcutaneous mouse model with three groups: unmodified collagen, 40 wt% L-serine collagen, and Bio-Gide® (commercial bilayer porcine collagen membrane). Residual area was quantified by serial sonography and confirmed histologically.

At 40 wt% L-serine, crystalline domain formation and β-sheet enrichment were observed, indicating structural alteration of the collagen matrix. In vivo, 40 wt% L-serine scaffolds showed significantly greater residual area (2.70 ± 1.45 mm²) versus unmodified collagen (0.37 ± 0.22 mm²; p<0.05). Bio-Gide® remained the most persistent (5.64 ± 2.76 mm²). Low-to-moderate L-serine concentrations (10–30 wt%) preserved the collagen triple-helix. The authors conclude that L-serine incorporation is feasible as a non-crosslinking stabilization strategy for collagen biomaterials, with osteogenic performance and defect-specific evaluation remaining for future studies.

## Key Contributions

- Non-crosslinking approach: L-serine modulates degradation via hydrogen bonding and hydration-mediated interactions — avoids cytotoxicity risks of chemical crosslinkers (glutaraldehyde, EDC/NHS)
- Dose-dependent structural effect: low L-serine preserves triple-helix; high (40 wt%) causes crystalline/β-sheet shift
- Clinically relevant gap to Bio-Gide remains, but L-serine scaffolds are dramatically more stable than unmodified collagen

## Methodology

- Base material: Type I bovine skin collagen (rapidly degradable — sensitive model for detecting stabilization)
- L-serine range: 10–40 wt% (range set by solubility/formability limits)
- Scaffold: 1 mm thick sheets; in vitro specimens 10×10 mm; in vivo specimens 5×10 mm
- In vivo: subcutaneous mouse implantation; serial sonography + histology
- Comparator: Bio-Gide® (bilayer porcine Type I+III collagen, Geistlich)
- Structural: XRD, FT-IR, circular dichroism, SEM
