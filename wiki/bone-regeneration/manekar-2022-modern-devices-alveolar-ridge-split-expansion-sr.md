## One-line Summary

SR+MA (24 studies, 1287 implants in 634 patients) of modern alveolar-ridge-split-and-expansion (ARSE) devices: ultrasonographic/piezo (USG) achieves 3.40 mm ridge width gain vs motorized ridge expander (MRE) 2.83 mm (p<0.0001), with overall implant survival 98.07%; USG fits osteo-mobilization, MRE fits minimally invasive osteo-condensation.

## Summary

Manekar et al. (2022, PROSPERO CRD42020213264) systematically reviewed the *device choice* for alveolar ridge split and expansion (ARSE) in horizontally deficient (2–6 mm) ridges, a gap left by prior reviews that catalogued devices without reporting outcomes. From 1020+10 records, PRISMA screening yielded **24 eligible studies (22 cohort + 2 RCT)**, totaling **1287 implants in 634 patients** (age 17–70 y, mean follow-up 13.23 months, ≥3 months minimum). The two dominant modern device families — **ultrasonographic/piezo (USG)** and **motorized ridge expander (MRE)** — were meta-analyzed (10 USG + 7 MRE articles).

The clinical bottom line is a device-to-biology mapping: **USG is the precise osteotomy tool best suited to osteo-mobilization (OM)** (vertical + horizontal corticotomies, technique-sensitive, more buccal-fracture risk, typically delayed implant placement), whereas **MRE drives minimally invasive crestal-osteotomy + osteo-condensation (CO-OC)** with non-cutting low-speed expansion drills, permitting **immediate implant placement** and fewer surgical stages. Despite USG's larger width gain, survival is essentially equal between devices (~98%). GRADE certainty is **"very Low"** (observational-dominant, only 2 small RCTs, no direct conventional-vs-modern comparison).

## Key Contributions

1. First SR+MA quantifying **ridge width gain (RWG)** and **implant survival** for modern ARSE devices specifically.
2. Pooled head-to-head: **USG 3.40 mm vs MRE 2.83 mm** RWG (p<0.0001).
3. **Overall implant survival 98.07%** across 1287 implants (USG ~98.42%, MRE ~98.97%).
4. Explicit decision framing — device choice follows the intended split biology: **OM → USG**, **CO-OC → MRE** (and MRE enables immediate placement).

## Methodology

- **Design**: SR + meta-analysis; PROSPERO CRD42020213264; PRISMA-P 2015.
- **Databases**: PubMed/MEDLINE, Cochrane CENTRAL, Grey Open, hand search + prior SRs; through September 2020.
- **PICO**: P horizontally deficient (2–6 mm) edentulous arch for implant; I ARSE with modern device; C conventional ARSE or GBR; O ridge width gain + survival.
- **Inclusion**: RCT/CCT/cohort/case-control; ≥10 implants; named modern device; ≥3 months follow-up; English; full text.
- **Exclusion**: case reports; <10 implants.
- **PRISMA**: 1030 records → 110 after dedup → 31 full-text → **24 included** (22 cohort + 2 RCT).
- **Risk of bias**: modified Newcastle-Ottawa (22 observational), modified Cochrane tool (2 RCT). **GRADE: very Low.**
- **Meta-analysis**: continuous SMD (RWG) + proportion (survival), fixed and random effects, on 10 USG + 7 MRE articles.

## Results

| Metric | USG (piezo) | MRE (motorized) | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Articles meta-analyzed | 10 | 7 | |
| Mean initial ridge width | 3.76 mm | 3.78 mm | comparable baseline |
| Mean final ridge width | 7.13 mm | 5.92 mm | |
| **Mean ridge width gain (RWG)** | **3.40 mm** | **2.83 mm** | USG vs MRE p<0.0001 |
| Implant survival | ~98.42% | ~98.97% | |
| Typical ARSE type | Osteo-mobilization (OM) | Crestal-osteotomy + osteo-condensation (CO-OC) | |
| Implant timing | Delayed (DII) | Immediate (III) | MRE = fewer stages |

- **Overall implant survival: 98.07%** (1287 implants).
- **Heterogeneity**: high in RWG meta-analyses (USG I²=96.42%; MRE I²=97.20%; abstract overall I²=88.39%). Survival heterogeneity low (USG I²=3.31%, MRE I²=0%).
- **Publication bias**: none overall by Egger (intercept 6.6634, p=0.6142, NS); survival funnels symmetric (USG Egger p=0.83, MRE p=0.48).
- **Complications** (descending): implant failure → buccal plate fracture / "bad split" → peri-implantitis; some MRE studies reported buccal plate dehiscence.
- **Caveat**: USG-vs-MRE difference is confounded by technique (OM vs CO-OC) and implant timing (delayed vs immediate); no study directly compared modern vs conventional (chisel/osteotome) devices.
