## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 25 studies (24 observational + 1 RCT) on the alveolar ridge split (ARS) technique: pooled gained horizontal ridge width 3.348 mm (95%CI 2.533-4.163) and pooled implant survival 98.1% (95%CI 96.9-98.9), establishing ARS as a reliable horizontal augmentation method for narrow alveolar crests.

## Summary

The alveolar ridge split (ARS, also called split-crest or ridge expansion) widens a narrow buccolingual ridge by splitting the cortical plate, creating room for implants without a second donor site. This Lin et al. (2023) systematic review and meta-analysis pooled 25 studies (24 observational — 12 prospective, 12 retrospective — plus a single RCT) searched across six databases from 2010 to 2023. Two outcomes were synthesized: the **pooled gained horizontal width was 3.348 mm** (95%CI 2.533-4.163, 14 studies) and the **pooled implant survival was 98.1%** (95%CI 96.9-98.9, 17 studies), a survival comparable to implants placed in native unreconstructed bone. The authors conclude ARS is a reliable horizontal bone augmentation technique for narrow crests with sufficient width gain and high survival, while cautioning that the evidence base is almost entirely non-randomized.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA to pool both **horizontal width gain (3.348 mm)** and **implant survival (98.1%)** specifically for ARS.
- Quantifies the maxilla-vs-mandible difficulty gradient: maxilla gains more width (thicker, more viscoelastic buccal wall; D2/D3/D4 bone) than the harder mandible (D1/D2), which favours two-stage treatment.
- Comparative benchmarking: ARS (~3.63 mm) yields slightly less width than bone block grafting (~4.25 mm) but shortens treatment, lowers morbidity, and enables immediate implant placement; flapless piezotome crest split cut operative time >50% vs autologous bone block grafting.
- Systematic complication catalogue (7 types) with buccal-plate fracture as the dominant intraoperative risk, escalating when ridge width <3 mm.

## Methodology

- PROSPERO CRD42022354569. Databases: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Web of Science, CNKI, SIGLE (grey literature); Jan 2010 - Nov 2023, no language restriction.
- PICOS: edentulous patients undergoing implant placement with ARS; primary outcome = horizontal width gain, secondary = implant survival. Excluded: nonhuman, n<10, no width/survival or no mean/SD, reviews.
- 235 de-duplicated records -> 42 full-text -> 25 included.
- Risk of bias: ROBINS-I (non-RCTs), RoB 2.0 (the RCT). Random-effects model (Comprehensive Meta-Analysis 3.0); Begg/Egger publication-bias testing; one-study-removed sensitivity analysis confirmed robustness.

## Results

- **Gained horizontal width: 3.348 mm** (95%CI 2.533-4.163); individual study range 1.6-5.3 mm.
- **Implant survival: 98.1%** (95%CI 96.9-98.9); individual range 93.9-100%.
- Study composition: 24 observational + 1 RCT (Mahmoud 2020). Risk of bias: 9 high, 16 moderate.
- Surgical detail: 20 full-thickness / 3 partial-thickness / 2 flapless flaps; graft in 21/25 studies (12 autologous, 9 xenograft); 6 mandible-only, 19 maxillae ± mandible.
- Complications (7 studies, 7 types): exposure, infection, bad split, dehiscence, fracture, paresthesia, soft tissue retraction. Buccal-plate fracture is most frequent intraoperatively; risk rises sharply with ridge width <3 mm — delayed implantation or two-stage approach recommended in such cases and in the dense mandible (greenstick fracture poorly controllable there).
- Caveat: Egger test indicated publication bias for the survival-rate outcome.
