## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional questionnaire survey (131/150 practitioners) on dental-bur selection, usage, sterilization and disposal: diamond burs preferred (75%) over tungsten carbide (15%), most reused until worn out (85%), with cutting efficiency known to fall with wear/grit/duration, variable sterilization, and universal dustbin disposal.

## Summary

This cross-sectional survey of 131 practitioners anchors the "bur types and their roles" question in real-world behavior and provides the field's working **bur taxonomy**: all rotary cutting instruments with bladed heads remove tooth structure by **cutting (tungsten carbide) or abrading (diamond)**, spanning steel → carbide → diamond. Selection is driven in practice by tradition, shape, the clinical procedure, the **substrate being cut**, and sterilization/disposal logistics — and the paper notes the literature offers few formal selection guidelines. Clinically, diamond burs dominate choice (75% vs 15% carbide), valued for cutting efficiency irrespective of grit, and are color-coded by coarseness (green/black super-coarse → yellow fine), with operators favoring sequential coarse-to-fine ordering for gross reduction. The study also documents that **cutting efficiency declines with bur wear, debris accumulation, grit size, and prolonged cutting**, yet 85% reuse burs until worn out and only 33% recognize the efficiency loss — alongside inconsistent sterilization and universal (100%) dustbin disposal, a cross-infection-control concern.

## Key Contributions

- Working bur taxonomy: cutting (carbide) vs abrading (diamond); steel → carbide → diamond range.
- Real selection drivers named: tradition, shape, procedure, substrate, sterilization, disposal.
- Diamond dominance (75%) over carbide (15%) in clinical preference.
- Documents CE decline (wear/debris/grit/duration) vs reuse-until-worn behavior (85%, only 33% aware).
- Flags infection-control gaps: variable sterilization, 100% non-segregated disposal.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional questionnaire: 20 closed-ended items (4 options each) across selection (6 Q) / usage (8 Q) / sterilization-disposal domains; mailed to 150 general practitioners in Visakhapatnam, India, with telephone follow-up; 131 responses (87%). Selection items probed grit/coarseness, color coding, and sequential tooth-reduction ordering. Results expressed as percentages.

## Results

- Bur preference: diamond 75%, tungsten carbide 15%.
- Reuse until worn out: 85%; agreed CE decreases with use: 33%.
- Sterilization: once daily 35%, every patient 35%.
- Disposal: dustbin 100%.
- Color coding: green/black super-coarse → yellow fine; sequential coding favored for gross reduction.
- CE physics: over short cuts medium/coarse/super-coarse cut comparably; efficiency falls with prolonged cutting.
