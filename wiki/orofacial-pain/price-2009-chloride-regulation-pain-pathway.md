## One-line Summary

Narrative review: cation-chloride cotransporters (NKCC1, KCC2) set the anion equilibrium potential that determines the strength and polarity of GABA-A/glycine inhibition; their dysregulation drives disinhibition-based hyperalgesia and allodynia after inflammation or nerve injury.

## 한줄요약

내러티브 리뷰 — NKCC1·KCC2가 결정하는 Cl⁻ 평형전위가 GABA-A·glycine 억제의 세기·방향을 좌우하며, 이 조절이 깨지면 염증·신경손상 후 탈억제성 통각과민·이질통이 발생한다.

## Summary

This review modernizes Melzack and Wall's Gate Control Theory by showing that spinal inhibition is regulated not only by transmitter release and receptor number, but by the transmembrane chloride gradient itself. Because GABA-A and glycine receptors are ligand-gated anion channels, their inhibitory action depends entirely on the anion (chloride) equilibrium potential. The cation-chloride cotransporters NKCC1 (chloride-accumulating) and KCC2 (chloride-extruding) set that potential. When KCC2 is downregulated or NKCC1 upregulated after peripheral inflammation or nerve injury, intracellular chloride rises, GABA-A/glycine signaling weakens or even reverses to excitatory, and the resulting disinhibition produces hyperalgesia and allodynia. The same disinhibition logic underlies other CNS disorders such as epilepsy. The authors frame therapeutic opportunities around restoring the chloride gradient or pharmacologically targeting GABA receptors.

## Key Contributions

- Recasts "dynamic spinal inhibition" of the Gate Control Theory in molecular terms centered on the chloride gradient.
- Identifies NKCC1/KCC2 cotransporters as the master control point of inhibitory tone via the anion equilibrium potential.
- Links KCC2 downregulation (or NKCC1 upregulation) to disinhibition-driven chronic pain, paralleling epilepsy mechanisms.
- Frames chloride-gradient modulation and GABA-receptor targeting as analgesic strategies.

## Methodology

Narrative mechanistic review synthesizing electrophysiology, molecular biology, and behavioral pain pharmacology. Covers presynaptic and postsynaptic inhibition in the superficial dorsal horn, primary afferent depolarization, and chloride homeostasis at the first central synapse. No quantitative pooling.

## Results

Qualitative. Pharmacologic block or genetic reduction of spinal inhibition reproduces hyperalgesia/allodynia, and enhancing inhibition reduces them — as Gate Control Theory predicts. Chloride cotransporter dysregulation is identified as a key pathophysiologic substrate of disinhibition in chronic pain.

## Clinical Relevance (orofacial / BMS link)

The chloride/GABA-A disinhibition mechanism is the molecular rationale for topical clonazepam (a GABA-A positive modulator) responsiveness in the peripheral subtype of burning mouth syndrome. It connects a basic-science inhibition framework to a concrete orofacial-pain therapeutic.
