## One-line Summary

Rat in vivo (thermal nociception) + in vitro electrophysiology study showing locally injected 5α-reduced neurosteroids produce potent, dose-dependent, enantioselective peripheral analgesia primarily through T-type Ca²⁺ channel blockade, with GABA-A potentiation amplifying but not independently driving the effect.

## 한줄요약

동물(rat) in vivo 열통각 + in vitro 전기생리 실험 — 국소 주사 5α-환원 신경스테로이드가 주로 T-type Ca²⁺ 통로 차단으로 강력한 말초 진통을 내고, GABA-A 강화는 단독 진통은 못 내나 T-type 차단 진통을 증폭한다.

## Summary

Neurosteroids are known central blockers of low-voltage-activated (T-type) Ca²⁺ channels and potentiators of GABA-A receptors, but their peripheral analgesic action had not been studied. Using locally injected steroids in adult rats (radiant-heat thermal nociception) and voltage-clamp recordings in vitro, the authors dissected the dual mechanism with mechanism-selective synthetic analogues. Alphaxalone (ALPX), allopregnanolone (3α5αP), and ACN produced potent, dose-dependent, enantioselective anti-nociception and modulated both T-type Ca²⁺ and GABA-A currents. Critically, an analogue with T-type but no GABA-A activity (ECN) was still analgesic, whereas one with GABA-A but no T-type activity (CDNC24) was not analgesic alone. Combining sub-threshold CDNC24 with ECN markedly enhanced analgesia in a bicuculline-sensitive manner. The conclusion is that peripheral analgesia from 5α-reduced neurosteroids is driven primarily by T-type Ca²⁺ channel blockade, while GABA-A channels do not carry baseline pain transmission but can enhance T-type–mediated anti-nociception.

## Key Contributions

- First demonstration of peripheral (not just central) analgesia by 5α-reduced neuroactive steroids.
- Mechanistic dissection establishing T-type Ca²⁺ channel blockade as the primary peripheral analgesic mechanism.
- Shows GABA-A channels modulate but do not independently mediate peripheral analgesia (no analgesia from GABA-A–only compound; bicuculline only partially reverses ALPX).
- Demonstrates synergy: sub-threshold GABA-A potentiation amplifies T-type–blockade analgesia.

## Methodology

In vivo: adult rats, radiant-heat paw-withdrawal latency after local injection of ALPX, 3α5αP, ACN, and selective analogues ECN (T-type only) and CDNC24 (GABA-A only); bicuculline co-injection to probe GABA-A contribution. In vitro: voltage-clamp recording of T-type Ca²⁺ and GABA-A currents. Enantioselectivity tested to confirm target specificity.

## Results

ALPX/3α5αP/ACN: potent, dose-dependent, enantioselective anti-nociception + modulation of both currents. ALPX analgesia only incompletely antagonized by bicuculline. ECN (T-type only) analgesic but weaker; CDNC24 (GABA-A only) not analgesic alone, but ineffective-dose CDNC24 + ECN → greatly enhanced, bicuculline-sensitive analgesia.

## Clinical Relevance (orofacial / BMS link)

T-type Ca²⁺ channels (CaV3.2) are implicated in trigeminal/facial pain, and neurosteroid decline is part of the proposed BMS pathophysiology in postmenopausal women. This paper provides the molecular plausibility linking falling endogenous neurosteroids to loss of peripheral anti-nociceptive tone.
