## One-line Summary

Retrospective clinical agreement study (n=35 orthodontic patients, 840 teeth) finding poor agreement (Cohen kappa 0.07–0.20) between an iTero Element 2 occlusogram and 8 µm AccuFilm carbon-paper occlusal records — the two methods are complementary, not interchangeable, and IOS alone cannot define occlusion.

## Summary

This Brazilian retrospective study (Orthod Craniofac Res 2026) asked a practical digital-workflow question: does the occlusal-contact map produced by an intraoral scanner (iTero Element 2) agree with the clinical reference of carbon paper? Using 35 Class I orthodontic patients and treating each of 840 teeth as an independent sampling unit, the authors compared an iTero occlusogram against an 8 µm AccuFilm carbon-paper occlusogram, surface by surface (16 defined surfaces from incisors to first molars), and quantified concordance with Cohen kappa.

The answer is **poor agreement**: most tooth surfaces fell in the 0.07–0.20 kappa range (Landis & Koch "poor/none"), with only the premolar lingual cusp (κ 0.38) and first-molar mesiolingual cusp (κ 0.27) reaching "slight." The scanner's red+orange definition *overestimated* contacts (~70% more than carbon), while red-only *underestimated* them — suggesting the orange band (0.1–0.2 mm proximity) is often a *lack* of contact, not a true contact. Both methods, however, agreed that contacts concentrate in the posterior region, consistent with a stable occlusion. The clinical recommendation is to combine IOS with carbon paper rather than rely on the scan alone, and to be wary of treating ClinCheck "red" contacts as excessive — 48.1% of intense red contacts were clinically ideal, so removing them in simulation risks aligner "overcorrection" and posterior open bite via unnecessary intrusion.

## Key Contributions

- **Tooth-surface-level IOS-vs-carbon concordance**: kappa across 16 surfaces, 840 teeth, paired within patient.
- **Quantified the direction of error**: iTero red+orange ≈ +70% contacts vs carbon; red-only fewer than carbon.
- **Reframed the "red is too much" belief**: 48.1% of intense (red) contacts were clinically ideal → caution against ClinCheck overcorrection.
- **Accuracy gap stated numerically**: articulating paper ~8–10 µm (matches patient perception threshold) vs iTero red minimum ~100 µm → up to ~10× lower accuracy.

## Methodology

- **Design**: retrospective, single-center (Unopar, Brazil), paired within-patient method-agreement study; CEP 3.384.099.
- **Sample**: 35 patients (23 M / 12 F), 15–30 y, Class I malocclusion, moderate crowding 3–5 mm, no extractions; excluded prior open bite/crossbite/orthodontics or missing permanent teeth. 840 teeth = sampling units.
- **Recording**: seated at 45°, maximum habitual intercuspation at maximum intensity, occlusal surfaces air-dried; carbon (8 µm AccuFilm, 1 operator) first, then iTero Element 2 full-arch (2 trained operators). Both mapped to occlusograms.
- **IOS contact definition**: colour scale red (0.0–0.1 mm) → orange (0.1–0.2 mm) → … → dark blue; only red and orange counted as contact; analyzed as red+orange, red-only, orange-only.
- **Statistics**: SPSS v28; % agreement/disagreement + Cohen kappa (Landis & Koch). Mean κ 0.14; true κ ~0.02–0.26 (95% CI).

## Results

- **Total contacts**: carbon 917; scan 1548 (red 745 = 48.1%; orange 803 = 51.9%).
- **Scan total / carbon by tooth**: incisors 350/159; canines 157/133; premolars 597/365; first molar 444/260. Most contacts posterior.
- **Red prevalence** higher anteriorly (incisors 60.3%, canines 54.8%, premolars 52.1%); **orange predominates in molars (69.1%)**.
- **Kappa (red+orange = contact)**: most surfaces 0.07–0.20 (poor). Best premolar LC 0.38, molar MLC 0.27 (slight). Worst premolar CG 0.04, canine MLS 0.06.
- **Kappa (red only = contact)**: same poor trend; best premolar LC 0.26, incisor DS 0.21, premolar BC 0.21; premolar CG κ = −0.03 (below chance).
- **Bottom line**: IOS red+orange overestimates and red-only underestimates carbon contacts; establishing occlusion from iTero data alone is not recommended — pair both methods.
