## One-line Summary

Retrospective clinical study (n=91 patients with partially or completely unopposed posterior teeth): partial occlusal contact did NOT reduce the extent of overeruption versus complete lack of contact, and partially-opposed teeth tipped MORE — partial contact should not be relied on to maintain vertical tooth position.

## Summary

The need to replace missing posterior teeth is rising as the population stays at least partially dentate for life, and positional drift of adjacent/opposing teeth must be considered before prosthetic replacement. This study asks a clinically practical question: when a posterior tooth has lost *full* opposing contact but still touches its antagonist over part of its surface (partial occlusal contact), does that residual contact protect it from overeruption?

In 91 patients, the authors compared completely unopposed posterior teeth against partially-opposed posterior teeth, measuring both the extent of vertical overeruption and the degree of tipping. The headline result is negative: there was **no significant difference in the extent of overeruption** between the partially-opposed and unopposed groups, and partial contact showed **no significant correlation** with overeruption extent. Worse, partially-opposed teeth actually **tipped more**, with a significant correlation between degree of tip and the presence of partial contact. The clinical message is that partial tooth contact cannot be relied upon to maintain vertical tooth position — its presence does not justify a wait-and-watch approach in the expectation of stability.

## Key Contributions

- Directly tests whether *partial* occlusal contact (not just total antagonist loss) modulates posterior overeruption in human patients.
- Dissociates the two movements an under-opposed tooth makes — vertical overeruption vs tipping — and analyses each against partial contact.
- Overturns the intuitive assumption that "some contact = stability": partial contact neither reduces overeruption nor stabilises the tooth, and is associated with *more* tipping.
- Supports proactive management/replacement of opposing teeth rather than relying on residual contact for vertical control.

## Methodology

- **Design**: retrospective clinical observational study (cross-sectional measurement of tooth position).
- **Sample**: 91 patients with posterior teeth that were either completely unopposed or partially opposed.
- **Groups**: unopposed (no occlusal contact) vs partially opposed (residual partial occlusal contact).
- **Outcomes per tooth**: extent of overeruption; degree of tipping.
- **Analyses**: between-group difference in overeruption and tipping; correlation of partial-contact presence with overeruption extent and with tipping degree; correlation between overeruption extent and tipping degree.

## Results

- **No significant difference** in extent of overeruption between unopposed and partially-opposed groups.
- Partially-opposed teeth showed a **greater degree of tipping** than the unopposed group.
- **No significant correlation** between extent of overeruption and degree of tipping.
- **No significant correlation** between extent of overeruption and presence of partial tooth contact.
- **Significant correlation** between degree of tooth tip and presence of partial tooth contact.

**Conclusions:**
1. Partial tooth contact does not prevent or reduce overeruption.
2. Partially-opposed teeth tip more than completely unopposed teeth.
3. Partial tooth contact should not be relied on clinically to maintain vertical tooth position.
