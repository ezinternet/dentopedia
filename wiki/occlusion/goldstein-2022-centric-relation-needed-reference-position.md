## Summary

Goldstein responds to the "uprising to abolish" centric relation (CR) — most directly the position advanced by Zonnenberg (2021) — by defending CR as a *needed* reference position rather than a disposable one. The bottom line is deliberately balanced: CR is a **universally recognized term with a long history of clinical success**, and it remains a **reproducible** maxillomandibular reference position that can be replicated by numerous well-documented, all-intermaxillary recording techniques. There is **consensus** on using CR as the restorative position for the patient needing full-arch reconstruction, and **no substantive clinical research contradicts** that use.

Crucially, the paper does not dismiss the critics. It concedes the legitimate grievances head-on: there is **little consensus on CR's definition or on the method of recording it**, and that ambiguity is exactly what fuels the abolition movement. The author's resolution is terminological discipline rather than abandonment — **if a clinician decides to use a different position, it should be given a different name**, so the literature does not keep redefining one term to mean different things. The review also concedes that there is **little scientific evidence dictating the exact condyle-in-fossa position**, deflating the historical debate over a single "ideal" condylar seating.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes the CR debate: the problem is **definitional/recording inconsistency**, not the concept of CR itself.
- Defends CR as **reproducible** and clinically validated for **diagnostic and full-arch restorative** use, citing a long track record and the absence of contradicting clinical research.
- Establishes a normative rule for terminology hygiene: **a different jaw position deserves a different name** — do not redefine CR.
- Acknowledges weak evidence for any exact condyle-in-fossa position, separating "use CR as a reference" from "the condyle must sit in one ideal spot."
- Serves as the **counterpoint** to the abandon-CR argument within the CR/CO/MICP terminology debate.

## Methodology

Narrative (non-systematic) review of literature on CR's definition, method of recording, anatomic considerations, and long-standing prosthodontic principles. No systematic search, pooling, or quantitative synthesis. Single-author advocacy review. (Abstract-only ingestion — full text not retrieved; detailed reasoning and cited evidence not captured.)

## Results

No quantitative outcomes (narrative review). Qualitative conclusions:

- CR is an established, reproducible reference position supported by substantial scientific evidence for diagnostic/restorative use.
- Consensus supports CR as the restorative position for full-arch reconstruction; no substantive clinical research contradicts it.
- Valid concerns remain over CR's definition and recording method.
- Insufficient evidence to specify the exact condylar position within the fossa.
- Recommendation: reserve "centric relation" for the defined position; name alternative positions differently.
