Cross-sectional human study (53 individuals, 84 molars unopposed ≥10 years): only 24% showed moderate-to-severe overeruption (≥2 mm) while 18% showed none — not all unopposed molars overerupt, even long-term.

## Summary

This study challenges the long-standing clinical belief that every permanent tooth without an antagonist will overerupt and eventually cause problems. Examining 84 molars that had been unopposed for at least 10 years in 53 individuals, the authors found that overeruption is common but far from universal: 18% showed no sign of overeruption, 58% only slight (<2 mm), and just 24% moderate-to-severe (≥2 mm) overeruption. Risk was lower when the antagonist was lost in adult age, and adjacent teeth governed mesiodistal/buccolingual position. The bottom line — **not all unopposed molars overerupt, even over the long term** — makes this a foundational reference for the "non-eruptor" qualifier in unopposed-tooth decision-making.

## Key Contributions

- Provides large-sample human evidence that **overeruption is not inevitable** for unopposed molars, refuting the universal-overeruption belief.
- Supplies the **~18% no-overeruption / 24% moderate-severe** distribution used as the non-eruptor figure in synthesis pages.
- Flags **age at antagonist loss** (adult loss → lower risk) as a clinically relevant modifier.
- Documents arch-specific malposition: **rotation more frequent in maxilla, tipping more common in mandible**, and the role of adjacent teeth in horizontal positioning.

## Methodology

- **Design**: cross-sectional clinical examination plus dental-cast evaluation.
- **Sample**: 53 individuals; 84 molars (61 maxillary, 23 mandibular) unopposed ≥10 years; 25 molars lacked both an antagonist and a mesially adjacent tooth.
- **Vertical grading (qualitative, 3 categories)**: (1) no overeruption, (2) slight <2 mm, (3) moderate-to-severe ≥2 mm. Mesiodistal/buccolingual position, rotation, and tipping also assessed.

## Results

| Vertical category | Teeth | % |
|---|---|---|
| No sign of overeruption | 15 | 18% |
| Slight overeruption (< 2 mm) | 49 | 58% |
| Moderate to severe overeruption (≥ 2 mm) | 20 | 24% |

- Antagonist loss **in adult age** → lower overeruption risk.
- **Adjacent teeth** important for mesiodistal/buccolingual position of the unopposed molar.
- **Rotation** more frequent in the maxilla; **tipping** more common in the mandible.
- **Conclusion**: not all molars without antagonists overerupt, not even long-term.
