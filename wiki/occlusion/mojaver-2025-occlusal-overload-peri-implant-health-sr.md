## One-line Summary
Narrative SR (160→80 studies, no meta-analysis) reporting occlusal overload/trauma associates with marginal bone loss (0.65–3.0 mm) and peri-implantitis incidence (20–50%), via a mechanical-overload × biofilm-inflammation dual pathway.

## Summary
University of Pennsylvania periodontics/prosthodontics group SR (Journal of Prosthodontics 2025). Of 160 references, 80 studies (clinical, experimental, FEA, prior SR) were synthesized **narratively** — no meta-analysis. The review concludes that occlusal trauma and excessive loading **do** associate with peri-implant marginal bone loss and peri-implantitis, but frames this as an association within a synergistic biomechanical-biological model rather than proven independent causation. Because implants lack a periodontal ligament (no force damping, no mechanoreceptor feedback), they are presented as inherently more vulnerable to overload than natural teeth.

A key qualifier runs through the data: overload effects appear **amplified when peri-implant inflammation is already present** (e.g., Jafari, Naert: marked loss in inflamed sites), and at least one animal study (Mattheos, 15 dogs) showed loss of osseointegration *without* marginal bone loss — i.e., overload can manifest differently depending on the biological context.

## Key Contributions
- **Overload → peri-implant bone loss association is supported** across 80 studies: occlusal-factor MBL ~0.65–1.20 mm (Table 1); traumatic-force bone level change 1.0–3.0 mm with peri-implantitis 20–50% (Table 2).
- **Dual-pathway model**: mechanical overload synergizes with bacterial biofilm-induced inflammation to accelerate breakdown (refines the purely microbial etiology framework for peri-implantitis).
- **Occlusal-scheme effect**: canine guidance and lingualized occlusion associated with less crestal bone loss than group function / monoplane.
- **Parafunction (bruxism)** flagged as a major modulator amplifying biomechanical stress.

## Methodology
- PICO: adults ≥18y with implants; occlusal trauma/increased loading vs normal forces; outcome = peri-implantitis / MBL / peri-implant pathology.
- PRISMA; PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane. (Date range internally inconsistent: abstract 2000–Oct 2023 vs criteria text 2010–Jan 2025.)
- RoB: Cochrane RoB, Newcastle-Ottawa, ROBVIS RoB 2.0; overall low–moderate.
- Narrative synthesis only; no meta-analysis; Egger's test mentioned but not included.

## Results
- 160 screened → 80 included.
- Table 1 examples: Kumari (lingualized) 0.65 mm/6y; Bahaa (canine guidance < group function) 0.85 mm; overload case reports 1.50 mm; Rismanchian — no significant difference early vs standard loading.
- Table 2 examples: Naert SR 1.5–3.0 mm / 30% (inflamed sites); Garaicoa-Pazmiño SR 1.5 mm / 32% (overload = risk factor); Xu (rats) 50% with periodontal disease; Mattheos (dogs) osseointegration loss *without* MBL; Koller cemented > screw-retained.

### Caveats (evidence weight)
- Despite n=80, evidence is weakened by design heterogeneity, inconsistent "overload" definitions, many small experimental/FEA/case-report entries, short follow-up (≤3y), surrogate radiographic outcomes, and likely positive-association publication bias. Associations, not causal proof.
