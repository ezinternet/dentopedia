## One-line Summary

Best Evidence Consensus Statement (J Prosthodont 2021; >15,000 articles screened → 313 SR/RCT/MA/clinical-trial articles analyzed): centric occlusion (condyle-seated) and maximal intercuspal position are usually NON-coincident in dentate and partially dentate patients, and complete mouth rehabilitation should be restored in centric occlusion.

## Summary

This Best Evidence Consensus Statement addresses two clinical focus questions about treatment position for complete mouth rehabilitation: (1) how often does centric occlusion coincide with the maximal intercuspal position in dentate and partially dentate patients, and (2) which position should be used when rehabilitating these patients. In this statement "centric occlusion (CO)" is the condyle-seated occlusal contact position (equivalent to occlusion in centric relation), distinct from the maximal intercuspal position (MIP/MICP), the position of maximum cusp interdigitation.

The literature review (abstract-only — full text not retrieved) screened more than 15,000 articles, restricting to systematic reviews, RCTs, meta-analyses, and clinical trials, leaving 313 articles for analysis. The bottom line: in most dentate and partially dentate patients CO and MIP do NOT coincide — the discrepancy is the norm. For complete mouth rehabilitation, the statement supports restoring so that CO and MIP coincide, anchored to centric occlusion. In a healthy population there is no conclusive evidence of adverse prosthodontic outcomes restoring in either position; however, CO–MIP discrepancy is associated with occlusal instability and temporomandibular joint disorders. The concluding recommendation is that partially and completely dentate patients requiring complete mouth rehabilitation be restored in centric occlusion.

## Key Contributions

- Documents that CO–MIP non-coincidence is the rule, not the exception, in dentate and partially dentate populations.
- Provides an evidence-based recommendation to restore complete mouth rehabilitation in centric occlusion (condyle-seated position).
- Clarifies that, in healthy patients, neither CO nor MIP shows conclusive evidence of adverse prosthodontic outcomes — but the CO–MIP discrepancy itself is linked to occlusal instability and TMD.

## Methodology

- Two focus questions on CO/MIP coincidence and on the treatment position for complete mouth rehabilitation.
- Search terms: intercuspal position, centric occlusion, centric relation, maximal intercuspal position, prosthodontic rehabilitation, occlusion.
- Initial yield >15,000 articles → restricted to SRs, RCTs, meta-analyses, and clinical trials → 313 articles for further analysis.
- abstract-only — full text not retrieved: per-article appraisal detail and the consensus grading method are not available.

## Results

- CO–MIP coincidence: most dentate and partially dentate patients do NOT have coincident CO and MIP.
- Preferred rehabilitation relationship: support for CO–MIP coincidence (anchored to centric occlusion) as the preferred occlusal relationship in complete mouth rehabilitations.
- Safety: no conclusive evidence of adverse prosthodontic outcomes restoring in either CO or MIP in a healthy population.
- Discrepancy harm signal: CO–MIP discrepancies are associated with occlusal instability and TMD.
- Conclusion: restore partially and completely dentate complete-mouth-rehabilitation patients in centric occlusion.
- abstract-only — full text not retrieved: no effect sizes, prevalence %, or discrepancy magnitudes beyond the >15,000 / 313 article counts.
