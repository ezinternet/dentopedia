## One-line Summary

Prospective pilot (n=32 restorations, 30 patients, 6 months) using T-Scan III showed single posterior implant prosthesis bite force doubles from 2 weeks to 3 months post-placement then stabilizes, while overall masticatory ability improves.

## Summary

This prospective pilot study measured occlusal force distribution at 5 time points (pre-delivery, immediately, 2 weeks, 3 months, 6 months) using T-Scan III in 30 patients receiving 32 single posterior implant-supported crowns. The study addresses a key clinical uncertainty: although light-contact protocol is established for implant occlusion, whether implant prosthesis bite force remains stable over time was unknown.

Key finding: implant prosthesis bite force was intentionally lighter than adjacent teeth at delivery (light-contact protocol), but increased approximately 2-fold from 2 weeks to 3 months (3.39% → 6.90%, P=.008), then stabilized through 6 months (7.31%, P=.900). Despite this, implant bite force remained lower than distal adjacent teeth at 6 months (P=.000). Posterior segment bite force significantly improved (+3.31% unilateral, +6.83% bilateral), indicating restored masticatory function. Patients with bilateral defects showed a concerning trend: increased inter-arch bite force discrepancy over time.

The study highlights that routine occlusal follow-up is essential because the light-contact standard at delivery does not prevent progressive occlusal force increase — confirming the 3-month check as a clinically critical visit.

## Key Contributions

- First prospective longitudinal dataset with 5-point T-Scan III measurement of occlusal force evolution after single posterior implant crown delivery
- Quantified timing of occlusal force stabilization: significant increase from 2 weeks → 3 months, no significant change from 3 months → 6 months
- Demonstrated posterior segment masticatory improvement for both unilateral and bilateral missing tooth scenarios
- Identified bilateral defect patients as higher risk for occlusal asymmetry progression despite restored posterior support
- Provided evidence-base for 3-month follow-up as the critical occlusal reassessment checkpoint

## Methodology

- **Design**: Prospective observational pilot, 5-phase measurement
- **Sample**: 30 patients (18F, 12M; age 27–75 years), 32 single posterior implant restorations; unilateral (n=17), bilateral (n=15)
- **Instrument**: T-Scan Sensor III (Tekscan); measurement at MIP, 3 recordings averaged
- **Measurement stages**: Pre-delivery → immediately → 2 weeks (final torque) → 3 months → 6 months
- **Occlusal adjustment performed** at pre-delivery and 2-week visits; not performed before 3- and 6-month measurements
- **Clinical standard**: Light contact at heavy bite; no contact at light bite; shimstock foil (8 µm) passes more easily than adjacent teeth
- **Statistics**: Wilcoxon signed-rank, Mann–Whitney U, Kruskal–Wallis, Spearman correlation (SPSSAU v20.0)
- **Exclusions**: Bruxism, severe TMD, anterior open bite, CNS medications, systemic disease

## Results

| Time point | Implant prosthesis bite force (%) | vs. adjacent |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-delivery baseline | Reference (light) | Significantly lower than controls (P=.000) |
| Immediately post | — | Lower than mesial and distal teeth |
| 2 weeks | 3.39 ± 2.61% | Lower than mesial and distal teeth |
| 3 months | 6.90 ± 4.77% | Not different from mesial (P=NS); lower than distal |
| 6 months | 7.31 ± 4.60% | Significantly lower than distal (P=.000) |

- **2-week → 3-month change**: Mean ×2.04 increase (P=.008)
- **3-month → 6-month change**: No significant difference (P=.900)
- **Posterior segment improvement**: Unilateral +3.31% (P=.013); bilateral +6.83% (P=.001)
- **Bilateral defect discrepancy**: Increased from 3.52% → 5.02% (P=.039); Level III bilateral deviation proportion increased 15.38% (P>.05)
- Sex, implant dimensions, arch location, crown material, opposing tooth type — no significant effect on bite force changes
