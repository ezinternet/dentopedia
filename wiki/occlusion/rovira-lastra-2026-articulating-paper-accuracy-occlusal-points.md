## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional test–retest validity study (n=32 natural dentitions, scanned silicone registration as reference standard): articulating paper detected 81% of posterior occlusal contact points (true-positive) with a 15% false-positive rate; 200 µm paper produced fewer false positives (9.4%) than 100 µm (20.6%), the mandibular arch outperformed the maxillary, and the pulling technique conferred no benefit.

## Summary

This study quantified how accurately conventional articulating paper locates occlusal contact *points* in healthy natural dentitions, using a scanned polyvinyl-siloxane (Occlufast Rock) registration thresholded to 100 µm as the reference standard. Across 32 adults and four registration conditions (100 vs 200 µm paper × passive "hold" vs active "pull" technique, per hemiarch, repeated at 2 weeks), articulating paper achieved 81% true-positive detection and a 15% false-positive rate. The two clinically actionable levers were paper thickness and the dual-arch coincidence criterion: 200 µm paper halved false positives versus 100 µm (9.4% vs 20.6%), and counting a contact only when blue marks coincided on both arches collapsed false positives to 2.0%. Two-thirds of false positives were marks on cusp tips, attributed to incidental rubbing. The "pulling" technique made no difference, and the mandibular arch yielded ~5% more true positives than the maxillary.

## Key Contributions

- Validity benchmark for articulating paper at the contact-*point* level (not contact area): 81% TP, 15% FP against a high-fidelity silicone reference (reference ICC 0.976–0.999).
- Counter-intuitive thickness finding: thicker 200 µm paper is *more* accurate (fewer false positives) than 100 µm — fewer cusp-tip artifacts (0.61 vs 1.75/hemiarch).
- Dual-arch coincidence rule drops false positives from 15% → 2.0%, a simple chairside accuracy gain.
- Cusp tips identified as the dominant false-positive site (~66% of FPs); a cusp-tip mark without a matching antagonist mark should be treated as artifact during adjustment.
- The "pulling" forceps technique offers no measurable benefit; mandibular-arch inspection is both more accurate and easier.

## Methodology

- Design: cross-sectional criterion-validity study with test–retest (2-week) and inter-rater reliability; STROBE-reported.
- Participants: 32 adults (25 F / 7 M), mean age 24.5 y, ≥24 natural teeth, no extensive restorations/prostheses/malocclusion/orofacial pain (3 of 35 excluded for image quality).
- Reference standard: 1-min max-force Occlufast Rock occlusal registration, flatbed-scanned, thresholded to 100 µm interocclusal contact.
- Conditions: Bausch 100 µm vs 200 µm blue paper; passive vs active (pull) technique; per hemiarch; marks captured with TRIOS 3 IOS.
- Analysis: FIJI/ImageJ — CIELab grayscale conversion, TurboReg alignment to silicone mask, contact point ≥100 px² (~0.5 mm); TP = overlap with reference, FP = no overlap; cusp-tip FPs classified separately. Three GLM with repeated measures (session, thickness, pulling, arch); ICC; α=0.05; powered for sensitivity 70%.

## Results

- True positives: 81% overall; mandibular > maxillary by ~5% (p<0.001). Thickness, session, technique: no effect on TP%.
- False positives: 200 µm 9.4% vs 100 µm 20.6% (p<0.001); ~15% per single arch → **2.0%** with the both-arch coincidence criterion.
- Cusp-tip false positives: 65.9% (95%CI 59.6–72.3) of all FPs; 1.2/hemiarch overall; 200 µm 0.61 vs 100 µm 1.75/hemiarch (p<0.001).
- Reliability (ICC): silicone reference inter-rater 0.999 / test–retest 0.976; articulating paper inter-rater 0.56–0.88 / test–retest 0.54–0.82; TP%/FP%/cusp-tip-FP% test–retest 0.571 / 0.615 / 0.247.
- Clinical recommendations: prefer 200 µm over 100 µm paper; confirm cusp-tip contacts on both arches; do not rely on the pulling technique.
