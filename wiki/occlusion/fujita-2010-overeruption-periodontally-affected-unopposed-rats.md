## One-line Summary

Animal study (60 adult male rats, 2×2 design, 4 wk, micro-CT) showing loss of antagonist causes unopposed-molar overeruption that becomes significantly *larger* under experimentally induced periodontitis, with the lingual alveolar bone level lowered most.

## 한줄요약

## Summary

In clinical practice, periodontally compromised teeth sometimes show pronounced overeruption, but whether periodontitis itself modulates the supraposition of an *unopposed* tooth was unclear. This controlled rat experiment dissociates the two drivers — loss of antagonist and periodontitis — in a 2×2 design and measures outcomes by micro-computed tomography (micro-CT).

The result is a clean dissociation: periodontitis on an **opposed** (antagonist-present) molar did **not** cause extrusion (though it did lower the alveolar bone level, confirming real periodontal breakdown). Removing the antagonist **did** cause overeruption. Combining both — an **unopposed molar with periodontitis** — produced **significantly more** extrusion than a healthy unopposed molar, and the **lingual alveolar bone level** dropped below that of every other group. The bottom line: loss of antagonist drives overeruption, and a compromised periodontium amplifies it.

For the unopposed-tooth overeruption synthesis, this paper anchors the mechanistic claim that overeruption is a **periodontal-ligament / alveolar-bone phenomenon** of the *supporting tissues* — not a pulp-dependent process. Whether a tooth is vital or endodontically treated is irrelevant to the active machinery here; what matters is the antagonist status and the health of the periodontium.

## Key Contributions

- Experimentally separates **loss of antagonist** from **periodontitis** as drivers of overeruption in a controlled 2×2 design (opposed/unopposed × healthy/periodontitis).
- Shows periodontitis alone does **not** extrude an opposed molar, but **amplifies** overeruption once the antagonist is removed.
- Localizes the accompanying periodontal change to the **lingual alveolar bone level**, which was lowest in the unopposed + periodontitis group.
- Provides the animal/mechanistic counterpart to clinical observations that compromised periodontal support accelerates supraeruption of unopposed teeth.

## Methodology

- **Animals**: 60 adult male rats, four equal groups (n = 15).
- **Periodontitis induction**: silk-thread **ligature around the cervix of the right mandibular molar** in two groups.
- **Unopposed-tooth simulation**: occlusal **grinding/reduction of the right maxillary molar crowns** in two groups (one with, one without induced periodontitis) to remove the antagonist.
- **2×2 groups**: opposed/healthy · opposed/periodontitis · unopposed/healthy · unopposed/periodontitis.
- **Duration**: 4 weeks.
- **Measurement**: **micro-CT** for molar vertical position and buccal/lingual alveolar bone levels.

## Results

- **Opposed molars**: no significant overeruption difference between periodontitis and healthy groups — periodontitis alone did not extrude.
- **Opposed + periodontitis**: alveolar bone level lower than healthy molars (ligature produced real periodontal breakdown).
- **Healthy unopposed molars**: extruded vs. molars with an antagonist; bone level **not** affected by the overeruption itself.
- **Unopposed + periodontitis**: **significantly larger extrusion** than healthy unopposed molars.
- **Lingual alveolar bone level** of unopposed periodontally affected molars was **lower than all other groups**.
- **Conclusion**: loss of antagonist causes overeruption, which is **more prominent in the presence of periodontitis**.
