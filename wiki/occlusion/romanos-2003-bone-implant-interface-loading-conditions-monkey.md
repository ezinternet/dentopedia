## One-line Summary

Animal study (9 Macaca fascicularis, 48 implants, 3-month loading) demonstrating that immediate and delayed functional loading both produce significantly higher BIC and bone area within threads than submerged non-loading, with no significant difference between the two loading protocols.

## Summary

This in vivo animal study compared histomorphometrically the bone-implant interface around titanium implants subjected to three different loading conditions in the Macaca fascicularis mandible: (A) unloaded submerged healing, (B) delayed functional loading after 3-month submerged healing, and (C) immediate functional loading at the time of implant placement. Nine monkeys received 48 progressive-thread titanium implants (8 mm × 3.5 mm) in healed mandibular ridges (3 months post-extraction). Groups B and C were loaded simultaneously — one side immediately, the other after 3-month submerged healing — using resin bridges upgraded to metal bridges at 1 month, for a total 3-month loading period. All implants osseointegrated. Loaded implants (B and C) showed compact cortical bone with thick trabeculae at the interface, whereas unloaded implants (A) showed cancellous bone with loose connective tissue. BIC and bone area (BA) within threads and around apices were significantly greater in both loaded groups versus unloaded. No significant difference existed between immediate and delayed loading. The authors conclude that functional occlusal loading stimulates bone formation and may be a key positive factor for osseointegration, and that immediate loading with progressive thread design implants achieves equivalent osseointegration to delayed loading.

## Key Contributions

1. **Loading stimulates bone formation:** Functional loading (immediate or delayed) significantly increased BIC and BA versus submerged non-loading — direct histomorphometric evidence that mechanical stimulation drives bone apposition at the interface.
2. **Immediate = delayed loading outcomes:** No significant histomorphometric difference between Groups B and C, supporting the biological feasibility of immediate functional loading when primary stability is adequate.
3. **Qualitative histological distinction:** Loaded implants showed compact cortical bone and extensive trabeculae; unloaded implants showed loose connective tissue in cancellous interface — a qualitatively distinct healing environment.
4. **All implants integrated:** 100% osseointegration across groups (n=48), with no gap at the metal-bone interface under any loading condition tested.

## Methodology

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Model | Macaca fascicularis (crab-eating macaque), 9 adults |
| Implants | 48 titanium, 8 mm × 3.5 mm, progressive thread |
| Site | Posterior mandible (post-extraction healed ridge, 3 months) |
| Group A | 2 monkeys, 6 implants — submerged, no loading, sacrifice at 3 months |
| Group B | 7 monkeys, 21 implants — submerged 3 months, then delayed loading × 3 months |
| Group C | 7 monkeys, 21 implants — immediate loading at placement × 3 months (contralateral to B) |
| Loading protocol | Temporary resin bridges → metal bridges at 1 month; occlusion verified |
| Outcomes | BIC (%), BA in threads and at apices (histomorphometry) |

## Results

- **Osseointegration rate:** 100% (48/48); no metal-bone interface gap in any group.
- **Group A (unloaded):** Cancellous bone with loose connective tissue at interface; lower BIC and BA.
- **Groups B & C (loaded):** Compact cortical bone, thick trabeculae; significantly higher BIC and BA than Group A.
- **B vs. C:** No significant difference (P <0.05 threshold not exceeded); immediate loading produced equivalent histomorphometric outcomes to delayed loading.
- **Clinical implication:** Occlusal loading — not just healing time — appears to be a key determinant of interface bone quality.
