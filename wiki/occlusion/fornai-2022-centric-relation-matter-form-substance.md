## One-line Summary

Letter/commentary (rebuttal to Zonnenberg et al. 2021) arguing that the term "centric relation" should be abandoned but condylar position remains clinically meaningful and must be monitored whenever occlusion is altered, proposing "Reference Position (RP)" as the replacement concept.

## 한줄요약

Zonnenberg(2021)에 대한 반박 서신으로, "centric relation" 용어는 폐기하되 교합 변경 시 과두 위치를 모니터링해야 한다고 주장하며 대체 개념으로 "Reference Position (RP)"을 제안한다.

## Summary

Fornai and colleagues respond to Zonnenberg, Türp & Greene's 2021 review, which concluded that the mandibular condyle is correctly situated in most orthodontic patients and that orthodontists can therefore disregard condylar position during treatment, relying on the plastic (adaptive) properties of the masticatory supporting structures.

Fornai et al. share one premise with the target review — the centric-relation *term* is poorly defined and the recording methods are controversial — but reach the opposite clinical conclusion. Their central argument is conceptual: a **"biological" condition is not the same as a "clinically normal" one.** Biological variation occurs in nature and is phenotypically expressed, but it can also be pathological. Because modern food technology has relaxed selective pressure on the masticatory system, morphological variation and malocclusion are common — yet frequency does not equal functional normality. Treating a frequently observed condylar position as "normal" therefore conflates prevalence with health.

From this they derive the clinical claim: because the maximal intercuspal position (MIP) determines condylar position within the glenoid fossa, changing the occlusion (orthodontically or prosthodontically) is likely to alter the original condyle-to-fossa relation. Relying on adaptive remodelling to absorb that change is risky, since remodelling is reduced in joints with inflammation/degeneration and is more limited in adults than in children. They therefore argue the clinician should carefully **monitor condylar position and mandibular function whenever the occlusal relationship is changed**, to prevent iatrogenic effects.

Finally, they propose abandoning "centric relation" and replacing it with **"Reference Position (RP)"** (sensu Piehslinger/Celar/Slavicek): an unstrained physiologic retral border position obtained without exploiting the shock-absorbing retrodiscal tissues — joint structures unloaded, ligaments not in tension, minimal muscle activity — and reported to be highly reproducible. (They note Gerber's warning against forcing a retruded mandibular position because of iatrogenic risk.)

## Key Contributions

- **Same diagnosis, opposite prescription**: agrees the CR term should be abandoned (as Zonnenberg argues) but insists condylar position must be actively monitored — not ignored.
- **"Biological ≠ clinically normal"** framing: malocclusion is a common biological variation that can nevertheless be pathological; prevalence is not a proxy for function.
- Couples the argument to a measurable threshold (Pullinger ≈2 mm sagittal slide) and a consensus statistic (29% for the GPT-9 CR definition) that quantify both the asymptomatic envelope and the terminological disarray.
- Offers a concrete replacement construct — **Reference Position (RP)** — rather than only criticizing the existing term.

## Methodology

Argumentative letter/commentary; no new primary data. The authors marshal existing literature — Pullinger, Seligman & Gornbein (occlusal slide cut-off), Piehslinger/Celar/Slavicek (Reference Position), Gerber (iatrogenic risk of forced retrusion), and the GPT-9 consensus survey — to rebut the clinical recommendation of the target review.

## Results

- **Sagittal occlusal (CR–MIP) slide cut-off ≈ 2 mm** (Pullinger, Seligman & Gornbein): in their sample **none** of the asymptomatic subjects had a slide >2 mm, and only **6%** had a slide between 1 and 2 mm — so a slide >2 mm is atypical of asymptomatic individuals.
- The **GPT-9 centric-relation definition received only 29% expert consensus** — quantifying the lack of agreement both sides acknowledge.
- TMJ adaptive remodelling is **reduced** in joint inflammation/degeneration and **more limited in adults than children** — undercutting reliance on remodelling to absorb occlusal-treatment-induced condylar change.
- TMJ skeletal morphology is associated with disc derangement, and small occlusal-treatment-derived changes in condylar relationship can improve TMD symptoms — evidence the authors cite for the functional relevance of condylar position.
