## One-line Summary

Systematic review (n=20 low-quality observational studies; NOS mean 3.36): most individual studies link CR–ICP (centric) discrepancy to muscle pain and joint disorders, but heterogeneity + low quality preclude any definite causal association with TMD.

## 한줄요약

체계적 문헌고찰(저질 관찰연구 20편, NOS 평균 3.36): 대부분의 개별 연구는 중심위–최대교두감합위 불일치(CR–ICP discrepancy, centric slide)를 근육통·관절장애와 연관시키지만, 이질성과 낮은 질로 인해 측두하악장애(TMD)와의 확정적 인과 연관은 성립할 수 없다.

## Summary

This systematic review asked a long-debated clinical question: is the **CR–ICP discrepancy** (the "centric slide" between centric relation [CR] and the intercuspal/maximum intercuspation position [ICP/MIP]) associated with **temporomandibular disorders (TMDs)**? Searching nine databases (1960–2016, all languages), the authors identified 467 candidate articles and analyzed 20 analytical observational clinical studies, appraised with the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS) under a PICO framework.

The included evidence was **low quality** (NOS mean 3.36) and methodologically heterogeneous. While **most individual studies reported an association** between CR–ICP discrepancy and muscle (pain) and joint disorders (noise, disc displacement, pain, crepitus, osteoarthritis, osteoarthrosis), the results lacked consistency. The authors concluded that, because of the heterogeneity of design/methodology and the low quality of the articles, **it is not possible to establish an association between CR–ICP discrepancy and TMD**, and that longitudinal studies with validated diagnostic criteria are needed to test whether the discrepancy is a TMD risk factor.

This is the abstract-only record (full text not retrieved). The faithful bottom line: the often-cited "centric discrepancy causes TMD" claim is **not supported by high-quality evidence** — the signal exists in individual reports but does not survive critical appraisal.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic review to directly test the CR–ICP discrepancy ↔ TMD relationship.
- Quality-graded the observational literature (NOS mean 3.36 = low), exposing how weak the evidence base behind a commonly-cited clinical claim actually is.
- Reframes the discrepancy → TMD link as an unproven hypothesis requiring longitudinal, validated study, not an established cause.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (no meta-analysis) of analytical observational clinical studies.
- **Search**: 1960–2016, all languages, 9 databases (PubMed, Cochrane, Medline, Embase, Scopus, EBSCOhost, BIREME, Lilacs, Scielo).
- **Selection / analysis**: two independent reviewers; PICO format.
- **Quality appraisal**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS).
- **Yield**: 467 candidates → **20 included** (3 in orthodontic patients + 17 in subjects without intervention).

## Results

- **20 studies**, **NOS mean 3.36** (low quality).
- Most studies: CR–ICP discrepancy **associated with** muscle pain and joint disorders (noise, disc displacement, pain, crepitus, osteoarthritis, osteoarthrosis).
- However: inconsistent results across studies + heterogeneity + low quality → **no definite association can be established** between CR–ICP discrepancy and TMD.
- Authors call for validated diagnostic criteria and **longitudinal studies** to assess the discrepancy as a possible TMD risk factor.
