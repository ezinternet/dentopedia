## One-line Summary
Systematic review finding that, on low-level evidence, tooth splinting does NOT improve survival of mobile teeth in advanced periodontitis, while occlusal adjustment of mobile/prematurely-contacting teeth may improve clinical attachment level (other parameters unclear).

## Summary
Dommisch and colleagues asked whether two common mechanical interventions actually help periodontitis patients with mobile teeth. From 1515 publications only five met inclusion (2 retrospective on splinting, 1 RCT + 2 prospective on occlusal adjustment), so the evidence is thin. Tooth splinting showed essentially no survival benefit — 8.4% tooth loss with splinting vs 10.1% without over two years. Occlusal adjustment fared better on one outcome: an RCT showed clinical attachment level gain for adjusted teeth, though effects on tooth loss, pocket depth, and mobility were heterogeneous and unclear. The practical message is that splinting mobile teeth should not be expected to save them, whereas selective occlusal adjustment of teeth with mobility or premature contacts may have a modest attachment benefit.

## Key Contributions
- Separates splinting from occlusal adjustment on hard clinical outcomes.
- Quantifies the absence of a splinting survival benefit (8.4% vs 10.1% tooth loss).
- Identifies a possible CAL benefit of occlusal adjustment (RCT-level, single outcome).

## Methodology
Systematic review; 1515 records → 51 full-text → 5 included (2 retrospective TS case series, low risk of bias; 1 RCT + 2 prospective OA studies, unclear risk of bias). Outcomes TL, CAL, PPD, TM over ~2 years post non-surgical therapy. Low overall evidence level.

## Results
Splinting: 26/311 teeth lost with TS (8.4%) vs 156/1541 without (10.1%) — no survival benefit. Occlusal adjustment: RCT showed CAL gain vs no OA; effects on TL/PPD/TM heterogeneous. Evidence level low throughout.

## Clinical Application
- Do not splint mobile periodontitis teeth expecting improved survival — splinting is for patient comfort/function, not prognosis.
- Selective occlusal adjustment of mobile or prematurely-contacting teeth may aid attachment, but the broader benefit is unproven; reserve it, don't routinize it.
