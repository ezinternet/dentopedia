GRADE systematic review (7 included studies; posterior bounded edentulous spaces in adults) finding mesio-distal migration and overeruption after tooth loss are mostly limited (on average up to ~2 mm) on VERY LOW quality evidence — so prosthetic replacement should not be the default mainstay of therapy.

## Summary

The single posterior **bounded edentulous space (BES)** — an edentulous gap bounded by remaining natural teeth — is clinically debated: does a missing posterior tooth lead to arch collapse via drift of adjacent teeth and overeruption of unopposed antagonists, mandating prosthetic replacement (fixed bridge, implant crown)? Faggion et al. asked this as an explicit PICO question (P: adult patients; I: restorative treatment; C: non-treatment; O: overeruption and mesio-distal movement) and answered it with a **GRADE-graded systematic review**.

The finding: occlusal changes after posterior tooth loss are, for most cases, **limited — on average up to ~2 mm** — and the clinical relevance of changes of that magnitude is questionable. Severe changes affected only a small number of unopposed teeth, and subject age may modulate severity. Crucially, the **quality of evidence is VERY LOW** (no study directly compared treatment vs non-treatment; controls were typically fully dentate patients). The authors conclude that **tooth replacement should not necessarily be regarded as the mainstay of therapy** for posterior BES, and present a **decision-tree-like scheme** to guide clinicians under conditions of low-quality evidence. This is the evidentiary anchor for a "risk-stratify, don't reflex-restore" stance on the unopposed/adjacent-tooth question.

## Key Contributions

- First to apply the **GRADE** system to the narrow question of restoring posterior BES in adults, making evidence-quality and recommendation-strength judgments transparent.
- Quantifies expected occlusal change after posterior tooth loss as **limited (average up to ~2 mm)** in most cases.
- Rates the supporting evidence as **VERY LOW quality**.
- Concludes **tooth replacement is not necessarily the mainstay of therapy** for posterior BES.
- Provides a **decision-tree scheme** for treatment options usable when evidence is weak.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review using selected PRISMA/AMSTAR components; **GRADE** for quality of evidence and strength of recommendation.
- **Search (up to and including February 2010)**: PubMed and Cochrane CENTRAL (in triplicate), LILACS, Google Scholar (English, French, German, Greek, Italian, Portuguese, Spanish), grey literature via OpenSIGLE, plus manual reference-list searching.
- **Outcomes**: quantitative mesio-distal change (BES narrowing) and overeruption (extrusion of unopposed teeth).
- **Selection**: 803 papers screened → **7 studies included**. No study compared restorative treatment against non-treatment directly; control groups were generally fully dentate patients.

## Results

- Overeruption and mesio-distal narrowing of posterior BES were **limited**; average change **up to ~2 mm** in most cases, of questionable clinical relevance.
- **Severe** occlusal change occurred in only a small subset of unopposed teeth → other factors also drive BES change.
- **Subject age** may correlate with severity.
- **Quality of evidence: VERY LOW** (GRADE).
- **Recommendation**: prosthetic replacement should not be the default therapy; more robust studies on long-term non-treatment effects are needed.
