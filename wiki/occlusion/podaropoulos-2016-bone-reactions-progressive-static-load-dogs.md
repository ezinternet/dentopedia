## One-line Summary

Animal (dog, n = 3, 24 implants) histomorphometric study: progressive static orthodontic loading (100→200→300 g over 9 weeks) significantly increased BIC vs unloaded controls (P = 0.018) in low-density maxillary bone, without increasing crestal bone resorption or peri-implant bone density at 1–2 mm.

## Summary

This experimental study tested Misch's progressive bone loading (PBL) concept at the histological level using an in vivo beagle dog model. Bilateral edentulous maxillary ridges were created in 3 beagle dogs; after 8 weeks of healing, 24 Biomet 3i implants were placed. Half were subjected to escalating static horizontal forces via Ni-Ti orthodontic coil springs (100 g × 3 wk → 200 g × 3 wk → 300 g × 3 wk = 9 wks total); the other half remained unloaded. Undecalcified ground sections were assessed for BIC, bone density at 1 mm and 2 mm from threads, and crestal bone resorption.

The progressive loading group showed significantly higher BIC (P = 0.018). Peri-implant bone density and crestal resorption were identical between groups. The authors concluded that controlled progressive orthodontic force on osseointegrated implants increases BIC in low-density bone without provoking marginal bone loss — histological support for the clinical PBL protocol.

## Key Contributions

1. **First progressive-force animal model**: Unlike the Gotfredsen series (constant static load at a fixed magnitude), this study applied stepwise-increasing force — the first animal-model test of the PBL protocol specifically.
2. **BIC increase without bone loss**: Progressive loading improved osseointegration quality (BIC) while crestal bone resorption remained identical to controls — evidence that controlled progressive force is not harmful at physiologic magnitudes.
3. **Bone density not different**: Peri-implant bone density at 1 and 2 mm was unchanged — adaptive response was localized to the bone-implant interface, not a diffuse densification effect.
4. **Methodological model**: Ni-Ti coil springs between paired implants provide controlled, measurable static horizontal/tensile forces — a cleaner loading model than bite-force approaches.

## Methodology

- **Model**: 3 beagle dogs; bilateral edentulous maxillary ridges posterior to canines; 8 weeks ridge healing
- **Implants**: 24 Biomet 3i implants (12 per group); 8-week submerged healing before loading
- **Progressive loading** (n = 12): Ni-Ti orthodontic coil springs between abutment pairs; 100 g × 3 wk → 200 g × 3 wk → 300 g × 3 wk (9 weeks total)
- **Control** (n = 12): unloaded, undisturbed healing for equivalent period
- **Histology**: Donath & Breuner undecalcified ground-section technique
- **Outcomes**: BIC (%), bone density @1 mm, bone density @2 mm, crestal bone resorption (mm)
- **Statistics**: Median regression models (quantile regression; Parente & Santos Silva 2013)
- **Note**: PDF is a Wiley HTML print; full numerical tables/photomicrographs are in the paywalled body and were not extractable. All values are from the abstract.

## Results

| Outcome | Result | P-value |
|---|---|---|
| BIC | Significantly higher in progressive loading group | **0.018** |
| Bone density @1 mm from threads | No difference | 0.734 (NS) |
| Bone density @2 mm from threads | No difference | 0.961 (NS) |
| Crestal bone resorption | No difference | 0.813 (NS) |

- Exact BIC percentages not available from accessible PDF text; statistical superiority of progressive loading group confirmed.
- Low-density maxillary dog bone (type III–IV analog) benefited from progressive loading with improved BIC.
- The absence of crestal resorption difference suggests progressive loading at 100–300 g range does not reach the pathological strain threshold in this model.
