## One-line Summary

SR+MA (5 studies, n=184 patients, J Prosthodont 2025) found no statistically significant difference between digital (CAD/CAM) and conventional removable complete denture workflows in laboratory costs (MD -239.77 USD, p=0.106), clinical costs (MD 74.39 USD, p=0.451), total costs (MD -357.76 USD, p=0.258), or number of sessions (MD -1.47, p=0.351); operator experience significantly affected clinical costs and session count.

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42024607329) compared digitally designed/manufactured removable complete dentures (RCDs, CAD/CAM milling or 3D printing) with conventionally fabricated RCDs (compression molding) in terms of cost-efficiency. Five studies (four retrospective, one prospective; 184 patients, 202 RCDs; 2019–2025) met inclusion criteria after screening 540 records. Pooled meta-analysis, using a currency-harmonized 2025-USD PPP framework, found no statistically significant differences between digital and conventional workflows in laboratory costs, clinical costs, total costs, or number of treatment sessions. However, operator experience (dental students vs. prosthodontic specialists) significantly influenced clinical costs and session counts, with experienced clinicians showing a non-significant trend toward fewer sessions and lower costs with digital workflows. The authors conclude there is no robust evidence for a cost-efficiency advantage of digital over conventional RCD workflows, and that workflow choice should be guided by clinical feasibility and operator/technician expertise rather than assumed economic superiority.

## Key Contributions

- First meta-analysis (rather than descriptive cost-analysis) of cost-efficiency between digital and conventional RCD workflows, incorporating a formal economic-evaluation data-harmonization method (inflation-adjustment + PPP standardization to 2025 international USD).
- Quantifies four outcomes across pooled studies: laboratory cost, clinical cost, total cost, and number of treatment sessions — all four showed no statistically significant digital-vs-conventional difference.
- Identifies operator experience, not workflow modality, as the dominant significant driver of clinical cost and session-count variability (clinical cost p<0.0001; sessions p=0.0001 by subgroup).
- Explicitly reconciles its null finding against a prior systematic review reporting lower digital costs, attributing the discrepancy to that review's cost-analysis-only (non-outcome-adjusted) methodology.

## Methodology

- PRISMA 2020-compliant SR+MA; Cochrane systematic review methodology.
- Databases: PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus (to Feb 10, 2025) plus hand search, OpenGrey, clinicaltrials.gov.
- PICO: edentulous adults receiving RCDs; digital CAD/CAM workflow vs. conventional compression-molding workflow; outcome = cost-efficiency (laboratory/clinical/total cost, session count).
- Inclusion: RCTs, prospective/observational studies, case series (English) directly comparing or reporting RCD workflow costs; excluded case reports, in vitro studies, reviews, and implant-/attachment-retained overdentures.
- Two independent reviewers screened via Rayyan (kappa=0.93 at full-text stage); ROBINS-I V2 for risk of bias; random-effects meta-analysis with Hartung-Knapp adjustment, REML/Q-profile for τ², Chi² subgroup tests by operator experience; R "meta" package (v4.4.2).

## Results

- 5 studies included: Srinivasan 2019 (Switzerland, prospective, n=18), Smith 2020 (US, retrospective, n=30), Arakawa 2022 (Switzerland, retrospective, n=32), Otake 2024 (Japan, retrospective, n=44), Casucci 2025 (Italy, retrospective, n=60). Total n=184 patients / 202 RCDs.
- **Laboratory cost**: MD -239.7 (2025 USD; 95% CI -714.4 to 234.8), p n.s. (t=-1.6); I²=77.7%.
- **Clinical cost**: MD 74.4 (95% CI -120.0 to 268.7), p n.s. (t=0.7); I²=97.8%; significant experience-subgroup effect (Chi²=260.9, p<0.0001).
- **Total cost**: MD -357.7 (95% CI -1340.1 to 624.6), p n.s. (t=-1.2); I²=73.8%.
- **Number of sessions**: MD -1.5 (95% CI -6.7 to 3.8), p n.s. (t=-1.2); I²=95.1%; significant experience-subgroup effect (Chi²=14.9, p=0.0001) — experienced clinicians trended toward fewer sessions with digital workflows.
- Risk of bias: 4/5 studies moderate, 1/5 high (ROBINS-I V2). No quantitative OHRQoL/patient-satisfaction data available across studies.
- Clinical takeaway: no cost-efficiency advantage for digital RCD workflows overall; any efficiency gain (fewer sessions, lower cost) is conditional on operator's digital-workflow experience, and digital equipment capital investment was not captured in any included study's cost accounting.
