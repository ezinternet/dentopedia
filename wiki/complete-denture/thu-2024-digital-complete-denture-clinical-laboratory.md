## One-line Summary

SR of 39 clinical studies (2000–2022) on digital complete denture fabrication: border-molded impression and trial denture placement remain essential steps; digital workflows save 205–233 min chairtime but up to 37.5% of patients experience retention loss and 31.3% require denture remakes.

## Summary

This systematic review (PRISMA 2020, PROSPERO CRD42023393061) from the University of Hong Kong and University of Bern systematically maps the clinical and laboratory procedure variations across 39 clinical studies on digital complete denture fabrication published between 2000 and 2022. It is the first SR to focus specifically on *which procedures* are used (rather than simply comparing outcomes), revealing substantial heterogeneity and identifying which steps most influence clinical success.

Of the 39 included studies, 26 assessed definitive complete dentures, 7 assessed denture bases only, 2 assessed trial dentures, and 4 assessed digital images. The majority of included studies had poor-to-fair methodological quality; only 7 were RCTs rated fair-to-good.

Key finding: despite the promise of fully digital workflows, border-molded impression technique and trial denture placement remain the most evidence-supported procedure choices. When impression technique was compared, border-molded impressions produced maxillary denture base retention of 14.5–16.1 N versus only 6.2–6.6 N for intraoral scanning — a statistically significant difference. Patient satisfaction and oral health-related quality of life outcomes were comparable between conventional, milled, and 3D-printed complete dentures in most RCTs.

## Key Contributions

1. **Procedure mapping**: First comprehensive mapping of clinical and laboratory procedure variability in digital complete denture fabrication across 39 studies.
2. **Impression technique impact**: Quantified retention deficit when intraoral scanning replaces border-molded impression (~60% lower retention force).
3. **Trial denture underuse**: Only 13/39 studies performed trial denture placement, yet omitting this step is associated with higher complication rates (esthetic errors, occlusal discrepancies, extra visits).
4. **Fabrication method parity**: Both milling (21 studies) and 3D printing (17 studies) achieve acceptable outcomes with similar patient satisfaction — material and process choice can be guided by cost/availability rather than clinical outcomes alone.
5. **Complication profiling**: Systematically catalogued complication rates — up to 37.5% retention loss, 31.3% remakes, 66.7% pressure sores — providing benchmark data for clinical expectations.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (no meta-analysis due to procedure heterogeneity)
- **Databases**: PubMed/Medline, Embase, Web of Science + Google Scholar hand search
- **Period**: January 2000–September 2022
- **Screened/included**: 1,780 after deduplication → 266 full-text → 39 included
- **Inclusion**: Clinical studies, English language, subjective or objective outcome measurement; excluded case reports, reviews, commentaries
- **Risk of bias**: 7 RCTs (fair-to-good); 13 cohort studies (poor quality); remaining studies fair-to-good
- **Data extraction**: Dual independent extraction; categories — impression technique, jaw relation record method, articulator use, trial denture, fabrication method, materials, complications

## Results

### Impression Technique
- 24/39 studies used border-molded impression (conventional technique)
- 6 studies used intraoral scanning (IOS) for edentulous jaw capture
- **Retention comparison** (maxillary denture base): border-molded 14.5–16.1 N vs IOS 6.2–6.6 N (p<0.05)
- Both clinicians and patients rated retention/stability of border-molded impression dentures as satisfactory

### Jaw Relation Records
- 7 studies used gothic arch tracing; 3 used facebow records
- Most studies used conventional occlusion rims; virtual articulator mounting available in 3Shape and exocad systems
- Standardization of jaw relation recording methods remains inconsistent across the literature

### Trial Denture
- Only 13/39 studies performed formal trial denture placement
- Omission associated with increased esthetic and occlusal complications requiring extra visits
- Both wax and 3D-printed trial dentures were used; 3D-printed trial dentures allow duplication for emergency backup

### Fabrication Method
- Milling (21 studies): primarily pre-polymerized PMMA blocks; higher initial material cost but established quality
- 3D printing (17 studies): DLP/SLA resin; lower material cost, faster turnaround
- Retention of milled bases generally slightly higher than heat-polymerized conventional bases
- 3D-printed bases: comparable or slightly higher retention than milled in several RCTs
- Maximum occlusal force similar across fabrication methods

### Clinical Time
- Digital workflow saves 205–233 minutes vs conventional
- Number of clinical visits: 2–6 (most common 2–4)
- Despite time savings, ≥1 extra visit required in most protocols for adjustments

### Complications
| Complication | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Loss of retention | Up to 37.5% of patients |
| Denture remake | Up to 31.3% |
| Pressure sores | Up to 66.7% |
| Extra/unscheduled visits | ≥1 in most protocols; 1–4 unscheduled |
| Fracture | Occasional (particularly 3D-printed at 18 months) |

### Patient-Reported Outcomes
- Patient satisfaction similar between conventional, milled, and 3D-printed complete dentures (multiple RCTs)
- OHIP-EDENT scores improved significantly from baseline in all fabrication groups
- No consistent preference for one fabrication method over another
