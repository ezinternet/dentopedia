## One-line Summary

Systematic review + meta-analysis (13 cross-sectional studies, 3275 participants, 11 anthropometric methods; J Prosthet Dent 2026) showing anthropometric facial/finger measurements correlate highly overall with the standard OVD recording method — thumb length strongest overall (r ≈ 0.63 common / 0.57 random), distal outer-canthus–rima-oris strongest in men (≈0.57–0.58), little-finger length strongest in women (≈0.46) — but with very high heterogeneity (I² up to 99%) and high risk of bias.

체계적 문헌고찰 + 메타분석(13편 단면연구, 3275명, 11개 인체계측법; J Prosthet Dent 2026) — 인체계측(안면·손가락) 측정이 표준 OVD 기록법과 전반적으로 높은 상관; 전체 최강은 엄지 길이(r≈0.63/0.57), 남성은 외안각–구열 거리(≈0.57–0.58), 여성은 새끼손가락 길이(≈0.46). 단 이질성 매우 높고(I² 최대 99%) 비뚤림 위험 높음.

## Summary

This Journal of Prosthetic Dentistry SR+MA puts numbers on the **anthropometric branch** of OVD determination that the narrative reviews ([[complete-denture/alhajj-2017-occlusal-vertical-dimension-complete-denture-review]], [[complete-denture/fayad-2025-vertical-dimension-determination-prosthodontics-review]]) only list. It asks: of the many external facial/finger measurements proposed as proxies for **occlusal vertical dimension (OVD)**, which correlates best with the standard recording method? Pooling 13 cross-sectional studies (3,275 participants), it ranks 11 anthropometric methods and reports sex-specific best predictors — useful when natural reference points are absent in edentulous patients, but tempered by extreme heterogeneity.

## Key Contributions

- First meta-analytic **ranking of anthropometric (facial/finger) proxies** by their correlation with the standard OVD method.
- **Sex-stratified** best predictors via subgroup analysis.
- PRISMA 2020 / PICOT reporting; QUADAS-2 risk-of-bias appraisal.

## Methodology

- **PICO:** edentulous or dentulous (28 sound teeth, Angle Class I) participants; anthropometric OVD methods vs standard OVD method; outcome = correlation; cross-sectional observational studies only.
- Databases: PubMed, Scopus, Embase + others.
- Correlations pooled under common-effects and random-effects models; heterogeneity by I²; subgroup analysis by sex; QUADAS-2 for bias.

## Results

- **Included:** 13 cross-sectional studies; **3,275 participants** (1,618 men, 1,657 women); **11 anthropometric methods**.
- **Strongest overall — thumb length:** common-effects **r = 0.63**, random-effects **0.57**, **I² = 99%**.
- **Strongest in men — distal outer canthus → rima oris:** common 0.57, random 0.58, I² = 86%.
- **Strongest in women — little-finger length:** common 0.46, random 0.46, I² = 94%.
- **Risk of bias:** high in patient-selection and index-test domains in most studies.
- **Conclusion:** anthropometric methods correlate highly with the standard method and can serve to record OVD; **thumb length is potentially the most accurate predictor** — but the **very high heterogeneity (I² up to 99%)** means no single proxy is dependable across populations, reinforcing the reviews' "combine techniques" message.
