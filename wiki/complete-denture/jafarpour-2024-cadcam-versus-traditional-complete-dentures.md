## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 11 clinical studies (JOR 2024) showing CAD/CAM complete dentures are comparable to traditional in patient satisfaction and OHRQoL, with milled CDs significantly superior in clinician satisfaction, and CAD/CAM workflows requiring lower laboratory and total costs.

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis (PRISMA 2020) from McGill University compared CAD/CAM-fabricated complete dentures (CDs) versus conventionally manufactured CDs across patient-reported outcomes (PROs), clinician-reported outcomes (CROs), post-insertion adjustment visits, and costs. Eleven clinical studies published 2012–2022 were included from a search of 4 databases (3,126 initial records). The manufacturing methods represented were milling (subtractive) and 3D printing (additive).

Key finding: CAD/CAM CDs achieve parity with traditional CDs on patient outcomes but offer clinician and economic advantages — particularly for milled systems. Evidence quality is limited by small sample sizes and high heterogeneity; further RCTs are needed.

## Key Contributions

1. **Manufacturing-method subgrouping**: First SR+MA to distinguish milled vs. 3D printed outcomes — milled CDs outperformed traditional in clinician satisfaction (ES = 1.42; I² = 0%), while 3D printed CDs did not.
2. **Cost advantage confirmed**: CAD/CAM (both milled and 3D printed) required significantly lower laboratory costs and total treatment costs than traditional workflows; reduced chair time (~205 min/case) offsets higher material costs.
3. **Patient-outcome parity**: No significant difference in overall patient satisfaction (pooled MD = −0.11; p = .84) or OHRQoL/OHIP scores (pooled ES = 0.39; p = NS) across manufacturing types.
4. **Adjustment visits**: Milled CDs showed fewer post-insertion adjustment visits; 3D printed CDs were equivalent to traditional.
5. **Evidence gap**: High heterogeneity (I² up to 93%) and small study sizes limit definitive conclusions — pooled numbers should be interpreted cautiously.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review with meta-analysis; random-effects model (DerSimonian and Laird); SMD via Hedge's d
- **Search**: MEDLINE (Ovid), Embase, Scopus, Cochrane CENTRAL; January 2000 – May 2022
- **Included studies**: 11 (3 crossover RCTs, 1 parallel RCT, 1 non-randomized CCT, 2 prospective, 3 retrospective, 1 cross-sectional)
- **Sample sizes**: 10–637 participants per study; most studies n < 30
- **CAD/CAM systems**: AvaDent (milling), DENTCA (3D print/SLA), Wieland, custom disk method, rapid prototyping
- **Outcomes measured**: patient satisfaction (VAS/Likert), OHRQoL (OHIP-EDENT, OHIP-G49, OHIP-EDENT-J), clinician satisfaction, number of adjustment visits, laboratory costs, total costs
- **Risk of bias**: Modified Jadad scale + Newcastle-Ottawa scale; most studies moderate-to-high risk
- **Heterogeneity threshold**: I² ≥ 50% = substantial; no funnel plots (n < 10 studies per outcome); fail-safe number used

## Results

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Patient satisfaction | CAD/CAM vs. traditional | MD = −0.11 (−1.15 to 0.93) | .84 (NS) | 90% |

| Post-insertion adjustments | Milled vs. traditional | Fewer adjustments with milled | Significant | — |

| Laboratory costs | CAD/CAM vs. traditional | CAD/CAM lower | **Significant** | — |
| Total costs | CAD/CAM vs. traditional | CAD/CAM lower | **Significant** | — |

**Clinical interpretation:**
- For patients: choose either workflow; both deliver comparable satisfaction and quality of life
- For clinicians: milled CDs are easier to achieve high-quality outcomes (fit, retention, extension)
- For practice economics: CAD/CAM workflows reduce overhead costs despite higher material costs
