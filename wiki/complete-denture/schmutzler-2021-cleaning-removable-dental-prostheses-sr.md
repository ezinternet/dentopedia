## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 44 prospective clinical studies showing that combined mechanical + chemical cleaning is the gold standard for biofilm removal from removable dental prostheses, with limited clinical evidence on effects to denture base material properties.

## Summary

This PROSPERO-registered systematic review (CRD42020189649) by Schmutzler et al. (2021) synthesizes prospective clinical evidence on denture hygiene for removable dental prostheses (RDPs). Published in the Journal of Evidence Based Dental Practice, the review included 44 studies identified from a search of PubMed, Embase, ICTRP, and ClinicalTrials.gov covering June 1975 to May 2020.

The central finding is that **combined use of mechanical and chemical denture hygiene is significantly more effective than either approach alone** for removing biofilms and microorganisms from the surface of RDPs in situ. Chemical agents alone (alkaline peroxide, sodium hypochlorite, Ricinus communis) show meaningful antimicrobial activity, but the addition of mechanical brushing consistently outperforms chemical soaking in isolation. Evidence for microwave disinfection and ultrasonic cleaning as adjuncts is also supported by included clinical studies.

Regarding the secondary question of effects on denture base material properties (color stability, surface roughness, dimensional stability), the review found only **limited clinical evidence**, with insufficient data to draw firm clinical conclusions.

## Key Contributions

1. **Combined protocol as gold standard**: Mechanical + chemical cleaning is superior to any single modality — the clearest actionable recommendation from this SR
2. **In situ clinical focus**: Restricted to prospective clinical studies (≥10 participants), avoiding the overoptimism common in in vitro studies of cleaning efficacy
3. **Candida management**: Multiple studies confirm reduction of Candida albicans with chemical soaking (especially overnight in alkaline peroxide), directly relevant to denture stomatitis prevention and treatment
4. **Material property evidence gap**: Despite concerns about chemical degradation of PMMA, clinical data are sparse — identifies a high-priority research gap
5. **Broad modality coverage**: Reviews brushing, effervescent tablets, sodium hypochlorite, Ricinus communis, microwave irradiation, and ultrasonic cleaning within one framework

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42020189649), no meta-analysis due to outcome heterogeneity
- **Search**: PubMed, Cochrane database (including PubMed, Embase, ICTRP, ClinicalTrials.gov), June 1975 – May 2020; manual search of retrieved studies
- **Initial yield**: 4,307 records; final inclusion: 44 prospective clinical trials
- **Inclusion criteria**: Prospective clinical trials, ≥10 participants, investigating in situ biofilm removal from RDPs or the effect of cleaning on denture base material properties, English language
- **Exclusion**: Retrospective, in vitro, <10 participants, non-English

## Results

### Biofilm / Microorganism Removal

- **Combined cleaning (mechanical + chemical)** is the most effective protocol — consistently outperforms single-modality approaches across included studies
- **Brushing alone**: Effective but technique-dependent; adequate as daily maintenance but insufficient for thorough disinfection
- **Alkaline peroxide (effervescent tablets)**: Standard chemical option; overnight soaking reduces biofilm mass and alters composition; effective against Candida
- **Sodium hypochlorite**: Potent antimicrobial; effective at dilute concentrations; some concerns about color changes at high concentrations with prolonged use
- **Ricinus communis solution**: Comparable to sodium hypochlorite in some studies for biofilm control
- **Microwave disinfection**: Clinical evidence supports efficacy; used particularly in context of denture stomatitis
- **Ultrasonic cleaning**: Effective and increasingly used; reduces need for manual brushing effort

### Material Properties (denture base)

- **Limited clinical evidence**: Insufficient prospective clinical data to make firm recommendations
- High-concentration sodium hypochlorite may affect color stability of PMMA
- Dimensional stability and surface roughness effects are largely unstudied in clinical settings
- More in vitro than clinical data available — emphasizes research gap

### Key Clinical Takeaway

The review endorses a **two-step protocol**: daily mechanical brushing combined with chemical soaking (e.g., overnight in alkaline peroxide tablet solution) as the evidence-based standard for RDP hygiene.
