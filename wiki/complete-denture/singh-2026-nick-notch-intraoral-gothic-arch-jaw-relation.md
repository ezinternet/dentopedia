## One-line Summary

Within-subject clinical comparative study (n=20 edentulous, 45-60 y) using split-cast verification on a semi-adjustable articulator: Nick and Notch wax registration gave the lowest jaw-relation discrepancy (centric 0.15 mm, protrusive 0.23 mm), intraoral Gothic arch tracing intermediate (0.42 / 0.51 mm), extraoral Gothic arch tracing the highest (0.74 / 0.86 mm); all pairwise differences significant (p<0.001).

## Summary

According to PubMed, this Bioinformation (2026) clinical study ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.6026/973206300222521); PMID 42282410; PMCID PMC13252300) compares three centric/protrusive jaw-relation recording methods in fully edentulous patients, verified by split-cast mounting on a Hanau Wide-Vue semi-adjustable articulator. Recording centric relation is one of the most critical and error-sensitive steps in complete denture fabrication: edentulism removes periodontal proprioception, leaving mandibular positioning to the TMJ and musculature. The three methods span the static–functional spectrum — **Nick and Notch** (static interocclusal wax/ZOE registration in habitual retruded closure) versus **intraoral** and **extraoral Gothic arch tracing** (functional graphic methods whose arrowhead apex marks centric relation). The study finds a consistent accuracy hierarchy (Nick and Notch most consistent, extraoral least) but concludes that method selection should be individualized — graphic tracing may still be preferable for patients with impaired neuromuscular control, habitual protrusive closure, or greater ridge resorption because of visual apex confirmation.

## Key Contributions

- Rare **three-way** comparison of jaw-relation recording methods in edentulous patients with a split-cast objective standard (most prior work compares only pairs).
- Establishes a clear discrepancy hierarchy at both centric and protrusive: **Nick and Notch < intraoral Gothic arch < extraoral Gothic arch** (all pairwise significant).
- Frames the magnitudes clinically: Nick and Notch (0.15-0.23 mm) within acceptable range; intraoral (0.42-0.51 mm) manageable with routine remount/occlusal adjustment; extraoral (0.74-0.86 mm) potentially clinically perceptible.
- Reinforces the biomechanical rationale that an intraoral tracing plate near the transverse hinge axis reduces error magnification versus a forward-projecting extraoral platform.

## Methodology

- **Design**: within-subject clinical comparative study; each of 20 patients received all three records (60 records total). Single department, 6 months.
- **Patients**: 20 fully edentulous, 45-60 y, both sexes, adequate opening and good ridges; excluded compromised systemic health, TMJ disorders, severely resorbed ridges.
- **Standardization**: plaster index duplication produced three identical record bases and occlusion rims per patient (one set per technique). Heat-cured acrylic bases, wax rims.
- **Mounting**: arbitrary face-bow → semi-adjustable articulator; maxillary cast via face-bow; mandibular cast first mounted with the **Nick and Notch centric record as the reference**.
- **Recording**:
  - *Nick and Notch* — V-notched rims, retruded closure, ZOE paste; 5 records, most-repeated chosen.
  - *Extraoral Gothic arch* — bearing plate + forward tracing platform, lamp-black tracing, apex marked, type III stone record.
  - *Intraoral Gothic arch* — intraoral tracing device, bearing pin to correct VD, inked plate, apex traced, type III stone record.
- **Verification**: split-cast with three reference points (anterior to incisive papilla; two posterolateral 10 mm anterior to posterior tuberosity borders); discrepancy at each point measured with digital Vernier caliper (0.01 mm), centric and protrusive.
- **Outcomes**: right-lateral (RLD), left-lateral (LLD), anterior (AD), and overall mean discrepancy.
- **Statistics**: SPSS 22.0; one-way ANOVA + Tukey HSD; p<0.05.

## Results

**Centric — overall mean discrepancy**

| Method | Overall discrepancy |
|---|---|
| Nick and Notch | **0.15 ± 0.03 mm** |
| Intraoral Gothic arch | 0.42 ± 0.10 mm |
| Extraoral Gothic arch | 0.74 ± 0.12 mm |

Overall ANOVA F = 200.90, p<0.001 (RLD F=162.50, LLD F=83.27, AD F=91.21; all p<0.001).

**Protrusive — overall mean discrepancy**

| Method | Overall discrepancy |
|---|---|
| Nick and Notch | **0.23 ± 0.08 mm** |
| Intraoral Gothic arch | 0.51 ± 0.13 mm |
| Extraoral Gothic arch | 0.86 ± 0.14 mm |

Overall ANOVA F = 146.70, p<0.001 (RLD F=99.94, LLD F=53.13, AD F=53.20; all p<0.001).

- **Tukey post hoc**: all pairwise differences significant at both positions; extraoral > intraoral > Nick and Notch at every site.
- **Protrusive > centric** error for all three methods — eccentric registration involves bilateral condylar translation, adding variables and magnifying inconsistency at the split-cast interface.
- **Mechanistic reading**: Nick and Notch records in a stationary habitual closure (no excursive base displacement) with dimensionally stable ZOE; intraoral beats extraoral because its plate is near the hinge axis (less angular-to-linear magnification) and its smaller device disturbs musculature/tongue less.
- **Clinical caveat from authors**: despite Nick and Notch's superiority, it depends on clinician guidance and patient neuromuscular cooperation; Gothic arch tracing may be advantageous when neuromuscular control is impaired, closure is habitually protrusive, or ridges are heavily resorbed.
