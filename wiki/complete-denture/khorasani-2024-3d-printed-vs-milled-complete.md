## Summary

This PRISMA-compliant SR+MA compared two digital complete denture fabrication methods — additive manufacturing (AM; 3D printing) and subtractive manufacturing (SM; CAD-CAM milling) — on two primary clinical outcomes: denture retention and patient satisfaction. From 1,098 initial records searched across 6 databases up to August 2024, only 4 studies met eligibility criteria (clinical studies in English comparing AM vs SM complete dentures with quantitative retention or satisfaction data).

Pooled meta-analysis using random effects models found:
- **Retention**: AM produced numerically higher mean retention values, but the difference was not statistically significant (SMD=0.165 N, 95% CI: −0.176 to 0.506, p=0.343, I²=58.72%)
- **Satisfaction**: SM showed numerically higher patient satisfaction, but the difference was not statistically significant (SMD=−0.595, 95% CI: −1.579 to 0.389, p=0.236, I²=85.94%)

The cautious conclusion is that no clinically meaningful difference currently distinguishes 3D-printed from milled complete dentures on these two outcomes. The very high heterogeneity (I²=59–86%) and small evidence base (n=4 studies) limit confidence in this conclusion.

## Key Contributions

1. **First direct SR+MA on retention and satisfaction** comparing AM vs SM complete dentures (prior reviews focused on accuracy, fit, or dimensional changes)
2. **No significant difference confirmed** for both primary outcomes despite directional trends (AM slightly better retention; SM slightly better satisfaction)
3. **Heterogeneity is the dominant signal**: I²=59–86% means pooled results may obscure important subgroup differences (3D printing technology type, resin material, layer thickness, build angle)
4. **Only 4 eligible clinical studies exist**: highlights a major gap in the literature given the rapid clinical uptake of both technologies
5. **Comprehensive risk of bias assessment**: RoB 2 for RCTs, ROBINS-I for non-randomized studies; publication bias via Egger test

## Methodology

- **Study type**: Systematic review + meta-analysis (PRISMA-compliant; PROSPERO CRD42024515941)
- **Databases**: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Embase, Cochrane Library, Google Scholar
- **Search cutoff**: August 2024
- **Language**: English only
- **PICO**: Edentulous patients / AM complete dentures / SM complete dentures / retention + patient satisfaction
- **Exclusions**: implant-supported/retained overdentures, partial dentures, in vitro, case reports, reviews, abstracts, letters
- **Statistical model**: Random effects (DerSimonian-Laird); SMD as effect size
- **Heterogeneity**: Cochran's Q, I², Tau²
- **Publication bias**: Begg's funnel plot + Egger test
- **Sensitivity analyses**: performed to check robustness

## Results

| Outcome | SMD | 95% CI | P-value | I² |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Retention | 0.165 | −0.176 to 0.506 | 0.343 | 58.72% |
| Patient Satisfaction | −0.595 | −1.579 to 0.389 | 0.236 | 85.94% |

**Retention direction**: AM > SM (not significant)
**Satisfaction direction**: SM > AM (not significant)

### Contextual comparisons
- Conventional PMMA heat-cured dentures: susceptible to microbial colonization, resin shrinkage, dimensional changes; require 6+ visits
- SM (milling): better surface properties (Candida adherence, roughness, wettability), fewer visits, improved mechanical properties; limitations include material waste and cost
- AM (3D printing): further waste reduction, greater customization, rapid prototyping; disadvantages include volumetric shrinkage during polymerization, postprocessing requirements, and in some reports lower patient satisfaction vs conventional

### Key limitations
- Only 4 studies eligible — extremely sparse evidence base
- High I² (especially satisfaction at 86%) makes pooled SMD unreliable
- No standardized measurement protocol across studies for retention or satisfaction
- English-language restriction may introduce language bias
- Short follow-up periods across included studies
