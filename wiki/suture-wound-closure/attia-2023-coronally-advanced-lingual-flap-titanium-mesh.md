## One-line Summary

RCT (n=14, posterior mandible horizontal GBR with titanium mesh) showing the coronally advanced lingual flap (CALF) achieved markedly greater lingual flap advancement (14.4 vs 3.9 mm) and zero titanium-mesh exposure vs 83.3% early exposure without CALF.

## Summary

In horizontal ridge augmentation of the posterior mandible with titanium mesh (TM), the lingual flap is notoriously hard to mobilize, and tension at the lingual aspect drives early mesh exposure along the incision line. This randomized controlled trial tested the coronally advanced lingual flap (CALF) technique against buccal advancement alone (NO-CALF). With CALF, lingual advancement rose from 3.9 to 14.4 mm and early titanium-mesh exposure dropped from 83.3% of cases to none, with no CALF-related complications. The authors conclude CALF reliably and safely advances the lingual flap coronally to facilitate and maintain tension-free primary closure.

## Key Contributions

- Defines and validates the CALF technique for the difficult lingual flap in posterior-mandible GBR.
- Quantifies the trade-off: CALF shifts advancement burden to the lingual side (more lingual, less buccal advancement) and eliminates early mesh exposure.
- Provides a controlled (RCT) signal that lingual mobilization is the limiting factor for primary closure over TM in this site.

## Methodology

- 14 patients randomized to NO-CALF (buccal advancement only) vs CALF (buccal advancement + CALF).
- Setting: horizontal ridge augmentation, posterior mandible, titanium mesh.
- Healing monitored weekly for 4 weeks, then at 2, 4, 6, 9 months for soft-tissue dehiscence (TM exposure) along the incision.
- Measured lingual and buccal flap advancement and any CALF-related complications.

## Results

- TM exposure: NO-CALF 83.3% early Class II exposures; CALF 0% (P < .0001).
- Mean lingual advancement: 3.9 ± 1.1 mm (NO-CALF) vs 14.4 ± 3.8 mm (CALF) (P < .0001).
- Mean buccal advancement: 15.8 ± 2.1 mm (NO-CALF) vs 10.5 ± 1.4 mm (CALF) (P < .0001).
- No complications related to the CALF technique.
- Conclusion: CALF facilitates and maintains tension-free primary wound closure during healing.
