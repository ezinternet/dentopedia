## Summary

Knoell measured the closure forces on the structural components of the **basic lattice stitch** across 21 wound repairs with closing tensions of 0.3-4.0 N, comparing them to a simple interrupted stitch. The lattice stitch dispersed **54.4 ± 3.2% less tissue tension in the perpendicular plane of closure** (the plane along which edges tend to tear and dehisce) than the simple interrupted stitch (p<.005). The clinical argument is that by redistributing tension away from this plane, the lattice stitch should reduce tissue tearing at closure, wound dehiscence, and necrosis — making it a candidate for any closure under tension and for repair of atrophic/fragile skin. Note the tested tension range (max 4.0 N) sits just below the 5 N "high-tension" model used in companion bench studies.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies tension dispersion of the lattice stitch (~54% offload from the perpendicular plane) vs simple interrupted.
- Provides a biomechanical rationale for tension-redistributing stitches in fragile/atrophic skin and high-tension repairs.

## Methodology

- 21 clinical wound closures, closing tensions 0.3-4.0 N.
- Closure forces measured on the lattice stitch's structural components vs a simple interrupted stitch.
- Outcome: % reduction of tissue tension in the perpendicular plane of closure.
- Device/exact decomposition method: not reported in the available abstract.

## Results

- Basic lattice stitch: **54.4 ± 3.2% less** perpendicular-plane tissue tension than simple interrupted (p<.005).
- Conclusion: lattice stitch significantly disperses closure tension; recommended for tension closures and atrophic-skin repair.
- **Living-document note:** abstract-only evidence, modest n, single biomechanical endpoint, no healing/scar outcomes.
