## One-line Summary
Radiographic review cataloguing the lesions that cause periodontal ligament (PDL) widening — ranging from benign (occlusal trauma, orthodontic force) to serious (osteosarcoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, scleroderma) — to guide differential diagnosis.

## Summary
PDL widening on a radiograph is a classic sign clinicians associate with occlusal trauma, but Mortazavi and colleagues assemble the full differential to show why that reflex can be dangerous. Normal PDL space is ~0.15–0.21 mm; widening can arise from benign and functional causes (occlusal trauma, orthodontic movement, periodontitis, periapical inflammation) but also from systemic disease (scleroderma) and malignancy (osteosarcoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, metastasis). The review provides radiographic cues — symmetric versus localized widening, lamina dura integrity, associated bone destruction — that help separate the benign majority from the serious minority. The clinical value is as a safety net: confirm the boring explanation only after the dangerous ones are radiographically improbable.

## Key Contributions
- A consolidated differential-diagnosis list for PDL widening.
- Explicitly separates benign/functional from malignant/systemic causes.
- Radiographic discriminators (symmetry, lamina dura, bone change) to avoid premature occlusal-trauma attribution.

## Methodology
Narrative review (Google Scholar, PubMed, PubMed Central, ScienceDirect) of lesions producing PDL widening, organized by etiologic category with radiographic features.

## Results
Normal PDL ~0.15–0.21 mm. Causes of widening span occlusal trauma, orthodontic force, periodontitis, periapical inflammation, scleroderma, and malignancies (osteosarcoma, NHL, metastasis). Generalized symmetric widening favors systemic/functional causes; localized widening with bone destruction raises malignancy concern.

## Clinical Application
- Do not equate PDL widening with occlusal trauma by default — localized widening with cortical/bone destruction warrants malignancy workup and referral.
- Generalized symmetric PDL widening can signal systemic disease (e.g., scleroderma), not occlusion.
