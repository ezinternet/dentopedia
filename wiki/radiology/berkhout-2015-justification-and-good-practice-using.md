## One-line Summary

EADMFR consensus/position paper: handheld portable intraoral X-ray units introduce new operator/third-party exposure and collimation risks; they should be used only exceptionally, after documented justification, with strict ALARA safeguards.

## Summary

This 2015 position paper from the European Academy of DentoMaxilloFacial Radiology (EADMFR) addresses the growing use of handheld, battery-powered portable X-ray devices for intraoral radiography. While these devices are increasingly marketed, the academy cautions that handheld operation introduces a distinct set of staff- and patient-safety challenges that fixed wall-mounted units do not, requiring new or revised risk assessments before any clinical use.

The core message is a justification-first stance: handheld portable X-ray devices should be used only after careful, documented evaluation (ideally supported by a medical physicist), only when handheld operation offers a demonstrable benefit over conventional modalities, and only when it introduces no new risk to operators or third parties. The paper anticipates that such justified situations will be **very exceptional**. The overarching concern is that the practical difficulties of handheld use can drive up (re)exposures and thereby violate the ALARA (as low as reasonably achievable) principle.

## Key Contributions

- **First EADMFR position on handheld portable intraoral X-ray**, classifying it as an exceptional-use modality rather than a routine alternative to fixed units.
- **Enumerates handheld-specific risks** demanding fresh risk assessment: difficulty applying rectangular collimation with beam-aiming devices, more complex receptor–exposure matching (often longer exposure times), operator movement from unit weight, operator/third-party protection, and use in uncontrolled environments.
- **Frames the problem through ALARA** — these difficulties can increase (re)exposures relative to conventional intraoral devices, violating ALARA.
- **Sets an explicit justification threshold**: documented evaluation + medical-physics support + demonstrable benefit + no new operator/third-party risk.

## Methodology

Expert consensus / position paper produced by EADMFR (not an experimental study). It applies the established radiation-protection triad — justification, optimization, and ALARA — to the specific technology of handheld battery-powered intraoral X-ray generators, organizing its guidance around the technical points of failure: beam-aiming devices, rectangular collimation, X-ray receptor selection, focus–skin distance, and backscatter shielding.

## Results

(abstract-only — full text not retrieved; the following are the paper's stated conclusions)

- Handheld portable X-ray use is expected to be **"very exceptional, and for justified situations only."**
- Special attention must be drawn to: **beam-aiming devices, rectangular collimation, X-ray receptor section/selection, focus–skin distance, and backscatter shielding.**
- The device must deliver **reproducible dose across the full range of environmental conditions** (e.g. battery charge status and temperature) — a quality requirement specific to portable units.
- The practical handheld difficulties (longer exposures, movement, collimation problems, uncontrolled environments) are the mechanisms by which ALARA can be violated, motivating the cautious, justification-gated recommendation.
