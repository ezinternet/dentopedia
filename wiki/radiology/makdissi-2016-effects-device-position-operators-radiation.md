## One-line Summary

Phantom + TLD bench study of a NOMAD Pro handheld dental X-ray unit in 3 hold positions: operator scatter dose is negligible overall (peak 0.0310 mGy on the left palm, perpendicular hold) and well below IRR-1999 limits, but device position significantly affects exposure — so positioning protocols, personal dosemeters, training and audits are required.

## Summary

Handheld intraoral X-ray devices (e.g., NOMAD Pro) are held by the operator during exposure, which dissolves the conventional "controlled area" / wall-mount geometry in which the operator stands behind a barrier at nil exposure. Although these units carry an integral lead backscatter shield, the operator's distance from the body and the device orientation vary with how it is held — making operator dose a function of handling technique. This bench study placed thermoluminescent dosemeters (TLDs) on a mannequin operator (eyes, thyroid, trunk, waist, hand, feet) and fired a tripod-mounted NOMAD Pro at a head phantom across three defined hold positions (close/parallel, arms-extended ~40 cm/parallel, and perpendicular/partially-extended), 30 one-second exposures each. Operator exposure was negligible and well below Ionizing Radiation Regulations 1999 (IRR-1999) limits in all positions — but it is a measurable increase over the nil exposure of a wall-mounted machine, and the device position significantly affected the total operator dose. The authors recommend personal dosimetry, strict positioning protocols, training, and regular audits.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies **operator** (not patient) radiation dose for a handheld intraoral X-ray unit, addressing the safety gap created when the source is held inside the controlled area instead of fired from behind a wall-mount/barrier.
- Isolates the operator-controlled variable — **how the device is held** (distance + orientation) — as a significant determinant of total operator exposure.
- Extrapolates bench dose to an occupational estimate from a realistic workload (100 intraoral radiographs/week × 46 weeks/year) and benchmarks it against IRR-1999.
- Grounds a practical operator-safety protocol: personal dosemeters, positioning discipline, training, and audit.

## Methodology

- **Device**: NOMAD Pro Handheld X-ray system (Aribex Inc.) with integral lead backscatter shield, remote control, tripod-mounted for reproducible geometry.
- **Patient simulation**: ATOM Max Dental and Diagnostic Phantom, model 711 HN (CIRS Inc.) as the head.
- **Operator simulation**: mannequin carrying pre-calibrated TLDs (Qados, Agar Scientific) near the eyes and at thyroid, trunk, waist, hand (right finger + left palm), and feet; 3 TLDs for background.
- **Three hold positions**:
  - Position 1 — close to body, parallel to ground.
  - Position 2 — arms fully extended (~40 cm), parallel to ground.
  - Position 3 — perpendicular to ground, arms partially extended.
- **Protocol**: 30 exposures × 1 s per scenario. Occupational dose extrapolated from 100 intraoral radiographs/week, 46 weeks/year.
- **Evidence level**: in-vitro / bench experimental (phantom + mannequin + TLD dosimetry). Ingested abstract-only (PMC full text not retrievable).

## Results

- **Background radiation**: 0.0110 mGy.
- **Peak operator dose** (background-subtracted): **0.0310 mGy on the left palm at Position 3** (perpendicular hold) — highest across all sites/positions.
- **Overall**: a **negligible** increase in operator exposure, **well below IRR-1999** recommended levels in all positions.
- **Caveat**: still an increase over the **nil exposure** of a wall-mounted unit.
- **Position effect**: device position relative to the operator **significantly** affects total operator exposure.
- **Recommendations**: personal dosemeters, strict positioning protocols, training, and regular audits to maintain low-dose compliance.
