## One-line Summary

Narrative review synthesizing 2013–2024 literature on polypharmacy (≥5 medications, 30–44% prevalence in older adults) causing xerostomia/hyposalivation and dysgeusia via distinct pharmacological mechanisms (anticholinergic M3-receptor blockade, β-blocker Na-K-2Cl cotransporter inhibition, thiazide carbonic-anhydrase inhibition), with a proposed interprofessional deprescribing-first management framework for dental practitioners.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3389/fdmed.2026.1758771](https://doi.org/10.3389/fdmed.2026.1758771)), this narrative review synthesizes contemporary evidence (2013–2024 literature search) on how polypharmacy — defined as concurrent use of ≥5 medications, affecting 30–44% of older adults globally — drives oral health deterioration in geriatric patients, with a specific focus on xerostomia and its co-occurring dysgeusia. The review maps ten major drug classes (anticholinergics, ACE inhibitors, β-blockers, calcium channel blockers, tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs, antipsychotics, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, NSAIDs, bisphosphonates) to specific oral manifestations and their distinct underlying pharmacological mechanisms in aging tissue, explaining why xerostomia and taste disturbance frequently co-occur with polypharmacy rather than being separately caused. A central clinical recommendation is that management of drug-induced xerostomia should prioritize medication review and deprescribing over pharmacological escalation — the review explicitly cautions against routine use of systemic sialogogues (pilocarpine, cevimeline) in this population, since their evidence base is largely restricted to post-irradiation hyposalivation and Sjögren's syndrome where residual glandular function exists; non-pharmacological measures (saliva substitutes, sugar-free gum, hydration) are favored as first-line given their safety profile in frail elders already burdened by multiple drugs.

Clinically, this reframes xerostomia/dysgeusia in polypharmacy patients as a drug-review problem first, not a sialogogue-prescribing problem, and positions dentists as frontline detectors who should trigger physician/pharmacist medication review rather than independently escalating pharmacotherapy.

## Key Contributions

- Maps ten drug classes to distinct oral manifestations and aging-specific pharmacological mechanisms (Table 1: anticholinergics, ACE inhibitors, β-blockers, CCBs, TCAs, SSRIs, antipsychotics, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, NSAIDs, bisphosphonates).
- Distinguishes mechanistically why different xerogenic drug classes cause hyposalivation: muscarinic M3-receptor blockade (anticholinergics/TCAs), altered central/peripheral serotonergic salivary reflex (SSRIs), β-adrenergic/Na-K-2Cl cotransporter disruption (β-blockers), carbonic-anhydrase inhibition reducing bicarbonate buffering (thiazide diuretics).
- Proposes a conservative, deprescribing-first management model for polypharmacy-associated xerostomia, explicitly cautioning against off-label systemic sialogogue use.
- Frames dentists as frontline detectors of oral adverse drug reactions within a structured dentist–pharmacist–physician–caregiver interprofessional model.
- Quantifies compounding risks: polypharmacy (≥5 drugs) independently predicts malnutrition and accelerates ADL/oral-self-care decline (OR 1.5–2.0), and low health literacy correlates with poor oral hygiene (r = −0.42 vs. plaque index).

## Methodology

Narrative, evidence-based literature review (not systematic; no PRISMA, meta-analysis, or formal quality/bias appraisal) searching PubMed/MEDLINE, Scopus, and Web of Science for English-language publications from January 2013 to March 2024, using concepts of polypharmacy, geriatric oral health, xerostomia, salivary gland hypofunction, medication-induced oral effects, and multidisciplinary care; reference lists of selected articles manually screened for additional studies. Evidence synthesized descriptively across epidemiology, mechanisms, specific complications, diagnostic/management challenges, management strategies, interprofessional collaboration, special populations, and future directions.

## Results

1. **Epidemiology**: US adult polypharmacy prevalence rose from 8.2% (1999–2000) to 17.1% (2017–2018); reaches 44.1% in adults ≥65y recently; >50% in cardiovascular disease/diabetes/multimorbidity groups; "excessive polypharmacy" (≥10 drugs) exceeds 10% in older populations.
2. **Risk amplification**: each additional prescribed drug raises medication-error odds by 16%; serious drug-drug interactions occur in ~13% of polypharmacy patients; >2/3 of patients on ≥10 drugs have ≥1 potentially serious interaction.
3. **Xerostomia prevalence benchmark**: 38.5% of older adults (mean age 74) in a special care dentistry cohort (Storbeck et al., cited) had xerostomia, with a dose-dependent relationship to medication count.
4. **Mechanistic map**: 10 drug classes linked to specific oral complications via distinct mechanisms (see Key Contributions); e.g., calcium channel blockers → gingival overgrowth via reduced hepatic clearance + fibroblast/ECM activity; bisphosphonates → MRONJ via suppressed bone remodeling/angiogenesis.
5. **Compounding risk factors**: polypharmacy (≥5 drugs) independently predicts malnutrition (2025 long-term-care cross-sectional study, cited) and accelerates ADL/oral-self-care dependency (OR 1.5–2.0); low health literacy correlates with poor medication adherence and oral hygiene (r = −0.42, literacy vs. plaque index).
6. **Management recommendation**: deprescribing/medication substitution favored over adding systemic sialogogues; sialogogues' evidence base is restricted to post-irradiation and Sjögren's populations with residual gland function, and their routine use in polypharmacy-induced xerostomia risks worsening polypharmacy burden and adherence in frail elders. Non-pharmacological measures (saliva substitutes, sugar-free gum/lozenges, hydration, high-fluoride toothpaste) recommended as first-line.
7. **Care model**: dentists identify and document oral ADRs and trigger physician/pharmacist communication; pharmacists evaluate anticholinergic burden and drug-drug interactions especially at care transitions; physicians/geriatricians coordinate deprescribing; caregivers provide daily hygiene support — most current interprofessional evidence remains descriptive rather than interventional (an acknowledged gap).
