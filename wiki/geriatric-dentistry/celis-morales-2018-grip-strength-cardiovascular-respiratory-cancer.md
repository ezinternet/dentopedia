## One-line Summary

Prospective cohort (n=502,293 UK Biobank, mean 7.1 years) found each 5 kg lower grip strength independently raised all-cause mortality hazard by 16–20%, cardiovascular mortality by 19–24%, respiratory mortality by 24–31%, and cancer mortality by 10–17%, with grip strength addition improving cardiovascular risk score discrimination (ΔC=0.012).

## Summary

This large UK Biobank prospective cohort (n=502,293, aged 40–69, mean follow-up 7.1 years) is one of the most definitive epidemiological studies linking handgrip strength (악력, GS) to disease-specific mortality across cardiovascular disease (심혈관질환, CVD), respiratory disease (호흡기질환), and cancer (암). For every 5 kg lower grip strength, fully adjusted hazard ratios were 1.16–1.20 for all-cause mortality, 1.19–1.24 for CVD mortality, 1.24–1.31 for respiratory mortality, and 1.10–1.17 for cancer mortality — in both men and women, surviving landmark analysis. Associations were stronger in younger participants, implying grip strength captures biological rather than purely chronological aging. Adding grip strength to a standard office-based risk score (age/sex/BMI/systolic BP/diabetes/smoking) raised the C-index for all-cause mortality by 0.013 and for CVD mortality by 0.012, establishing grip strength's potential as a low-cost primary-care screening tool. The clinical implication for geriatric dentistry is that grip strength measured in or near a dental visit may serve as a physical frailty (신체노쇠, Physical Frailty) signal that predicts systemic decline, complementing oral frailty (구강노쇠, Oral Frailty) indices.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates a dose–response relationship between grip strength and mortality across CVD, respiratory disease, and cancer simultaneously in the largest cohort to date
- Landmark analysis excludes reverse causality as a major explanation for the associations
- Muscle weakness thresholds (≤26 kg men; ≤16 kg women) confer clinically meaningful elevated risk for nearly all health outcomes
- Grip×age interaction: hazard ratios larger in younger (≤55 years) participants, supporting grip strength as an early biomarker of biological aging
- Grip strength addition to office-based risk score provides statistically and clinically meaningful improvement in mortality prediction

## Methodology

- **Design**: Prospective population-based cohort
- **Dataset**: UK Biobank (22 assessment centres, England/Wales/Scotland)
- **n**: 502,293 (54% women, aged 40–69 at baseline 2007–2010)
- **Exposure**: Mean grip strength (right + left, kg); per 5 kg decrement and sex/age-specific quartiles; muscle weakness cut-offs: ≤26 kg (men), ≤16 kg (women) per Foundation for the National Institutes of Health (FNIH) Sarcopenia Project
- **Follow-up**: Mean 7.1 years (mortality); 6.1 years (incidence)
- **Analysis**: Cox proportional hazards (4 adjustment models: sociodemographic → height → comorbidities → lifestyle); landmark analysis (exclude events ≤2 years); Harrell's C-index for prediction; grip×age interaction terms; internal validation (~50/50 split)

## Results

### Mortality — HR per 5 kg lower grip strength (fully adjusted, landmark analysis)

| Outcome | Women HR (95% CI) | Men HR (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|
| All-cause mortality | 1.20 (1.17–1.23) | 1.16 (1.15–1.17) |
| CVD mortality | 1.19 (1.13–1.25) | 1.22 (1.18–1.26) |
| All respiratory mortality | 1.31 (1.22–1.40) | 1.24 (1.20–1.28) |
| COPD (만성폐쇄성폐질환) mortality | 1.24 (1.05–1.47) | 1.19 (1.09–1.30) |
| All cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.13–1.21) | 1.10 (1.07–1.13) |
| Colorectal cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.04–1.32) | 1.18 (1.09–1.27) |
| Lung cancer mortality | 1.17 (1.07–1.27) | 1.08 (1.03–1.13) |
| Breast cancer mortality | 1.24 (1.10–1.39) | — |
| Prostate cancer mortality | — | 1.05 (0.96–1.15) NS |

### Risk prediction improvement (ΔC-index, grip added to office-based score)

| Outcome | ΔC-index |
|---|---|
| All-cause mortality | +0.013 |
| CVD mortality | +0.012 |
| Incident CVD | +0.009 |

- Deaths: 13,322 / 502,293 (2.7%) during mean 7.1-year follow-up
