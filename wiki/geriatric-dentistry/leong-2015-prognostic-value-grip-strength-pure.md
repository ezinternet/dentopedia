## One-line Summary

Large prospective cohort (PURE, n=139,691, 17 countries, 4.0-year follow-up) demonstrates grip strength is a stronger predictor of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality than systolic blood pressure, with HR 1.16 per 5 kg grip-strength reduction for all-cause mortality.

## Summary

The PURE study enrolled 142,861 participants from 17 countries (low, middle, and high income) between 2003–2009, following 139,691 with known vital status for a median 4.0 years. Grip strength, measured by a Jamar dynamometer, was inversely associated with all-cause mortality (HR 1.16 per 5 kg reduction), cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.17), non-cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.17), myocardial infarction (HR 1.07), and stroke (HR 1.09). Critically, grip strength was a stronger predictor of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality than systolic blood pressure — supporting its role as a simple, inexpensive, universally applicable physical-frailty screening measure. These associations were consistent across country-income strata (except cancer and respiratory hospitalization), establishing the generalizability of grip strength as a prognostic biomarker.

In the context of geriatric dentistry, this paper underpins the physical-frailty (신체노쇠, Physical Frailty) dimension of the oral frailty (구강노쇠, Oral Frailty) cluster. The oral frailty literature (Dibello 2022, Zhu 2024) shows oral frailty is associated with physical frailty (OR 1.67) — grip strength is the standard measurement instrument for that physical-frailty endpoint. Dental clinicians can apply grip strength screening as a rapid co-indicator of overall frailty risk during geriatric oral health assessment.

## Key Contributions
- Establishes grip strength as a **stronger mortality predictor than systolic blood pressure** in the world's largest multi-country prospective cohort.
- Demonstrates consistency across low/middle/high-income country strata — universally applicable biomarker.
- Provides the physical-frailty evidential anchor for the oral frailty research cluster: "physical frailty" in oral frailty SRs (e.g., Zhu 2024) relies on grip strength as its operational definition.
- Motivates grip strength measurement in dental geriatric settings as a simple, chair-side frailty co-screen.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Large prospective cohort (PURE study)
- **n**: 139,691 participants (142,861 enrolled; 17 countries)
- **Follow-up**: Median 4.0 years (IQR 2.9–5.1)
- **Exposure**: Grip strength (Jamar dynamometer)
- **Primary outcomes**: All-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, non-cardiovascular mortality
- **Secondary outcomes**: MI, stroke, diabetes, cancer, pneumonia/COPD hospitalization, fall injury, fracture
- **Analysis**: Multivariable Cox proportional hazards; Fine & Gray competing-risk model; adjusted for age, sex, education, physical activity, diet, BMI, smoking, alcohol

## Results

| Outcome | HR per 5 kg grip reduction | 95% CI | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| All-cause mortality | 1.16 | 1.13–1.20 | <0.0001 |
| Cardiovascular mortality | 1.17 | 1.11–1.24 | <0.0001 |
| Non-cardiovascular mortality | 1.17 | 1.12–1.21 | <0.0001 |
| Myocardial infarction | 1.07 | 1.02–1.11 | 0.002 |
| Stroke | 1.09 | 1.05–1.15 | <0.0001 |
| Cancer (high-income only) | 0.916 | 0.880–0.953 | <0.0001 |

- Deaths during follow-up: 3,379 (2% of 139,691)
- Grip strength vs systolic blood pressure: grip strength was the stronger predictor of both all-cause and cardiovascular mortality.
- No significant association: incident diabetes, fall injury, fracture, pneumonia/COPD hospitalization.
