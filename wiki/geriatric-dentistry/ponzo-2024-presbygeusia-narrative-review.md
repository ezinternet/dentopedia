## One-line Summary

Updated narrative review (15 observational studies of healthy elderly ≥65y vs. younger controls, ROBINS-I risk-of-bias assessed) concluding presbygeusia (age-related taste alteration without pathology) is a real, relatively frequent phenomenon — driven by reduced fungiform papillae density, taste bud homeostasis decline, altered saliva composition/mucin, central nervous system changes, and oral microbiome shifts — but its clinical impact on dietary choices in the elderly is likely modest, since cultural/psychological/social factors dominate food-preference determination.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1007/s40520-024-02739-1](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40520-024-02739-1)), this updated narrative review set out to determine whether "presbygeusia" — age-related taste impairment — exists as a distinct entity in healthy elderly people, separate from taste dysfunction caused by disease, medication, or other pathology. After screening 837 records down to 15 eligible observational studies (each explicitly restricted to healthy elderly without confounding comorbidities or drug effects) and appraising risk of bias with ROBINS-I, the authors found that 12 of 15 studies support the existence of presbygeusia: taste thresholds (especially for sour and bitter) rise measurably with age even in the absence of disease, while sweet perception is relatively preserved. Proposed mechanisms span the periphery (reduced fungiform papillae density and vascularization, taste bud stem-cell/homeostasis decline, altered salivary flow and mucin glycosylation impairing bitter-receptor activation, ENaC/TRPV1 gene variants), the CNS (altered brain activation patterns during taste processing), mechanical factors (reduced mastication, dentures), retro-nasal olfactory loss, and oral microbiome shifts. Critically, the review separates the mechanistic reality of presbygeusia from its clinical relevance: despite measurable threshold changes, the link between taste alteration and actual food preference/choice in the elderly is weak, since cultural, psychological, and social factors (income, education, cooking knowledge, living situation, past experience) explain far more variance in elderly dietary behavior than sensory decline alone (genetics account for only ~20% of food-preference variation).

Clinically, this provides the physiological baseline against which pathological taste loss (medication-induced, autoimmune, viral, radiation-induced) should be measured in older dental patients — some degree of taste blunting, particularly for sour/bitter, is expected with normal aging and should not automatically be attributed to a treatable cause, though its practical dietary impact is usually modest compared to other patient factors.

## Key Contributions

- Directly tests whether "presbygeusia" is real by restricting evidence review to studies of healthy elderly explicitly excluding comorbidities and drug effects — a stricter evidentiary bar than most prior taste-aging literature.
- Compiles a comprehensive mechanistic map spanning peripheral (fungiform papillae, taste bud homeostasis, gene polymorphisms, saliva/mucin composition), central (brain activation), mechanical (mastication/dentures), olfactory, and oral-microbiome pathways.
- Finds 12/15 included studies support presbygeusia's existence, with sour/bitter most affected and sweet relatively preserved.
- Explicitly separates the mechanistic reality of age-related taste decline from its (limited) clinical/nutritional significance — food choice in the elderly is driven far more by cultural/social/psychological factors than by taste threshold changes.

## Methodology

Updated narrative review with systematic-style search (PubMed, Cochrane Library, EMBASE, CINAHL to May 30, 2023) using ('taste alteration*' OR 'taste dysfunction*' OR 'taste problem*') AND (aging OR age-related OR elderly). 837 records screened by two independent reviewers → 762 excluded on title/abstract → full-text review for eligibility (adults ≥65y without evident pathological causes vs. healthy younger controls) → 15 observational studies included, each independently risk-of-bias appraised via ROBINS-I (overall moderate risk across all 15; serious risk in 3 due to selection bias/uncontrolled confounders).

## Results

1. **Prevalence**: general population TA prevalence 5–20% (complete ageusia <3%, more common in men); elderly-specific prevalence 10–30%.
2. **Taste-quality pattern**: sour and bitter most compromised with age; sweet perception relatively maintained across most cited studies (some regional/methodological exceptions noted).
3. **Study balance**: 12/15 included studies support presbygeusia's existence (in favor: Fark 2013, Fukunaga 2005, Mojet 2001/2003, Nakazato 2002, Pavlidis 2013, Pingel 2010, Sato 2022, Schiffman 1979/1981/1994, Yamauchi 2002); 3 present controversial/negative findings (Drewnowski 1996 — no age-related salt-taste decline in food matrix; Wang 2020 — sweet/salty identification unaffected by age though overall scores declined; Wayler 1990 — no significant recognition-threshold difference for salt/sucrose between age groups).
4. **Thresholds**: electrogustometry threshold increases begin at age 60 (chorda tympani/glossopharyngeal areas) and age 70 (greater petrosal nerve area); amino acid taste thresholds ~2.5× higher in elderly (75–87y) vs. young (17–27y) subjects.
5. **Mechanisms (Table 1)**: reduced fungiform papillae density/vascularization; taste bud/stem-cell homeostasis decline; ENaC (SCNN1B)/TRPV1 gene polymorphisms affecting suprathreshold salt perception; decreased salivary flow and altered mucin glycosylation impairing bitter-receptor activation; central nervous system activation changes (EEG/fMRI); reduced mastication and denture use; olfactory epithelium/retro-nasal stimulation decline; oral microbiome shifts.
6. **Clinical relevance**: despite measurable presbygeusia, its link to food preference/choice is weak — genetics explain only ~20% of food-preference variance; socio-cultural factors (income, education, cooking knowledge), biological/psychological/situational factors (living alone), and product characteristics dominate elderly dietary decision-making.
