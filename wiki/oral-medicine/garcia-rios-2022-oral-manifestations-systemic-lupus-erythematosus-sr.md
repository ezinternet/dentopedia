## One-line Summary

Systematic review (15 STROBE-appraised observational studies, PRISMA/PROSPERO CRD42021291356) finding oral ulcers the most prevalent oral manifestation of SLE (11 of 14 prevalence studies), most commonly on the hard palate, with hyposalivation, pigmentation, glossodynia, cheilitis and secondary Sjögren also frequent.

## 한줄요약

전신홍반루푸스(SLE) 환자의 구강 증상을 다룬 15편 관찰연구 체계적 고찰 — 구강궤양(oral ulcer)이 가장 흔한 증상(14편 중 11편)이며 경구개(hard palate)에 호발, 타액감소·색소침착·설통·구순염·이차 쇼그렌도 빈번. 구강궤양은 청소년기 발병 SLE에서 특히 흔하다.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3390/ijerph191911910](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph191911910)), this PRISMA systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42021291356) of 15 observational studies (2012–2022, 11/15 low risk of bias on STROBE) qualitatively synthesizes the oral manifestations of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). Oral ulcers are by far the most prevalent finding — reported as the dominant manifestation in 11 of 14 prevalence-reporting studies — and are themselves an EULAR/ACR classification criterion for SLE. The hard palate is the most frequent site (agreed by all 6 location-reporting studies), followed by the soft palate and lower-lip vermilion. Beyond ulcers, the review documents hyposalivation (>75% in one study), antimalarial-related hyperpigmentation, oral mucosal lesions, caries, periodontal disease, candidiasis, glossodynia, fissured tongue, cheilitis, dysphagia and dysgeusia, plus secondary Sjögren. A juvenile- vs adult-onset gradient is consistent: oral ulcers are significantly more common in juvenile-onset SLE (e.g., 45.5% vs 17.5%), whereas hyposalivation and secondary Sjögren are more characteristic of adult/late-onset disease.

Clinically: treat oral ulcers — especially on the hard palate and especially in younger patients — and unexplained hyposalivation as early red flags that should prompt SLE consideration and rheumatology referral; because SLE oral lesions mimic lichen planus, pemphigus, syphilis and COVID-19, a differential diagnosis is mandatory, and ulcer recurrence/persistence may signal higher disease activity and worse prognosis.

## Key Contributions

- First-line consolidation of 15 STROBE-appraised observational studies establishing oral ulcers as the dominant oral manifestation of SLE and a diagnostic anchor.
- Identifies the hard palate as the most consistent location of SLE oral lesions.
- Documents a juvenile- vs adult/late-onset split: ulcers favor juvenile onset; hyposalivation and secondary Sjögren favor adult/late onset.
- Ties oral findings to mechanism and activity (CD34 polymorphism, VEGF dysregulation, antiphospholipid antibody, immunosuppressant-related periodontal/candidal disease).

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA systematic review, qualitative synthesis (no meta-analysis); PROSPERO CRD42021291356.
- **Question (PCO)**: oral manifestations in SLE patients vs healthy controls.
- **Databases**: PubMed/Medline, Web of Science, Scopus (searched 19 Nov 2021, updated 15 Feb 2022); MeSH terms for SLE (incl. Libman–Sacks disease) AND "oral manifestations".
- **Eligibility**: 2012–2022, English/Spanish, identifying oral manifestations in SLE; systemic-only reports excluded.
- **Extraction**: single reviewer (PGR) — study type, manifestations, locations, treatment, differential diagnosis, associations (p-value).
- **Quality**: STROBE 22-item scale by consensus (16–22 low / 8–15 moderate / ≤7 high risk of bias).

## Results

- **PRISMA flow**: 180 records (PubMed 70, WoS 25, Scopus 85) → 65 duplicates removed → 115 screened → 94 excluded → 21 full-text → 6 excluded → **15 included**.
- **Risk of bias**: 11/15 low, 4/15 moderate, 0 high.
- **Oral ulcers**: most prevalent manifestation in **11 of 14** prevalence-reporting studies; a formal SLE classification criterion.
- **Location**: **hard palate** most frequent (all 6 location-reporting studies), then soft palate and lower-lip vermilion.
- **Oral involvement overall**: 61.4% (Zakeri et al.) — aphthous ulcers, erosion, hyperkeratosis, pigmentation predominant.
- **Hyposalivation**: >75% of SLE patients (Leite et al.); worsens with age and disease activity; drives xerostomia.
- **Other lesions**: antimalarial hyperpigmentation, mucosal lesions, caries, periodontal disease, candidiasis, glossodynia, fissured (cleft) tongue, angular cheilitis, dysphagia, dysgeusia.
- **Mechanistic associations**: CD34 polymorphism and VEGF-pathway dysregulation linked to oral ulceration; antiphospholipid (APL) antibody associated with *lower* ulcer prevalence; periodontal disease more common when SLE duration >8 years.
- **Juvenile vs adult onset**: oral ulceration 45.5% (juvenile) vs 17.5% (adult) (Fonseca et al.); 23.1% vs 15.4% (Artim-Esen et al.); secondary Sjögren significantly more prevalent in late-onset SLE.
- **Conclusion**: oral ulcers, hyposalivation, pigmentations, glossodynia, cleft tongue, cheilitis, arthritis and secondary Sjögren are the most frequently SLE-associated oral findings; ulcers in young patients and hyposalivation in adult/late-onset patients are especially noteworthy as early-diagnosis signals.
