## One-line Summary

Web-based cross-sectional survey of 634 Italian dentists: 29% were unaware that post-traumatic trigeminal neuropathic pain (PTTN) exists and >80% received no teaching about it, yet 70% had encountered suspected cases — most managed with irreversible procedures (retreatment/apicectomy/extraction) and only ~1 in 3 referred to a pain specialist, exposing a misdiagnosis-driven public-health gap.

## Summary

Viscuso et al. ran an anonymous Google Forms epidemiological survey of 634 Italian dentists (Oct–Dec 2021) to quantify awareness, education, clinical encounter, and management of **post-traumatic trigeminal neuropathic pain (PTTN)** — a neuropathic pain in the trigeminal distribution that follows nerve trauma (commonly root canal therapy or extraction) and persists >3 months. The headline finding is a large knowledge gap with clinical consequences: roughly **1 in 3 dentists did not know PTTN exists**, **>80% were never taught about it**, yet **~70% had encountered a suspected case**. Because the differential diagnosis against odontogenic pain is missed, dentists predominantly respond with **irreversible procedures** (endodontic retreatment, apicectomy, extraction of the suspected or even adjacent teeth) instead of referral — only about one in three referred to a pain specialist. The authors frame this as a preventable public-health problem with high socioeconomic cost and propose education and orofacial-pain specialist integration.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies the **awareness gap** (28.7% unaware PTTN exists) and the **education gap** (>80% untaught) among practising dentists.
- Shows the gap drives **misdiagnosis and unnecessary irreversible treatment** rather than referral.
- Demonstrates statistically that **unawareness propagates** — dentists unaware of PTTN also fail on differential-diagnosis awareness, encounter recognition, and referral (p<0.05).
- Estimates population scale: applying the 60.7% root-canal-trigger rate to worldwide root canal volume → **>150 million individuals at risk**.

## Methodology

- Cross-sectional web-based epidemiological survey (self-labelled prospective), IRB University of Cagliari PROT. NP/2021/5460; Declaration of Helsinki; anonymous informed consent.
- **n = 634** Italian dentists recruited via social media, Oct–Dec 2021; ~3-min Google Forms questionnaire.
- 9 substantive multiple-choice items: existence awareness, teaching received, differential-diagnosis awareness, encountered cases, referral history, triggering events, and management after root canal vs after extraction.
- Statistics: Python (Pandas/SciPy); chi-square and Fisher's exact tests; alpha 0.05.

## Results

| Domain | Finding |
|---|---|
| Aware PTTN exists | 71.3% yes / **28.7% no** |
| Received teaching on PTTN | ~80% no (discussion: >80% untaught) |
| Aware differential dx needed | 88.3% yes |
| Ever referred suspected neuropathic pain | 49% yes / 51% no |
| Encountered suspected PTTN | **71.7%** yes |

- **Triggering events** (n=412, multiple answers): well-performed root canal **60.7%** (most common); extraction **42.7%**; spontaneous 37.10%; other procedures 21.4%.
- **After root canal** (n=370): pharmacologic 57.6%; endodontic retreatment 48.6%; extracted devitalized tooth 22.4%; apicectomy 8.6%; referral ~30% (<1 in 3); "didn't know what to do" 13.8%.
- **After extraction** (n=339): pharmacologic 70.8%; surgical site revision ~25%; treated/extracted neighboring teeth 4.10%; referral 30.7%; "didn't know what to do" 11.4%.
- **Inferential**: unawareness of PTTN significantly associated with negative responses across all items (p<0.05); only PTTN-aware dentists referred; referral after root-canal pain significant (p=0.003).
- **Costs** (cited): direct €1396/patient/yr (Belgium, 5y); neuropathic-pain indirect costs up to €14,446/patient (Germany).

**Bottom line**: most dentists meet PTTN but lack the knowledge to recognize it, so the default response is irreversible dental treatment of an innocent tooth rather than specialist referral — driving diagnostic delays (>2 years reported) and avoidable harm.
