## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3390/ijms21082941](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms21082941)), this review condenses Japan's official 2018 Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency (issued by the Japanese Society of Clinical Nutrition), with a dedicated section on zinc-deficiency taste disorders as one of the guideline's principal clinical applications. The guideline establishes actionable serum-zinc cutoffs (<60 μg/dL deficiency, 60–80 μg/dL marginal deficiency, >80 μg/dL normal) within a 4-criterion diagnostic algorithm (symptoms/low ALP + exclusion of other disease + low serum zinc + symptomatic response to zinc administration), while explicitly acknowledging that static serum zinc has limited sensitivity for marginal deficiency — several cited studies found symptomatic, treatment-responsive patients with serum zinc as high as 75–78 μg/dL. Zinc-deficiency taste disorder is a large and growing clinical problem in Japan (~240,000 affected patients, a 1.7-fold increase since 1990, more common in women and the elderly), mechanistically linked in rat models to taste-cell microvilli rupture, impaired basal-cell differentiation, prolonged taste-cell turnover, and reduced bitter-taste-receptor (TAS2R40/TAS2R107) expression. Oral zinc replacement (standard Japanese first-line agent: Polaprezinc 150mg/day) improves taste disorder symptoms in 50–82% of patients across various zinc-salt regimens, but the review's most clinically important finding is that efficacy is markedly delayed and cumulative — only 13.6% response at 4 weeks, rising to 47.7% at 12 weeks and 58.8% at 24 weeks — meaning a full 3-month treatment trial is required before a zinc-deficiency taste disorder can be judged treatment-resistant. The review extends beyond taste disorders to document zinc deficiency's broader systemic roles in inflammatory bowel disease (via IRF/IL-23-mediated M1 macrophage activation, with serum zinc <80 μg/dL in >75% of Crohn's disease patients and inversely correlated with disease activity) and liver cirrhosis (via reduced zinc-dependent ornithine transcarbamylase activity impairing hepatic ammonia detoxification).

Clinically, this provides the practical diagnostic and treatment protocol counterpart to the mechanistic zinc-deficiency discussion raised in COVID-19-related taste/saliva dysfunction literature: it establishes that (1) serum zinc alone can miss clinically significant deficiency, (2) empirical zinc trials of ≥3 months are warranted before concluding a taste disorder is zinc-unresponsive, and (3) zinc deficiency as a taste-disorder etiology is not COVID-specific but a common, often under-recognized standalone condition, particularly in the elderly and in patients with chronic disease.

## Key Contributions

- Provides an actionable clinical diagnostic algorithm with explicit serum-zinc cutoffs (<60 μg/dL deficiency, 60–80 μg/dL marginal, >80 μg/dL normal) within a 4-criterion framework (symptom/low-ALP + disease exclusion + low serum zinc + zinc-responsiveness).
- Quantifies zinc-deficiency taste disorder epidemiology in Japan (~240,000 patients, 1.7× increase since 1990, female-predominant, rising with population aging).
- Documents the critical treatment-duration finding for Polaprezinc: efficacy climbs from 13.6% (4 weeks) to 47.7% (12 weeks) to 58.8% (24 weeks) — establishing ≥3 months as the minimum trial duration before judging treatment failure.
- Extends zinc deficiency's clinical relevance beyond taste disorders to inflammatory bowel disease (IRF/IL-23-mediated M1 macrophage activation, GATA-3/IL-4-mediated M2 suppression) and liver cirrhosis (ornithine transcarbamylase-mediated ammonia detoxification impairment), framing zinc deficiency as a multi-system rather than isolated oral condition.
- Provides rat-model mechanistic evidence for zinc-deficiency taste disorder: taste-cell microvilli rupture/vacuolation, impaired basal-cell differentiation, prolonged cell turnover, reduced bitter-taste-receptor (TAS2R40/TAS2R107) expression.

## Methodology

Narrative review condensing Japan's Practical Guideline for Zinc Deficiency 2018 (Japanese Society of Clinical Nutrition), combined with the authors' own prior cohort data (299-patient cirrhosis cohort from 14 Japanese institutes; IBD case-control/registry data) and murine mechanistic experiments (metallothionein knockout mice; TPEN zinc-chelation with TNBS-induced colitis models). No formal systematic literature search methodology is reported, consistent with a guideline-summary review format.

## Results

1. **Diagnostic criteria**: serum zinc <60 μg/dL = deficiency, 60–80 μg/dL = marginal deficiency, >80 μg/dL = normal — though several cited studies found symptomatic, zinc-treatment-responsive patients with serum zinc up to 75–78 μg/dL, underscoring the diagnostic criterion's limited sensitivity.
2. **Taste disorder epidemiology**: ~240,000 affected patients in Japan (1.7× increase since 1990), male:female ratio 2:3, rising with population aging; zinc-deficiency taste disorder is one of several taste-disorder subtypes (alongside idiopathic, drug-induced, psychogenic, and systemic taste disorder — idiopathic taste disorder is considered likely zinc-deficiency-associated as well).
3. **Treatment response rates**: 50–82% improvement across different zinc-salt regimens (Yoshida 82% at 67.5mg zinc gluconate; Sakai 76% at 87mg zinc picolinate; Heckmann 50% at 20mg zinc gluconate; Sakagami 80% at 150mg Polaprezinc, the Japanese standard first-line agent).
4. **Treatment-duration dependency**: Polaprezinc efficacy rises from 13.6% (4 weeks) to 47.7% (12 weeks) to 58.8% (24 weeks) — a ≥3-month trial is required before concluding treatment failure.
5. **Population zinc intake**: recommended intake 10mg/day (men)/8mg/day (women) in Japan vs. actual mean intake 8.9mg/day (men)/7.3mg/day (women) — roughly half the population at risk.
6. **Adverse effects/monitoring**: nausea/vomiting (mitigated by post-meal dosing), copper deficiency (anemia, leukopenia) — serum zinc and copper should be monitored every 3–4 months during therapy.
7. **Systemic mechanistic extensions**: serum zinc <80 μg/dL in >75% of Crohn's disease patients, inversely correlated with Harvey-Bradshaw Index (r=−0.75, p<0.05); zinc deficiency shifts macrophages toward pro-inflammatory M1 (IL-23p19-producing) phenotype via IRF5, driving Th17-mediated colonic inflammation; in cirrhosis, serum zinc correlates positively with albumin/BCAA-tyrosine ratio and negatively with ammonia, and zinc supplementation (150mg/day, 3-month RCT) significantly improved both parameters.
