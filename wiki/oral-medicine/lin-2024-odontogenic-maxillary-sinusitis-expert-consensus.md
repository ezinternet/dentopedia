## Summary
This open-access expert consensus, led by Beijing TongRen Hospital (Capital Medical University), addresses the lack of standardized diagnostic and treatment protocols for odontogenic maxillary sinusitis (OMS) by bringing together otolaryngologists and dentists from 15+ major Chinese medical centers. The consensus synthesizes reviewed literature and practical expert experience to define OMS characteristics, propose a classification system, establish diagnostic criteria, and outline multidisciplinary treatment algorithms. Epidemiologically, CT-based studies show OMS accounts for 45–72% of unilateral maxillary sinus opacification cases; a meta-analysis of 31 studies gives an aggregated prevalence of ~51%. Because OMS has distinct oral infectious pathogeny, it is resistant to conventional rhinosinusitis therapy, and multidisciplinary cooperation between ENT and dentistry is "absolutely urgent."

## Key Contributions
- Standardized OMS classification distinguishing etiology (iatrogenic vs. infectious) and disease stage
- Evidence-based diagnostic criteria incorporating CT/CBCT, nasal endoscopy, dental examination
- Multidisciplinary treatment decision algorithms: dental cause elimination + sinus drainage (ESS) with timing guidance
- Addresses management of complications: OAF, recurrence, co-existing fungal ball, extrasinus spread
- Calls attention to OMS underrepresentation in the literature and advocates for higher-quality future research

## Methodology
- Expert consensus based on systematically reviewed literature
- Expert group: 20+ authors from 15 institutions; ENT, oral surgery, periodontics, implantology, endodontics, oral pathology
- Beijing TongRen Hospital ENT and Stomatology departments as lead centers
- Published as open-access article in International Journal of Oral Science (Nature Publishing Group)

## Results

### Epidemiology
- ~15% of acute rhinosinusitis is odontogenic (retrospective cohort, n=385)
- ~25% of chronic maxillary sinusitis attributable to dental pathologies
- CT-based: 45–72% of unilateral maxillary opacification cases are OMS (multiple studies, 130–190 patients each)
- Meta-analysis (31 studies): 51% per-sinus, 50% per-patient CT-based OMS prevalence
- Equal sex distribution; predominantly unilateral; mostly chronic but acute presentations possible
- Severe cases: orbital, intracranial, or parapharyngeal spread possible

### Diagnostic Approach
- Oral cause identification mandatory (periapical X-ray, CBCT for dental-sinus relationship)
- CBCT superior to conventional CT for spatial detail
- Nasal endoscopy: purulent discharge from middle meatus
- Clinical symptoms: unilateral nasal obstruction, foul nasal odor, dental history in posterior maxilla

### Treatment Framework (Consensus Recommendations)
- Appropriate dental treatment (primary): extraction / endodontic therapy / implant removal / periodontal treatment / OAF repair based on specific etiology
- Nasal treatment (adjunctive or concurrent): functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS/ESS) for sinus drainage and mucosal clearance
- Antibiotics: adjunctive (covering oral anaerobes)
- Multidisciplinary decision-making determines sequence (dental first vs. simultaneous vs. sinus surgery first)
- Complication management: OAF closure, recurrent sinusitis protocol, fungal ball co-treatment
