## One-line Summary

ENT-perspective narrative review of odontogenic sinusitis (ODS) presenting dual-confirmation diagnosis criteria, evidence that antibiotics fail for treatable dental pathology, 90%–100% combined resolution when both dental and sinus disease are treated, and primary ESS as a valid option for high symptom-burden patients.

## Summary

Odontogenic sinusitis (ODS) is bacterial maxillary sinusitis arising from adjacent infectious dental pathology (apical periodontitis [AP], marginal periodontitis, oroantral fistula [OAF]) or iatrogenic dental injury (implants, foreign bodies, sinus bone grafts). It accounts for 25%–40% of all chronic maxillary sinusitis and 45%–75% of unilateral maxillary sinus opacification on CT. ODS is distinct from rhinosinusitis in microbiology (alpha-hemolytic streptococci and anaerobes predominate), CT pattern (relative sparing of posterior ethmoid and sphenoid), and treatment response (oral antibiotics fail for treatable dental pathology). Diagnosis requires dual confirmation: otolaryngologists confirm sinusitis via nasal endoscopy (middle meatal purulence, edema, polyps), while dental specialists confirm dental pathology through examination and imaging. Radiologists miss 60%–70% of dental pathology on CT. When both the dental source and sinusitis are addressed, resolution rates are 90%–100%. Primary ESS resolves symptoms 7–12 days faster than primary dental treatment (35–56 days). Asymptomatic ODS occurs in ~15% of cases; complicated ODS (orbital, intracranial, osseous spread) in ~7%.

## Key Contributions

### Diagnostic Framework

ODS suspicion is driven by:
- Unilateral maxillary sinus opacification on CT
- Overt adjacent dental pathology on CT
- Unilateral middle meatal purulence on nasal endoscopy
- Subjective foul smell (two prospective cohort studies show specificity for ODS)
- Alpha-hemolytic streptococci and/or anaerobes in sinus cultures
- Relative sparing of posterior ethmoid and sphenoid sinuses

**Critical nuances:**
- 30% of AP-ODS cases show no overt dental pathology on CT — dental specialist referral still required
- Maxillary sinus mucosal thickening alone (without endoscopic purulence) is NOT ODS — present in 45%–100% of patients with dental pathology
- No dental pain in many ODS patients; patients rarely volunteer dental history

### Treatment Principles

| Scenario | Recommended approach |
|---|---|
| OAF-related ODS | ESS + OAF closure concurrent or in close proximity; 90%–100% success |
| AP-ODS (treatable) | Dental extraction OR root canal therapy; ~60% primary success (up to 77%); ESS for failures |
| Implant-related ODS | Primary ESS preferred; implant removal only if peri-implantitis or mobility |
| High symptom burden ODS | Primary ESS to resolve symptoms faster, then dental treatment |
| No treatable pathology (temporary OAC, closed OAF, sinus foreign body) | Medical therapy ± transnasal/transoral foreign body removal; no dental intervention |
| Complicated ODS (extrasinus spread) | Open all diseased sinuses; multidisciplinary management |

### ESS Extent for Uncomplicated ODS
- Maxillary antrostomy alone is acceptable regardless of CT extent
- Opening all diseased sinuses is also reasonable
- Balloon sinuplasty is NOT appropriate for ODS (severe edema/polyps prevent adequate drainage)

### Asymptomatic ODS
- ~15% of ODS cases are asymptomatic (range 1%–47%)
- Still warrants dental treatment to eliminate infectious source
- ESS discussion appropriate if sinusitis persists after dental treatment

## Methodology

Single-author narrative review; literature search through mid-2021; 55 references cited. Synthesizes data from international multidisciplinary consensus statements (IFAR 2020, IFAR 2021), prospective cohort studies, retrospective series, and case series. Presents evidence in 7 representative clinical scenarios.

## Results

| Key metric | Value |
|---|---|
| ODS / all chronic maxillary sinusitis | 25%–40% |
| ODS / unilateral maxillary opacification on CT | 45%–75% |
| Radiologist dental-pathology miss rate | 60%–70% |
