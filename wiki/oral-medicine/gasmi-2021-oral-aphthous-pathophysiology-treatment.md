## Summary

This review synthesizes the pathophysiology, clinical types, and treatment of oral aphthous ulcers. It frames three morphological types (minor, major, herpetiform) as a management-guiding classification and stresses systematic history/examination to separate truly idiopathic recurrent aphthous stomatitis (RAS) from ulcers secondary to systemic disease (Behçet, IBD, hematinic deficiency). Microbial/microbiota contributions are reviewed. The treatment ladder places topical corticosteroids as first line, reserving systemic medication for progressive or refractory disease.

## Key Contributions

- Uses the three morphological types as a management framework.
- Emphasizes workup to distinguish idiopathic RAS from systemic causes.
- Endorses topical corticosteroids as first-line therapy.
- Reviews microbial/microbiota involvement in pathogenesis.

## Methodology

Narrative review; PubMed and Google Scholar search across aphthous/canker-sore/microbiota/treatment keywords; descriptive synthesis.

## Results

Etiology ranges from local trauma to systemic disease and rare syndromes. Topical corticosteroids are first line for symptom control; systemic agents are added on progression. Higher prevalence in developed countries; slight female predominance overall, more marked in the herpetiform type.
