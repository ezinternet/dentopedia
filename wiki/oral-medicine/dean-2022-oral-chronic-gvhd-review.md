## Summary

Chronic oral graft-versus-host disease (cGVHD) is a leading cause of late morbidity in allogeneic HCT survivors, diagnosed in 30-50% of recipients (>90% within 12 months). It comprises three increasingly distinct entities: lichenoid mucositis (resembling OLP), immune-mediated salivary dysfunction (sicca/Sjögren-like), and tissue sclerosis producing trismus (scleroderma-like). Management follows NIH 2014 diagnostic/staging criteria and escalates from topical corticosteroid rinses to systemic immunosuppression, balancing disease control against infection, hyperglycemia, and secondary-malignancy risk. Oral cGVHD lesions are also a recognized OPMD/malignant-transformation site.

## Key Contributions

- Consolidates the three discrete oral cGVHD entities.
- Applies NIH 2014 diagnostic/staging criteria to the mouth.
- Maps a topical-to-systemic therapy ladder with adverse-effect trade-offs.
- Frames cGVHD as both a morbidity driver and malignant-transformation site.

## Methodology

Narrative mini-review by two oral-medicine specialists; descriptive synthesis of diagnosis, pathophysiology, and management literature; no quantitative pooling.

## Results

cGVHD affects skin, eyes, and mouth most commonly. Lichenoid mucositis ranges from asymptomatic to disabling ulceration; salivary dysfunction drives xerostomia, caries, and candidiasis; sclerosis causes trismus correlated with cutaneous (not mucosal) involvement. Topical corticosteroids are first line; refractory disease needs systemic targeted immunosuppression.
