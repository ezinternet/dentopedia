## One-line Summary

Comparative cross-sectional study (58 primary Sjögren's syndrome patients vs. 55 age/gender-matched healthy controls) finding significantly higher rates of gustatory dysfunction (ageusia/hypogeusia for sweet 34%, bitter 19%, salty 10%, sour 11%), olfactory dysfunction (anosmia OR 5.2), dysgeusia (52.6% vs. 9.4%), and burning tongue sensation (45.6% vs. 0%), all associated with significantly worse oral health-related quality of life (OHIP-14), while halitosis rates did not differ between groups.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1186/s12903-020-01169-5](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12903-020-01169-5)), this comparative cross-sectional study directly measured chemosensory function in 58 primary Sjögren's syndrome (pSS) patients versus 55 age/gender-matched healthy controls, using objective testing (Sniffin' Sticks odor identification, taste-strip gustometry for sweet/sour/salty/bitter) alongside patient-reported outcomes (dysgeusia, burning tongue sensation, halitosis, OHIP-14 quality-of-life score). pSS patients showed markedly worse gustatory function (objective taste-strip score 4.11 vs. 6.11, p<0.0001) and elevated anosmia risk (OR 5.2), plus much higher rates of dysgeusia (52.6% vs. 9.4%) and burning tongue sensation (45.6% vs. 0%) — but no significant difference in halitosis. All chemosensory impairments correlated with significantly worse oral-health-related quality of life across every OHIP-14 sub-domain. Notably, the discussion cites prior evidence that salivary flow rate correlates only weakly with chemosensory dysfunction severity, suggesting the taste/smell impairment in Sjögren's is not simply a passive downstream effect of hyposalivation, but may involve a separate inflammatory mechanism (proposed: interferon-pathway-mediated disruption of taste-bud transduction and turnover) acting in parallel with — not solely through — reduced salivary flow.

Clinically, this reinforces that in autoimmune-driven dry mouth (as opposed to medication-induced or age-related hyposalivation), taste complaints should be actively screened for and treated as a distinct symptom domain rather than assumed to resolve with salivary substitution alone; the study also flags a striking undertreatment gap — only 4% of pSS patients used artificial saliva versus 81% using artificial tears, despite comparable symptom burden.

## Key Contributions

- Directly quantifies both olfactory (odor identification test) and gustatory (four-quality taste-strip) dysfunction in primary Sjögren's syndrome using objective test batteries, not only self-report.
- Quantifies odds ratio for anosmia in pSS (OR 5.2, 95% CI 1.9–14.3) versus matched healthy controls.
- Demonstrates dysgeusia and burning tongue sensation are markedly elevated in pSS (52.6% and 45.6%) while halitosis is not — dissociating the oral symptom cluster and showing chemosensory/burning-mouth symptoms are Sjögren's-specific in a way halitosis is not.
- Links impaired chemosensory function to significantly worse oral-health-related quality of life across all four OHIP-14 sub-domains (functional, physical, psychological, social).
- Highlights that salivary flow rate correlates only weakly with chemosensory dysfunction severity in the cited literature, pointing to a parallel inflammatory (interferon-pathway) mechanism rather than hyposalivation being the sole cause of taste impairment.

## Methodology

Comparative cross-sectional study (single center, Institute of Rheumatology, University of Belgrade, 2017–2019 recruitment). 58 pSS patients (American-European Consensus classification criteria, age 25–77y) vs. 55 age/gender-matched healthy controls. Clinical/lab workup included Schirmer's I test, Rose Bengal score, salivary scintigraphy (Tc99m), labial salivary gland biopsy (Chisholm-Mason grading), and ANA/RF/anti-SS-A/anti-SS-B serology. Chemosensory testing: Sniffin' Sticks 12-odor identification test (anosmic/hyposmic/normosmic categories) and taste strips for sweet/sour/salty/bitter (0=loss, 1=reduced, 2=normal per quality; max score 8), plus self-reported VAS 0–10 for smell and taste. Patient-reported outcomes: structured questionnaire for dysgeusia/burning tongue sensation/halitosis, OHIP-14 (Serbian-validated) for oral-health-related quality of life, ESSDAI for disease activity, ESSPRI for patient-reported symptom burden. Statistics: independent-samples t-test, chi-square, odds ratios with 95% CI.

## Results

1. **Olfactory function**: self-reported VAS smell 8.57±2.21 (pSS) vs. 9.56±0.72 (controls), p=0.016; anosmia 3.8% vs. 0.0%, hyposmia 36.5% vs. 13.2% (χ²=9.9, p=0.002); OR for anosmia 5.2 (95% CI 1.9–14.3, p<0.001).
2. **Gustatory function**: self-reported VAS taste 8.48±2.10 vs. 9.54±0.67, p=0.014; objective taste-strip score 4.11±1.82 vs. 6.11±1.93, p<0.0001; impaired taste by quality — sweet 34.0% vs. 7.5% (p<0.0001), sour 10.6% vs. 0.0% (p=0.054), salty 10.0% vs. 5.7% (p=0.018), bitter 19.1% vs. 1.9% (p=0.001).
3. **Dysgeusia**: 52.6% (pSS) vs. 9.4% (controls), χ²=23.6, p<0.0001; distorted bitter taste the most common descriptor (36.7%).
4. **Burning tongue sensation (BST)**: 45.6% (pSS) vs. 0.0% (controls), χ²=31.6, p<0.0001 — a symptom essentially unique to the pSS group in this cohort.
5. **Halitosis**: 31.6% vs. 28.3%, not significant (χ²=0.40, p=0.434) — the sole oral symptom NOT differentiating pSS from controls, suggesting distinct pathophysiology from the chemosensory/burning-mouth cluster.
6. **Oral-health-related quality of life**: OHIP-14 6.79±7.03 (pSS) vs. 2.27±8.46 (controls), p<0.001, elevated across all four domains; VAS-EQ5D 6.67±2.02 vs. 8.28±1.02, p<0.0001 (lower = worse in pSS).
7. **Treatment gap**: only 4% of pSS patients used artificial saliva vs. 81% using artificial tears despite comparable/worse subjective severity — the salivary/gustatory domain appears comparatively undertreated relative to the ocular domain in routine pSS management.
