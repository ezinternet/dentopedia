## One-line Summary

Narrative review of erythema multiforme (EM): an HSV/Mycoplasma-triggered skin-and-mucosal disease with acral target lesions and painful oral involvement, distinct from SJS/TEN; managed with symptomatic care plus etiologic antiviral therapy.

## Summary

Erythema multiforme is an inflammatory skin and mucosal disease most often triggered by HSV, with Mycoplasma pneumoniae a key alternative that must be ruled out (it requires antibiotics). Characteristic acral typical/atypical target lesions accompany oral involvement that can be severely painful and warrant hospitalization. The review stresses differentiation from Stevens-Johnson syndrome/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis, outlines symptomatic and etiologic management, and addresses the difficult persistent/recurrent subset where suppressive antivirals fail and breakthrough immunomodulatory treatments are used.

## Key Contributions

- Distinguishes EM from SJS/TEN as a separate infection-driven entity.
- Identifies HSV as the dominant trigger; Mycoplasma as must-rule-out.
- Outlines symptomatic plus etiologic (antiviral) management.
- Addresses refractory recurrent EM and breakthrough therapies.

## Methodology

Comprehensive narrative review integrating clinical, etiologic, histopathologic, and management literature.

## Results

Estimated prevalence 0.01-1%, young adults, female preponderance. Oral mucosa affected alone or with other sites; severe oral pain may require hospitalization. Recurrent EM is usually HSV-driven and treated with suppressive antivirals; antiviral-refractory cases use immunomodulatory breakthrough options.
