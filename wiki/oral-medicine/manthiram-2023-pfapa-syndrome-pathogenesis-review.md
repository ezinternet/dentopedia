## Summary

PFAPA (periodic fever, aphthous stomatitis, pharyngitis, cervical adenitis) is the most common childhood periodic-fever syndrome, presenting with regularly timed fevers plus oral aphthae, pharyngitis, or cervical adenitis. This review summarizes recent genetics: no causative rare mutations have emerged, suggesting a complex polygenic disorder with susceptibility loci shared with Behçet disease and recurrent aphthous ulcers. Tonsillectomy is effective even in patients not meeting full diagnostic criteria, though the tonsillar immune profile differs. These findings blur PFAPA's nosological boundaries with related recurrent-fever and aphthous phenotypes.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes PFAPA as a complex/polygenic autoinflammatory disorder.
- Links susceptibility to Behcet disease and recurrent aphthous ulcers.
- Documents tonsillectomy efficacy beyond strict criteria.
- Raises questions about PFAPA's diagnostic definition.

## Methodology

Narrative review of recent genetic-association, linkage, and tonsillectomy-outcome studies.

## Results

No causative rare mutations identified — susceptibility appears common-variant/polygenic. Shared loci connect PFAPA to Behçet disease and recurrent aphthous ulcers. Tonsillectomy effective in broader cohorts, with distinct tonsillar immunology and partly identified response predictors.
