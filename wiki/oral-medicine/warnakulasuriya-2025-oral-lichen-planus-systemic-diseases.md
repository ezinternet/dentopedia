## One-line Summary

Critical review (J Dent Res 2025) synthesizing recent (≤3 y) meta-analyses: oral lichen planus (OLP) patients carry significantly elevated prevalence and risk of diabetes mellitus (PP 9.77%, OR 1.64), Hashimoto thyroiditis (8.60%, OR 2.2), hypo/hyperthyroidism, celiac disease (8.66%, OR 18.44), hepatitis C (7.14%, OR 4.09) and B, steatohepatitis, cirrhosis, and emotional disorders (depression 31%, anxiety 55%, stress 41%); many comorbidities are asymptomatic and act as cancer risk factors, so OLP warrants oral-systemic vigilance and referral.

## Summary

Oral lichen planus (OLP) is a T-cell-mediated chronic inflammatory oral mucosal disease affecting ~1% of the population and classified as an oral potentially malignant disorder (malignant transformation 1.14%–2.28%). This critical review reframes OLP as a marker of substantial *systemic* disease burden: drawing almost entirely on meta-analyses published in the prior three years (mostly the authors' own Granada group), it tabulates pooled prevalence and odds ratios for ~20 systemic conditions and pairs each significant association with a biological-plausibility mechanism and a concrete clinical recommendation for the dentist. The recurring clinical message is that many of these comorbidities (subclinical thyroid disease, asymptomatic hepatitis B/C, steatohepatitis, undiagnosed celiac disease) are silent and unrecognized, and several act as risk factors for cancer (liver, thyroid, small intestine, and — via HCV — oral cavity). A practical pharmacologic caveat is highlighted: corticosteroids used to treat OLP can worsen glycemic control and blood pressure, making interdisciplinary management important in diabetic and hypertensive patients.

## Key Contributions

- A single consolidated evidence table of meta-analytic prevalence + OR for systemic disease in OLP (see below).
- Mechanistic framing per association: shared chronic inflammation (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β), autoimmune cross-reactivity / molecular mimicry (thyroid, celiac), and viral antigen presentation (hepatitis).
- Translation into dentist-actionable vigilance: glycemia and blood-pressure monitoring (especially on corticosteroids), thyroid- and celiac-symptom awareness, vigilance for asymptomatic liver disease, and mental-health referral.

## Methodology

Narrative critical review (confidence `narrative-review`). Quantitative backbone = recent meta-analyses (De Porras-Carrique / González-Moles / García-Pola / Serni, 2022–2024) whose risk-of-bias was assessed with the Joanna Briggs Institute prevalence tool (10 domains). All cited primary studies required reticular white lesions for OLP diagnosis; most used biopsy confirmation. Sjögren's syndrome excluded for insufficient evidence. Not a de novo systematic search.

## Results

Pooled prevalence (PP) and odds ratio (OR, vs general population) — significant associations:

| Comorbidity | PP | OR (95% CI) | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anxiety | 54.76% | 3.51 (2.10–5.85) | <0.001 |
| Stress | 41.10% | 3.64 (1.48–8.94) | 0.005 |
| Depression | 31.19% | 6.15 (2.72–13.89) | <0.001 |
| Hypertension | 24.17% | 1.28 (1.01–1.63) | 0.04 (weak) |
| Diabetes mellitus | 9.41% (abstract 9.77%) | 1.64 (1.34–2.00) | <0.001 |
| Celiac disease | 8.66% | 18.44 (2.40–141.43) | 0.005 |
| Hashimoto's thyroiditis | 8.60% | 2.23 (1.76–2.82) | <0.001 |
| Hypothyroidism | 8.14% | 1.65 (1.08–2.53) | 0.02 |
| Hepatitis C | 7.14% | 4.09 (2.77–6.03) | <0.001 |
| Steatohepatitis | 7.06% | 5.71 (0.97–33.60) | 0.05 |
| Liver cirrhosis | 4.27% | 5.58 (1.83–16.96) | 0.002 |
| Hepatitis B | 3.90% | 1.62 (1.01–2.40) | 0.02 |
| Hyperthyroidism | 2.84% | 2.11 (1.22–3.63) | 0.007 |

Non-significant / weak associations: fibromyalgia, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren's, lupus, psoriasis, vitiligo, and hepatocellular carcinoma itself (OR 0.94).

Disease-specific points:
- **Diabetes**: mainly type 2; meta-analysis 116 studies / 17,609 patients. Indirect estimate — ~7.8 million OLP patients worldwide are diabetic, ~2.8 million unaware. Association likely bidirectional; corticosteroid OLP therapy raises glucose.
- **Thyroid** (75 studies / 15,176): Hashimoto's most prevalent; mechanism via cross-immunity and TSHR overexpression in OLP lesions. Hashimoto's carries broad cancer risk; oral cancer risk in OLP+Hashimoto's not yet studied.
- **Celiac**: strongest OR (18.44) but wide CI; molecular mimicry via tissue transglutaminase proposed. Routine screening not yet recommended.
- **Liver** (146 studies / 21,187): HCV is dominant — increases oral cancer risk in OLP (RR 3.67); HCV RNA found in lichen planus lesions; direct-acting antivirals reduce HCC risk 50%–80% and improve OLP. Europe has highest hepatitis B/C prevalence in OLP. 90% of liver carcinomas derive from cirrhosis.
- **Emotional** (51 studies / 6,815): highest prevalences (anxiety 54.8%, stress 41.1%, depression 31.2%); proposed unidirectional emotional→OLP via stress-driven proinflammatory state.
- **Grinspan's syndrome** (OLP+DM+hypertension, 1966): currently in question — no meta-analysis confirms concurrent DM+hypertension prevalence; hypertension evidence is weak.

## Clinical Bottom Line

Treat OLP as a sentinel for hidden systemic disease. In OLP patients: monitor glycemia and blood pressure (especially when prescribing corticosteroids), stay alert to thyroid-dysfunction and celiac symptoms, maintain vigilance for asymptomatic hepatitis B/C and steatohepatitis, and refer for depression/anxiety/stress. Causal direction is unproven for most links and routine screening is not yet evidence-justified — so the actionable layer is awareness, symptom-triggered work-up, and interdisciplinary referral rather than blanket testing.
