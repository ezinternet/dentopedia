## One-line Summary

NeuPSIG (PAIN) review concluding primary BMS is a neuropathic pain condition with neurophysiologically verifiable lesions, divisible into peripheral and central subtypes (often overlapping) that differ in treatment response and psychiatric comorbidity.

## Summary

Primary burning mouth syndrome (BMS) is defined as intraoral burning or dysaesthetic sensation recurring daily for more than three months without clinically evident causative lesions (IHS 2013), often accompanied by dysgeusia and xerostomia. Although clinical signs of neuropathy are absent, sensitive diagnostic methods reveal neuropathic involvement at multiple levels of the neuraxis: peripheral small-fiber damage (thermal QST, electrogustatometry, epithelial nerve fiber density), trigeminal lesions peripherally or in the brainstem (brainstem reflexes, trigeminal neurography, evoked potentials), and decreased central inhibition (deficient brainstem reflex habituation, positive QST signs, neurotransmitter-PET indicating deficient striatal dopamine). On this basis the review divides BMS into a peripheral neuropathic subtype and a central subtype that may overlap within a patient. The peripheral type responds to peripheral lidocaine blocks and topical clonazepam; the central type does not respond to local treatment and frequently associates with depression/anxiety. A unifying hypothesis is that, in postmenopausal women, decline in neuroprotective gonadal hormones plus rising stress hormones renders vulnerable small fibers and basal ganglia susceptible to neurotoxic damage.

## Key Contributions

- Provides an authoritative neurophysiologic case that primary BMS is genuinely neuropathic despite normal routine clinical exam.
- Establishes a clinically actionable two-subtype model (peripheral vs central) with distinct treatment responses.
- Links electrogustatometry abnormalities to small Aδ taste afferent involvement alongside somatosensory small fibers.
- Articulates the postmenopausal neurosteroid/gonadal-decline hypothesis for BMS vulnerability.

## Methodology

Expert/narrative NeuPSIG review synthesizing neurophysiologic evidence across peripheral small-fiber testing, trigeminal system electrophysiology, and central inhibition/neurotransmitter imaging. No pooled statistics.

## Results

Qualitative. The subtype classification is the key clinical translation: peripheral-type BMS is the subgroup expected to respond to topical/peripheral therapy (lidocaine block, topical clonazepam), whereas central-type BMS needs centrally acting therapy and attention to psychiatric comorbidity.

## Clinical Decision Point

Subtype determination guides therapy: a positive response to peripheral lidocaine block suggests peripheral neuropathic BMS amenable to topical clonazepam; non-response plus psychiatric comorbidity points to central-type management.
