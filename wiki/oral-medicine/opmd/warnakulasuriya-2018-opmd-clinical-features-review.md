Narrative review of the clinical features of the full OPMD spectrum (leukoplakia, erythroplakia, OSF, OLP, lichenoid/GvHD lesions), stressing clinical subtyping, mandatory biopsy, and timely specialist referral.

## Summary

This focus-issue lead review describes the clinical appearance and presentation of the OPMD spectrum as a diagnostic prelude for clinicians. It expands the umbrella to include oral lichenoid lesions and oral GvHD lesions and reinforces biopsy as essential to confirm a provisional clinical diagnosis. Leukoplakia is the most common OPMD; erythroplakia is rarer but higher-risk. Homogeneous versus non-homogeneous leukoplakia subtyping offers limited prognostication. Because many OPMDs are asymptomatic early and detected on routine dental examination, the dentist's screening role is emphasized.

## Key Contributions

- Catalogs the clinical appearance of each OPMD.
- Expands the OPMD umbrella to lichenoid and GvHD lesions.
- Reinforces biopsy as essential for diagnosis.
- Highlights the dentist's screening/referral role.

## Methodology

Narrative clinical review by a single authoritative author; descriptive synthesis without quantitative pooling.

## Results

Leukoplakia most common; erythroplakia rarer but higher malignant risk. Homogeneous vs non-homogeneous subtyping aids limited prognostication. Population-specific entities (OSF in areca users, palatal lesions in reverse smokers). Early asymptomatic presentation underscores routine-exam detection.
