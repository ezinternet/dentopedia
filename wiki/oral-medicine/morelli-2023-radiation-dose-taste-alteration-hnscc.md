## One-line Summary

Prospective observational cohort (31 HNSCC patients undergoing IMRT, CiTAS/EORTC questionnaires at baseline, 3 weeks, 3 months) finding acute dysgeusia correlates significantly with mean dose to submandibular and parotid glands (p=0.05 each) but NOT with dose to the taste-bud-bearing tongue mucosa itself, suggesting radiation-induced taste alteration is substantially mediated through salivary gland damage rather than direct taste bud injury alone.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1007/s11547-023-01707-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11547-023-01707-5)), this prospective observational cohort study followed 31 head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) patients undergoing intensity-modulated radiotherapy (IMRT), contouring the taste-bud-bearing tongue mucosa as a distinct organ at risk separate from the anterior/medium/base tongue thirds and major salivary glands, and correlating dosimetry to each structure with patient-reported taste alteration (CiTAS), dry mouth, and mouth pain at baseline, 3 weeks, and 3 months post-treatment. The central finding challenges the classical "taste bud radiation atrophy" model: dose to the taste buds themselves did not correlate with dysgeusia, but did correlate with dry mouth and mouth pain — while dysgeusia instead correlated significantly with mean dose to the submandibular and parotid glands. This is consistent with an independent finding (Stieb et al., cited) that salivary gland dosimetry — not taste-bud dose — predicts late taste impairment, together suggesting that radiation-induced dysgeusia in HNSCC patients is substantially mediated through salivary gland damage/hyposalivation rather than direct cytotoxic injury to taste bud cells alone. Symptom trajectories showed CiTAS scores roughly doubling from baseline to 3 weeks post-RT then partially recovering by 3 months, while global health status recovered nearly fully but mouth dryness/saliva thickness remained markedly elevated — dissociating taste/QoL recovery from persistent salivary dysfunction.

Clinically, this reframes radiation-induced taste alteration as a largely salivary-gland-mediated phenomenon rather than a purely local taste-bud effect, supporting IMRT strategies that prioritize salivary gland sparing (not just taste-bud-region sparing) as the more direct lever for reducing acute dysgeusia in HNSCC patients — a mechanistic parallel to how xerostomia and taste dysfunction co-occur in other etiologies covered elsewhere in this wiki.

## Key Contributions

- One of the few prospective dosimetric analyses to isolate taste-bud-bearing tongue mucosa as a distinct OAR (contoured separately from the whole oral cavity/entire tongue), enabling direct dose-response testing against patient-reported dysgeusia.
- Counterintuitive key finding: taste-bud dose did NOT correlate with dysgeusia (it correlated instead with dry mouth and mouth pain), while dysgeusia correlated with salivary gland (submandibular/parotid) dose — challenging the classical taste-bud-atrophy model of radiation-induced taste loss.
- Corroborated by an independently cited finding (Stieb et al.) that salivary gland dosimetry, not taste-bud dose, predicts late taste impairment — reinforcing a salivary-gland-mediated mechanism.
- Documents the temporal trajectory: CiTAS subdomain scores roughly double from baseline to 3-week follow-up then partially recover by 3 months, while mouth dryness/saliva thickness recovers far less than global quality of life.
- Synthesizes prior dose-threshold literature (60 Gy oral-cavity threshold for near-universal taste loss; 40–50 Gy thresholds from other cohorts; 20 Gy anterior-tongue threshold for temporary taste loss) into a coherent anatomic-sensitivity picture.

## Methodology

Prospective observational cohort study (single center, University of Florence, Italy; recruitment September 2019–November 2020). 31 analyzed HNSCC patients (71% oropharyngeal, 60% HPV-positive among those) treated with photon IMRT (tomotherapy, 3-dose-level simultaneous integrated boost 69.9/59.4/52.8 Gy in 33 fractions); 77.4% received concurrent cisplatin. Taste-bud volume contoured as a distinct OAR (per latest consensus guidelines) separately from anterior/medium/base tongue thirds; major salivary glands contoured per Brouwer et al. Patient-reported outcomes (CiTAS 18-item scale across 4 subdomains, EORTC QLQ-C30, EORTC QLQ-H&N43) assessed at baseline, 3 weeks post-RT, and 3 months post-RT. Statistics: univariable logistic Cox regression and ordinal logistic regression (hypothesis-generating, p=0.05 threshold).

## Results

1. **Mean doses delivered**: anterior tongue third 23.85 Gy, medium third 35.50 Gy, base tongue 47.67 Gy; taste buds 32.72 Gy (median); parotid glands 25/23 Gy (R/L); submandibular glands 47.8/39.4 Gy (R/L).
2. **Dysgeusia correlation**: significant with submandibular gland mean dose (95% CI 0–0.02, p=0.05) and parotid gland mean dose (95% CI 0–0.02, p=0.05) — but NOT with taste-bud mean dose.
3. **Dry mouth correlation**: significant with mean dose to anterior tongue third (p=0.04), medium third (p=0.04), taste buds (p=0.03), and submandibular glands (p=0.02).
4. **Pain mouth correlation**: significant with mean dose to taste buds (p=0.04), submandibular glands (p=0.03), base tongue (p=0.02).
5. **Symptom trajectory**: CiTAS general-taste-alteration score rose from 1.17 (baseline) to 2.59 (3 weeks) then partially recovered to 1.83 (3 months) — consistent with the known 4–12 month full-recovery window for radiation-induced taste dysfunction. Global health status recovered nearly fully (69.35→62.37→71.39) while mouth dryness/saliva thickness remained markedly elevated at 3 months (11.29→49.46→46.67), dissociating taste/QoL recovery from persistent salivary dysfunction.
6. **Dose-threshold context from prior literature**: ~60 Gy to the entire oral cavity associated with near-universal taste loss; other cohorts report 40–50 Gy oral-cavity thresholds for elevated taste impairment risk; anterior-tongue doses as low as 20 Gy can cause temporary taste loss regardless of base-tongue inclusion in the high-dose region.
