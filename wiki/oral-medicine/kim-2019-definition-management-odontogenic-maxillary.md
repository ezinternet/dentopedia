## Summary

Odontogenic maxillary sinusitis (OMS) is now recognized to account for 30–40% of all chronic maxillary sinusitis (CMS) cases — far higher than the previously cited 10–12% in otorhinolaryngologic literature. This single-author narrative review by Soung Min Kim (Seoul National University) provides a comprehensive framework covering the anatomical basis (Schneiderian membrane, OMU, root-to-sinus floor proximity ~1.97 mm), etiology, microbiology, imaging diagnosis, and surgical management of OMS.

The maxillary sinus floor is intimately related to the posterior teeth: mean root-to-sinus floor distance is only 1.97 mm, and the molar region accounts for 47.68% of OMS cases (first molar 22.51%, third molar 17.21%). Tooth extraction is the most common cause; other etiologies include oroantral fistula (OAF), periapical infection, periodontal disease, and iatrogenic causes (implant displacement, endodontic overfill, sinus perforation during augmentation).

The polymicrobial microbiology of OMS is dominated by aerobic S. aureus and S. pneumoniae and anaerobic Peptostreptococcus and Prevotella spp. (>75% of cases); MRSA found in 10–12%. The bacterial biofilm (BB) hypothesis — slow-replicating strains in extracellular polymeric matrix resistant to antibiotics — explains recalcitrance in chronic OMS; BB detected in 70% of CRS samples. Actinomyces-related OMS requires apical surgery because orthograde endodontic treatment cannot eliminate extraradicular bacteria.

Surgical approaches are compared: Caldwell-Luc Procedure (CLP) — easy access but causes postoperative maxillary cysts (POMC), paresthesia, and sinus wall sclerosis; Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS) — flexible paranasal approach, gradually replacing CLP, but incompletely addresses odontogenic source; Modified Endoscopy-Assisted Maxillary Sinus Surgery (MESS) — newer combined intraoral + endoscopic approach preserving SM physiology without POMC risk.

Oroantral fistula (OAF) is the most frequent OMS complication (>80% from posterior maxillary tooth extraction). Management of OAF depends on defect size: direct closure, buccal advancement flap, palatal island/rotational pedicled flap, or absorbable membrane/buccal fat pad for large defects.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes that OMS prevalence (30–40% of CMS) is far higher than traditionally cited (10–12%)
- Classifies OMS etiologies: tooth extraction, OAF, periapical lesion, periodontal disease, caries, iatrogenic (implant, endodontic, sinus augmentation)
- Describes polymicrobial microbiology and the bacterial biofilm hypothesis for recalcitrant OMS
- Compares three surgical approaches (CLP, FESS, MESS) with complication profiles
- Provides algorithm for OAF closure based on defect size
- Highlights Actinomyces and fungal sinusitis as special microbiologic challenges

## Methodology

Narrative review. Literature searched in MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Cochrane Library. Illustrative clinical cases with CT imaging included (IRB: Seoul National University S-D20170005). Results synthesized based on clinical experience.

## Results

- **OMS prevalence:** 30–40% of all CMS (vs. historical 10–12%)
- **Etiology by tooth region:** molar 47.68% (1st molar 22.51%, 3rd molar 17.21%, 2nd molar 3.97%); premolar 5.96%; canine 0.66%
- **Root-to-sinus floor distance:** mean 1.97 mm
- **Secondary periapical lesion rate in OMS:** 16–65%
- **Bacterial biofilm:** detected in 70% of CRS samples; S. aureus, H. influenzae, P. aeruginosa dominant
- **MRSA in OMS:** 10–12%
- **OAF:** >80% of OMS complications from posterior tooth extraction
- **CLP complications:** POMC, paresthesia from infraorbital nerve, sinus wall sclerosis; impairs future implant reconstruction
- **FESS advantages:** flexible, preserves SM, lower morbidity; disadvantage: incomplete odontogenic source resolution
- **MESS advantages:** preserves sinus physiology, no POMC, OMU enlargement for PNS ventilation
