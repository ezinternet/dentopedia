## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA, 13 studies: 5 clinical trials + 8 case-control) finding the evidence for antihypertensive drugs causing more xerostomia/hyposalivation than controls is inconclusive and of low methodological quality overall — clinical trials show mixed/non-significant salivary flow changes, while case-control studies more consistently (though not uniformly) show lower salivary flow in antihypertensive-treated patients, with no single drug class identifiable as most xerogenic due to study heterogeneity.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3390/ijerph17072478](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph17072478)), this PRISMA-guided systematic review searched four databases for studies comparing xerostomia, hyposalivation, and salivary flow rate between patients taking antihypertensive drugs and untreated controls, ultimately including 13 studies (5 clinical trials, 8 case-control studies) covering β-adrenergic blockers, α-adrenergic blockers, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, heart glycosides, and centrally-acting antihypertensives. The review's central, somewhat counterintuitive finding is that current literature CANNOT confirm that antihypertensive-treated patients suffer more xerostomia or hyposalivation than untreated patients: among 5 clinical trials, only 2 found statistically significant salivary flow decreases while 1 found a statistically significant INCREASE in parotid flow after captopril (an ACE inhibitor) — directly complicating the common assumption that ACE inhibitors are reliably xerogenic. Case-control studies painted a more consistent (though still heterogeneous and low-quality) picture, with most showing lower unstimulated/stimulated whole saliva flow in antihypertensive-treated patients, and the two studies assessing xerostomia directly both finding it significantly more prevalent in the treated group. Due to substantial heterogeneity in drug classes studied, saliva collection methods, and outcome definitions, the review could not determine which specific antihypertensive class produces the most xerostomic effect, and no meta-analysis was possible. Notably, none of the 13 included studies used the Xerostomia Inventory — the only tool specifically validated for assessing medication-induced xerostomia — highlighting a persistent methodological gap in this literature.

Clinically, this tempers the confident drug-class-mechanism claims found in broader polypharmacy reviews (e.g., that ACE inhibitors and β-blockers are straightforwardly xerogenic via specific receptor mechanisms): the direct antihypertensive-specific evidence base is thin, old (most recent trial from 2006), and methodologically weak, so individual patient assessment (rather than blanket assumption by drug class) remains warranted when a hypertensive dental patient presents with dry mouth or taste complaints.

## Key Contributions

- The first systematic review specifically isolating antihypertensive drugs as a class and testing three focused questions: (1) more xerostomia/hyposalivation vs. controls? (2) lower salivary flow vs. controls? (3) which antihypertensive class is most xerogenic?
- Delivers a counterintuitive, conservative conclusion that directly tempers the common clinical assumption (echoed in broader polypharmacy literature) that antihypertensives are reliably xerogenic — current evidence cannot confirm this at the systematic-review level.
- Separately analyzes clinical-trial vs. case-control evidence and finds they point in somewhat different directions: trials show mixed/mostly non-significant changes (including a significant parotid-flow INCREASE with captopril), while case-control studies lean toward lower salivary flow in treated patients.
- Documents that none of the 13 included studies used the Xerostomia Inventory, the only tool validated specifically for medication-induced salivary gland disorder assessment — a clear methodological gap for future research to address.
- Applies rigorous dual risk-of-bias appraisal (Cochrane tool for trials, modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale for case-control studies), finding overall poor-to-fair methodological quality across the entire evidence base.

## Methodology

PRISMA-guided systematic review (3 researchers: 2 independent screeners + 1 arbiter), searching PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, CINAHL, and Cochrane Library with no start-date limit through November 19, 2019, using combined terms for saliva/dry mouth/hyposalivation/salivary flow AND hypertension/antihypertensive drugs, plus manual reference-list hand-search. Included original human studies (clinical trials, longitudinal, cross-sectional) comparing antihypertensive-treated patients against a control/placebo group without antihypertensive treatment, other disease, or other pharmacological treatment; excluded non-English/Spanish studies, studies with concomitant disease/other drugs, studies without a placebo/CG comparator, and studies presenting only graphical (non-numerical) salivary flow data. Xerostomia assessed via questionnaire, VAS, Fox et al. questions, or modified Xerostomia Inventory; hyposalivation via standard SWS/UWS cutoffs or modified Schirmer test. Screening: 6201 records → 2905 after dedup → 26 full-text assessed → 13 included (5 clinical trials, 8 case-control). Quality appraisal via Cochrane risk-of-bias tool (trials) and modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale (case-control, AHRQ good/fair/poor classification). No meta-analysis performed due to heterogeneity.

## Results

1. **Overall verdict**: current literature cannot confirm that antihypertensive-treated patients have more xerostomia/hyposalivation than untreated patients, when clinical trial evidence is weighted alongside more heterogeneous case-control evidence.
2. **Clinical trials (5 studies)**: only Nederfors et al. 2004 assessed xerostomia degree directly, finding thiazide/furosemide groups' xerostomia levels increased vs. stable placebo. Only 1/5 trials found a statistically significant unstimulated whole saliva (UWS) decrease (with an α-/β-adrenergic blocker combination, propranolol+phentolamine); one trial found a statistically significant stimulated parotid saliva (SPS) flow INCREASE after captopril (an ACE inhibitor) — directly contradicting a simple "ACE inhibitors cause xerostomia" assumption.
3. **Case-control studies (8 studies)**: xerostomia significantly more prevalent in antihypertensive-treated patients in both studies directly assessing it; hyposalivation (1 study, Nonzee et al. 2012) significantly greater in treated patients; UWS decreased in 5/6 studies measuring it (statistically significant in 3), stimulated whole saliva (SWS) decreased in 4/5 studies (significant in 3), SPS significantly decreased with diuretics (1 study).
4. **Drug-class specificity**: due to heterogeneous study designs, outcome measures, and drug combinations across the 13 studies, no single antihypertensive class (β-blockers, α-blockers, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, heart glycosides, centrally-acting drugs) could be identified as the most xerogenic.
5. **Study quality**: all 13 included studies rated poor-to-fair; clinical trials mostly scored "unclear" risk of bias due to undescribed methodological parameters; case-control studies scored 4–6 on the modified Newcastle-Ottawa scale (5 poor, 3 fair) with the main bias concentrated in selection parameters.
6. **Methodological gap**: none of the 13 studies used the Xerostomia Inventory — the only validated tool for medication-induced xerostomia assessment — and available clinical trials were old (most recent 2006, oldest 1981), none with a calculated target sample size.
