## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA, PROSPERO CRD42022347208; 39 studies, n=14–148, modified Newcastle–Ottawa quality) of pediatric cancer-therapy-induced oral mucositis therapies: chlorhexidine best for incidence, honey best for duration, olive oil best for pain, and palifermin reduces incidence/severity/duration, while calcium phosphate showed no effect — but overall evidence remains inconclusive for firm protocols.

## Summary

Cancer-therapy-induced oral mucositis (OM) is a high-incidence (40–100%) inflammatory injury of the oral mucosa distinct from the wiki's other oral-medicine entities (recurrent aphthous stomatitis, lichen planus, BMS). This PRISMA-compliant systematic review (no meta-analysis) isolates the **pediatric** (≤18 y) population — most prior evidence is adult or mixed — across patients undergoing chemotherapy, radiotherapy, HSCT, or combinations. From 2655 unique records, 39 studies were included and ranked by which OM outcome (incidence, severity, duration, pain) each intervention class best addresses. The headline rankings: **chlorhexidine** most effective for incidence, **honey** for duration, **olive oil** for pain, and **palifermin** (recombinant human keratinocyte growth factor, KGF) reducing incidence/severity/duration in acute leukemia. **Calcium phosphate** showed no effect across all 3 studies. The authors frame the pediatric dentist as a member of the oncology care team, but conclude the evidence is still insufficient to establish accurate clinical protocols.

## Key Contributions

- First/recent SR to isolate the pediatric population for cancer-therapy OM management.
- Ranks ~10 main intervention classes by which OM outcome each best addresses, rather than a single overall verdict.
- Positions the pediatric dentist within the oncology care team for OM assessment and intervention.

## Methodology

- PICO: which treatments are most effective for treatment and prevention of cancer-induced OM in pediatric patients?
- Search: PubMed/MEDLINE, NICE, ICTRP, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science up to March 2024; English/Portuguese; last 20 years.
- Screening: 2655 unique records → 59 after title/abstract → **39 included** (Rayyan; two independent screeners, Cohen's κ = 0.82, third for consensus).
- Quality: **Modified Newcastle–Ottawa Scale** (0–9 stars); bias high (≤5), moderate (6–7), low (≥8) → 9 high-bias, 16 moderate, 14 low-bias.
- Study geography: Brazil (8), Italy (6), India (3) most common; sample size 14–148; treatment duration 3–29 days.
- No meta-analysis: heterogeneity of cancer types, protocols, outcome measures, and durations precluded pooling.

## Results

Intervention frequency: **LLLT/photobiomodulation** most studied (8 studies, 20%), then palifermin (3), zinc (3), honey (3), calcium phosphate (3), olive oil (2), oral cryotherapy (2), vitamin E (2), glutamine (2), chlorhexidine (2).

Efficacy by outcome (per SR synthesis):
- **Incidence** — chlorhexidine most effective; also reduced by glutamine, palifermin, olive oil.
- **Severity** — chlorhexidine, honey, palifermin, olive oil, vitamin E, glutamine similar.
- **Duration** — honey most effective; also palifermin.
- **Pain** — olive oil highest; honey, LLLT, zinc-based equally effective.
- **No effect** — calcium phosphate (all 3 studies); TGF-β2, chewing gum, ketamine mouthwash (single studies); plain oral cryotherapy alone and vitamin E (one study) inconsistent.

Mechanistic/per-therapy notes: chlorhexidine works via removal of pathogenic oral bacteria; honey/olive oil via decreased pro-inflammatory cytokines (manuka honey ↓severity+pain, natural honey ↓severity+duration); glutamine 400 mg/kg/day reduced incidence in ALL after high-dose methotrexate; cryotherapy effective only with propantheline (compliance-limited); palifermin has conflicting safety reports. Evidence remains inconclusive — larger samples and standardized outcome measurement are needed.
