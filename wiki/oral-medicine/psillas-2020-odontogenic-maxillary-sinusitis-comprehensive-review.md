## Summary
This comprehensive narrative review from Aristotle University of Thessaloniki covers the full spectrum of odontogenic maxillary sinusitis (OMS): anatomy, epidemiology, etiology, bacteriology, diagnosis, and treatment. Key epidemiological finding: approximately 30% of unilateral maxillary sinusitis cases have an underlying dental cause (Patel & Ferguson), with some reports reaching 51.8% (Maillet et al.). The most commonly involved teeth are the maxillary second and first molars, whose roots are in closest anatomical proximity to the sinus floor. The Schneiderian membrane serves as the last barrier once alveolar bone thins with age. Treatment mandates eliminating the dental cause (extraction, endodontic therapy, implant removal, OAF repair) combined with ESS for drainage restoration in established disease.

## Key Contributions
- Summarizes all major OMS etiologies: dentoalveolar surgery > odontogenic infection > dental implants > endodontic material displacement
- Establishes anatomical proximity hierarchy: 2nd molar > 1st molar > 3rd molar > 2nd premolar > 1st premolar
- Distinguishes OMS bacteriology from rhinogenic sinusitis: polymicrobial, anaerobic-containing oral flora
- Documents the diagnostic triad: unilateral symptoms + dental history + CT evidence of periapical/implant pathology
- Confirms treatment principle: dental surgery alone may be insufficient once sinus mucosal changes are established; combined dental + ESS is preferred

## Methodology
- Narrative (not systematic) review
- Search: PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, ScienceDirect (Jan 2018–Jan 2019)
- Inclusion: Articles on OMS published after 2000
- Search term: "odontogenic sinusitis"
- Organized by topic: anatomy / epidemiology / etiology / bacteriology / diagnosis / treatment

## Results

### Anatomy
- Maxillary sinus: average volume 15–20 mL; full development at 12–14 years
- Root proximity to sinus floor (descending order): 2nd molar → 1st molar → 3rd molar → 2nd premolar → 1st premolar
- Aging → alveolar bone thinning → Schneiderian membrane as sole barrier
- Pneumatization can cause premolars/canines/3rd molars to protrude into sinus cavity

### Epidemiology
- ~30% of unilateral maxillary sinusitis: dental etiology (Patel & Ferguson)
- Up to 51.8% in some studies (Maillet et al.)
- Pre-1970s estimate: 10–12%; rising with FESS adoption

### Etiology
- Most common: dentoalveolar surgery → OAF formation / Schneiderian membrane perforation
- Others: apical periodontitis, periodontal disease, dental implants (displacement/peri-implantitis), endodontic material overfill

### Bacteriology
- Mixed aerobic-anaerobic; oral flora (streptococci, anaerobes, gram-negative rods)
- Distinct from non-odontogenic sinusitis (typically S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae)

### Diagnosis
- Unilateral nasal obstruction, discharge, foul odor; dental history of posterior maxillary treatment
- CT (or CBCT): unilateral opacification + periapical pathology / displaced material
- Nasal endoscopy: pus in middle meatus, mucosal edema

### Treatment
- Dental intervention (cause elimination) + ESS (sinus drainage) — combined approach superior to either alone
- Antibiotics: adjunctive, not curative
- Multidisciplinary ENT + dental/oral surgery team mandatory
