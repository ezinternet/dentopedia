## One-line Summary

Narrative review (bibliographic synthesis of ~90+ studies, >65,000 COVID-19 patients/survivors combined) finding gustatory dysfunction (ageusia/dysgeusia) persists in 1–45% and saliva secretory dysfunction (xerostomia/hyposalivation) in 2–40% of COVID-19 survivors at 3-week–12-month follow-up, correlated with each other and mechanistically linked via ACE2/TRPV1 receptor expression in taste buds/salivary glands and infection-induced zinc deficiency.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1159/000531373](https://doi.org/10.1159/000531373)), this narrative bibliographic review by Tsuchiya characterizes persistent gustatory (ageusia/dysgeusia/hypogeusia) and saliva secretory (xerostomia/dry mouth/hyposalivation) dysfunctions after recovery from COVID-19, and proposes shared pathogenic mechanisms. Drawing on 64 studies (23,476 survivors) for gustatory sequelae and 7 studies (654 survivors) for saliva secretory sequelae — plus comparator acute-phase literature — the review finds both symptom clusters persist for months to a year post-recovery, correlate with each other across diverse cohorts (Italy, Colombia, China, India, Israel, Egypt, Turkey, Saudi Arabia), and share a plausible common pathophysiology: SARS-CoV-2 cellular-entry receptors (ACE2, TMPRSS2, TRPV1) are co-expressed in both taste buds and salivary gland acinar/ductal cells, and viral infection induces zinc deficiency that independently impairs both taste bud regeneration and salivary secretion. Gustatory sequelae show a clear geographic/ethnic gradient (lowest in East Asia, highest in the Middle East and Europe), while saliva secretory sequelae show no such clear gradient by ethnicity, gender, or age.

Clinically, this reinforces that dentists and dental hygienists should expect a substantial fraction of post-COVID-19 patients to present with combined taste and dry-mouth complaints lasting well beyond acute infection, and that these two complaints should be assessed together rather than in isolation, given their shared mechanism and prognostic correlation.

## Key Contributions

- First review focused specifically on **persistent** (post-recovery sequelae, not acute-phase) gustatory and saliva secretory dysfunction after COVID-19, building on the same author's prior acute-phase and xerostomia-specific reviews.
- Quantifies prevalence across follow-up windows (1–45% gustatory at 21–365 days; 2–40% saliva secretory at 28–230 days) showing symptoms do not resolve quickly once persistent beyond ~1 month.
- Documents a geographic/ethnic gradient for gustatory sequelae (East Asia lowest at 3.8%, Middle East highest at 20.6%) but explicit absence of such a gradient for saliva secretory sequelae.
- Proposes three converging pathogenic mechanisms common to taste and saliva dysfunction: ACE2/Furin/TMPRSS2 cellular entry in taste buds and salivary glands, TRPV1 viral interaction, and SARS-CoV-2-induced zinc deficiency.
- Extends the mechanistic model to explain Omicron's comparatively milder oral symptom profile via its weaker ACE2-receptor binding affinity.

## Methodology

Narrative/bibliographic literature review (not a formal systematic review; no meta-analysis, PRISMA, or GRADE framework applied) searching PubMed/Medline, LitCovid, ProQuest, medRxiv, and bioRxiv (cutoff September 30, 2022) with terms combining SARS-CoV-2/COVID-19/sequelae/persistent with ageusia/dysgeusia/gustatory dysfunction/xerostomia/dry mouth/salivary secretion dysfunction. Excluded non-English articles, studies lacking RT-PCR/serological confirmation of infection, and studies including primary Sjögren's syndrome patients (to avoid confounding from an independent xerostomia/dysgeusia etiology). Scope: 64 studies/23,476 survivors (persistent gustatory), 86 studies/42,759 patients (acute gustatory, for comparison), 7 studies/654 survivors (persistent saliva secretory), 13 studies/2,283 patients (acute saliva secretory, for comparison).

## Results

1. **Gustatory sequelae**: prevalence 1–45% at 21–365-day follow-up; pooled ~13.6–13.8% at ~1–2 months declining only slightly to ~12.7% by ~6 months (not statistically different across follow-up windows), i.e., once present, symptoms are slow to resolve; some survivors report symptoms at 1-year follow-up.
2. **Geographic/ethnic gradient (gustatory)**: mean ± SD prevalence — Europe 16.2 ± 11.0%, North America 13.8 ± 8.8%, South America 11.5 ± 5.0%, East Asia 3.8 ± 2.0% (significantly lowest, p<0.005 to p<0.0005 vs. all other regions), South Asia 10.0 ± 1.4%, Middle East 20.6 ± 7.5% (highest).
3. **Gender/age (gustatory)**: mixed evidence across cohorts — several (Saudi, Swiss, German) show female predominance and older-age association; others (South Korean n=900, Indian, Turkish) show no significant association with gender, age, or disease severity.
4. **Saliva secretory sequelae**: prevalence 2–40% at 28–230-day follow-up; lower than acute-phase prevalence (mean 18.5% vs. 42.5%, p<0.001) but persists over months (16.2 ± 0.84% at 1–2 months; 20.5 ± 6.8% at >2–4 months); unlike gustatory sequelae, no clear gradient by ethnicity, gender, age, or disease severity across the cited cohorts.
5. **Co-occurrence**: xerostomia and ageusia/dysgeusia prevalence moderately correlated across 20 studies spanning 8 countries, supporting shared underlying pathophysiology rather than coincidental co-occurrence.
6. **Mechanistic evidence**: ACE2, Furin, and TMPRSS2 are co-expressed in taste buds/papillae and in acinar/ductal/myoepithelial cells of all major and minor salivary glands (immunohistochemistry, postmortem biopsy studies cited); TRPV1 is similarly co-expressed in tongue epithelium and salivary gland cells; serum zinc is significantly lower in COVID-19 patients (64.8 ± 7.5 μg/dL) than healthy controls (88.3 ± 14.0 μg/dL), and zinc is independently essential for taste bud regeneration and salivary gland function; Omicron's weaker ACE2-RBD binding affinity (vs. Delta and wild-type) correlates with its reported lower rates of ageusia/dysgeusia and xerostomia.
