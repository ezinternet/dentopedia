## One-line Summary

Umbrella/systematic review (11 review articles, 31 pharmacological interventions, PROSPERO CRD42023408948) confirming carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine as first-line for classical trigeminal neuralgia, lamotrigine/baclofen second-line, and botulinum toxin A / IV lidocaine for refractory cases, with microvascular decompression as first-line surgery.

## Summary

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN, tic douloureux) produces sudden, electric-shock-like paroxysms of facial pain in the trigeminal distribution, triggered by light touch to the face, lips, or oral mucosa or by ordinary functions (chewing, talking, tooth-brushing). It is classified (ICHD-style) as **classical** (neurovascular compression at the trigeminal root entry zone — the most common cause), **secondary** (posterior-fossa tumor/vascular abnormality or multiple sclerosis), or **idiopathic** (no cause found). This umbrella review of 11 review articles covering 31 drugs distills the pharmacological ladder: carbamazepine and oxcarbazepine remain first-line for classical TN; lamotrigine and baclofen are second-line; gabapentin/pregabalin, combinations, BTX-A, and IV lidocaine fill refractory niches; and microvascular decompression (MVD) is the first-line surgery for classical (vascular-compression) TN.

For a dentist this page is a **differential-diagnosis safety net**: TN trigger zones on the face and intraoral mucosa, plus pain provoked by chewing or tooth-brushing, are frequently mistaken for odontogenic pain — risking unnecessary endodontic treatment or extraction of a sound tooth. Recognizing the paroxysmal, trigger-evoked, electric-shock character (and the absence of a dental source on exam/imaging) should prompt a trial of carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine and neurology referral rather than irreversible dental intervention.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic review to organize and compare TN pharmacotherapy efficacy/safety across multiple international reviews (588 → 370 → 127 → **11** reviews; **31** interventions).
- Establishes a clear, dentist-usable management ladder anchored on carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine.
- Specifies the diagnostic framing (classical / secondary / idiopathic) and names MVD as first-line surgery for classical TN.

## Methodology

- Umbrella review (systematic review of reviews) per PRISMA 2009; protocol PROSPERO CRD42023408948.
- PICOS inclusion: reviews/meta-analyses of RCTs of drug therapy for TN. Exclusions: rapid reviews, non-English, lab/uncontrolled/qualitative studies, case reports/series.
- Databases: PubMed, CRD, EBSCO, Web of Science, Scopus, Cochrane; collection ended 1 Dec 2022.
- Two independent reviewers + third for disagreements; standardized extraction template (intervention, control, evidence level, efficacy, adverse effects, conclusions).

## Results

**First-line (classical TN) — carbamazepine & oxcarbazepine**
- Doses: **carbamazepine 0.4–1.2 g/day**; **oxcarbazepine 0.9–1.8 g/day**.
- Oxcarbazepine has fewer adverse effects; may be first-line for secondary TN (monitor MS patients).
- Side effects: drowsiness, vertigo, nausea, diplopia, reduced neuromuscular control, cognitive effects; transaminase elevation and hyponatremia at higher doses.

**Second-line — lamotrigine & baclofen** (for non-responders or intolerable carbamazepine side effects).
- **Gabapentin**: RCT meta-analysis (n=1331) — efficacy comparable to carbamazepine, considerably fewer side effects; second-line/refractory.
- **Pregabalin**: fewer side effects; pregabalin + carbamazepine effective in refractory TN; benefit at 1 year.
- **Baclofen**: effective for MS-secondary TN; levorotatory > racemic; baclofen + carbamazepine significantly superior (low-grade evidence).

**Combinations** — carbamazepine + gabapentin / ropivacaine / tizanidine / pimozide improve results and tolerance; ropivacaine trigger-zone block + oral gabapentin was the only high-grade-evidence combination.

**Refractory / acute** — **IV lidocaine** for acute exacerbations; **botulinum toxin A** (intradermal/submucosal, **25–75 U**; ~70–100% response, ~60–80% pain reduction, no major adverse events) for refractory cases; sumatriptan SC 3 mg + oral 100 mg/day (low-grade evidence).

**Ruled out** — proparacaine, dextromethorphan, tocainide reported inappropriate for TN.

**Surgical referral** — microvascular decompression (MVD) first-line for classical TN. Novel agents awaited: vixotrigine, basimglurant (mGlu5 NAM), erenumab/Aimovig (anti-CGRP).

**Caveat** — except carbamazepine, most drugs are inadequately studied (small samples, low evidence grade); the umbrella design inherits this low certainty.
