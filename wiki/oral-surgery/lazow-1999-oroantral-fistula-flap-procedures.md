## Summary

Lazow (1999) provides a comprehensive historical review of soft tissue flap procedures for oroantral fistula (OAF) closure, published in *Operative Techniques in Otolaryngology–Head and Neck Surgery*. The paper covers ~70 years of technique literature (1920–1993) and synthesizes them into a practical decision protocol.

Two universal principles frame every repair:
1. The sinus must be infection-free with adequate nasal drainage (Caldwell-Luc or FESS + antibiotics + decongestants).
2. Closure must be tension-free over intact bone with a broad-based, well-vascularized flap.

**Outcome context**: Acute OAC primary repair achieves ~95% success. Once an OAF has epithelialized (defect >5 mm present >3 weeks), secondary closure success falls to as low as 67%.

## Key Contributions

### Defect biology and natural history
- Small acute OAC (≤2 mm) — often heals spontaneously without sinus infection.
- Defect >5 mm present >3 weeks — almost universally epithelializes into chronic OAF requiring surgical closure.
- The fistula tract forms between pseudostratified ciliated columnar respiratory epithelium (antrum) and stratified squamous oral mucosa.

### 4-Step size-based closure protocol
| Step | Defect size / Location | Recommended flap |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 5–10 mm, lateral or midalveolar | Buccal sliding flap (Berger technique) |
| 2 | 5–10 mm, palatal or midalveolar | Palatal rotation flap |
| 3 | 10–20 mm, anterior | Anteriorly-based dorsal tongue flap |
| 4 | >2 cm, posterior | Temporalis myofascial flap |

### Local flap techniques

**Buccal sliding flap (Berger 1939)**
- Trapezoidal mucoperiosteal flap elevated from divergent incisions at anterior/posterior defect edges, carried to height of mucobuccal fold.
- Periosteum scored horizontally on flap undersurface to allow advancement.
- Closure with mattress sutures to palatal mucosa.
- Limitation: loss of vestibular depth (may require secondary vestibuloplasty in denture patients).

**Buccal fat pad (BFP) graft (Egyedi 1977)**
- Suitable for defects >2 cm.
- Axial blood supply from buccal and deep temporal branches of maxillary artery + transverse facial artery.
- After trapezoidal buccal flap raised, 1-cm vertical periosteal incision posterior to zygomatic buttress exposes BFP for advancement.
- Advantage: readily reepithelializes; avoids vestibular depth loss (mucoperiosteum returned to original depth).

**Palatal rotation flap (Ashley 1939)**
- Full-thickness ipsilateral mucoperiosteal rotation flap based on greater palatine artery.
- Flap width at anterior extent slightly wider than bony defect; length 3–4 cm.
- Technical pitfalls: kinking of greater palatine artery, bunching at rotation axis, painful exposed bone at donor site.

**Palatal island flap (James 1980)**
- Most versatile palatal option.
- Greater palatine neurovascular bundle dissected free from undersurface of mobilized flap; anterior island sectioned, tunneled under intact palatal bridge, rotated over defect.
- Advantages: flexible lateral rotation, less exposed raw bone.
- Disadvantage: neurovascular pedicle vulnerable to trauma.

### Distant flap techniques

**Anteriorly-based dorsal tongue flap**
- Indicated for moderate (10–20 mm) anterior defects.
- Flap: 2.5–3.0 cm wide (≥20% wider than defect), up to 5–6 cm length (short of gustatory papillae), 5–6 mm thick (includes several mm muscle to protect submucosal vascular plexus).
- Two-stage: pedicle divided at 3 weeks under LA + sedation; trimmed monthly starting 3 months after pedicle return.
- Massengill (1970): no significant speech articulation or glossal mobility disturbance.
- Requires general anesthesia for flap raising.

**Temporalis myofascial flap**
- Reserved for large (>2 cm) posterior defects.
- Axial flap based on deep temporal arteries (off internal maxillary artery).
- Hemicoronal approach ± zygomatic osteotomy; flap advanced transbuccally into oral cavity.
- Readily epithelializes (no skin graft required).
- Significant morbidity potential (hemorrhage, infection, flap failure).

### Grafts and alloplastic implants
- Autogenous bone graft indications: bony defect >5 mm, failed conservative closure, need for alveolar ridge recontouring (preprosthetic).
- Alloplastic options reviewed: tantalum plate, 24-karat gold plate, gold foil, soft methylmethacrylate, hydroxyapatite block, PTFE membrane.
- Gold plate technique: buccal + palatal flaps raised; plate adapted to sound bone ≥5 mm circumferentially; primary closure not required (scaffold for surrounding mucosal/periosteal/sinus membrane ingrowth). Expected extrusion by 6 weeks.

## Methodology

- Narrative review of 30 published references (1920–1993).
- Illustrated with 7 diagrams depicting each technique.
- No systematic search methodology, no statistical synthesis.
- Single-author review, SUNY–Health Science Center, Brooklyn, NY.

## Results

- Acute primary repair success: ~95%
- Chronic secondary repair (OAF established): as low as 67%
- Spontaneous healing: defects ≤2 mm without sinus infection
- Surgical closure required: virtually all defects >5 mm present >3 weeks
