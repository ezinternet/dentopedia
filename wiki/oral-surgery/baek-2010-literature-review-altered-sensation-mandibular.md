## One-line Summary

Narrative review (15 studies, PubMed 1990–2009) of altered sensation following posterior mandibular implant placement: incidence 0–55% across studies, mainly IAN/mental nerve, mostly transient (resolves within 6 months), with NSAIDs recommended for non-surgical management and microneuroanastomosis for surgical cases.

## Summary

This Korean master's thesis (Catholic University of Korea, Dept. of Oral & Maxillofacial Surgery, 2010) reviews 15 studies on altered sensation following posterior mandibular implant placement, searching PubMed (1990–2009) and the Korean OMFS journal. The reported incidence varies widely (0–55%) depending on study design: large prospective series (Bartling: 8.5%; Vazquez: 0.08%) report far lower rates than retrospective questionnaire studies (Ellies & Hawker: 36%; Kwon et al. Korean dentist survey: 55%). The inferior alveolar nerve (하치조신경, IAN) and mental nerve (이신경) are most commonly affected; the lingual nerve (설신경) is rarely involved. Most events occur after the first surgery, and transient cases (the majority) resolve spontaneously within 6 months. NSAIDs (ibuprofen 800 mg) are first-line non-surgical treatment; microneuroanastomosis is considered when anesthesia persists >3 months or dysesthesia >4 months without improvement. Prevention relies on maintaining a safety zone ≥2 mm above the mandibular canal on panoramic and ≥1 mm on CT, with careful attention to mental foramen anterior loop anatomy. The thesis concludes that standardized, objective criteria for assessing altered sensation post-implant are needed.

## Key Contributions

- Synthesizes the full incidence range (0–55%) and explains why retrospective studies overestimate relative to prospective designs
- Maps the three nerves at risk: IAN (most common), mental nerve (anterior loop risk), lingual nerve (rare, flap-related)
- Collates treatment algorithm: non-surgical (NSAIDs/steroids) → conservative surgical (implant removal/reposition) → microneurosurgery (with explicit indications/contraindications table)
- Provides the Worthington panoramic magnification correction formula for safe implant length planning
- Documents legal consequences: 16 Korean cases (1992–1999), 4 compensations, 6 lawsuits — highlighting medicolegal stakes

## Methodology

- **Design**: Narrative literature review (문헌 고찰)
- **Search**: PubMed "altered sensation" + "mandibular implant" (1990–2009); Korean OMFS journal
- **n selected**: 15 articles; 5 with incidence data (3 retrospective, 2 prospective)
- **Inclusion**: Clinical studies involving IAN, mental nerve, or lingual nerve; incidence data; prospective or retrospective patient/dentist survey
- **Exclusion**: Maxillary/anterior mandibular sensory disturbance; nerve-avoidance technique papers; other reviews; legal-dispute papers

## Results

| Study | Design | N | Incidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kwon et al. (Korean) | Retrospective questionnaire (dentists) | 47 dentists | 55% | Mostly IAN; 79% of affected cases were surgeons with ≥5 yr experience |
| Ellies & Hawker 1993 | Retrospective questionnaire (patients) | 112 patients | 36% | 23% resolved; 13% persistent; 81% after 1st surgery |
| Ellies 1992 | Retrospective questionnaire (patients) | 212 respondents | 37% | 24% transient; 13% permanent; 82% resolved ≤6 months |
| Bartling et al. | Prospective | 94 patients, 405 implants | 8.5% (8/94) | No permanent cases; all resolved; safety-zone protocol ≥2 mm panoramic |
| Lydia Vazquez et al. 2007 | Prospective | 1527 patients, 2584 implants | 0.08% (2 patients) | Both transient; resolved 3–6 weeks |

**Symptom profile** (Ellies 1992): numbness 74%, tingling 33%, frozen sensation 26%, painful 22%, swollen 19%, thick 18%, itching 5%

**Risk factors**: Female sex (43%), diabetes mellitus (75%)

**Safety zone**: ≥2 mm above mandibular canal/mental foramen on panoramic; ≥1 mm on CT; anterior loop of mental nerve: implant placed mesially

**Microneurosurgery indications**: Nerve severance observed; total anesthesia >3 months; dysesthesia >4 months; severe hyposthesia no improvement >4 months

**Nerve injury mechanisms (Hirsch & Branemark)**: (1) direct mechanical trauma, (2) nerve/vessel compression, (3) intravascular injury → thrombosis, (4) osteoma formation
