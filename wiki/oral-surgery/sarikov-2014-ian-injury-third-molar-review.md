## One-line Summary

Literature review of inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) injury after mandibular third molar extraction (14 of 84 sources analyzed); IAN paresthesia incidence ranges 0.35–8.4% with permanent injury very rare and most cases recovering, and identifies age >24, horizontal impaction, and trainee surgeon as key risk factors alongside radiographic OPG signs of intimate canal-root contact.

## Summary
Literature review of IAN injury following mandibular third molar extraction. Searched PubMed (January 2009–June 2014); English; ≥6 months follow-up; injury assessed by patient report, radiographic evaluation, and neurosensory testing. 84 sources reviewed; 14 most relevant selected and analyzed.

IAN paresthesia incidence: 0.35–8.4% depending on technique, patient population, and impaction pattern. Most paresthesias are transient; permanent nerve injury is very rare. Risk can be predicted by radiographic signs of intimate IAC-root relationship. Age >24, horizontal impaction, and trainee (vs experienced) surgeons increase risk.

## Key Contributions
- Establishes the baseline IAN injury incidence range: 0.35–8.4% — wide range reflecting population and technique heterogeneity
- Three primary modifiable/assessable risk factors: age >24, horizontal impaction, trainee surgeon
- Radiographic risk signs (OPG): narrowing of canal, root deflection, canal interruption, root grooving — indicators of intimate nerve contact
- Prognosis: recovery is the norm; permanent dysesthesia is rare — supports informed consent framework
- Treatment options: steroids, B vitamins, low-level laser therapy; nerve repair surgery for severe cases

## Methodology
- **Database**: PubMed
- **Period**: January 2009–June 2014
- **Inclusion**: English; ≥6 months follow-up; injury by patient report + radiographic + neurosensory testing
- **Selected**: 14 studies from 84 reviewed sources

## Results

| Risk Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Age >24 years | Increased risk (root completion, closer nerve proximity) |
| Horizontal impaction | Highest risk angulation type |
| Trainee vs experienced surgeon | Increased risk |
| OPG signs of IAC contact | Predictive of injury |

- IAN paresthesia incidence: **0.35–8.4%**
- Permanent injury: very rare
- Most cases recover spontaneously or with conservative management
