Systematic review and meta-analysis comparing coronectomy versus complete extraction for mandibular third molars with high inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) proximity; coronectomy significantly reduces IAN injury risk (permanent injury effectively eliminated), with predictable coronal root migration (~2–3 mm over 2 years) and reoperation/root retrieval needed in ~5–12% of cases and no significant difference in infection or dry socket.

## Summary
This systematic review and meta-analysis (J Oral Maxillofac Surg 2024;82(1):73-92) compares coronectomy (intentional partial odontectomy leaving roots in situ) versus complete extraction for mandibular third molars with high inferior alveolar nerve (IAN) proximity. High-risk criteria include panoramic/CBCT signs such as root darkening, canal narrowing, or canal deflection at the root apex. The primary outcome is IAN injury (permanent or transient neurosensory deficit). Secondary outcomes include root migration, reoperation rate, infection, and dry socket.

## Key Contributions
- Coronectomy significantly reduces IAN injury risk in high-risk mandibular third molar cases
- Permanent IAN injury is nearly eliminated with coronectomy vs. complete extraction
- Root migration is predictable (coronally, ~2–3 mm over 2 years) and rarely clinically problematic
- Reoperation (root retrieval) required in approximately 5–12% of cases
- No significant difference in infection or dry socket rates between coronectomy and complete extraction
- Evidence supports coronectomy as the preferred technique when IAN proximity is confirmed

## Methodology
- PRISMA 2020 systematic review
- Databases: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane, Scopus, Web of Science
- Inclusion: RCTs and controlled studies in high-risk IAN proximity patients
- Meta-analysis: random-effects model; heterogeneity by I²
- Outcomes: IAN injury, root migration (mm), reoperation rate, infection, dry socket

## Results
- **IAN injury**: Marked reduction in OR with coronectomy; permanent IAN injury effectively eliminated
- **Transient IAN injury**: Also significantly reduced with coronectomy
- **Root migration**: Mean coronal migration ~2–3 mm over 2 years; most roots remain subosseous; clinical consequences rare
- **Reoperation**: ~5–12% of retained roots require later retrieval; most retrievals straightforward
- **Infection**: Comparable between groups (no significant difference)
- **Dry socket**: Comparable between groups
- Heterogeneity moderate; variation in study design and surgical protocols
