## One-line Summary

Narrative review with case series (Kyung Hee University) of local flap techniques for OAF closure — buccal advancement flap (small OAFs), buccal fat pad (medium-large), palatal rotational flap (> 10 mm) — with a size-and-sinusitis decision algorithm.

## Summary

Oroantral fistula (OAF) — also called oroantral communication (OAC) — is an abnormal epithelialized tract between the oral cavity and maxillary sinus, most commonly caused by posterior maxillary tooth extraction or implant surgery. This narrative review from the Kyung Hee University Department of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery (Seoul) describes a clinical decision algorithm and the three most widely used local soft-tissue flap procedures: the buccal advancement flap, the buccal fat pad (BFP), and the palatal rotational flap. Distant flaps (tongue, auricular cartilage, temporalis) are acknowledged but not covered in depth.

**Core principle**: If sinusitis is present, infection control must precede fistula closure. The OAF tract itself acts as drainage — premature closure risks worsening sinus disease.

## Key Contributions

### Size-Based Treatment Algorithm

| OAF size | Sinusitis? | First step |
|---|---|---|
| < 3 mm | No | Observe — spontaneous closure possible |
| < 5 mm | No | Simple suture |
| ≥ 5 mm | No | Flap procedure |
| Any size | Acute | Amoxicillin/clavulanate 1 g/125 mg TID × 10-14 d + decongestants + NSAIDs → irrigation; then flap |
| Any size | Chronic | ESS or Caldwell-Luc operation → then flap |

OAF closure within 48 hours of onset is recommended to prevent further complications.

### Three Local Flap Techniques

#### 1. Buccal Advancement Flap (Rehrmann 1936 / Moczair sliding variant)
- **Design**: Trapezoid mucoperiosteal flap advanced over defect
- **Best for**: Small OAFs; simple anatomy
- **Advantage**: Good blood supply; closest to defect; easiest technique
- **Disadvantage**: Buccal sulcus depth reduction → may impair denture retention
- **Moczair variant**: Shifts flap distally ~1 tooth distance — less sulcus reduction but risks periodontal disease and gingival recession from dentogingival detachment

#### 2. Buccal Fat Pad (BFP; Egyedi technique)
- **Anatomy**: Lobulated fatty tissue enclosed in thin fibrous capsule; blood supply from multiple sources (buccal and deep temporal branches of maxillary artery, transverse branch of superficial temporal artery, branches of facial artery)
- **Harvest**: Vestibular incision at maxillary tuberosity → blunt dissection under periosteum → gentle traction
- **Best for**: Medium-to-large OAFs; where buccal vestibule depth must be preserved
- **Advantages**: Rich multi-source vascularity; negligible donor-site morbidity; no buccal sulcus reduction; exposed fat self-epithelializes within 2-6 weeks without secondary coverage
- **Disadvantages**: Risk of necrosis and new fistulae for very large defects; technique-sensitive; prior head/neck radiation reduces BFP size and mobility → consider alternative
- **Recommended modification**: Double-layered closure (BFP + oral mucosa overlay) to minimize postoperative complications

#### 3. Palatal Rotational Flap
- **Best for**: OAFs > 10 mm; late presentations requiring full-thickness tissue
- **Design**: Full-thickness mucoperiosteal flap including greater palatine artery; rotation through tunnel under oral mucosa
- **Key maneuver**: Subepithelial dissection on rotating region to release stiffness, avoid arterial twisting, prevent necrosis
- **Advantages**: Abundant keratinized tissue; robust vascularity (greater palatine artery); does not reduce buccal vestibule depth; best for large defects
- **Disadvantages**: Flap stiffness limits rotation; donor site exposed palatal bone → secondary healing (~2 weeks; cover with collagen sponge or palatal splint)
- **Note**: Circular excision of fistula tract often reveals larger defect than appears at entrance

## Methodology

- PubMed literature search (narrative, not systematic)
- Illustrative case series from Kyung Hee University OMS department (exact n not formally stated for the series; CBCT/panoramic/Waters' view diagnostic workup shown)
- Flap techniques compared on: indication, blood supply, sulcus depth impact, donor site, complications

## Results

All OAF cases presented were treated successfully with complete closure using the described local flap techniques. Specific outcome data (success rates, complications rates) are not quantitatively reported — this is a narrative review with illustrative cases, not a clinical study.

**Diagnostic workup recommended**:
- Panoramic radiograph + Waters' view: initial sinus opacity screening
- CBCT: confirms OAF tract extent and sinus involvement
- Valsalva / cheek-blowing / probing: useful adjuncts but may miss small perforations or widen tract if over-applied
