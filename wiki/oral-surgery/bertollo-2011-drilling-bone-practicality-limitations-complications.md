## Summary
This 2011 chapter from Bertollo and Walsh (UNSW Sydney's Surgical and Orthopaedic Research Laboratories) is a foundational reference on the biomechanics of bone drilling. Although written for an orthopaedic audience covering joint arthroplasty and fracture fixation, the physical principles transfer directly to dental implant osteotomy: any rotating drill bit converts mechanical work energy into thermal energy at the cutting face, and the resulting temperature spike — if above species- and exposure-dependent thresholds — produces osteonecrosis that compromises healing.

The chapter consolidates the classic time–temperature thresholds that define every modern irrigation and drill-sequencing protocol: 47 °C sustained for 1 minute reduces bone's regenerative capacity (Eriksson & Albrektsson 1983); 50 °C for 30 s causes cellular necrosis (Lundskog 1972); 56 °C produces frank osteonecrosis (Matthews & Hirsch 1972). Without irrigation, k-wire insertion and cortical drilling routinely exceed 100 °C, and oscillating saws in total knee arthroplasty surpass that level — illustrating why cooling is non-negotiable.

For the implant clinician, this chapter is most useful as the *engineering rationale* behind sequential dental drilling, saline irrigation, low rpm in cortical bone, and — by extension — the non-cutting compaction-drilling (osseodensification) paradigm of Huwais 2017, which sidesteps the heat-generation problem by displacing rather than excavating bone. It does not, on its own, dictate any dental protocol; it provides the thermodynamic substrate on which dental-specific osteotomy decisions rest.

## Key Contributions
- Codifies the time–temperature osteonecrosis thresholds (47 °C/1 min, 50 °C/30 s, 56 °C) that govern all bone drilling, dental included.
- Identifies primary heat sources at the machining face: plastic deformation and shear failure of bone, plus friction.
- Lists the operational variables surgeons can manipulate: drill geometry, diameter, rpm, feed-rate, axial thrust force, initial bit temperature, and irrigation.
- Documents that uncooled cortical drilling and k-wire insertion routinely exceed 100 °C, providing the empirical case for mandatory cooling in implant osteotomy.
- Sets the engineering precondition for non-cutting compaction approaches: any cutting drill inevitably generates heat in proportion to volumetric bone removal.

## Methodology
Narrative synthesis of three decades of bone-drilling literature spanning orthopaedics, neurosurgery, craniomaxillofacial, and ENT surgery. The authors organise the material thematically: thermodynamics of drilling, biology of thermal damage, drill-bit geometry and materials, operational variables (rpm, feed-rate, thrust, irrigation), and complications. No original experimental data are presented in the front section reviewed here; the chapter cites in vitro and in vivo animal data from the broader literature.

## Results
Numerical results in the extracted text are limited to thermal-necrosis thresholds and observed temperatures, not effect sizes:
- 47 °C × 1 min — reduced bone regenerative capacity
- 50 °C × 30 s — cellular necrosis
- 50 °C cortical — implicated in reduced regenerative capacity
- 56 °C — osteonecrosis
- k-wire / uncooled cortical drilling: > 100 °C
- Oscillating saws in TKA: > 100 °C

Drill-geometry-specific effect sizes for dental implant osteotomy were not extractable from the available text.
