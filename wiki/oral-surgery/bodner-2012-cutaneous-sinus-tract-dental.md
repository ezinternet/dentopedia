## One-line Summary

Retrospective case series (n=28 children, mean age 10.25y): cutaneous sinus tract (CST) of dental origin most often arises from a carious mandibular first molar presenting on the mandibular–submandibular skin; mean 6.5-month diagnostic delay; resolves rapidly after root canal therapy or extraction, with scar revision needed in 29% of (longer-standing) cases.

## Summary

Cutaneous sinus tract (CST) of dental origin is a chronic draining skin lesion fed by a periapical (odontogenic) infection. It is frequently misdiagnosed as a primary dermatologic or surgical lesion, leading to repeated, ineffective skin-directed interventions while the offending tooth is overlooked. This retrospective series of 28 children (mean age 10.25 years, range 4–16; male:female 1:1.74) characterizes the pediatric presentation. Lesions most often appeared in the mandibular–submandibular area, the mandibular first molar was the most common source tooth (followed by the mandibular incisor), and dental caries was the leading etiology. The mean delay to correct diagnosis was 6.5 months (range 0.3–12). After appropriate dental treatment (root canal therapy or extraction of the source tooth), the cutaneous tracts resolved rapidly. Surgical scar revision was required in 8/28 patients (29%) for cosmetic improvement, and these patients had longer-standing lesions — supporting the principle that early treatment of the dental infection can yield spontaneous, scar-free healing of the skin lesion. Last-dental-visit data indicated poor preventive dental care in this population.

## Key Contributions

- One of the larger pediatric series (n=28) characterizing CST of dental origin, where pediatric-specific data were previously sparse.
- Localizes the typical pediatric presentation: mandibular–submandibular skin, mandibular first molar source, caries etiology.
- Quantifies a clinically important diagnostic delay (mean 6.5 months) driven by misdiagnosis as a skin lesion.
- Links lesion duration to outcome: longer-standing lesions more often need scar revision (29%); early treatment can heal the skin without a scar.
- Frames the entity as a sentinel of poor pediatric preventive dental care.

## Methodology

- Retrospective review of 28 children with CST of dental origin.
- Recorded: age, sex, skin-lesion site, duration to diagnosis, teeth involved, etiology, treatment, outcome, and last dental care visit.
- Treatment was root canal therapy or extraction of the offending tooth; surgical scar revision performed when cosmetically indicated.

## Results

- Mean age 10.25 y (range 4–16); male:female 1:1.74 (female predominance).
- Most common skin site: mandibular–submandibular area.
- Most involved tooth: mandibular first molar, then mandibular incisor.
- Etiology: caries most common.
- Diagnostic delay: mean 6.5 months (range 0.3–12).
- After dental treatment, CSTs resolved rapidly.
- Scar revision in 8/28 (29%), associated with longer lesion duration.
- Preventive dental care was poor (per last dental visit).
- Pediatric CST differs from adults in lesion site, duration, and involved tooth, but is similar in etiology and treatment.
