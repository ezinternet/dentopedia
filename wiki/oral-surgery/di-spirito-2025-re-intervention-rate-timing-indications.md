## Summary

This is the first umbrella review (systematic review of systematic reviews) to quantify the long-term re-intervention burden after mandibular third-molar coronectomy. Pooling 6 systematic reviews (5896 subjects, 7913 successful coronectomies), the overall re-intervention rate was 4.45%, with most re-interventions occurring early (mean 10.4 months; range 6 months to 10 years). It reframes the coronectomy discussion away from "does it cause re-operations" toward "how often, when, and why" — clarifying that root migration is common (12.20%) but usually self-limiting, and that re-intervention is genuinely driven by root exposure, infection, and pain.

## Key Contributions

- First SR-of-SRs estimate of re-intervention rate, timing, and indications for coronectomy.
- Distinguishes root migration (a frequent sequela) from re-intervention (an infrequent event).
- Reconfirms low IAN injury (0.76%) at the highest available evidence-aggregation level.

## Methodology

- PRISMA 2020, PROSPERO CRD4202455688; databases Scopus, MEDLINE/PubMed, BioMed Central, Web of Science, Cochrane, PROSPERO to 30 Dec 2024.
- Inclusion: coronectomy patients with ≥6-month follow-up.
- AMSTAR 2 quality appraisal; overlap quantified by Corrected Cover Area (CCA).
- 83 records → 6 systematic reviews included; narrative synthesis with descriptive statistics.

## Results

- Overall re-intervention rate: **4.45%**.
- Timing: 6 months–10 years; **mean 10.4 months**.
- Re-intervention indications: root exposure **16.76%**, infection **4.55%**, pain **2.84%**.
- Root migration: **12.20%** (usually stabilizes ~1 year).
- IAN injury: **0.76%**.
