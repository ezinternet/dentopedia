## Summary
This prospective observational study (Indian J Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg 2022;74:5832-5835) describes etiology, microbiology, management, and outcomes of 150 deep neck space infection (DNSI) cases at a tertiary care hospital in India. DNSI is a potentially life-threatening condition due to proximity to the airway and risk of mediastinal spread. The study documents the epidemiology of DNSI in an Indian tertiary care context, highlighting odontogenic infections as the primary source and Ludwig's angina as the most common clinical presentation. Streptococcus is the dominant organism. Surgical drainage is required in a substantial proportion.

## Key Contributions
- Odontogenic origin accounts for 42.66% of DNSI cases — the single most common etiology
- Ludwig's angina is the most frequently involved deep neck space (24.66%)
- Streptococcus spp. is the most common causative organism (31.33%)
- I&D surgical drainage required in 38% of cases; remainder managed conservatively
- Emergency tracheostomy performed in a subset with airway compromise
- Mediastinitis documented as most serious complication
- Diabetes mellitus is a significant comorbidity predisposing to Gram-negative infections

## Methodology
- Prospective observational study
- Setting: Tertiary care hospital, India (ENT + Oral Surgery)
- n = 150 consecutive DNSI patients
- Data: demographics, source of infection, space involved, CT/ultrasound findings, pus cultures, management (conservative/I&D/tracheostomy), complications, outcomes
- Descriptive statistical analysis

## Results
- **Etiology**:
  - Odontogenic: 42.66% (most common)
  - Tonsillitis/peritonsillar: second most common
  - Parapharyngeal/retropharyngeal/other: remainder
- **Space involved**:
  - Ludwig's angina (bilateral submandibular + sublingual): 24.66% — most common
  - Parapharyngeal: frequent
  - Retropharyngeal: particularly in pediatric cases
- **Microbiology**:
  - Streptococcus spp.: 31.33%
  - Staphylococcus aureus: second most common
  - Klebsiella, E. coli, Pseudomonas: minority; more frequent in diabetics
  - Mixed flora with anaerobes common
- **Management**:
  - I&D: 38%
  - Conservative (IV antibiotics): remainder without drainable collection
  - Emergency tracheostomy: performed in airway-compromised patients (~10–12% of Ludwig's angina)
- **Complications**: Mediastinitis, aspiration pneumonia, airway obstruction
- **Mortality**: Low overall; highest in mediastinitis cases
