## One-line Summary

Dual-part paper (narrative review + SR, 11 studies, 2000–2021): local irrigation/debridement is evidence-based first-line treatment for pericoronitis, yet ~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics, making pericoronitis one of the top drivers of inappropriate antibiotic use in dentistry.

## Summary

Schmidt et al. (2021) is a dual-part review addressing the gap between what dentists should do for pericoronitis and what they actually do. Part A synthesises evidence-based guidance from professional societies, government health agencies, and peer-reviewed literature. Part B is a systematic review (MEDLINE + Web of Science, 2000–2021) of 11 studies (6 questionnaire-based, 5 patient-cohort) quantifying actual antibiotic prescribing behaviour.

Pericoronitis is inflammation of pericoronal soft tissue around an incompletely erupted tooth, predominantly the lower third molar (95% of cases), with a peak incidence in 20–29-year-olds (~4.92%). It is a plaque-induced bacterial process whose primary treatment is local — irrigation, debridement, and removal of the pericoronal stagnation space — not systemic antibiotics. Antibiotics are reserved for spread of infection (deep neck spaces, Ludwig's angina, peritonsillar abscess) or systemic involvement (fever, trismus, dysphagia). Despite this, Part B found that ~75% of surveyed dentists prescribe antibiotics for pericoronitis; in patient cohort studies, antibiotics were given to >50% of pericoronitis patients. Pericoronitis ranked in the top 4 diagnoses driving antibiotic prescribing in questionnaire studies and 1st or 2nd in patient cohort studies.

## Key Contributions

- Provides a full clinical framework: pericoronitis classification (transient/non-transient; acute/chronic), etiopathogenesis, risk factors (URI, stress, menstruation, smoking), complications (Ludwig's angina, quinsy), and stepwise management.
- Establishes local therapy — irrigation/debridement as primary intervention — with operculectomy or extraction reserved for recurrent/persistent cases.
- Demonstrates a stark evidence-practice gap: ~75% of dentists prescribe antibiotics, primarily because of diagnostic uncertainty (47.3%), time pressure (30%), and treatment delay decisions (72.5%).
- Identifies amoxicillin and metronidazole as the most prescribed (and the evidence-appropriate empirical choice) for severe cases.
- Highlights aPDT (antimicrobial photodynamic therapy) as a promising adjunctive option with no resistance risk, though still early-stage evidence.
- Shows dentistry is increasing antibiotic prescribing while other specialties decrease; 10% of all US antibiotic prescriptions are from dentists; 96.6% of dental antibiotic prescriptions in one study were for irrational or uncertain indications.

## Methodology

- **Part A**: Narrative/evidence synthesis using PICO framework; sources include NICE, NHS Faculty of General Dental Practice (FGDP) UK 2020 guidelines, National Dental Advisory Committee Scotland 2016, and peer-reviewed literature.
- **Part B SR**: MEDLINE + Web of Science (January 2000 – May 2021), no language restriction; MeSH: "Pericoronitis AND Antibiotics"; three-author independent screening/extraction; consensus for disagreements.
- **Included studies**: 11 (6 questionnaire, 5 patient cohort); excluded: studies <15 participants, in vitro, reviews, case reports, conference summaries, letters, studies not distinguishing antibiotic vs non-antibiotic treatment.
- No meta-analysis; results tabulated descriptively by study type.

## Results

### Evidence-Based Treatment (Part A)

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Mild/moderate pericoronitis | Local irrigation, debridement; NSAIDs for pain; no antibiotics |
| Recurrent pericoronitis (1st episode, not severe) | Operculectomy ± gingivoplasty; extraction not indicated |
| 2nd or subsequent episode | Extraction; do not delay once decided |
| Severe: infection spread / systemic symptoms | Metronidazole + amoxicillin; consider combination in severe cases; culture if possible |
| Penicillin allergy | Erythromycin |
| Surgical approach (drainage) | Cautery or laser preferred over scalpel |
| Adjunct | aPDT promising but insufficient evidence for routine recommendation |

- Trismus, dysphagia, floor-of-mouth swelling, difficult breathing → hospital transfer.
- Warfarin interaction with metronidazole: anticoagulant effect enhanced; alcohol contraindicated with metronidazole.

### Antibiotic Prescribing Reality (Part B)

- **Questionnaire studies (n=6)**: ~75% of dentists prescribed antibiotics for pericoronitis; top-4 diagnosis for antibiotic use in all questionnaire studies.
- **Patient cohort studies (n=5)**: >50% of pericoronitis patients received antibiotics; pericoronitis ranked 1st or 2nd among all diagnoses for antibiotic use.
- **Most prescribed agents**: Amoxicillin and metronidazole (appropriate choice, but overused; also consistent with [[drug/antibiotics/de-angelis-2025-antibiotic-third-molar-extraction-prevention-sr]] findings).
- **Lower prescribing in UK military (Combes) and Germany (Mahmoody)**: attributed to better guideline adherence.
- **Why dentists overprescribe**: diagnostic uncertainty, time pressure, patient expectations, postponing definitive treatment, social background considerations.
- **Dentist self-awareness**: 91% worried about antibiotic resistance; 47.7% felt inadequately trained; 93% wanted practical guideline updates.
