## Summary

This narrative review provides a comprehensive framework for sterilization and disinfection in dental practice, addressing the full instrument-reprocessing cycle from initial cleaning through sterile storage.

### Instrument Washing and Cleaning
Cleaning must always precede disinfection and sterilization — it removes organic/inorganic burden that would otherwise shield microorganisms. Automated washer-disinfectors achieve >7-log₁₀ microbial reduction and are superior to manual cleaning in reproducibility.

### Disinfection Levels (Spaulding Classification)
- **Noncritical items** (intact skin contact): low-level disinfection — QACs, alcohols
- **Semicritical items** (mucous membrane): high-level disinfection — glutaraldehyde 2%, OPA, H₂O₂, peracetic acid, chlorine-based systems
- **Critical items** (sterile tissue): sterilization required

### Heat Sterilization
**Autoclave (moist heat / pressurized steam)**
- Parameters: 121–132°C at 12 psi for ≥15 min
- Types: N (unwrapped, immediate use), B (bagged, storable), S (factory-defined programs)
- Biological indicator (BI): *Geobacillus stearothermophilus* spores
- Advantages: non-toxic, sporicidal, rapid penetration; disadvantage: corrodes/combusts heat-sensitive materials

**Dry Heat**
- Parameters: 160°C for ≥60 min; mechanism: oxidation of microbial proteins
- BI: *Bacillus atrophaeus*
- Lower corrosion risk than autoclave — preferred for burs and rotary instruments
- Cannot sterilize plastics, fabrics, or liquids

### Chemical Sterilization
**Unsaturated Chemical Vapor**
- Alcohol + formaldehyde at 270°C in pressurized chamber, 20–40 min
- Very low corrosion (low water content); instruments must be completely dry before cycle
- BI: *B. atrophaeus* / *B. subtilis*

**Glutaraldehyde 2%**
- High-level disinfectant for impressions and thermolabile instruments
- Effective against HIV, coronaviruses, spores; BI: *B. subtilis* / *G. stearothermophilus*
- Health risk if mishandled; requires controlled environment and ventilation

**Vaporized Hydrogen Peroxide (HPV)**
- 20-min cycle: 3 min application + 17 min chemotoxic phase
- HPV achieves 6-log₁₀ reduction vs <4-log for aerosolized H₂O₂ (aHP); safer residual leakage
- Decomposes to water + oxygen — no toxic residues; BI: *G. stearothermophilus*
- Systematic review (Ahmed & Mulder): HPV significantly reduces bacteria, viruses, and spores on dental clinic surfaces

### Radiation Sterilization
**UVC Irradiation (220–290 nm)**
- Damages microbial DNA → prevents replication
- ZAPARAY™ UVC LED chamber: up to 9-log₁₀ on Petri dishes; 3.23–6.25 log on non-rinsed dental tools; rinsing before exposure improves efficacy
- Systematic review (Eslami et al., 35 articles): effective for dental impressions, toothbrushes, prostheses, titanium implants; UVC highest efficacy among UV subtypes
- Limitations: cannot penetrate shadows/cracks; risks to skin/eyes; may discolor plastics
- BI: *Bacillus pumilus*

**Gamma Rays**
- High penetration through dense/complex devices; no significant heating — safe for heat-sensitive materials
- Dose must be carefully controlled to avoid material degradation
- BI: *B. pumilus*; equivalent efficacy with X-rays and electron beams at 1–6 kGy (McEvoy et al.)

### Gas Sterilization
**Ethylene Oxide (EtO)**
- 5-stage cycle (~2.5 hr + aeration); high penetration at room temperature
- Sterilizes plastics and electronics safely; carcinogenic, explosive — high cost, strict environmental controls
- BI: *B. atrophaeus*; de Sousa Iwamoto et al.: EtO preserved ECM molecular structure of dental scaffolds

**Ozone**
- Antimicrobial via oxidation of lipid structures, proteins, nucleic acids; penetrates porous surfaces
- No toxic residues; room must be vacated during use (respiratory hazard at high concentrations)
- BI: *B. subtilis*

### Biological Indicator (BI) Protocols
BIs are the only globally accepted method for confirming sterilization efficacy (WHO, ADA, CDC).

| Sterilization method | BI organism |
|---|---|
| Autoclave (moist heat) | *Geobacillus stearothermophilus* |
| Dry heat | *Bacillus atrophaeus* |
| Chemical vapor / glutaraldehyde | *B. subtilis* / *G. stearothermophilus* |
| Hydrogen peroxide (HPV) | *G. stearothermophilus* |
| UVC irradiation | *Bacillus pumilus* |
| Gamma radiation | *Bacillus pumilus* |
| Ethylene oxide | *Bacillus atrophaeus* |
| Ozone | *Bacillus subtilis* |

**Frequency requirements:**
- ADA/OSAP/CDC (USA): weekly
- WHO: weekly for autoclave and dry heat
- Mexico NOM-013-SSA-2015: bi-monthly minimum (legally binding)

**Common causes of BI failure (Patiño-Marín et al. 2024):** lack of staff training, inadequate equipment maintenance, incorrect temperature/time/preheating, poor BI interpretation — human error is the primary factor.

### Advanced Decontamination Technologies
- **Antimicrobial coatings**: silver (Ag), copper (Cu), and TiO₂ photo-activated surfaces significantly reduce microbial loads on high-touch and storage surfaces
- **Far-infrared radiation (FIR)**: reduced CFUs to zero over 30-day storage vs 68.2% contamination in untreated packages — operates by lowering humidity in storage areas
- **Air purification**: HEPA filters + UVC reduce airborne pathogens; VOC control + continuous ventilation recommended in storage areas
- **RFID/barcode labeling**: automated instrument tracking through sterilization → storage → use cycle reduces human error

### Sterile Storage
- CDC recommended conditions: 20–25°C, 30–60% relative humidity; storage areas separate from treatment areas
- **Event-related sterility** (now preferred over time-related): instrument remains sterile unless compromised by an event (package damage, moisture exposure, handling error)
- Storage duration guidelines:
  - Paper/plastic pouches: ≤6 months
  - Rigid containers with intact seals: ≤12 months
  - Reusable cloth wraps: ≤1 month
- Packaging materials per ISO 11607-1 must be compatible with the sterilization method used
- Specialized markers (VWR Lab Markers, Fisherbrand) or industrial markers (Sharpie Industrial) should be used to ensure label integrity through sterilization cycles

## Key Contributions
1. Integrates all major sterilization methods into a single clinical reference framework for dental practices
2. Maps BI organisms to each sterilization method — directly actionable for quality assurance protocols
3. Addresses storage as a critical phase of the sterilization chain (commonly overlooked)
4. Reviews advanced technologies (HPV, FIR, antimicrobial coatings) with supporting evidence
5. Highlights human error as the dominant cause of sterilization failures — argues for training as the highest-yield intervention

## Methodology
Narrative review; no systematic search protocol or PRISMA reporting. Draws on peer-reviewed studies, international guidelines (CDC, WHO, ADA, OSAP, ISO 11607-1), and Mexican standard NOM-013-SSA-2015. Published open-access in Cureus (2025-02-15).
