## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 63 studies finds electrolyzed water (EW) is an effective broad-spectrum disinfectant for dental surfaces, materials, and waterlines (up to 98.1% microbial kill, comparable to glutaraldehyde), with antiviral activity including against SARS-CoV-2-related viruses, though high risk of bias limits conclusions (35.18% NPQIP "Yes" rate).

## Summary

This systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42020206986; PRISMA 2020; AMSTAR-2) synthesized evidence on electrolyzed water (EW) for microbiologic control in the dental setting during the COVID-19 pandemic. From 290 screened articles, 63 studies met inclusion criteria — 58 in vitro and 5 RCTs. Due to substantial heterogeneity, a qualitative synthesis was performed.

**Electrolyzed water types** tested include acidic EW (AEW, pH ~2.5), slightly acidic EW (SAEW, pH 5–6.5), superoxidized water, electrolyzed hydrogen water, and oxidative potential water (OPW). All generate reactive oxidizing species (free chlorine, reactive oxygen species) responsible for antimicrobial activity.

**Key finding**: EW is effective across a wide spectrum of oral pathogens and dental substrates, with strong in vitro evidence and limited but supportive RCT data. The overall methodological quality is low.

## Key Contributions

- First SR on EW specifically for dental microbiologic control in the COVID-19 context.
- Demonstrates broad-spectrum activity: antibacterial (caries, periodontal, endodontic pathogens), antifungal (*Candida*), and antiviral (HBV, HIV, HSV-1, poliovirus).
- DUWL decontamination: up to 98.1% microbial kill, maintained ≤6 weeks, comparable to glutaraldehyde.
- Material compatibility: PVS and alginate impressions, acrylic resin prostheses, titanium surfaces — all tolerate EW without dimensional or structural compromise.
- Identifies critical RoB gap: only 35.18% NPQIP entries answered "Yes"; randomization and blinding rarely reported.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review; qualitative synthesis only (heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis).
- **Databases**: MEDLINE/PubMed, EMBASE, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane CENTRAL, LILACS; grey literature (OpenGrey, PQDT-ProQuest); manual journal search.
- **Inclusion**: All experimental study designs (RCTs, non-RCTs, animal studies with control, in vitro) on EW in the dental setting evaluating microbiologic outcomes.
- **Studies**: 63 included (58 in vitro + 5 RCTs); search through September 2021.
- **RoB**: Modified NPQIP tool; 756 entries; 35.18% "Yes", 48.15% "No".

## Results

### Antibacterial (32 in vitro studies)

EW effectively reduces CFU and inhibits biofilm of key pathogens:

| Pathogen group | Representative organisms | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Caries-associated | *S. mutans*, *L. acidophilus* | Significant CFU reduction, biofilm inhibition |
| Periodontopathogens | *P. gingivalis*, *F. nucleatum*, *A. actinomycetemcomitans*, *T. denticola*, *P. intermedia* | Significant viable bacteria reduction |
| Endodontic pathogens | *E. faecalis*, *C. albicans* | Time-dependent CFU reduction ≈ NaOCl |

### Antifungal (9 in vitro studies)
*Candida albicans*: significant CFU reduction across multiple EW types.

### Antiviral (limited studies)
HBV, HIV, poliovirus type 1, HSV-1: reduced infectivity in a time-dependent manner.

### Clinical trials (5 RCTs)
1. EW oral rinse → reduced *S. mutans* vs. tap water (caries).
2. AEW irrigation → effective plaque/gingivitis control.
3. Acid water rinse ≈ chlorhexidine for plaque in dentine.
4. Superoxidized water + SRP → reduced PPD and gingival bleeding at 30 days.
5. OPW vs. 1% NaOCl for necrotic primary teeth pulpectomy → no significant difference.

### Dental office disinfection (15 studies)

| Application | Finding |
|---|---|
| DUWL (handpieces, scalers) | Up to 98.1% kill; sustained ≤6 weeks; ≈ glutaraldehyde |
| PVS / alginate impressions | Effective disinfection; no dimensional or surface compromise |
| Acrylic resin prostheses | Effective; no material damage |
| Titanium/implant surfaces | Effective |
| Toothbrushes, other surfaces | Effective |

### Risk of Bias
- Only 35.18% NPQIP "Yes" entries across 756 items.
- Major deficiencies: no sample size justification (85% "No"), no randomization/blinding specification (>80% "No"), no error bar definitions, no normality testing.
- Despite high RoB, the directional consistency of results across diverse studies supports EW's antimicrobial efficacy.
