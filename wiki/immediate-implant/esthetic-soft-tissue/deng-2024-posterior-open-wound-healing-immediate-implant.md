## One-line Summary

Retrospective cohort (32 patients / 40 posterior immediate implants; 20 reactive soft tissue [RST] vs 20 absorbable collagen sponge [ACS]): both materials gave comparable total soft-tissue healing scores, gingival recession, and hard-tissue (HBW, MBL) changes at ~6 months — ACS scored better tissue colour but worse fibrous repair.

## Summary

This single-centre retrospective cohort (West China Hospital of Stomatology, Sichuan University) addresses an under-reported question: how should the **open wound** of a fresh **posterior** socket be sealed during **immediate implant placement (IIP)** when flap-to-flap primary closure is not feasible? Flap-to-flap primary healing has long been regarded as the ideal but is often hard to achieve over fresh extraction sockets, so the authors compared two open-wound (secondary-intention) sealing strategies: autologous **reactive soft tissue (RST)** versus an **absorbable collagen sponge (ACS)** substitute.

Forty implants placed immediately into posterior sockets (32 patients; mean age 41.84 y) were split 20/20 between RST and ACS. Soft-tissue healing was assessed with a novel scoring system plus gingival recession; hard-tissue healing was tracked radiographically via horizontal bone width (HBW) and interproximal marginal bone level (MBL). Over ~6 months the two strategies were largely equivalent: no difference in total soft-tissue score (2 weeks), comparable gingival recession, and no intergroup difference in HBW/MBL changes. The only sub-score differences favoured ACS on tissue colour (P = 0.016) but RST on fibrous repair (P = 0.043). The authors conclude both RST and ACS are effective for open wound closure in immediate posterior implants.

## Key Contributions

- Direct head-to-head of **RST vs ACS** for sealing the open wound in **posterior** immediate implant placement — a comparison with limited prior reporting.
- Legitimises **open (secondary-intention) wound healing** as an alternative to the traditional ideal of flap-to-flap primary closure over fresh sockets.
- Introduces a **novel soft-tissue healing scoring system** (including tissue-colour and fibrous-repair components) alongside radiographic HBW and MBL endpoints.
- Finds **clinical equivalence** of the two materials on overall soft- and hard-tissue outcomes at ~6 months, with only divergent sub-scores (colour vs fibrous repair).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Retrospective observational cohort, single centre; registered ChiCTR2200059810, ethics WCHSIRB-CT-2021-442.
- **Sample**: 38 assessed → 6 excluded (inclusion criteria / incomplete CBCT) → **32 patients** (18 F, 14 M; mean 41.84 y, range 22–73), **40 implants**, all in posterior sockets.
- **Groups**: 20 implants RST (autologous reactive soft tissue) vs 20 implants ACS (absorbable collagen sponge).
- **Soft-tissue outcome**: novel healing scoring system + gingival recession (measured before extraction and after restoration).
- **Hard-tissue outcome**: horizontal bone width (HBW) and interproximal MBL on radiographs.
- **Follow-up**: ~6 months overall; soft-tissue score reported at 2 weeks postoperatively.

## Results

| Endpoint | Finding |
|---|---|
| Total soft-tissue healing score (2 wk) | No significant difference RST vs ACS |
| Tissue colour | ACS **better** than RST (P = 0.016) |
| Fibrous repair | ACS **worse** than RST (P = 0.043) |
| Gingival recession | Comparable (pre-extraction and post-restoration) |
| HBW change | No intergroup difference |
| MBL change | No intergroup difference |

**Bottom line**: Within its limitations, both RST and ACS were effective for open wound closure, achieving ideal soft- and hard-tissue healing in immediate posterior implant placement. Choice between them may hinge on the colour-vs-fibrous-repair trade-off and on whether autologous tissue harvest (RST) is preferred over a ready-made substitute (ACS).
