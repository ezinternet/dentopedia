## One-line Summary

Conceptual review (Cosyn & De Bruyckere, Ghent) proposing 11 buccal-dehiscence morphotypes for IIP from vertical extent (Type II-a/b/c), lateral extent (contained/partially-contained/non-contained), and facial soft tissue support; 2 morphotypes (Type III + extended defect) are absolute contraindications, the other 9 map to 3 escalating regenerative strategies graded by regenerative potential.

## Summary

Immediate implant placement (IIP) in compromised sockets is supported only by low-level case-series/cohort evidence, partly because studies rarely report the *vertical and lateral extent* of a buccal dehiscence — the very dimensions that determine whether and how a socket can be regenerated. Cosyn & De Bruyckere (Ghent) address this by proposing a conceptual classification: **11 buccal-dehiscence morphotypes** built from vertical extent (Elian Type II / Chu II-a <4 mm, II-b 4-6 mm, II-c >6 mm), a **new lateral-extent scheme** (contained → partially contained → non-contained → extended), and facial soft tissue support (Type III). Two morphotypes — **Type III** (dehiscence + facial recession) and **extended defects** (interproximal bone loss reaching the adjacent tooth) — are absolute contraindications because the regeneration chamber loses its essential soft-tissue or interproximal-bone support, carrying a high esthetic-failure risk. The remaining nine are amenable to IIP and stratify into three regenerative-potential tiers, each with a defined surgical recipe. The framework is unvalidated and requires future clinical studies; the authors stress ARP + late placement as the easier, better-evidenced alternative for less-experienced surgeons.

## Key Contributions

- **Morphotype → strategy decision framework** translating socket morphology into a reproducible IIP case-selection and treatment scheme.
- A **new lateral-extent classification** (contained/partially-contained/non-contained/extended) ordered by regenerative potential, refining Kan's V/U/UU scheme and emphasizing the under-studied lateral (cervical/interproximal) dimension.
- **Safe / Danger / Failure zone** model for 3-D positioning: safe zone begins 4 mm below the facial soft-tissue margin (vertical) and requires a buccal gap >2 mm (horizontal); danger zone 2 mm × 1 mm; failure zone → exposed shoulder / no buccal wall / advanced recession. Computer-assisted surgery + bone-track drill advocated as standard of care.

## Methodology

Conceptual / narrative review — no systematic search, primary data, or meta-analysis. Synthesizes existing classifications (Elian, Chu, Kan) and bone-healing biology (regeneration-chamber structural stability + mesenchymal stem cell migration from surrounding bone/periosteum) with the authors' expert experience, illustrated by clinical cases and CBCT/3-D reconstructions. Strategies are proposals requiring clinical validation → `confidence: narrative-review`.

## Results

**Morphotype → regenerative-strategy decision framework** (the core deliverable):

| Regenerative potential | Morphotypes | Regenerative strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Contraindicated** | Type III (dehiscence + facial recession); Extended defect (to adjacent tooth) | Do **not** place an immediate implant (high esthetic-failure risk; consider ARP + late placement) |
| **Relatively high** | Type II-a (all lateral types); Type II-b contained | **Modified trimodal** — flapless mandatory, **barrier omitted**, wider buccal gap + slightly **thicker CTG** (extra soft-tissue augmentation for II-a non-contained, most prone to cervical collapse) |
| **Moderate** | Type II-b partially contained; Type II-c contained | **Four-pillar + non-supportive barrier** (native collagen membrane — surrounding bone still gives support; no lateral/apical pouch) |
| **Low** | Type II-b non-contained; Type II-c partially contained; Type II-c non-contained | **Four-pillar + supportive barrier + composite bone graft** (autogenous cortico-cancellous shell from tuberosity OR porcine supportive barrier over the bony edges; moldable graft + autogenous bone chips) |

Decision-tree order: (1) confirm a dehiscence via CBCT + clinical exam; (2) exclude the two absolute contraindications (Type III; extended defect — and the basal-bone contraindication where the tooth lies outside the bony envelope); (3) classify the morphotype → read off regenerative potential → apply the matched strategy. **Regenerative potential is inversely related to complication risk.**

Diagnostic caveats: CBCT over-reports dehiscence (very thin walls invisible → low specificity/PPV); bone sounding + interproximal probing approximate true crestal/lateral anatomy, but intrasurgical confirmation is always required. Flapless surgery is mandatory in any compromised socket (flap reflection disrupts buccal-wall blood supply). Supporting datum: a 10-year RCT treated ≥5 mm-vertical dehisced sockets with a tuberosity cortico-cancellous shell, achieving durable regeneration and only 0.13 mm midfacial recession with concomitant CTG.

**Limitations**: all nine strategies are unvalidated (need CBCT/outcome/histology studies); the high/moderate/low tiering is an estimated model; strategies are framed only for IIP (not vs. early placement or ARP + late placement); apical-fenestration and acutely infected sockets are out of scope.
