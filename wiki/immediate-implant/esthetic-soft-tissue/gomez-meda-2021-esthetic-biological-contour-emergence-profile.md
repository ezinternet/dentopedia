## Summary

The Esthetic Biological Contour (EBC) concept provides a structured, zone-based framework for emergence profile design of implant-supported restorations. The concept integrates implant 3D position, peri-implant soft tissue thickness, biologic width requirements, and restoration contour into a unified decision-making framework applicable to both provisional and definitive restorations in the esthetic zone.

The emergence profile is divided into four functional zones:

| Zone | Location | Ideal Contour | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 1 | Implant–abutment interface / sub-crestal | Platform-driven | Biologic width accommodation |
| Zone 2 | Sub-critical / connective tissue zone | Concave to neutral | Connective tissue support, crestal bone stability |
| Zone 3 | Critical contour / junctional epithelium zone | Slight convexity | Gingival margin position control |
| Zone 4 | Supragingival / esthetic zone | Convex (crown-like) | Esthetic outcome |

## Key Contributions

- **Zone 1**: Governed by implant platform position and connection design. Proper apico-coronal implant placement (1–3 mm sub-crestal for bone-level implants) is prerequisite for adequate Zone 2 concavity without compromising biologic width.
- **Zone 2 (subcritical contour)**: The most biologically critical zone. A concave or neutral contour here maintains crestal bone stability by allowing connective tissue to adapt without compressive ischemia. Convex subcritical contours increase the risk of crestal bone loss and peri-implant mucosal inflammation.
- **Zone 3 (critical contour)**: Positioned at the gingival margin crest. Convexity in this zone displaces the gingival margin apically; concavity elevates it. Clinicians can use Zone 3 contour to fine-tune gingival margin level during provisionalization.
- **Zone 4**: The visible supragingival portion matching the natural emergence of the tooth crown. Primary esthetic driver — shaped to blend with adjacent natural teeth.

The EBC concept integrates three clinical parameters:
1. **Implant 3D position** (apico-coronal, bucco-lingual, mesio-distal): determines the spatial room available for each zone. A palatal/apical implant position allows more generous convexity in Zone 4 without encroaching on biologic width.
2. **Soft tissue thickness**: Mucosa ≥2 mm buccally is associated with greater crestal bone stability (Linkevicius data). Thin biotype requires more conservative (concave) Zone 2 design.
3. **Implant design features**: collar height, connection geometry (internal vs. platform-switching) influence the starting profile at Zone 1.

## Methodology

Narrative literature synthesis and concept proposal. No original clinical data. Key evidence base:

- Su et al. (2010) — critical vs. subcritical contour distinction
- Linkevicius et al. (2009, 2015) — soft tissue thickness and crestal bone stability
- Cochran & Hermann (1997, 2001) — biologic width around titanium implants (~3–4 mm for bone-level)
- Galindo-Moreno et al. (2014) — abutment height ≥3 mm required to prevent bone resorption
- Rompen et al. (2007) — concave abutment contours improve soft tissue stability
- González-Martín et al. (2020) — contour management for immediate and delayed cases

## Results

- No original data; all quantitative benchmarks from cited studies:
  - Biologic width around bone-level implants: ~3–4 mm (connective tissue + junctional epithelium).
  - Soft tissue thickness ≥2 mm buccal → crestal bone stability.
  - Abutment height ≥3 mm → biologic width established without bone loss.
  - Concave subcritical contour → reduced peri-implant inflammation and bone resorption vs. convex.
- Clinical significance: **Proper emergence profile design supports esthetic outcomes and provides favorable biological response to implant-supported restorations** (authors' stated conclusion).
- The EBC framework applies equally to interim provisional and definitive restorations — provisionalization is the primary shaping tool.
