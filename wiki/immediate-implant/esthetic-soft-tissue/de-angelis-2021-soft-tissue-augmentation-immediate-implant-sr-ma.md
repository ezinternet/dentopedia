## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 7 studies (n=272) demonstrates that simultaneous SCTG at IIPP significantly reduces mid-buccal recession (WMD 0.54 mm), increases facial soft tissue thickness (WMD 0.79 mm), and reduces marginal bone loss (WMD 0.13 mm) at 12 months, with low heterogeneity (I² 0–16%).

## Summary

De Angelis et al. (2021) conducted a PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42020181407) to quantify the benefit of sub-epithelial connective tissue grafting (SCTG) simultaneous with immediate implant placement and provisionalization (IIPP) in the esthetic zone. Seven studies published between 2007 and 2021, involving 272 patients, were included. All studies used a flapless surgical approach with non-traumatic extraction and buccal gap grafting. Meta-analysis used random-effects models (weighted mean difference).

The central finding is that SCTG during IIPP confers statistically significant advantage across all three pooled outcomes at 12 months: less mid-buccal mucosal recession, greater facial soft tissue thickness, and less marginal bone loss. Heterogeneity was low across all outcomes (I² ≤ 16%). Implant survival was 100% across all but one study. A key clinical implication is that SCTG not only improves esthetic soft tissue outcomes but also appears to protect marginal bone.

Limitations include the short observation window (all studies ≤12 months), variability in measurement methods, and medium-to-high risk of bias in the included trials.

## Key Contributions

- Pooled WMD estimates for MBML (0.54 mm), FSTT (0.79 mm), and MBL (0.13 mm) provide usable thresholds for clinical decision-making on SCTG indication.
- Low I² (0–16%) across all three outcomes suggests the SCTG benefit is consistent across protocols and patient populations.
- The MBL finding — that SCTG reduces marginal bone loss by 0.13 mm at 12 months — is clinically relevant because it links soft tissue volume to hard tissue protection.
- Identifies xenogeneic collagen matrix as an emerging SCTG alternative requiring further evidence.

## Methodology

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Study design | Systematic review + meta-analysis |
| Search period | January 1966 – January 2020 |
| Databases | MEDLINE, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, Embase |
| Studies included | 7 (4 RCTs, 1 prospective observational, 2 retrospective observational) |
| Patients | 272 |
| Follow-up | ≥12 months (all studies at 12 months) |
| Primary outcome | Mid-buccal mucosal level (MBML) |
| Secondary outcomes | Facial soft tissue thickness (FSTT); marginal bone loss (MBL) |
| Statistical model | Random-effects; weighted mean difference (WMD) |
| Quality assessment | Cochrane RoB 2 (RCTs); Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (observational) |

Surgical protocol across included studies: flapless, non-traumatic extraction, bone-level implants placed 2–4 mm apical to facial gingival margin, buccal gap filled with various grafts (xenograft, autograft, allograft, or combinations). SCTG placed supra- or subperiosteal on buccal aspect. Provisional restoration for 3–6 months.

## Results

**Meta-analysis outcomes at 12 months**

| Outcome | Studies (n) | WMD | 95% CI | p | I² |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MBML (recession) | 5 | 0.54 mm less in SCTG | 0.33–0.75 | <0.001 | 16% |
| FSTT (thickness) | 4 | 0.79 mm more in SCTG | 0.37–1.22 | <0.001 | not reported |
| MBL (bone loss) | 4 | 0.13 mm less in SCTG | 0.07–0.18 | <0.001 | 0% |

**Implant survival**: 100% in 6 of 7 studies; Zuiderveld reported one implant loss in each arm.

**Risk of bias**: 3 RCTs high risk (inadequate randomization reporting), 1 RCT medium risk; all observational studies medium quality (7–8 stars Newcastle-Ottawa).

**Key clinical thresholds**:
- MBML benefit of 0.54 mm is clinically meaningful given that recession of even 0.5 mm in the esthetic zone is visible and affects patient satisfaction.
- FSTT gain of 0.79 mm reduces risk of titanium show-through and supports long-term stability.
- MBL reduction of 0.13 mm at 12 months — small but consistent across studies (I²=0%).
