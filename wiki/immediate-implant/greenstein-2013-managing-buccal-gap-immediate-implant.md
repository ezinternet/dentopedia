## One-line Summary

CE narrative review (Greenstein & Cavallaro 2013) synthesizing animal and human data to provide 14 clinical guidelines for buccal gap management in immediate implant placement, with a key < 2 mm vs. ≥ 2 mm grafting threshold.

## Summary

When a dental implant is placed into a fresh extraction socket, a gap (also called the "jumping distance") forms between the implant surface and surrounding bone. This gap has both horizontal width and vertical depth dimensions. The buccal aspect is of greatest concern because the buccal plate is thin and its resorption leads to soft-tissue recession. Greenstein and Cavallaro reviewed animal and human data to provide practical guidelines for gap management.

**Key clinical decision thresholds:**
- **Gap < 2 mm**: Usually heals spontaneously without biomaterial; clinician may leave open (clot fills) or add DBBM for contour maintenance
- **Gap ≥ 2 mm**: Spontaneous bone fill less predictable; grafting with DBBM recommended
- **Gap > 3–4 mm**: Bone fill documented even without grafts in some human cases, but risk of incomplete fill increases

**Buccal plate thickness** is a critical determinant:
- > 2 mm: Does not usually resorb vertically in humans (Spray et al)
- < 1–2 mm: Hard-tissue augmentation recommended
- ~33% of patients exhibit ~1 mm buccal gingival recession after immediate implant placement

**Immediate implant survival** in fresh sockets and healed ridges is equivalent (97.3–99%), including at infected sites and sites with periapical lesions.

## Key Contributions

1. **14-point clinical guideline set** synthesizing available evidence on buccal gap management
2. Critical distinction between created (artificial) defects in animal models vs. natural gaps — created defects heal more completely and should not be over-extrapolated to clinical settings
3. DBBM identified as the most-studied biomaterial: reduces horizontal bone resorption ~25%, results in more coronal BIC and thicker buccal plate vs. no graft; no single material demonstrably superior
4. Augmentation reduces **horizontal** bone resorption but NOT **crestal** bone loss — an important nuance for outcome expectations
5. Implant positioning emphasized over grafting as primary buccal bone preservation strategy

## Methodology

Narrative CE review of experimental animal studies (primarily dog models, Araújo, Botticelli, Caneva series) and human clinical investigations. Includes a treatment options table comparing all flap and flapless scenarios with their respective advantages and disadvantages.

## Results

### Clinical Guidelines Summary (selected)

| # | Guideline | Key threshold/value |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No flap elevation in esthetic zone; lingual flap only if needed | — |
| 2 | Gaps < 2 mm heal spontaneously; gaps > 2 mm need grafting | 2 mm |
| 5 | Thin buccal plates resorb; ~33% patients show ~1 mm recession | 1 mm plate threshold |
| 6 | Biomaterial in gap < 2 mm maintains horizontal width but not crestal height | — |
| 9 | Implant should be 2 mm from buccal plate | 2 mm safety zone |
| 10 | Avoid oversized implants that encroach on buccal plate | — |
| 11 | Place implants 1 mm subcrestal; platform switching reduces crestal resorption | 1 mm |
| 13 | Flapless overfill with DBBM + provisional crown supports soft tissue | — |

### Implant Positioning Effect on Recession
- Buccally placed: 1.8 mm recession vs. lingually placed: 0.6 mm recession (3× difference)
- Wider implants near buccal plate → greater bone loss than narrower implants

### Flap vs. Flapless
- No consensus on vertical bone loss differences
- More recent data: flapless approach preserves horizontal bone width better
- Flapless only adds blood supply disruption from extraction (PDL blood supply lost); flap elevation additionally disrupts periosteal supply (~0.5 mm extra bone loss)
