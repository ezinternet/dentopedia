## One-line Summary
Clinical case report with literature review documenting successful osseointegration of an immediately placed implant despite the absence of primary stability, arguing that biological factors (bone density, cortical bone, healing potential) can compensate for mechanical deficiency when delayed loading (6+ months) and a modified surgical protocol are used.

# Immediate Implant Placement Without Primary Stability: Case Report and Evidence

**Source**: [[immediate-implant/kim-2016-immediately-placed-implant-without-primary]]  
**Year**: 2016 | **Study Type**: Clinical Case Report | **Focus**: Successful osseointegration despite mechanical instability

## Clinical Challenge Addressed

### Primary Stability Paradigm Reconsidered
Traditional implantology teaches that **primary stability is mandatory** for osseointegration. This case documentation **challenges that absolute requirement**, showing successful integration despite compromised mechanical fixation.

**Key Principle**: Biological factors can compensate for mechanical deficiency when conditions optimized.

## When Primary Stability Cannot Be Achieved

### Clinical Scenarios
- Compromised extraction socket (large defect, poor bone walls)
- Narrow or short bone height
- Grade IV (poor quality) bone
- Large implant-socket gap
- Socket with concave walls providing minimal thread engagement

### Previous Assumption
These situations → extraction → grafting → delayed placement  
**New Evidence**: Immediate placement still possible with modified protocol

## Achieving Success Without Primary Stability

### Required Modifications

**Surgical Approach**:
- Careful socket preparation preserving bone contours
- Precise implant positioning to maximize any bone contact
- Gap management (bone graft, membrane, or spontaneous healing) for socket defects
- Tissue handling to promote soft tissue adaptation

**Biologically Supportive Factors**:
- Fresh socket provides growth factors and healing dynamics
- Extraction trauma + immediate implant may trigger favorable healing response
- Delayed loading allows biological integration without mechanical stress
- Extended healing timeline (vs. 3-4 months standard) acceptable trade-off

**Loading Protocol**:
- **Delayed loading essential** (6+ months minimum)
- No early or immediate loading without primary stability
- Extended osseointegration observation period required
- Monitor for any signs of movement before functional loading

## Treatment Timeline Implications

**Advantage vs. Delayed Placement**:
- Single surgical stage (immediate vs. two surgical stages for delayed)
- Extraction → immediate implant → delayed loading, then restoration
- Avoids second surgery for implant placement
- Reduces total treatment time despite longer implant healing

**Time Comparison**:
- Immediate (no primary stability): Extraction + 6-month healing + restoration = 8 months
- Delayed: Extraction + 3-month socket healing + implant surgery + 4-month healing + restoration = 10+ months

## Clinical Decision Criteria

### When Appropriate to Attempt
- Socket defect significant but finite (not complete loss)
- Bone quality acceptable even if quantity limited
- Patient accepts extended loading timeline
- Esthetics or functional demands justify approach
- Patient compliance likely (delayed loading compliance critical)

### When Contraindicated
- Severe bone deficiency (insufficient for implant stability even 0-mm fit)
- Poor patient compliance (cannot wait for loading)
- High implant failure consequences (critical esthetic zones without backup)
- Systemic factors compromising healing (severe diabetes, immunosuppression, smoking)

## Radiographic and Clinical Monitoring

**Pre-operative**:
- Assess socket bone volume and contours
- Evaluate implant sizing options
- Plan gap management strategy

**Intra-operative**:
- Document any implant mobility during insertion attempt
- Confirm reasonable fit despite lack of primary stability
- Execute gap management protocol

**Post-operative Monitoring**:
- Clinical: No mobility on percussion, no inflammation
- Radiographic: Observe bone changes over healing months
- Timeline: Avoid loading until radiographic evidence of osseointegration
- Success marker: Stable implant, bone apposition visible, no radiolucency

## Evidence Limitations

- Small sample size (case report format)
- Long-term follow-up duration critical but short-term outcomes documented
- Biological response variables uncontrolled
- Success definition variable between studies
- Need for prospective comparative trials

## Evidence Grade
**Case Report/Series** — Documents feasibility and successful outcomes but lacks comparative data on failure rates vs. standard protocols
