SR+MA of 48 RCTs (n=9,618) in non-mental-health medical settings — MI yields an overall OR of 1.55 (1.40–1.71) and is effective even in brief consultations; dental outcomes are explicitly cited as a responsive domain, though the paper has author-bias and subsequent methodological critiques.

## Summary
This is the cross-domain reference paper for MI evidence in medicine. Lundahl et al. pooled 48 RCTs (n=9,618) of MI in medical care settings (excluding pure mental health/substance abuse) and reported a modest but statistically robust overall advantage of OR 1.55 (95% CI 1.40–1.71). MI works across many outcome types — HIV viral load, body weight, alcohol/tobacco, sedentary behavior — and the paper specifically calls out **dental outcomes** as an area where MI shows promise.

The most operationally useful finding is that MI **works in brief consultations**. This matters because dental and medical encounters are typically time-constrained; if MI required 1-hour psychotherapy-style sessions, it would be unimplementable. The evidence here suggests properly trained clinicians can deliver MI effect in standard appointment lengths.

Two cautions on this paper:
1. **Author bias**: Stephen Rollnick (co-developer of MI itself) is on the author list. The paper is honest, but it's not an independent assessment.
2. **Subsequent critique**: Mullin (2013) and van der Wouden & Rietmeijer (2014) published critical commentaries in the same journal pointing out methodological concerns. Cite with awareness.

This paper sits in our wiki not because the primary outcomes are dental, but because:
- It's the highest-power general MI evidence
- It explicitly flags dental outcomes as a positive domain
- It establishes that brief-consultation MI is feasible — directly relevant to clinical workflow

## Key Contributions
- Largest medical-settings MI meta-analysis at time of publication
- Quantifies overall MI advantage across diverse outcomes (OR 1.55)
- Demonstrates MI effectiveness in brief consultations — implementation-feasibility evidence
- Names dental outcomes as one of the promising domains
- Identifies non-responsive outcomes (eating disorders, self-care behaviors) — bounds the claim
- Demonstrates moderator robustness (location, patient characteristics)

## Methodology
RCT-only inclusion. MI vs. comparison conditions in medical care settings. Unique-MI-effect isolation. Random-effects model. Outcome and setting subgroup analyses.

## Results
| Outcome | Value |
|---|---|
| Studies | 48 |
| Participants | 9,618 |
| Overall effect | OR 1.55 (95% CI 1.40–1.71), p < 0.001 |
| Brief consultation effectiveness | Confirmed |

**Effective domains**: HIV viral load, dental outcomes, death rate, body weight, alcohol/tobacco use, sedentary behavior, self-monitoring, confidence in change, treatment approach.

**Not particularly effective**: eating disorders, self-care behaviors, some medical outcomes (e.g., heart rate).

**Moderator robustness**: MI effect held across delivery locations and patient characteristics.

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a clinic deploying MI:
- This is the **citation backbone** for the claim "MI works in brief medical encounters" — directly applicable to dental appointment structure
- Dental outcomes named explicitly as promising — links cleanly to dental-specific SR+MAs (Jahanshahi 2022, Cartes-Velásquez 2024)
- The OR 1.55 ("modest advantage") sets honest expectations — MI doesn't double success rates; it improves them moderately
- The non-effective domains (eating disorders, self-care for chronic disease) caution against overgeneralization

## Open Questions
- Has a non-Rollnick-authored independent MA reached similar effect estimates?
- The 2013 publication date is dated — a refresh covering 2013–present would clarify whether OR 1.55 is stable
- Heterogeneity of pooled outcomes (HIV load vs. body weight in one OR) is methodologically uncomfortable
- See Mullin 2013 and van der Wouden 2014 PEC commentaries for the technical critique
