## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 7 RCTs (n=474; 6 pooled) found NO significant difference between motivational interviewing and standard oral health instruction on plaque, bleeding on probing, or gingival inflammation — evidence insufficient, mostly high risk of bias.

## Summary

This 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42023458021) updated the evidence on motivational interviewing (MI) for periodontal patients by including recent RCTs through May 2023. From 2108 screened records, 7 RCTs (474 patients) entered qualitative synthesis and 6 entered the meta-analysis. Five of the seven trials carried high risk of bias. Pooled random-effects analysis showed no significant difference between MI and standard oral health instruction for plaque level, bleeding on probing, or gingival inflammation, and insufficient evidence for any effect on attachment loss or probing depth. The authors conclude current evidence does not support MI as an adjunctive intervention, while urging cautious interpretation given heterogeneity and bias.

## Key Contributions

- Most current MI-specific periodontal SR+MA, capturing post-2022 RCTs (e.g., Arnett 2022) that earlier reviews missed.
- Isolates MI delivered without other psychological co-interventions.
- Provides a negative/null quantitative anchor against optimistic narrative claims of MI benefit.

## Methodology

- 5 databases + gray literature, ClinicalTrials.gov, WHO ICTRP; search to May 2023.
- PICOT: patients ≥12 y with gingivitis/periodontitis/peri-implantitis; MI alone vs standard oral health instruction; RCTs only.
- Cochrane RoB 2; standardised mean difference with 95% CI; random-effects; heterogeneity via Tau²/I².
- Comorbidity (diabetes/hypertension) and orthodontic patients excluded.

## Results

- 7 RCTs / 474 patients; 6 in meta-analysis.
- Risk of bias: 5 high, 2 some concerns.
- No significant pooled difference: plaque, bleeding on probing, gingival inflammation.
- Attachment loss / probing depth: insufficient evidence.
- Conflicting primary trials: one RCT showed significant gingival index gain with MI; another reported higher plaque scores in the MI group.
