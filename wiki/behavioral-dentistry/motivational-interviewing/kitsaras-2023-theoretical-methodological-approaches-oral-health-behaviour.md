## One-line Summary
Narrative review (Community Dent Oral Epidemiol 2023) mapping the theory/method/delivery landscape of oral-health behaviour change: overarching theories like COM-B and the Behaviour Change Wheel must be selected with care, interventions should be co-designed with users/stakeholders from the start, and technology is now a major delivery vehicle — motivational interviewing is one technique within this wider, theory-driven design space.

## Summary

Note: abstract-only — full text not retrieved; this page is grounded in the published abstract (PMID 36749671).

Oral health behaviour change interventions are gaining momentum globally and, after lagging other health disciplines, the field is now developing fast — largely by importing theories from medicine and healthcare, with varying results. Kitsaras et al. argue that this borrowing is valuable but hazardous: the sheer variety and variation of available theories creates dilemmas, and theory must be selected to fit the problem rather than adopted by default.

The review organizes the work into three distinct decisions — **designing** (which theory/framework), **developing** (how the intervention is built), and **delivering** (through what channel). For design, it highlights **COM-B** (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behaviour) and the **Behaviour Change Wheel** as the leading opportunities, with the caveat that careful consideration is vital. For development, it elevates **co-design / co-development with intended users and stakeholders from the start** as a defining feature of successful, effective interventions — something oral-health work must implement consistently rather than occasionally. For delivery, it identifies **technology-based approaches** as a major emerging vehicle.

The clinical/research takeaway: with multiple options at each stage, careful, user-inclusive, adaptable selection is essential, and the field should learn from the experience of other disciplines while maximizing the theories, frameworks, methodologies, and techniques now available.

## Key Contributions
- Provides a meta-level map of the oral-health behaviour-change field, separating the design / development / delivery decisions.
- Names **COM-B** and the **Behaviour Change Wheel** as the leading overarching theory and framework, while warning that theory proliferation is itself a hazard.
- Establishes **co-design with users and stakeholders from the outset** as a core requirement, not an optional extra.
- Flags **technology** as a major delivery channel for the field going forward.
- Frames specific techniques such as motivational interviewing as elements *selected within* a wider, theory-driven design space — useful context for interpreting why MI's measured efficacy is mixed across studies.

## Results
- No quantitative effect sizes (conceptual review; abstract-only — full text not retrieved).
