## One-line Summary
Systematic review (8 papers, 5 RCTs) of MI in general dental practice — all 5 RCTs showed MI-inclusive interventions improved oral health/behaviour; MI recommended as a trainable dental-team skill.

## Summary
Kay et al. extract, from a larger oral-health-promotion systematic review, the subset of evidence dealing specifically with motivational interviewing in **general dental practice** so that practitioners can judge whether to invest in MI training. MI is framed not as a persuasion technique but as **autonomy support**: the clinician accepts that the patient's choices are their own, drops the "I have the fix" stance, and builds the therapeutic alliance needed for chairside behaviour change. Across 8 quality-screened papers (7 externally valid, 5 RCTs), every RCT showed a positive effect on oral health or health behaviour. The clinical message is that MI is a worthwhile addition to the dental team's skill set.

## Key Contributions
- Context-restricts broad MI evidence to the general-dental-practice chairside setting.
- Articulates the conceptual core (autonomy support, empathy, reflective listening, affirming patient strengths) that distinguishes MI from traditional advice-giving.
- Provides a UK rationale for MI training of the whole dental team.

## Methodology
- Subset of a larger SR (effectiveness of oral-health promotion in dental practice). 20 online resources searched (incl. Ovid Medline, Embase), papers since 1994.
- 8 included; quality + external-validity appraised; 5 RCTs identified. Narrative synthesis (no meta-analysis).

## Results
- 5/5 RCTs: MI-inclusive interventions positively affected oral health and/or health behaviour.
- No quantitative pooled effect (narrative). No comparison of MI-training methods.
- Recommendation: train dental personnel in MI as a high-value, low-cost skill addition.
