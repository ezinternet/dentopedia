## One-line Summary
SR+MA of 6 RCTs (n=2,776) showing MI significantly reduces ECC (dmfs); session quality — not quantity — and ≥3-year follow-up are the conditions for effect to emerge.

## Summary
Jahanshahi et al. pooled 6 RCTs (n=2,776) testing MI delivered to caregivers as an ECC prevention strategy. MI showed a statistically significant effect on reducing dmfs (the primary-dentition equivalent of DMFS), the publication-bias test was clean, and sensitivity analysis confirmed no single study drove the result.

Two practical messages come out clearly:

1. **Session quality beats session count.** Increasing the number of MI sessions doesn't proportionally increase effect; what matters is whether sessions are MI-faithful (open questions, reflective listening, eliciting change talk) rather than just "extra education appointments."

2. **3-year follow-up is the operational horizon.** Shorter follow-up under-detects the effect — caregivers need time for behavior change to translate into measurable dmfs differences.

The evidence base is small (6 RCTs) and fixed-effect was used, so the precision of the pooled estimate is optimistic. But for a counseling intervention with no harm potential, the directional evidence supports adoption.

## Key Contributions
- First (or among first) SR+MA on MI for ECC specifically
- Clinically meaningful outcome (dmfs, not just behavior change proxies)
- Sensitivity analysis robust — result not driven by any single trial
- Identifies session **quality** over **quantity** as the operative variable
- Establishes ≥3-year follow-up as the realistic effect-detection horizon

## Methodology
RCT-only systematic review. PECO framework: caregivers (P), MI intervention (E), standard oral health education (C), ECC measured by dmfs (O). Five databases searched, 1989 → April 2020. Fixed-effect model, mean difference with 95% CI. Egger's test (visual) for publication bias. Sensitivity analysis by single-study exclusion.

## Results
| Outcome | Value |
|---|---|
| RCTs included | 6 |
| Participants | 2,776 |
| MI effect on ECC (dmfs) | Statistically significant reduction |
| Publication bias (Egger visual) | Not detected |
| Sensitivity analysis | Pooled effect robust to single-study exclusion |
| Effect across age groups | Consistent (infants and children) |
| Session count vs. quality | Quality is the operative variable |
| Minimum follow-up for effect | ≥3 years |

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a clinic running pediatric prevention programs:
- MI for caregivers has SR+MA-level evidence as an effective ECC prevention adjunct
- Don't measure success by "number of MI sessions delivered" — measure by **MI fidelity** (could use MITI coding for staff training/QA)
- Set caregiver/parent expectations: visible dmfs differences take **years**, not months
- For hygienist team: MI training is a higher-yield CE topic than another "oral hygiene education technique" workshop
- Pairs with Cartes-Velásquez 2024 (adult MI evidence) for full-lifespan MI program design

## Open Questions
- Random-effects re-analysis would tighten the uncertainty quantification — fixed-effect may understate it
- MI fidelity measurement is rarely reported in primary trials; without it, "MI quality" recommendation is hard to operationalize
- Korean caregiver context (working parents, grandparent caregiving prevalence) may modulate effect — primary research lacking
