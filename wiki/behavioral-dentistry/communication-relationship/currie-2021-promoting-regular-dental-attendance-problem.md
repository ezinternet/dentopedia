## Summary
About one-third of the UK adult population attends dentists only with acute pain ("problem-orientated attenders"). Currie et al. asked what interventions exist to convert these patients to regular attendance — and found almost nothing. Three studies met inclusion: a dental anxiety clinic, an advertising campaign for a "free dental update" open-house event, and the policy-level introduction of free dental check-ups in Scotland.

The substantive finding is therefore not "what works" but "almost no one has tried." The reporting quality of the three studies is poor — intervention components are not clearly described, making replication difficult. From the synthesis, the authors name four design targets for future interventions: dentist communication, dentist-patient relationship, raising patient awareness of need, and cost/access (free check-ups).

This paper is most useful as a **gap map**: when designing patient engagement programs for irregular attenders, you can be sure you're not duplicating existing work, because almost no existing work exists.

## Key Contributions
- Establishes the near-empty evidence base for converting problem-orientated dental attenders
- Maps the three existing interventions to the Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF) for behavior change
- Names the four high-priority design targets for future interventions
- Highlights the reporting quality problem (intervention description omissions) as a barrier to field progress

## Methodology
Eight-database systematic review limited to adult problem-orientated or irregular dental attenders. TDF-based data extraction. Effectiveness and patient view data extracted where available. Three studies met inclusion. Narrative synthesis only.

## Results
| Intervention | Type | Findings |
|---|---|---|
| Dental anxiety clinic | Service redesign | Targets fear-driven avoidance |
| Free dental update advertising campaign | Public outreach | Open-house events letting public meet dentists |
| Free dental check-ups (Scotland policy) | Policy/cost removal | Population-level intervention |

All three were poorly reported, limiting replication.

**Future intervention targets named:**
1. Dentist communication
2. Dentist-patient relationship
3. Increasing awareness of need
4. Cost barrier (free check-ups effect)

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a Korean private practice trying to convert "pain-only" patients to regular recall:
- The UK evidence base is essentially empty — you're not behind, the field is
- The four targets identified (communication, relationship, awareness, cost) align with what's practical in private practice: dentist communication training (link to Khalifah 2019), relationship-building visits (which insurance/회원제 systems can partially fund), patient education about silent disease progression, and pricing strategies that reduce check-up barriers
- This is also a candidate area for **MI** (Motivational Interviewing) deployment — see Cartes-Velásquez 2024 and Jahanshahi 2022 for MI evidence in dental contexts
- Don't expect a published "recipe" — you'll need to design and evaluate locally

## Open Questions
- Korean NHIS context: free check-ups are partially covered (구강검진 사업) but still underused — does the Scottish policy evidence transfer? Unstudied.
- Dental anxiety clinics as a service model — feasible in private practice or only in NHS-style hospital settings?
- The "free open-house" model could be tested as a marketing intervention in private clinics; no quantitative evidence on ROI exists.
