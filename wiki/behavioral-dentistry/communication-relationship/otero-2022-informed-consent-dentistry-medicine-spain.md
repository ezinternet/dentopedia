## One-line Summary

Narrative review of Spanish informed-consent (IC) law and jurisprudence plus an INFLESZ readability analysis of official dental ICDs: IC is a care PROCESS in which the signature is the last link (it is NOT the ICD form), defective IC has produced more convictions than malpractice, and every analysed dental ICD (INFLESZ 41.24–52.18) fell below the 55-point readability threshold for the general Spanish public.

## Summary

This narrative/legal review (Spain) makes one central argument: **informed consent (IC) is not the informed consent document (ICD)**. IC is a *care process* of shared decision-making between clinician and patient — the patient first receives all relevant information (risks, benefits, alternatives, possibility of technique failure), then deliberates, and the **signature on the ICD is merely the last link** in that chain. Treating the signed form as equivalent to consent is the recurring error the paper warns against; verbal information is gradual and cannot be replaced by any written document.

The medico-legal stakes are large. Around **90% of legal claims** against doctors and dentists relate to defects in transmitting information and in IC, and — the paper's most-cited claim — **to date there have been more convictions for defective IC than for malpractice**. Omission or defective IC generates civil liability and compensation (physical *and* moral damage) once proven damage exists, even when only one treatment was available and even when the patient would have proceeded had they been fully informed. Spanish jurisprudence applies a "double standard": without physical damage, not all risks must be disclosed; with physical damage and no malpractice, exhaustive information is demanded retrospectively. Dentistry is largely configured by the courts as **satisfactive/voluntary medicine** (e.g., whitening, some orthodontics), where the *less necessary* the intervention, the *greater* the disclosure required.

## Methodology

- Detailed analysis of **Law 41/2002** (Spain's national regulation of patient autonomy, information, and clinical documentation), regional autonomous-community statutes, and jurisprudence; framed against historical instruments (Nuremberg Code 1947, Declaration of Helsinki 1964, Oviedo Convention 1977, Belmont Report 1978).
- PubMed index search (informed consent / patient information / IC legal / health literacy), articles from 2002 onward.
- Readability of a sample of official dental-society **ICDs** scored with **INFLESZ** software (0–100; ≥55 = accessible to the general public).

## Results

- **IC vs ICD (core distinction).** IC is a process; the ICD is a procedure-specific record. Generic ICDs and forms whose header does not match the procedure are legally invalid. A valid ICD carries the clinician's signature (proof verbal information was given), the patient/representative signature, the date, a revocation section, and a copy for the patient.
- **More convictions for defective IC than for malpractice** — defective/omitted IC is the dominant origin of practitioner legal liability; ~90% of claims relate to information defects.
- **Readability deficit.** The 9 analysed dental ICDs (orthodontics, implant, filling, extraction, endodontics, periapical surgery, immature-tooth pulp treatment, periodontics, oral surgery) scored INFLESZ **41.24–52.18** — all "somewhat difficult," all **below 55**, the cut-off for general-public accessibility. Signing an ICD does not mean it was read or understood. Fixes: shorter sentences/words, fewer technicalities, subsections, graphics/drawings.
- **What IC information must include**: risks, benefits, treatment alternatives, and the possibility of technique failure, **tailored** to the patient's needs and capabilities; individual risks (age, comorbidities) must be added. A repeat of a prior procedure (e.g., a second implant) still requires a fresh IC because risks may have changed.
- **Validity requirements** (not all in statute): capacity, information, understanding, voluntariness (no coercion). Written IC is mandatory for surgical/invasive procedures; verbal suffices for routine acts (e.g., oral-cavity exam).
- **Regional variation**: e.g., Valencia Law 10/2014 requires information ≥24 h before the procedure and forbids informing a sedated/asleep patient or one already in the procedure room — clarifications absent elsewhere; national law only says "sufficiently in advance."
- **Adherence / relationship**: involvement and felt autonomy in shared decision-making raise trust, adherence, and outcomes; judicialisation and defensive medicine erode the trust relationship and slow decisions.
