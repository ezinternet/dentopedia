## Summary

This 2011 AHRQ-funded systematic review updates the 2004 evidence report on health literacy, asking whether low health literacy is associated with poorer use of care, worse outcomes, higher costs, and disparities across all age groups. It synthesizes **96 good- or fair-quality studies (111 articles)** — 98 on health literacy, 22 on numeracy, 9 on both — restricted to studies using directly measured health literacy or numeracy rather than proxies such as education level.

The review establishes a consistent association between low health literacy and a defined cluster of poorer outcomes, making it a foundational empirical justification for plain-language counseling, patient education, and communication interventions in clinical practice. A direct gap for dentistry: **no evidence was found on oral health literacy and outcomes** as of 2011.

## Key Contributions

- Consolidates the evidence linking low health literacy to poorer process and health outcomes across age groups.
- Separates numeracy as a distinct construct (concluding evidence remains limited and inconsistent).
- Documents that poor health literacy partially explains racial disparities in some outcomes.
- Flags the absence of any oral-health-literacy outcome evidence — directly relevant to dental communication and patient education.

## Methodology

- **Databases**: MEDLINE, CINAHL, PsycINFO, ERIC, Cochrane Library.
- **Dates**: health literacy 2003–22 Feb 2011; numeracy 1966–22 Feb 2011.
- **Inclusion**: studies comparing outcomes by directly measured health literacy/numeracy.
- **Quality**: two-reviewer abstraction; only good/fair-quality studies retained.
- abstract-only — full text not retrieved; study-level effect sizes not extracted here.

## Results

Low health literacy was **consistently** associated with:

- More hospitalizations
- Greater use of emergency care
- Lower mammography screening
- Lower influenza vaccination
- Poorer ability to take medications appropriately
- Poorer ability to interpret labels and health messages
- Among the elderly: poorer overall health status and higher mortality

Poor health literacy partially explained racial disparities in some outcomes. Numeracy findings were limited by few and inconsistent studies.

**Dentistry-relevant gap**: the review found no evidence on oral health literacy and outcomes, leaving the dental analogue of this evidence base unestablished as of 2011.
