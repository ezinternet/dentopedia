## Summary
Shared decision-making (SDM) is widely endorsed yet inconsistently practiced because clinicians who agree in principle hit concrete barriers. This primer, co-produced by the International Society for Shared Decision Making, describes SDM **from the clinician's vantage** — what it is and isn't, the common objections ("no time," "patients don't want it," "it's just consent"), and how patient decision aids fit the workflow. It is general-medicine guidance, but it supplies the method behind the "shared decision-making" that Ho 2025 lists as a patient-centered-care component, and it is the operational backbone for aligning expectations in option-sensitive dental decisions (implant vs. alternatives, aesthetic trade-offs). Being a purposive sample of SDM advocates, it leans pro-adoption; treat it as a how-to, not an effect estimate.

## Key Contributions
- Clinician-facing reframing of SDM that targets the principle-to-practice gap.
- Structured responses to the recurring clinician objections.
- Places patient decision aids/decision support inside the encounter.

## Methodology
- Purposive sample of 25 people from 13 countries (ISDM); a preliminary statement revised substantially via structured asynchronous collaboration.

## Results
- A clinician-oriented account of SDM and practical answers to common barriers.
- Argues SDM is feasible in routine care, supported by decision aids.
