## Summary

Patient education is a relatively young science. Historically it consisted of one-way transfer of biomedical knowledge and advice, an approach now shown to be ineffective and at times counterproductive. Care has shifted from a paternalistic "doctor knows best" stance to **patient-centred care**, in which education is tailored to the individual's needs.

The article's core message is to raise awareness of patients' **health literacy** — their knowledge, motivation, and competences to access, understand, appraise, and apply health information when making decisions about health care, disease prevention, and health promotion. Low health literacy is common and manifests as difficulty with reading, writing, numeracy, communication, and electronic technology, each of which impedes access to and comprehension of health-care information.

Because clinicians cannot reliably identify which patients have low health literacy, professional organizations recommend applying **universal health-literacy precautions to all patients**, regardless of measured literacy or education level. This converts the general instruction to "communicate clearly" into a concrete, repeatable chairside routine.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes patient education from biomedical knowledge-transfer (ineffective) to **patient-centred, tailored education**.
- Defines **health literacy** and emphasizes that low health literacy is widespread and hard to detect by inspection.
- Consolidates the **universal health-literacy precautions** into an actionable checklist applied to every patient.

## Methodology

Narrative review / masterclass synthesizing professional-organization recommendations and the health-literacy literature; no primary data, no pooled estimates. Confidence: `narrative-review`. Written in a musculoskeletal-physiotherapy context, but the precautions are generic and transfer directly to dental patient education.

## Results

**Universal health-literacy precautions — apply to every patient:**

| Precaution | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Avoid jargon** | Use plain language; replace medical terminology |
| **Chunk information** | Break it into small, concrete, actionable steps |
| **Limit scope** | **No more than three key points or tasks per visit** |
| **Confirm understanding** | Use the **teach-back** cycle — have the patient restate in their own words; re-explain and re-check if needed |
| **Reading level** | Printed material at **≤ sixth-grade** reading level |
| **Visual aids** | Use visuals to enhance comprehension |

These precautions are recommended for all patients regardless of literacy or education, because clinicians cannot reliably tell who has low health literacy.
