## One-line Summary
Cross-sectional study (n=150 children 2–14y, Sharjah; Frankl/Venham/categorical scales) — no sex difference, significant age-group differences (11–14y showed most notable crying p=.034, cooperativeness p=.002, apprehensiveness p=.003); children show heightened dental anxiety.

## Summary
This supplies measured primary data for the dental-anxiety subcategory, the empirical counterpart to the pediatric review (Cristea 2026) and the adult phobia framework (jkda-2021). Using three validated instruments (Frankl, Venham, categorical) on 150 children at end of visit, the study found **no sex difference** but **significant age-group differences**, with the 11–14y group most affected on crying, cooperativeness, and apprehensiveness. The authors' takeaway is operational: understand child behavior and use effective communication with both child and guardian, and implement behavior-management strategies. Methodologically it is a single-center, single-rater, end-of-visit snapshot — useful as a prevalence/association signal, not an intervention test.

## Key Contributions
- Three-scale measured pediatric anxiety/behavior data in one cohort.
- Age (not sex) as the salient demographic association in this sample.
- Reinforces child + guardian communication as the management lever.

## Methodology
- Cross-sectional, n=150 (2–14y), 23-week recruitment; guardian consent; exclusions (>14y, refusal, medically compromised).
- Mann–Whitney U (sex), Kruskal–Wallis (age groups); single trained rater.

## Results
- No significant sex difference across all scales.
- Significant age-group differences across nearly all scales; 11–14y most notable (crying .034, cooperativeness .002, apprehensiveness .003).
- Overall heightened anxiety at dental attendance.
