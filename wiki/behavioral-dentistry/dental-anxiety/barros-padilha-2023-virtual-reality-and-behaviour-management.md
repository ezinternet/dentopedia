## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA/PICO) of 22 RCTs (n=2,558 children, ~5–12 y) finding immersive 3D VR distraction effectively reduces dental anxiety and pain during treatment, generally surpassing conventional behaviour-management techniques; a few studies found tablets/screen distraction matched or beat VR.

## 한줄요약

소아 치과 22편 RCT(총 2,558명) 체계적 고찰 — 몰입형 3D 가상현실(VR) 주의분산이 치료 중 치과불안·통증을 유의하게 줄이며 전통적 행동조절(Tell-Show-Do)보다 대체로 우월했으나, 일부 연구에서는 태블릿/화면 주의분산이 동등하거나 더 효과적이었다.

## Summary

This PRISMA-guided systematic review (PROSPERO CRD4202340967) asks, in PICO terms, whether VR (I) manages anxiety and pain (O) during a paediatric dental consultation (P) better than alternative behavioural-control techniques (C). From 525 abstracts, 22 RCTs (n=2,558 children) met inclusion after deliberately excluding 32 trials that used 2D audiovisual glasses without true 3D immersion. The headline finding is strong, consistent evidence that **immersive 3D VR distraction reduces dental anxiety and pain during treatment**, and that **no included study found conventional non-screen techniques superior to VR**. VR is mainly deployed perioperatively (during treatment delivery) and works by diverting attention to a pleasant virtual environment, modifying pain perception rather than its pathophysiology. Important caveats temper the enthusiasm: several head-to-head trials found tablets or screen distraction equal to or better than VR (notably during inferior alveolar nerve block), heterogeneous anxiety/pain scales prevented meta-analysis, and nearly all trials used headsets in children below manufacturer minimum-age limits while only one of 22 assessed cybersickness.

## Key Contributions

- Restricts inclusion to **immersive 3D VR** (excludes 2D audiovisual glasses), isolating the effect of true immersion as a behaviour-management tool.
- Aggregates 22 RCTs (n=2,558) showing VR consistently reduces anxiety (16 studies) and pain (12 studies) versus controls, frequently surpassing Tell-Show-Do/passive distraction.
- Surfaces the **boundary conditions**: tablets/screen distraction can match or beat VR (e.g., during nerve blocks; non-painful procedures show no benefit).
- Highlights a **safety/off-label gap** — near-universal use below manufacturer age recommendations and almost no cybersickness/eye-strain monitoring.

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA systematic review (no meta-analysis); PICO framework; PROSPERO CRD4202340967.
- **Search**: PubMed/Medline, SCOPUS, Web of Science, January 2023; Parsifal for de-duplication.
- **Selection**: 525 → 446 (post-dedup) → 54 full-text → 22 included; 32 excluded for non-immersive 2D glasses. Two independent reviewers, third adjudicator; Cohen's Kappa = 1.0.
- **Risk of bias**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (adapted for RCTs) — 2 very low, 13 moderate, 7 high.
- **Comparators**: passive distraction, Tell-Show-Do, digital screen/audiovisual, tablet, smartphone games, audio.
- **Outcomes**: anxiety/pain via CPMAS, MCDAS, MCDAS(f)-r, FIS, VPT (anxiety) and WBFS, VAS, FLACC, MBPS (pain); plus salivary cortisol, pulse oximetry, pulse rate.

## Results

- **All 22 included studies were RCTs**; total n = 2,558; mostly ages 5–12 y; conducted across 9 countries.
- VR's benefit on anxiety and pain was statistically significant vs control in most studies; physiological gains included reduced salivary cortisol (p<0.001) and shorter treatment time.
- **VR devices**: Oculus Go (2, 9%), VR Box (4, 18%), HTC (2); 4–5 studies left device unspecified.
- **Exceptions / null results**:
  - Felemban et al. — VR ≈ screen distraction (heart rate, pain) during buccal infiltration.
  - Al-Halabi — tablets > VR for anxiety/pain during inferior alveolar nerve block.
  - Alshatrat et al. — no significant pain reduction in non-painful procedures.
  - Zaidman et al. — VR reduced pain at rubber-dam placement but limited benefit during local anaesthesia.
- **Positive signals**: high satisfaction with 3D video glasses (Nuvvula), increased fun (Atzori), less anxiety in MIH-affected children using 3D VR games (Özükoç, p<0.05).
- **Quality/limitations**: heterogeneous scales blocked meta-analysis; mixed developmental stages; off-label pediatric use near-universal; cybersickness assessed in only 1 study.
