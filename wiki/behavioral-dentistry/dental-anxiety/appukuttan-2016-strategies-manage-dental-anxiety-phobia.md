## One-line Summary

Adult-focused narrative literature review giving a stepwise dental-anxiety framework: identify (semistructured interview + validated scales CDAS/MDAS/DFS + objective physiologic measures) → manage psychotherapeutically (behavioral/cognitive, with CBT the most-supported) → escalate to pharmacology (sedation/GA) per anxiety severity.

## 한줄요약

성인 중심 서술형 종설: 치과불안을 면담·검증된 자가보고 척도(CDAS/MDAS/DFS)·객관적 생리지표로 식별하고, 불안 정도에 맞춰 심리치료(행동/인지, CBT가 가장 근거 우세)에서 약물(진정/전신마취)로 단계적으로 올라가는 의사결정 틀을 제시한 종설.

## Summary

Appukuttan's review organizes dental anxiety/phobia management into a single clinical decision framework built on two stages: **identification** then **treatment matched to severity**. Identification combines a calm semistructured interview (reason for visit, prior experience, specific fears, expectations — and referral to a psychologist/psychiatrist when anxiety is part of a wider disorder) with **subjective self-report scales** and **objective physiologic measures**. The output is a categorization — mildly, moderately, or extremely anxious, or dental-phobic — that drives treatment selection.

Treatment is broadly **psychotherapeutic, pharmacological, or combined**, chosen by anxiety level, patient characteristics, clinical situation, and the dentist's training. Psychotherapy is either behaviorally or cognitively oriented; **cognitive behavior therapy (CBT), the combination of the two, is presented as the currently most accepted and successful psychological treatment** for situational anxiety and specific phobia. Pharmacology (sedation tiers up to general anesthesia) is reserved for patients who cannot or will not cooperate with psychotherapy, who are phobic, or who have special needs. A recurring theme: psychological interventions act slower (multiple sessions) but produce **durable** anxiety reduction with low dropout and more return for future care, whereas pharmacological control works **short-term only** and is rated less acceptable by patients.

## Key Contributions

- A clinically usable **assessment-to-management ladder** for the general dentist, spanning interview → scales → physiologic monitoring → severity-matched therapy.
- Explicit naming of **CBT as the most-supported psychological treatment**, with the full behavioral/cognitive toolbox arranged around it.
- A **pharmacological escalation map** (minimal/anxiolysis → moderate/conscious → deep sedation → GA) gated by the IOS sedation-need tool and ASA status.
- Catalog of **technological adjuncts** (computer-controlled LA delivery, electronic dental anesthesia, computer-assisted relaxation learning; the "4 S" sensory-trigger rule for restorative care).

## Methodology

Non-systematic **narrative review** — no search strategy, inclusion criteria, or formal quality appraisal. It synthesizes prior scales, RCTs, and SR/MAs into a clinically organized overview, so its evidence weight is that of a narrative review while individual cited findings retain their own study designs.

## Results

### The assessment ladder

**Subjective — semistructured interview + validated scales** (clinician judgment alone is unreliable because patients mask anxiety; administering a scale both discloses and reduces anxiety):

| Scale | Items | Score range | High-anxiety cutoff | Note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CDAS** (Corah) | 4 | 4–20 | >15 (possible phobia) | No LA-injection item |
| **MDAS** (Modified) | 5 | 5–25 | ≥19 | Administration does not raise anxiety |
| **DFS** (Dental Fear Survey) | 20 | 20–100 | ≥60 | 3 dimensions: avoidance, somatic, fear of stimuli |

Single-item options (Dental Anxiety Question, VAS) exist; none is a gold standard. Only ~20% of UK dentists report using scales.

**Objective — physiologic measures**: blood pressure, pulse rate, pulse oximetry, finger temperature, and **galvanic skin response** (validated; reads epidermal-sweat skin conductance that rises with anxiety).

**Output categorization** → mildly / moderately / extremely anxious / dental-phobic, which sets the treatment tier.

### The psychotherapeutic → pharmacological decision logic

- **Mild–moderate anxiety** → psychological interventions first; occasionally an anxiolytic or minimal/conscious sedation.
- **Extreme / phobic** → most often a **combined** approach (CBT + pharmacology).
- **Pharmacology is reserved** for patients who cannot/will not cooperate with psychotherapy, who are dental-phobic, or who have special needs.

**Psychotherapeutic toolbox**
- *Behaviorally oriented*: relaxation (Jacobson progressive muscular relaxation — tense 5–7 s / relax 20 s over 4 muscle groups; diaphragmatic breathing; autogenic; Öst applied relaxation; Benson relaxation response), guided imagery, biofeedback (EMG/EEG/respiratory-rate), hypnosis, acupuncture/acupressure, distraction (music, video glasses), **enhancing control** (tell-show-do, modeling, stop-signal raised-hand), **systematic desensitization** (Wolpe: fear hierarchy + relaxation + graded exposure; imaginal variant; flooding only with caution), positive reinforcement.
- *Cognitively oriented*: restructure negative cognitions via encouragement, altering expectations, thought-stopping, attentional focus.
- **CBT** = behavior + cognitive therapy combined — the most accepted/successful psychological treatment. Components: psychoeducation, graded exposure, cognitive restructuring, behavioral experiments, relaxation, self-assertiveness training. Requires dedicated dentist training.

**Pharmacological tiers** (candidates must be ASA I–II for in-office sedation):

| Tier | State | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Minimal sedation / anxiolysis | Responds normally to verbal commands | Mild–moderate anxiety; oral sedative ± N₂O/O₂ |
| Moderate / conscious sedation | Purposeful response to verbal command | Moderate–severe anxiety |
| Deep sedation | Not easily aroused; airway may need support | Selected severe cases |
| General anesthesia | Not arousable | Dental-phobic, severe learning/psychiatric/physical-disability, special care |

The **IOS (Indicator of Sedation Need)** tool scores three domains (anxiety; medical/behavioral; treatment complexity), each 1–4, summed 3–12: 3–4 minimal, 5–6 moderate, 7–9 high, 10–12 very high / GA. Conscious-sedation routes: inhalational (N₂O, typically 30–40%, ≤70% ceiling), oral (benzodiazepines — diazepam/midazolam/triazolam), IV (rapid onset, titratable; benzodiazepine ± opioid; advanced training required).

### Environment, communication, and adjuncts

Office ambience (soft music, no bright lights, slightly cooler room, short waits), iatrosedative two-way communication (eye contact, honest answers, no false reassurance), sensory-adapted "Snoezelen" environment, and aromatherapy (lavender lowers salivary/serum cortisol, GSR, systolic BP; better for moderate than severe anxiety). LA-specific adjuncts: computer-controlled local anesthetic delivery, electronic dental anesthesia (TENS/gate-control, variable efficacy), computer-assisted relaxation learning. Restorative anxiety is reduced via the **"4 S" rule** (eliminate Sight, Sound, Sensation, Smell triggers) plus ART, air abrasion, chemomechanical caries removal, and lasers.

### Psychological vs pharmacological (head-to-head)

Both reduce dental anxiety. Psychological response is **not immediate** (needs several sessions) but is **durable**, with low dropout and more patients returning for future care. Pharmacology is effective **short-term only** and is perceived as less acceptable by patients — hence psychotherapy first, pharmacology as escalation or adjunct.
