## One-line Summary

Randomized controlled crossover trial (n=73 adults, each patient own control across two implant-surgery sessions) showing VR distraction with a high-resolution headset and natural-scene content significantly reduced experienced pain (−1.71 NRS), state anxiety (STAI-S −3.45), and dental anxiety (MDAS −4.55) versus no VR — confirmed by physiology (lower EMG, heart rate, GSR) — with 90.4% of patients wanting to reuse VR; the main downside was increased surgeon distress from the bulky headset.

## Summary

This is the first randomized controlled trial of intraoperative **virtual reality (VR) distraction in adult dental implant surgery**, a procedure the authors single out as one of the most anxiety-provoking in dentistry. Using a **within-subject crossover design**, 73 patients each underwent two first-molar implant placements — one with an Oculus Quest 2 headset showing natural scenery, one without — so each patient served as their own control, removing between-subject differences in pain threshold and baseline anxiety. Outcomes were measured both by validated questionnaires (STAI, MDAS, NRS) and by objective psychophysiology (EMG, EKG/HRV/heart rate, skin conductance), giving the subjective findings independent corroboration.

VR significantly lowered **experienced** pain, state anxiety, and dental anxiety, raised satisfaction, and reduced both memory vividness and perceived treatment time. It did **not** affect *expected* (pre-operative) pain or *trait* anxiety — consistent with VR acting on the in-the-moment, situational experience rather than on dispositional traits or pre-formed expectations. The headache for the clinician: **surgeon distress was higher** with VR because the bulky headset obstructed access and visibility, though this eased as the operator habituated. Within this wiki it reinforces the physiological-monitoring RCT logic of [[behavioral-dentistry/dental-anxiety/pandey-2025-monitoring-patient-anxiety-pain-physiological-rct]] and brings adult evidence into an otherwise pediatric-heavy folder.

## Key Contributions

- First RCT of VR distraction in **adult implant surgery** specifically (vs prior routine-dental or pediatric work).
- **Crossover within-subject design** eliminates pain-threshold/anxiety confounding between arms.
- **Dual validation**: self-report (STAI/MDAS/NRS) confirmed by objective EMG/EKG/SCR physiology.
- Confirms two cognitive effects: VR reduces **memory vividness** (Elaborated Intrusions theory) and **time perception**.
- Surfaces the practical **clinician-side cost**: bulky headset raises surgeon distress and impedes surgical access.

## Methodology

- **Design**: randomized, controlled, crossover RCT; GEE modeling intervention type, gender, age; intention-to-treat; SPSS v18; α=0.05. Registered IRCT20230612058461N1.
- **Sample**: 215 screened → 90 eligible → 73 analyzed (36 M / 37 F; mean age 44.29 ± 12.98 y). Powered to ≥70 (α 0.05, 90%).
- **Intervention**: Oculus Quest 2 (3664×1920 px, 100° FOV, 90 Hz) with natural-scene content and external speakers (patient still hears surgeon); control = same surgery, no headset.
- **Standardization**: fixed 25-min operative time per session, identical implants (WhiteSky/Bredent) at sites 36/46, inferior alveolar nerve block, identical antibiotic/analgesic regimen, solitary silent waiting.
- **Outcomes**: expected & experienced pain (NRS), STAI-S/STAI-T, MDAS, satisfaction, surgeon Treatment Distress Rating, memory vividness (phone at 1 wk), time-perception ratio, EMG (SCM), EKG (HR/HRV), SCR/GSR.

## Results

| Outcome | VR vs no-VR | p |
|---|---|---|
| Experienced pain (NRS) | −1.712 | <0.001 |
| Expected pain (NRS) | NS | 0.526 |
| State anxiety (STAI-S) | −3.452 | <0.001 |
| Trait anxiety (STAI-T) | NS | 0.382 |
| Dental anxiety (MDAS) | −4.548 | <0.001 |
| Patient satisfaction | +1.137 | <0.001 |
| Memory vividness | −0.689 | <0.001 |
| Time perception | −0.128 | <0.001 |
| **Surgeon distress** | **+3.452 (worse)** | <0.001 |
| EMG RMS amplitude | −14.158 | <0.001 |
| Heart rate | −10.300 | <0.001 |
| GSR RMS | −5.389 | <0.001 |

- **90.4%** of patients wanted to reuse VR at future visits.
- Gender did not significantly affect anxiety/pain; age affected only satisfaction (younger patients more satisfied, −0.037/year).
- Higher-baseline-anxiety patients showed the **largest** drop in memory vividness.
- VR helped most when anxiety stemmed from a discrete trigger (injection, room, the word "surgery") and less when the fear was of being vulnerable/unaware during surgery.
- HRV decreased (opposite to the predicted direction), the one physiological marker discordant with the others.
- Main limitation: bulky headset impedes the surgeon; calls for smaller, higher-quality devices.
