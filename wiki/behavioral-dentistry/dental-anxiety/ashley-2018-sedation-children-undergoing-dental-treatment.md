## One-line Summary

Cochrane SR+MA (2nd update; 50 RCTs, 3704 children ≤16y): moderate-certainty evidence that oral midazolam 0.25–1 mg/kg improves cooperative behaviour vs placebo (SMD 1.96, 95% CI 1.59–2.33; 6 studies, 202 participants, I²=90%); the other 33 sedatives are too sparse/heterogeneous to pool, and 81% of trials are at high risk of bias.

## Summary

This is the second update (search to 22 February 2018) of the Cochrane review on conscious sedation for behaviour management in paediatric dentistry, originally published 2005. Children's dental fear can manifest as uncooperative behaviour, a barrier to treatment; when behavioural techniques are insufficient, conscious sedation is used to relieve anxiety and enable care. The review asks which agents, doses and regimens are effective.

Across 50 RCTs (3704 children up to 16 years), 34 different sedatives were used — frequently combined with inhalational nitrous oxide — with widely varying doses, routes and timing. This heterogeneity, plus a high risk of bias in 81% of trials, meant meta-analysis was feasible only for **oral midazolam versus placebo**. That comparison yielded moderate-certainty evidence of benefit. No reliable conclusions could be drawn for any other sedative or for secondary outcomes (e.g. completion of treatment, physiological/adverse effects) because of inconsistent or inadequate reporting. Where adverse effects were reported, they were few and minor.

Bottom line: oral midazolam is the only conscious-sedation agent with poolable, moderate-certainty evidence of efficacy for paediatric dental behaviour management; the rest of the evidence base remains fragmented and at high risk of bias, and the authors recommend future agents be tested against oral midazolam or inhaled nitrous oxide.

## Key Contributions

- Highest-evidence (Cochrane SR+MA) appraisal of conscious-sedation efficacy in paediatric dentistry, updating the 2005 and 2012 versions.
- Identifies oral midazolam as the only agent supported by a quantitative, moderate-certainty estimate of benefit over placebo.
- Exposes the field's fragmentation (34 agents, 50 trials, mostly high risk of bias) and sets oral midazolam / inhaled nitrous oxide as the comparators future trials should beat.

## Methodology

- Design: systematic review with meta-analysis (Cochrane Oral Health), GRADE certainty assessment.
- Inclusion: RCTs of conscious sedation comparing ≥2 drugs/techniques/placebo, delivered by the dentist or dental team, in children up to 16 years; cross-over trials excluded.
- Searches: Cochrane Oral Health Trials Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE Ovid, Embase Ovid (to 22 Feb 2018), plus ClinicalTrials.gov and WHO ICTRP; no language/date limits.
- Two authors independently extracted data in duplicate and assessed risk of bias; trial authors were contacted when reports were unclear. Studies grouped as placebo-controlled, dosage, and head-to-head comparisons.

## Results

- 50 studies, 3704 participants. Risk of bias: 40 (81%) high, 9 (18%) unclear, 1 low.
- 34 different sedatives, often with inhalational nitrous oxide; dosing/route/timing varied widely.
- **Oral midazolam vs placebo (primary outcome, cooperative behaviour): SMD 1.96 (95% CI 1.59 to 2.33), P < 0.0001, I² = 90%; 6 studies; 202 participants** — moderate-certainty evidence favouring midazolam at 0.25–1 mg/kg.
- All other agents and all secondary outcomes: no conclusions possible (inconsistent/inadequate reporting, extreme heterogeneity).
- Adverse effects: few and minor where reported.
- Caveat: the pooled estimate rests on 6 small, clinically heterogeneous, high/unclear-risk-of-bias studies — hence moderate, not high, certainty despite the large effect size.
