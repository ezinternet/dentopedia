## One-line Summary
Systematic review (BMC Oral Health, 17 RCTs, n=1788) of conscious sedation for managing dental anxiety during third molar extraction: heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis so a narrative synthesis was done — 6 studies showed some anxiety improvement (notably midazolam and fentanyl/methohexital combinations), but the evidence is overall inconclusive and conflicting because outcome measures are not standardized.

## Summary
This systematic review asks whether pharmacological conscious sedation actually helps manage **dental anxiety** in the reproducible surgical model of third molar extraction. Following PRISMA, it pooled only RCTs and screened by patient-reported anxiety or stress-hormone outcomes (observer-rated anxiety excluded). Seventeen RCTs (1788 patients; 7 crossover, 10 parallel) covering eleven-plus sedative agents qualified. The agents, anxiety scales (VAS, Corah DAS, STAI, ISAR), and assessment timing were so heterogeneous that a meaningful meta-analysis was impossible, so the authors performed a **narrative synthesis**.

Six studies reported some statistically significant anxiety advantage for sedation (e.g. triazolam or midazolam vs. placebo/no-intervention, dose-dependent midazolam, propofol vs. midazolam, and fentanyl+midazolam±methohexital combinations giving the lowest anxiety), and two stress-hormone studies showed lower cortisol with midazolam and altered perioperative renin with remifentanil. But nine studies were null, underpowered, or had crossover analyses that did not remove the period effect. The clinical bottom line is **inconclusive and conflicting**: pharmacological conscious sedation did not consistently demonstrate an anxiety-management benefit. The authors attribute this not to a true absence of effect but to the field's **lack of standardized, validated outcome measures and assessment timing** — and call for a standardized trial design (validated patient-reported + objective hormone/ANI outcomes, placebo arms, pre-anesthetic assessment timing). They also note behavioral/CBT approaches may give more durable fear reduction and that opioid anxiolysis is weakly supported.

## Key Contributions
- First SR dedicated to conscious sedation for dental anxiety in the standardized third-molar-extraction model.
- Synthesizes 17 RCTs / 1788 patients across 11+ agents and multiple anxiety instruments.
- Pinpoints the **non-standardized-outcome gap** as the reason the evidence cannot support firm conclusions.
- Lays out a concrete standardization agenda for future anxiety-sedation trials.

## Methodology
- SR of parallel + crossover RCTs; PRISMA-reported (protocol not registered).
- Searched MEDLINE/PubMed, EMBASE, SCOPUS, Cochrane CENTRAL (1978–Mar 2019, no language limit) + reference hand-search.
- Population: adults ≥16y, third molar extraction. Intervention: any conscious sedation (IV/inhalation/oral/combination) vs. any sedation or none.
- Outcomes restricted to patient-reported anxiety or stress-hormone alteration; observer-rated anxiety excluded.
- Risk of bias via Cochrane Handbook (7 RCT domains + 5 crossover-specific domains).
- Narrative synthesis only — heterogeneity in agents, scales, and timing precluded meta-analysis.

## Results
- **Included**: 17 RCTs (7 crossover, 10 parallel), n=1788 (range 12–997), from a yield of 125 records → 50 full-text.
- **Agents**: midazolam, dexmedetomidine, nitrous oxide, clonidine, remifentanil, zaleplon, triazolam, chlordemethyldiazepam, propofol, fentanyl, methohexital.
- **Patient-reported anxiety** (14 studies): 6 with some significant between-arm difference favoring sedation (incl. midazolam dose-response and fentanyl+midazolam±methohexital lowest); midazolam>diazepam by VAS but not STAI; 9 studies null/underpowered/unclear (e.g. midazolam vs. clonidine, remifentanil vs. placebo — no difference).
- **Stress hormones** (3 studies): lower cortisol with midazolam vs. placebo; significant perioperative renin variation with remifentanil vs. placebo (ACTH/aldosterone unchanged); midazolam vs. NO/O2 unclear.
- **Risk of bias**: allocation concealment unclear in all 17; detection bias unclear in 13; 4 high attrition risk; 4 high "other bias" from incorrect crossover analysis.
- **Conclusion**: inconclusive and conflicting; no consistent anxiety benefit demonstrable given the lack of standardized outcomes.
