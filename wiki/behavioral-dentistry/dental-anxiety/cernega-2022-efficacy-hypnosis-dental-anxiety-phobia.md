## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 19 adult clinical trials (1979–2021; meta-analysis restricted to 5 STAI studies): hypnosis shows promising but highly heterogeneous, sometimes null/slightly-negative effects on dental anxiety, while CBT carried the most consistent efficacy evidence.

## Summary

This PRISMA-guided systematic review with meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42020172052) evaluated hypnosis and other non-pharmacological interventions for reducing dental anxiety and phobia in adults. From 257 records, 19 clinical trials (1979–2021) were included after screening. Across the narrative review, hypnosis — most often delivered as standardized audiotape self-hypnosis — showed a generally positive effect on dental fear reduction, supported by fMRI evidence of reduced amygdala/ACC/insula/hippocampus activation in phobic subjects. However, the quantitative meta-analysis could pool only 5 studies (all using the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory, STAI) because of severe measurement heterogeneity across the 15 different anxiety-measurement methods used. The pooled result was inconclusive: effect sizes ranged from −4.30 to 6.20 with high heterogeneity, and some studies showed null or slightly negative effects. Notably, **CBT delivered in varied formats (including one-session treatment) showed the strongest, most consistent efficacy evidence** of all reviewed techniques. The authors conclude hypnosis is a promising adjunct but that methodological standardization is needed before its efficacy can be reliably quantified.

## Key Contributions

- One of the few SR+MAs to attempt quantitative pooling of hypnosis for **adult** dental anxiety — adds the adult-intervention dimension to a pediatric-heavy literature.
- Identifies **CBT, not hypnosis, as the technique with the strongest efficacy signal**.
- Demonstrates that scale heterogeneity (15 measurement methods; DAS most used at 12 studies, VAS 9, STAI 7) is the binding constraint on synthesizing this literature.
- Positions **audiotape self-hypnosis** as the most practical, GDP-deployable option, while noting live individualized hypnosis is more effective.
- Cites neurophysiological (fMRI) plausibility for a hypnotic anxiolytic mechanism.

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA SR+MA; protocol PROSPERO CRD42020172052 (registered 14 Jul 2020).
- **Eligibility**: RCTs, cross-sectional, comparative, validation, evaluation studies; English, 1979–2021; hypnosis for dental anxiety/phobia, any age.
- **Search**: five databases + grey literature; MeSH/keywords (dental anxiety/fear/phobia × hypnosis/hypnotic/hypnotherapy/sedation).
- **Flow**: 257 → 223 (de-dup) → 35 full-text → 25 → **19 included** (case reports, questionnaire-only, and non-comparable-scale studies removed).
- **Meta-analysis**: ProMeta 3; OR + mean difference; random-effects, 95% CI; restricted to the **5 STAI studies** owing to scale heterogeneity.
- **Risk of bias**: NHLBI/RTI quality tools — 15 good, 4 fair, 0 poor.

## Results

- **Hypnosis techniques**: audiotape self-hypnosis most common (10 studies used audio; 7 significant, 1 marginal), plus progressive muscle relaxation, CBT, Chiasson's optical technique, hypnotherapy, nitrous oxide sedation, benzodiazepine premedication.
- **Meta-analysis (5 STAI studies)**: high heterogeneity, effect-size range −4.30 to 6.20 → **no reliable pooled effect**.
- **CBT**: most consistent efficacy evidence, including one-session formats; one included study named it treatment of choice for dental phobia.
- **Physiological**: one study showed reduced diastolic BP and heart rate intraoperatively with hypnotherapy (audio pillow + relaxation music) vs increases in non-hypnosis controls.
- **Neuroimaging**: reduced left amygdala, bilateral ACC, insula, hippocampus activation under hypnosis in phobic subjects (Halsband & Wolf fMRI).
- **Limitations**: no double-blinding possible (subjective self-report outcomes), suggestibility confound, two pediatric studies in an adult-focused review, same-patient test/control carryover in some designs.
