## Summary
Khalifah and Celenza systematically reviewed 50 articles (2000–2016) on teaching and assessing dentist-patient communication skills. The paper's most useful artifact is the **26-skill, 4-category taxonomy** of dental communication: generic (cross-scenario), case-specific (presentation-specific), time-specific (encounter-phase), and emerging (shared decision-making, MI, technology-mediated). For curriculum or in-house training design, this gives a defensible structure rather than starting from scratch.

The honest finding on **methodology**: teaching uses a mix of passive (lectures) and active (role-play, OSCE) approaches, and assessment instruments are all valid/reliable — but the underlying study designs are weak (single-group pre/post, cross-sectional), so there's no robust comparison of which method is most effective. The field knows how to measure communication skill acquisition but hasn't generated good comparative effectiveness evidence.

For dental clinics adding communication training (not just dental schools), this paper tells you:
- Use a 4-category framework, not ad-hoc topics
- Mix teaching modalities — passive alone is insufficient
- Don't expect the literature to tell you which exact training program is best — it doesn't

## Key Contributions
- 26-skill, 4-category taxonomy of dentist-patient communication
- Comprehensive review of teaching modalities used in dental schools
- All assessment instruments found valid and reliable
- Identifies the "emerging skills" bucket as the growth area — shared decision-making, MI, technology-mediated
- Calls for more robust study designs in communication research

## Methodology
Seven-database OVID search (MEDLINE, EBM-Cochrane, Embase, ERIC, BIOSIS, CAB Abstracts, PsycINFO) plus manual journal search. 2000–2016 inclusion. Thematic analysis and categorization. Quality review. Narrative synthesis (no MA).

## Results
- **50 articles** included
- Predominant design: comparative, with strong showings of single-group pre/post and cross-sectional
- **26 skills** identified, grouped into:
  - **Generic skills** — active listening, empathy, rapport
  - **Case-specific skills** — context-dependent (anxious patient, breaking bad news, pediatric)
  - **Time-specific skills** — opening, information gathering, closing
  - **Emerging skills** — shared decision-making, MI, e-health/tele-dentistry communication
- Teaching: combination of passive (didactic) and active (role-play, simulated patients, video review, OSCE prep)
- Assessment: all measures (OSCE, simulated patient encounters, video review, self-report) found valid and reliable
- Gap: no comparative effectiveness evidence on teaching method superiority

## Why It Matters for Clinic Operations
For a clinic running internal CE/communication training:
- Adopt the 4-category framework as the curriculum spine — covers known territory without missing major skill types
- "Emerging skills" bucket maps to current operational needs (shared decision-making for implant treatment plans, MI for periodontal compliance, tele-dentistry communication post-COVID)
- Mix teaching modalities: passive (a single lecture) is insufficient on its own — pair with role-play and feedback
- For assessing staff communication: any of the validated instruments (OSCE-style simulated patient with rubric, video review) is defensible; pick by feasibility
- Don't expect "evidence-based best teaching method" — the field doesn't have it, so locally pilot and measure

## Open Questions
- Which teaching method is most effective per dollar/hour invested? Unknown — the literature lacks the comparative trials.
- Does communication skill training translate to **patient outcomes** (not just learner OSCE scores)? Unstudied at the synthesis level.
- Korean dental school curricula — degree of alignment with the 26-skill taxonomy is unmapped.
- Hygienist/DCP communication training is a separate question — this SR is dentist-focused.
- Search ended 2016 — post-COVID tele-dentistry communication skills are not captured; needs update.
