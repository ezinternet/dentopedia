## Summary

Contrepois and colleagues systematically reviewed marginal-adaptation literature for ceramic crowns (469 → 54 studies, 17 ceramic systems, 1994-2012). The vast majority (94.9%) of measured marginal gaps were clinically acceptable, but heterogeneous measurement methods made meta-analysis and head-to-head system ranking impossible. Four parameters were identified as influencing marginal adaptation: finish-line configuration, the predefined cement space, the veneering process, and cementation. Micro-CT is recommended for future standardized, non-destructive measurement.

## Key Contributions

- **94.9% of marginal gaps within clinical acceptability** across 17 ceramic systems.
- **Four influential factors**: finish-line configuration, predefined cement space, veneering process, cementation.
- Ceramic systems **cannot be ranked** for marginal fit on current evidence (heterogeneity).
- Recommends **micro-CT** for non-destructive 3D marginal-gap research.
- Refines Goodacre 2001: in the ceramic/CAD-CAM era, **finish-line configuration does influence marginal adaptation** (one of the four factors), qualifying the earlier "no effect" conclusion.

## Clinical Implications [근거중간]

- Ceramic crown systems, in general, deliver clinically acceptable marginal fit — system choice should be driven by esthetics/strength rather than an assumed marginal-fit advantage (no system can be ranked superior on this evidence).
- The **predefined cement space** (CAD die-spacer setting) and **cementation technique** are operator-controllable levers on the final marginal gap — worth optimizing in the digital workflow.
- **Veneering** can distort coping margins; monolithic designs avoid this veneer-firing distortion pathway.
- Largely in-vitro evidence: expect intraoral, aged, loaded marginal gaps to be somewhat larger than reported laboratory values.
