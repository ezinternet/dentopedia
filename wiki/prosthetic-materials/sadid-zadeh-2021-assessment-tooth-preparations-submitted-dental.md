## Summary

Sadid-Zadeh et al. audited 392 STL files of posterior tooth preparations submitted to three Buffalo, NY CAD-CAM laboratories for monolithic zirconia crowns. Using two calibrated evaluators and 3Shape 3D Viewer, they scored finish-line design/width/quality, occluso-cervical dimension, total occlusal convergence (TOC), line-angle form, intercuspal angulation, undercut, and unsupported enamel. The finding: real-world clinician preparations broadly miss the ideal geometry — most exceed the recommended TOC, and the majority carry at least one disqualifying defect. The paper makes a quality-control argument: because milling cannot compensate for poor preparation geometry, defects should be screened at lab intake.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies adherence to preparation principles specifically for monolithic zirconia crowns, where prior literature focused on lithium disilicate / generic zirconia.
- Provides location-stratified data on occluso-cervical dimension shortfalls (premolar vs first vs second molar).
- Reinforces the recurring message that preparation guidelines learned in school are not maintained in practice.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional descriptive audit. 392 STL files (intraoral or desktop scanned) collected over one month (May–June 2017) from 3 of 19 contacted labs. Posterior monolithic zirconia preparations only; anterior, layered zirconia, and non-zirconia excluded. STL files sectioned to measure TOC and occluso-cervical dimension by location; finish-line width averaged across 8 points; finish-line quality, undercut, and unsupported enamel scored visually. Descriptive statistics only.

## Results

- Occluso-cervical dimension below threshold (<3 mm premolars, <4 mm molars): 39% premolars, 77% first molars, 91% second molars.
- Most preparations had TOC >20° vs the recommended 10–20°.
- >50% of preparations had undercut, unsupported enamel, and/or unacceptable finish-line quality.
