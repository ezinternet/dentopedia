## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 10 in-vitro cyclic-loading studies: abutment-screw preload loss after dynamic fatigue ranges 16.1%–39%, and the internal connection — especially Morse taper — best resists screw loosening in single-tooth implant restorations.

## 한줄요약

10편의 in-vitro 반복하중 연구를 묶은 체계적 문헌고찰. 동적 피로 후 지대주 나사의 전하중 손실은 16.1%~39%였고, 단일치 임플란트 보철에서는 내부연결(internal connection), 특히 모스테이퍼(Morse taper)가 나사 풀림에 가장 잘 견딘다.

## Summary

Pardal-Peláez & Montero (2017) systematically reviewed in-vitro studies that subjected single-unit internal- and external-connection implants to cyclic (dynamic fatigue) loading and measured abutment-screw preload loss via post-load removal (counter) torque. From 545 screened papers, 10 met inclusion criteria. Across highly heterogeneous test conditions, post-loading torque loss ranged 16.1%–39%, and the consensus conclusion was that the internal connection together with the Morse taper best resists cyclic fatigue in terms of screw loosening. The authors stress that screw loosening is multifactorial — connection type, screw design/material, abutment type, the settling effect, and occlusal loads all contribute — and that methodological heterogeneity precluded a meta-analysis.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies the range of post-fatigue abutment-screw torque loss across the literature: **16.1%–39%**.
- Establishes that **internal connection + Morse taper** is the geometry most resistant to cyclic-loading-induced screw loosening in single-tooth implants.
- Frames screw stability as a **five-factor problem**: connection type, screw design/material, abutment type, settling effect, internal (occlusal) loads.
- Documents the **settling effect** (2%–10% preload loss without load) and that **retightening ~10 min after tightening** recovers preload (Siamos: −17%–19% torque loss).

## Methodology

- Systematic review (no meta-analysis) per PRISMA; PubMed + EMBASE + Cochrane Library; English-language, 1995–2016.
- **Inclusion**: studies comparing internal vs external connection implants under cyclic loads, abutment-screw loosening measured by counter-torque.
- **Exclusion**: connection type unspecified, not comparing internal vs external, no cyclic loading, multi-unit prostheses, bacterial-filtration-only, finite-element analyses.
- Screening: 545 → 70 abstracts (post-dedup) → 35 full-text → **10 included**.
- Test-bed heterogeneity: embedding substrate mostly acrylic resin (E ≈ 17 GPa, near bone), some aluminium (70 GPa), stainless steel (190–210 GPa), or unspecified metal; some used thermocyclers; all but one (Park) used tightening/loosening sequencing before loading.

## Results

- **Number of studies**: 10 in-vitro cyclic-loading studies.
- **Cycles**: 16,667 to 1,000,000 (one ISO 14801 study used 5 million cycles).
- **Loads**: 0–400 N.
- **Tightening (insertion) torques**: 20–45 Ncm.
- **Post-loading preload (torque) loss**: **16.1%–39%** overall (baseline 16.1%–25%; Jorge 19.7%–39.0%; Morse internal connection 32.88% vs external + UCLA abutment 39%).
- **Best geometry**: **internal connection + Morse taper** best resisted screw loosening — in external connections axial loads act directly on the screw, whereas internal/Morse connections transfer forces deeper, reducing micromovement and improving stress dissipation. Suggested mapping: internal connection for single prostheses, external connection for multiple prostheses.
- **Screw factors**: coatings (TorqTite, Gold-Tite) helped per some authors, but titanium-alloy screws outperformed Gold-Tite in Tsuge & Hagiwara; Piermatti found screw design (long rod + machined journal) the most important stability factor; Coppedê found conical-head screws loosen less than flat-head; larger screw-joint diameter favors torque maintenance.
- **Settling/retightening**: all screws lose 2%–10% preload from settling even unloaded; retightening recovers it.
- **Loading effect**: in all studies except Tsuge & Hagiwara, screws were looser after cyclic loading than at baseline.
- **Caveat**: lack of standardized cyclic-loading methodology (ISO 14801 does not mandate a standard torque) introduces confounding bias; screw loosening is multifactorial.
