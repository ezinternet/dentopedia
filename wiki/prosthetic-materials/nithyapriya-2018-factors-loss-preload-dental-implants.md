## One-line Summary

Narrative/literature-search review (MEDLINE-PubMed, 10-year window; 84 articles screened → 36 analyzed, no quality appraisal) cataloguing the factors that cause loss of preload in implant abutment screws — material (gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti), connection type (internal > external hex), calibrated torque, retorque after 10 min, lubrication, settling effect, and abutment collar length.

## Summary

Screw loosening is the most common implant complication, and its principal upstream cause is **loss of preload** — the decay of the axial clamping force that the tightened abutment screw exerts to hold the abutment against the implant. This review searches MEDLINE-PubMed over a 10-year window (84 articles screened, 36 analyzed) to assemble the full list of factors that drive preload loss. The central mechanism is the **settling effect (embedment relaxation)**: microroughness on the two mating surfaces collapses under initial torquing, so clamping force bleeds off and the screw loosens. The review's bottom line is a set of clinical levers that preserve preload — gold (vs Ti) screws, internal (vs external hex) connections, lubrication, a calibrated torque device, and a retorque 10 min after initial tightening. Note the paper frames itself as a "systematic review" and uses a PRISMA search flow, but performs **no risk-of-bias appraisal and no meta-analysis**, so it is best read as a factors catalogue (`narrative-review`).

## Key Contributions

- Consolidates the determinants of preload loss into one map, so individual connection/torque studies can be located against a shared mechanism.
- Foregrounds the **settling effect / embedment relaxation** as the unifying cause linking surface condition, material, lubrication, and retorque.
- Distills six actionable clinical rules for maximizing preload (material, calibrated torque, retorque timing, contact area, connection type, lubrication).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Literature search following PRISMA *search/flow* conventions; **no quality assessment performed** ("the quality of the various studies was not considered… no quality assessment has been done").
- **Database**: MEDLINE-PubMed, searched September 2016 back over the preceding 10 years; supplemented by hand-search and cross-references.
- **Search string**: MeSH "abutment screw," "preload," "dental implants" with Boolean AND/OR — e.g. `([dental implants] AND abutment screw) AND preload`.
- **Inclusion**: loss of preload, screw loosening/fracture, screw geometry, connection type (external hex platform, Morse taper), material properties (stiffness, resilience) and materials (gold, Ti, Ti-alloy), abutment-screw surface texture, condition of mating surfaces, lubrication, torque value, rate of tightening (10, 20, 35 N, retorque after 10 min), joint integrity; English; human or animal studies; RCT, SR, cohort.
- **Exclusion**: bruxism/functional habits, clinical syndromes (epilepsy, psychological disorders, osteoporosis), implant fracture, non-English, off-topic, full text unavailable, duplicates, letters/commentaries; non-RCT/SR/cohort designs eliminated "to reduce bias."
- **Yield**: **84 articles screened → 36 included.**
- **Data extracted**: reference, design, number of implants, group specification, initial torque, preload, loss of preload.

## Results

The factors found to cause loss of preload, as enumerated by the paper:

| Factor | Study pool | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| **Material properties / type** (stiffness, resilience) | 6 articles, 102 implants | Preload ranks **gold > Ti-alloy > surface-treated Ti > pure Ti**; gold highest. |
| **Torque method** | 2 articles (1 an SR) | **Calibrated torque device mandatory** — avoid over-/under-tightening (prevents implant-abutment misfit). |
| **Rate & sequence of tightening / retorque** | 7 articles | **Retorque after 10 min** of initial torque efficiently maintains preload (tightening rates tested: 10, 20, 35 N). |
| **Implant-abutment connection type** (external hex vs Morse taper / internal) | subset of 7 articles | 2 studies (56 implants): joint *design* not significant; others (51 implants): **internal hexagon > external hexagon** preload. |
| **Screw surface texture / condition of mating surfaces (settling effect)** | (mechanism, discussion) | Microroughness collapse on initial torquing = **settling effect / embedment relaxation** → clamping-force loss → loosening. |
| **Lubrication / coatings** | 3 studies, 85 implants | Dry lubricants — 60–80 nm Ti nanoparticles, Vaseline, human saliva — **reduce friction, regulate the settling effect, maintain preload**. |
| **Abutment collar length** | 1 article, 15 implants | **Greater collar height → significantly greater torque loss** after cyclic loading. |
| **Contact area** | (summary) | Abutments with more extensive implant contact area → lower incidence of torque loss. |

**Clinical bottom line**: lubricated **gold** screws with an **internal** connection, placed with a **calibrated** torquing device and **retorqued after 10 min**, give maximum preload. Knowledge of these determinants lets clinicians avoid screw loosening and the implant failure it precedes.
