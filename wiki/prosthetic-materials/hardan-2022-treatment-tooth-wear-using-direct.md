## One-line Summary

Systematic review (16 clinical studies, follow-up up to 10 years, PRISMA 2020) of direct vs indirect restorations for tooth wear: high heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis and no restoration technique or material was shown superior for clinical performance.

## Summary

Tooth wear is a common clinical problem with no standardized treatment protocol. This PRISMA 2020 systematic review (search up to 29 April 2022 across PubMed/MedLine, Scopus, ISI Web of Science, Scielo, EMBASE) asked whether **direct or indirect restorations** give better clinical outcomes for treating worn dentition. From 2776 records, 16 clinical studies (RCTs and observational) were included for qualitative synthesis. Because of high heterogeneity in study design, materials, follow-up, and outcome criteria, a **meta-analysis could not be performed**. Studies covered anterior and posterior teeth with extensive wear and used materials ranging from ceramo-metal and full gold crowns to lithium disilicate, zirconia, polymer-infiltrated ceramic networks (PICN), and direct/indirect resin composites, with follow-ups up to 10 years. Findings on which restoration type performs best were **contradictory**, and the authors conclude there is **no evidence for the superiority of any technique or material**.

## Key Contributions

- First SR to focus on the **choice of technique (direct vs indirect) and material (ceramic vs resin-based)** for tooth wear, rather than on procedural steps.
- Maps the clinically used materials and their reported survival/failure behavior for worn dentition.
- Documents that most studies grade outcomes with **USPHS / FDI** criteria and report survival rates.
- Bottom line for practice: with current evidence, prefer **conservative, reversible, additive** approaches; no restorative method is proven superior.

## Methodology

- **PICOS:** Population = worn dental substrate; Intervention = indirect restorations; Control = direct restorations; Outcomes = FDI/USPHS criteria; Design = clinical trials.
- **Inclusion:** clinical studies of direct/indirect restorations for tooth wear; ≥6-month follow-up; English. **Excluded:** in vitro, case series/reports, reviews, pilot studies.
- Dual blinded screening on Rayyan; kappa agreement; third-author adjudication.
- Risk of bias scored across selection, performance/detection, incomplete-data, reporting, and other bias domains — many included studies rated high risk.

## Results

- 2776 records → **16 included** studies.
- **No meta-analysis** (heterogeneity too high); qualitative synthesis only.
- Etiology split into **chemical** (erosion, GERD, diet, bulimia) and **mechanical** (attrition, bruxism); no study compared outcomes by etiology or by anterior vs posterior region.
- Selected findings:
  - **Milosevic 2016** (n=164, composite, 8 y): year-1 failure 5.4%; longer time-to-failure with older age and adequate posterior support; bruxism / increased OVD not associated with failure.
  - **Taubóck 2021** (n=13, 11 y): direct composite at raised VDO performs well long-term in severe wear.
  - **Mehta 2021 / (b)** (n=34): higher failure risk for molars, posterior-mandibular, and multi-session anterior restorations; premolars < molars in risk.
  - **Smales 2007**: resin composites fail by fracture (often repairable); ceramo-metal crowns fail by complete loss (often needing RCT/extraction).
  - **Oudkerk 2020** (PICN, 2 y): high survival/success.
  - **Redman 2003 / Hemmings 2000 / Vailati 2013**: resin composites and veneers give worthy short-to-medium-term survival for localized anterior wear while preserving tooth structure and vitality.
