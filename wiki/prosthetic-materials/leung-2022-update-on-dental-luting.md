## One-line Summary

Narrative review (University of Hong Kong, 2022) of dental luting materials, classifying 7 cement families by chemistry, bonding mechanism, and clinical indication, and detailing properties, indications, and emerging modifications — with the thesis that no single cement satisfies all ideal requirements.

## Summary

A luting cement retains an indirect restoration by filling voids between it and the prepared tooth, providing retention through mechanical interlocking, chemical bonding, or both. This review organizes the common luting materials — zinc oxide eugenol/non-eugenol, zinc phosphate, zinc polycarboxylate, glass ionomer cement (GIC), resin-modified GIC (RMGIC), hybrid calcium aluminate/glass ionomer (CaAl/GI), and resin cements — across three classification axes and maps each to clinical indications based on its properties. The central thesis is that **no currently available cement fulfils every requirement of an ideal luting agent** (biocompatible, insoluble, strong, antibacterial, aesthetic, easy to use), so material selection is always a property-driven trade-off. The review closes with emerging modifications (nano-hydroxyapatite/bioactive-glass GIC, antimicrobial and self-healing resin cements, restorative composite as a luting agent) and a novel sustainable material, castor-oil polyurethane cement.

## Key Contributions

- **Three-axis classification** that turns a scattered material zoo into a decision framework:
  - *Chemistry*: water-based (ZOE/non-eugenol, polycarboxylate, zinc phosphate, GIC, CaAl/GI) vs resin-based (conventional, self-adhesive resin); RMGIC is hybrid.
  - *Bonding*: non-adhesive (ZOE, zinc phosphate — friction only) / chemically adhesive (polycarboxylate, GIC, CaAl/GI) / micromechanically adhesive (both resin cements); RMGIC is both.
  - *Indication*: temporary (ZOE/non-eugenol, polycarboxylate) vs permanent (the rest).
- Property-to-indication mapping for each material (see Results).
- Synthesis of recent R&D directions: bioactivity, antimicrobial loading, self-healing, and composite/castor-oil alternatives.

## Methodology

Narrative literature review — no systematic search, no risk-of-bias appraisal, no meta-analysis. Cited evidence spans in-vitro bond-strength tests to multi-year prospective clinical studies but is not pooled. Confidence is therefore `narrative-review`: useful as an organizing reference, not as a graded evidence base. For graded comparisons see the linked SR pages.

## Results

Material-by-material clinical summary:

| Cement | Bonding | Key strength | Key weakness | Typical indication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zinc oxide eugenol / non-eugenol | Non-adhesive | Easy removal, pulp-sedative, good seal | Eugenol may inhibit resin polymerization | Provisional cementation (use non-eugenol before composite) |
| Zinc phosphate | Non-adhesive | "Gold standard," high modulus | Initial pH <2.0 → sensitivity; soluble → 2° caries | Cast metal post-core, metal/metal-supported crowns |
| Zinc polycarboxylate | Chemically adhesive (weak) | Less pulp irritation, high tensile | Compressive strength ½–⅔ of zinc phosphate; 2.5 min working | Long-term provisionals / max provisional retention |
| GIC | Chemically adhesive | Fluoride release → anticariogenic; biocompatible | Brittle (low flexural strength); soluble | Metal/metal-ceramic, moderate-retention cases |
| RMGIC | Chemical + micromechanical | Stronger & tougher than GIC; fluoride release | More cytotoxic to pulp than GIC | Where GIC strength is insufficient |
| Hybrid CaAl/GI | Chemically adhesive, self-adhesive | Bioactive (Ca/PO₄, remineralization), antibacterial | Sparse long-term clinical data | Metal/PFM, posts, high-strength ceramics (zirconia, Li-disilicate) |
| Conventional resin | Micromechanical | Highest strength/bond/aesthetics | Technique-sensitive, hard excess removal | All-ceramic, veneers, RBBs, post-cementation |
| Self-adhesive resin | Micromechanical | Easy, less postop sensitivity | Lower bond strength | General indirect restorations (NOT RBBs, short crowns, veneers) |

Resin-cure notes (ISO 4049:2019): self-cured (class 1) for thick/opaque/metal; light-cured (class 2) for veneers/high aesthetics; dual-cured (class 3) versatile but degree of conversion without light may be as low as **10.82%**, and under 4 mm ceramic can fall below self-cure alone — extending light curing to **120 s** improves conversion. For ceramic restorations <2.0 mm, light/dual-cured outperform self-cured.
