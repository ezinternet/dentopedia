## One-line Summary

SR+MA (13 studies; GRADE very low): after lateral luxation of permanent anterior teeth, pooled pulp-necrosis prevalence is 57% overall — 58% in mature-apex vs only 12% in immature-apex teeth — and EIRR ~11%, so routine prophylactic pulp extirpation in mature teeth should be applied with caution.

## 한줄요약

SR+MA (13편; GRADE 매우 낮음): 영구 전치 측방탈구(lateral luxation) 후 치수괴사 통합 유병률은 전체 57%이며 성숙치근 58% 대 미성숙치근 12%, 외부염증성치근흡수(EIRR)는 약 11%다. 따라서 성숙치에 대한 일률적 예방적 치수적출은 신중히 적용해야 한다.

## Summary

Lateral luxation displaces a tooth non-axially, usually with alveolar bone fracture, and clinicians must decide whether to intervene endodontically prophylactically or monitor. This SR+MA (authored by Dental Trauma Guide / IADT-lineage investigators including Lauridsen and Andersson) quantifies the risk that drives that decision. Pulp necrosis is far more likely in mature-apex teeth (58%) than immature ones (12%), but even in mature teeth it stays below 60%, and external inflammatory root resorption (EIRR) is ~11% — so the authors argue against an absolute rule to extirpate the pulp in mature laterally luxated teeth. Apex maturity is the dominant prognostic axis. Certainty is very low.

## Key Contributions

- Maturity-stratified pulp-necrosis risk for lateral luxation specifically (not all luxation types pooled).
- Argues against an absolute prophylactic-endodontics rule for mature laterally luxated teeth.
- Couples necrosis risk with EIRR risk, both higher in mature apex.

## Methodology

- A priori PROSPERO protocol; search (10 Jul 2023): PubMed, LILACS, Web of Science, EMBASE, Scopus, Cochrane; no language/year limit.
- 13 studies (1985–2020), hospital settings; risk-of-bias assessment; GRADE.
- Meta-analysis of proportions (pooled prevalence) + relative risks.

## Results

- Pulp necrosis overall: 57% (95% CI 42–72%).
- Immature apex: 12% (95% CI 8–18%, I²=0%).
- Mature apex: 58% (95% CI 42–73%, I²=86%).
- EIRR: 11% (95% CI 4–27%, I²=95%); higher with mature apex (RR 1.26, 95% CI 1.12–1.42, I²=0%).
- ROB moderate/high in 9 studies; GRADE very low in 14/15 outcomes.
