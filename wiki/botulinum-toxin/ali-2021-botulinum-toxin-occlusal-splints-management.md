## One-line Summary
3-arm RCT (n=42, 12 months) in implant-overdenture sleep bruxers: both BTX and occlusal splint significantly improved PSQI sleep quality and patient satisfaction over overnight-OD-removal control (p≤0.0013), with BTX achieving the lowest scores at 9–12 months.

## Summary
This 3-arm RCT (NCT04366869, MSA Cairo + Taibah + Ibb) compared botulinum toxin (BTX) injection (n=14), conventional occlusal splint (n=14), and overnight OD removal (control, n=14) in implant-overdenture-rehabilitated sleep bruxers (definite SB by AASM + Bruxoff ambulatory EMG/ECG). Over 12 months, BTX and splint groups significantly improved patient satisfaction (TMD numeric scale: baseline ≈9 → 12-mo 3.8 BTX, 4.3 splint, 7.49 control) and sleep quality (PSQI: ≈8 → 5.04 BTX, 6.94 splint, 8.30 control), with BTX achieving the lowest scores at 9 and 12 months for both outcomes. Mechanical (prosthodontic) complications were highest in the control group.

## Key Contributions
- Only RCT addressing the specific population of implant-overdenture bruxers — clinically distinct from dentate TMD/myalgia cohorts.
- 12-month follow-up exceeds most BTX-for-bruxism RCTs.
- Uses Bruxoff (validated EMG/ECG) for definite-bruxism diagnosis rather than self-report alone.
- Captures mechanical prosthodontic complications, a critical outcome for overdenture practice.
- Demonstrates BTX advantage materializes at later timepoints (9-12 mo) for sleep quality — implies treatment durability beyond expected ~3-month BTX pharmacodynamics.

## Methodology
Single-center 3-arm parallel RCT with 1:1:1 allocation (n=14 each) by randomizer.org and sealed envelopes; assessor-only single blinding. Inclusion: definite SB (AASM + Bruxoff), implant-retained single-arch OD loaded ≥1 year, opposing natural dentition. All groups received new ODs with new nylon caps at baseline to control for prosthesis wear. Groups: I = remove OD overnight (control); II = conventional occlusal splint; III = BTX injection. Outcomes (baseline, 3, 6, 9, 12 mo): PSQI (sleep quality), TMD/numeric scale (patient satisfaction), prosthodontic mechanical complications.

## Results
**Patient Satisfaction (TMD/NS, lower = better):**

| Timepoint | Control | Splint | BTX | BTX vs. control p |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline | 8.51 ± 1.1 | 8.72 ± 1.25 | 9.02 ± 1.05 | ns |
| 3 mo | 7.92 ± 1.84 | 6.02 ± 2.97 | 5.5 ± 0.88 | 0.001 |
| 6 mo | 8.71 ± 0.88 | 5.1 ± 1.88 | 4.3 ± 0.87 | 0.0001 |
| 9 mo | 7.76 ± 1.06 | 4.7 ± 0.91 | 4.1 ± 1.77 | 0.0013 |
| 12 mo | 7.49 ± 1.87 | 4.3 ± 1.07 | 3.8 ± 1.02 | 0.0001 |

| Baseline | 8.30 ± 1.78 | 8.17 ± 1.52 | 7.91 ± 2.25 |
| 3 mo | 7.70 ± 3.42 | 6.83 ± 2.91 | 6.20 ± 2.34 |

- BTX-splint difference for PSQI widened at 9 and 12 months (different letter coding indicates between-group significance).
- Mechanical complications highest in the control arm.

## Clinical Implications
- For implant-overdenture bruxers, doing nothing prosthetically (just removing OD overnight) is the worst option for both sleep quality and prosthetic durability.
- Both BTX and conventional occlusal splints meaningfully improve PSQI and patient satisfaction; BTX has a slight 9-12 month edge for sleep quality.
- Reduced mechanical complications with active intervention is clinically meaningful (cost of repairs, patient downtime).
- Choice between BTX and splint should consider patient compliance (splint requires wearing), AE profile (BTX muscle/bone considerations from De la Torre Canales 2024), and prosthesis-specific factors.
- Findings apply specifically to implant-OD bruxers; extrapolation to fully dentate bruxers requires caution given Sahin 2024 / Saini 2024 equivalence findings in other populations.
