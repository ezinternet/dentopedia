## One-line Summary
SR+MA (22 studies: 2 RCTs, 3 prospective, 17 retrospective) on endodontically treated posterior teeth: over the short term (2.5–3 y), low-quality evidence shows no difference in tooth survival or restorative success between direct composite resin and indirect restorations (pooled RCT OR for tooth retention 2.06 [0.08–53.52], P=.66), but observational data are confounded because direct restorations were used on teeth with more remaining tissue or worse prognosis.

## Summary
This PRISMA-guided systematic review and meta-analysis addresses a question with surprisingly thin high-level evidence: when restoring an **endodontically treated posterior tooth**, does a direct composite resin restoration perform differently from an indirect restoration (mostly complete crowns; few inlays/onlays)? Searching MEDLINE, CENTRAL, and EMBASE (1275 records; 938 after de-duplication), the authors included 22 studies (2 RCTs, 3 prospective, 17 retrospective), deliberately separating composite resin from other direct materials (amalgam, glass ionomer).

The headline finding is twofold. (1) Over the short term (2.5–3 years), **low-quality evidence suggests no difference** in tooth survival or restorative success between direct and indirect restorations. (2) The observational literature is dominated by **confounding by indication**: clinicians placed direct restorations on teeth with more remaining coronal tissue (≥1 marginal ridge) or, conversely, on teeth with an unfavorable prognosis / when patients could not afford a crown. Because treatment was not randomly assigned, naive survival comparisons in cohort studies are invalid — 9 of the observational studies were rated "critical" and 11 "serious" risk of bias. The clinical takeaway is not "direct equals indirect," but rather that the evidence cannot yet separate the restoration type's effect from the baseline condition of the tooth.

## Key Contributions
- First SR+MA to isolate **direct composite resin vs indirect** specifically on endodontically treated **posterior** teeth, separating composite from amalgam/GI.
- Quantifies the short-term equivalence signal: pooled RCT OR for tooth retention 2.06 (95% CI 0.08–53.52, P=.66) and for restorative success 2.31 (95% CI 0.59–9.08, P=.29).
- Names and operationalizes **confounding by indication** via a target-RCT/ROBINS-I framework, showing why observational direct-vs-indirect survival comparisons are untrustworthy.
- GRADE appraisal: low certainty; explicit call for RCTs that control for remaining coronal tooth tissue.

## Methodology
- **Design**: SR + random-effects meta-analysis (Mantel–Haenszel OR, RevMan v5.4.1); PRISMA reporting.
- **Search**: MEDLINE/PubMed, CENTRAL, EMBASE (6 Dec 2020); RCT + prospective + retrospective trials since 2000 comparing direct composite resin and indirect restorations; English/German/Dutch.
- **Selection**: 2 independent reviewers + 3rd for disputes; 1275 → 938 de-duplicated → 41 full texts → 22 included (+12 via hand search).
- **Risk of bias**: ROB2 (RCTs), ROBINS-I (observational); confounding by restorative indication graded low→critical against a specified target RCT; GRADE for the body of evidence.
- **Outcomes**: tooth retention/survival and restorative success; pooling limited to studies clearly reporting posterior composite resin and indirect restorations.

## Results
- 22 studies; teeth per study 57 to 1,462,936 (median 220). 13 short-term (2–5 y), 9 medium-term (7.3–11 y). Sources: 5 databases, 14 university, 3 private.
- Indirect restorations were predominantly complete crowns; only 2 studies included partial restorations (24 gold partial crowns, 69 inlays). Posts in 13 studies (median 46%).
- **Short-term tooth retention (RCT pooled)**: OR 2.06 (0.08–53.52), P=.66 — no difference; **low** quality.
- **Short-term restorative success (RCT pooled)**: OR 2.31 (0.59–9.08), P=.29 — no difference; all failures repairable.
- **Observational MA not feasible** (heterogeneity, inability to isolate posterior composite outcomes).
- Bias: 9 studies "critical", 11 "serious", driven by confounding on prognosis/coronal structure.
- Apparent "worse direct" signals (e.g., Jirathanyanatt 2019 fracture HR 3.9 [1.8–8.7]; Stenhagen 2020 more reinterventions) occurred where direct resin was placed on poorer-prognosis teeth — illustrating the confounding rather than a true material effect.
