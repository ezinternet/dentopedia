## One-line Summary

Scoping review (PRISMA-ScR; 30 studies, 27 in-vitro + 3 case reports, 2000–Jun 2025) mapping CAD/CAM-milled vs 3D-printed inlays/onlays/veneers — both reach clinically acceptable marginal fit (<120 µm); CAD/CAM is faster (45–90 min) with higher ceramic strength (~1800–2500 N), 3D printing gives better adaptation/customization (esp. veneers) but longer fabrication (90–150 min) and lower hardness/color stability; no long-term clinical trials exist.

## Summary

This scoping review (Arksey & O'Malley framework, PRISMA-ScR reporting) maps the clinical-outcome evidence comparing two digital fabrication routes — CAD/CAM subtractive milling vs 3D-printing additive manufacturing — across inlays, onlays, and veneers. From 1566 records, 30 studies were included (27 in-vitro, 3 case reports; **no large-scale clinical trials**). Both fabrication methods produce restorations within clinically acceptable marginal/internal adaptation (≤120 µm, ISO-aligned). The central trade-off: CAD/CAM milling is faster (45–90 min), has the strongest validated ceramic outcomes, and superior hardness/color stability; 3D printing offers better adaptation and customizability — strongest for veneers — but longer fabrication (90–150 min incl. postprocessing), lower hardness, and weaker staining/color resistance. The authors position the two as complementary: CAD/CAM as the validated default for single-unit ceramics, 3D printing reserved for high-customization, minimally invasive, or complex-geometry cases until long-term clinical data mature.

## Key Contributions

- **Third evidence axis for indirect restorations**: where sibling scoping reviews map survival (Prott 2025) and fracture-by-prep-design (Sirikatitham 2026), this maps the **fabrication-method** axis (milling vs printing) across all three restoration classes.
- **Inlays** (most studied): 3D printing achieves equal or smaller internal gaps than milling (Ahlholm 2019: 40–60% smaller; Lim 2023: 3D-printed + zirconia best fit/accuracy), but milled ceramics retain higher hardness and color stability; printed resins stain more.
- **Onlays** (mixed): pressable ceramics > milled lithium disilicate in fracture resistance; 3D-printed onlays often show better fit/reproducibility, but outcomes are material/printing/prep-dependent; preparation design (shoulder) and scanning modality (indirect) measurably improve adaptation; cement chemistry (etch-and-rinse > self-adhesive) matters.
- **Veneers** (strongest 3D-printing case): 3D-printed zirconia highest load-bearing (Ioannidis: F_initial 1650 N, F_max 2026 N), exceeding functional thresholds for ultrathin occlusal veneers; 3D-printed veneers showed lower marginal discrepancy than milled (Daghrery p=0.001); build angle critical (optimal ~30°, worst 90°).
- **Material hierarchy**: lithium disilicate = esthetic/mechanical gold standard (pressed > milled for fracture); printed zirconia = high load-bearing; hybrid ceramics/printable composites = workflow flexibility, moderate fit, lower durability; PEEK/PEKK = provisional/implant niche.

## Methodology

- Frameworks: Arksey & O'Malley + Levac refinements; PRISMA-ScR reporting.
- Databases: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library + Google Scholar (gray literature); Jan 2000–Jun 2025, English only.
- Flow: 1566 records → 452 duplicates removed → 1114 screened → 330 full-text → **30 included** (27 in-vitro, 3 case reports).
- Two independent reviewers + third-reviewer arbitration + expert consultation; standardized data charting.
- Narrative thematic synthesis (no meta-analysis due to heterogeneity); **no formal risk-of-bias assessment** (declared beyond scoping-review scope).
- Aggregate benchmarks: marginal fit ≤120 µm both methods (3D-printed 40–100 µm); fracture resistance milled lithium disilicate ~1800–2500 N vs printed resin/hybrid 900–1800 N; fabrication 45–90 min (CAD/CAM) vs 90–150 min (3D printing).

## Results

| Restoration | CAD/CAM milling | 3D printing | Bottom line |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inlays | Higher hardness, color stability; faster | Equal/superior internal fit (some 40–60% smaller gaps) | Both acceptable; ceramic milling for durability, printing competitive on fit |
| Onlays | Better internal adaptation in some studies; validated ceramics | Often better marginal fit/reproducibility (material-dependent) | Mixed; pressable ceramics strongest, prep design & cement decisive |
| Veneers | Best overall fit (zirconia) in some comparisons | Strongest opportunity; printed zirconia exceeds functional load; lower discrepancy (Daghrery p=0.001) | Greatest 3D-printing potential; build angle ~30° optimal |

- Both methods clinically acceptable on fit; ceramics consistently beat resin-based systems on fracture resistance; esthetic outcomes (translucency/color) highly material-dependent.
- CAD/CAM preferred for chairside speed and validated ceramic survival; 3D printing for design versatility but longer processing and weaker durability.
- **No long-term clinical validation** — findings are predominantly in-vitro; clinical extrapolation should be cautious.
