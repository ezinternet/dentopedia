## Summary

Spray-and-stretch (분사신장) is the classic Travell-Simons myofascial technique: a **vapocoolant** spray briefly cools the skin over the muscle and its referral zone, reflexively lowering muscle tone so the muscle can be passively stretched and the trigger point inactivated. In the masticatory system its evidence has been thin compared with exercise and manual therapy. Koole et al. (2019, *J Prosthet Dent*) directly tested whether the **spray alone** — without painful passive stretching — increases **maximal mouth opening (MMO)** in patients with orofacial pain.

In 60 orofacial-pain patients and 61 asymptomatic controls, vapocoolant was swept twice over both cheeks (mandibular angle → temple) and MMO re-measured. MMO **increased in most participants**, with a significantly larger relative and absolute gain in the **pain group than controls (P<.001)**, and a larger response in women. The authors' clinical takeaway: the cooling stimulus can deliver the benefit of the stretch maneuver while **sparing patients the discomfort of passive stretching**.

> Ingested abstract-only (no PMC full text). Effect-size magnitudes in mm and longitudinal/pain outcomes are not captured here; treat this as evidence of an *immediate* range-of-motion effect, not of durable symptom relief.

## Key Contributions

- First-line **quantification of vapocoolant spray on MMO** in a real orofacial-pain population (most spray-and-stretch data are from neck/trunk/limb myofascial pain).
- Supports a **comfort-driven rationale**: achieve the stretch effect without the pain of passive stretching → better tolerance/compliance in myogenous TMD.
- Symptomatic patients respond **more** than asymptomatic controls, consistent with the technique acting on hypertonic/trigger-point-laden masticatory muscle.

## Methodology

- **Design**: prospective two-group interventional study; single immediate-effect measurement, no randomization, no sham/comparator arm.
- **Participants**: 61 controls (general dental office) vs 60 orofacial-pain patients (from >750 oral-surgery patients); pain verified by palpation of TMJs and masticatory/neck/shoulder muscles.
- **Intervention & outcome**: baseline MMO (interincisal distance, TMJ equilateral-triangle gauge, ×2) → vapocoolant sprayed ×2 on both cheeks → MMO ×2.
- **Statistics**: 2-way ANOVA (group, sex-corrected); covariance model for age (α=.05).

## Results

| Factor | Finding | P |
|---|---|---|
| Group (MMO gain, relative & absolute) | Pain patients > controls | <.001 |
| Baseline MMO between groups | No difference | .175 |
| Sex (baseline MMO) | Women > men | .008 |
| Sex (MMO gain) | Trend, not significant | .090 |
| Age (covariance model) | No significant effect | .73 |

- MMO increased after spraying in most participants.
- Conclusion: spray-and-stretch raises MMO, more so in orofacial-pain patients and in women; **pain from passive stretching can be prevented**.
