## One-line Summary

In-vivo micro-CT study of 56 Wistar rats (young + adult, unilateral maxillary molar extraction): reduced occlusal function did NOT hinder condylar process height or base width growth, but the extraction-side condyle of growing rats failed to gain cross-sectional surface (became thinner while keeping its length); adult condyles were largely unaffected by the occlusal change.

## Summary

De Carli et al. used longitudinal in-vivo micro-CT to track how unilateral reduction of masticatory function (unilateral maxillary molar extraction) reshapes the mandibular condylar process, and whether the effect differs between growing (4-week) and adult (26-week) rats. Over a 3-month follow-up they measured condylar process height, base width, and average cross-sectional surface, separating the extraction-side (unopposed) from the contralateral (overloaded) condyle.

The central finding is a **dissociation between length and thickness**: condylar height and base width grew normally regardless of occlusal function, but the **cross-sectional surface** of growing animals was occlusion-dependent — young controls gained area while young experimental rats did not. The authors interpret this as the growing condyle becoming **thinner while maintaining its length** when occlusal stimulus is reduced. Adult condyles showed essentially no change, consistent with a closing adaptive window with age. Notably, the unopposed and overloaded sides were affected similarly, suggesting that a purely functional asymmetry (without a mandibular lateral shift) does not generate a measurable left/right condylar asymmetry.

## Key Contributions

- First in-vivo longitudinal micro-CT examination of condylar morphology under **unilateral** masticatory function in both growing and adult rats.
- Separates three condylar parameters and shows only **cross-sectional surface** (not height or width) is occlusion-sensitive.
- Documents an **age-dependent adaptive window**: growing condyles remodel (thin out) under hypofunction; adult condyles are refractory.
- Shows overloaded vs unopposed sides are similarly affected — functional asymmetry alone (no mandibular shift) does not induce condylar asymmetry.

## Methodology

- **Design**: animal experimental, longitudinal in-vivo micro-CT (T0 / 1 month / 3 months).
- **Sample**: 56 Wistar rats — young (4 wk) and adult (26 wk); each age = 16 experimental + 12 control.
- **Intervention**: maxillary right molar extraction; all animals on soft diet (controls included, to isolate extraction from surgical effect). Right = unopposed/hypofunctional, left = overloaded/hyperfunctional.
- **Imaging/analysis**: Quantum GX micro-CT (90 kV, 88 μA, 30 µm voxel); OsiriX, reoriented to condylar base; blinded operator.
- **Outcomes**: condylar process height, base width, average cross-sectional surface (T90 − T0).
- **Statistics**: two-way ANOVA (Age × Group) + Tukey; Kruskal-Wallis for height; reliability >96%, low random error (Dahlberg).

## Results

- **Height**: no control-vs-experimental difference; large age effect (young +1.36 mm vs adult +0.24 mm, P < .001).
- **Base width**: no control-vs-experimental difference (Group P = .922); age effect (young +1.85 mm vs adult +0.20 mm, P < .001).
- **Cross-sectional surface**: significant Age×Group interaction (P = .002). Young control +0.25 mm² (P = .043); young experimental no increase. YC > YE by 0.19 mm² (P = .010); YC > all adult groups (0.28 mm², P < .001). Adults: no significant change. YO vs YU: no difference.
- Adults had larger baseline condylar dimensions; young rats nearly reached adult-T0 values by T2.
