## Summary

This finite-element analysis (FEA) study examines how three common mandibular deformities — prognathism (전돌), retrusion (후퇴), and deviation (편위/facial asymmetry) — affect temporomandibular joint (TMJ) stress during **unilateral molar occlusion**, the loading scenario that commonly occurs during mastication. Patient-specific FE models were built for 35 individuals (11 asymptomatic controls, 10 prognathism, 5 retrusion, 9 deviation).

The central finding is a **TMD-risk ranking by deformity type**: any mandibular deformity increases pressure on the condyle and articular disc relative to asymptomatic subjects, but **facial asymmetry (deviation) significantly increases TMJ stress compared with prognathism or retrusion** — making the deviation patient the highest-risk phenotype for temporomandibular disorder (TMD). Within deviation patients, chewing on the **non-deviated side** raises TMJ stress further, so the clinical recommendation is to chew **bilaterally or on the deviated side**.

Note: this page is grounded in the Taylor & Francis abstract only (full text paywalled). Numeric stress magnitudes (MPa), mesh/material details, and validation are not available; findings are directional/qualitative.

## Key Contributions

- Single FEA framework comparing all three common mandibular deformities plus an asymptomatic control, using patient-derived geometry.
- Isolates clinically realistic **unilateral molar occlusion** as the loading condition rather than symmetric occlusion.
- Establishes a TMD-risk hierarchy: **deviation (facial asymmetry) > prognathism / retrusion**.
- Provides an actionable chewing-side recommendation for deviation patients (chew bilaterally or on the deviated side; avoid the non-deviated side).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Finite-element analysis (FEA), patient-specific models.
- **Sample**: 35 individuals — 11 asymptomatic, 10 mandibular prognathism, 5 mandibular retrusion, 9 mandibular deviation.
- **Loading**: Unilateral molar occlusion.
- **Outcomes**: Stress on the mandibular condyle and articular disc; deviation-group comparison of deviated- vs non-deviated-side chewing.
- Mesh, material properties, boundary conditions, and numeric stress values are not recoverable from the abstract.

## Results

- Mandibular deformity increases condyle and articular-disc pressure during unilateral molar occlusion (vs asymptomatic).
- Facial asymmetry (deviation) significantly increases TMJ stress vs prognathism or retrusion.
- In deviation patients, chewing on the non-deviated side produces higher TMJ stress than chewing on the deviated side.
- Conclusion: mandibular deviation carries the highest TMD risk; recommend bilateral or deviated-side chewing.

## Clinical Bottom Line

Among mandibular deformity patients, **facial asymmetry (deviation) is the biomechanically highest-risk phenotype for TMD** under everyday unilateral chewing. Counsel deviation patients to avoid habitually chewing on the non-deviated side — bilateral or deviated-side chewing lowers TMJ stress. (Evidence weight: in-vitro FEA, small subgroups, abstract-only extraction — directional guidance, not a quantified threshold.)
