## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 5 RCTs (279 RDC/TMD-diagnosed masticatory myofascial pain patients): manual therapy relieved pain in all studies but was NOT superior to counseling or botulinum toxin; no meta-analysis (heterogeneity), all studies at unclear risk of bias.

## Summary

This PRISMA-guided systematic review evaluated whether manual therapy (MT) is effective for myofascial pain related to temporomandibular disorders (TMD). To control diagnostic heterogeneity, inclusion was restricted to randomized controlled trials that diagnosed masticatory myofascial pain with the RDC/TMD. Five RCTs (279 patients, ages 12–69 y) met criteria; 156 patients received MT alone or MT + counseling, and 123 served as controls (botulinum toxin, home physical therapy, counseling, or no treatment).

The consistent finding was that **MT relieved pain in every included study**, supporting its role as a low-cost, reversible, noninvasive conservative treatment. However, **MT was not superior to its comparators head-to-head**: it was statistically similar to a single botulinum toxin injection (Guarda-Nardini), and MT + counseling was not statistically better than counseling alone (Michelotti). MT did beat *no treatment* (Kalamir 2012) and *counseling alone* (Kalamir 2013), and adding specialist MT to a home program beat the home program alone (Tuncer). Because the studies were methodologically heterogeneous and used different comparators, **no meta-analysis was performed**, and all 5 studies carried an overall *unclear* risk of bias on the Cochrane tool — so the authors stress that the evidence is inconclusive and that MT efficacy is closely intertwined with patient counseling/education.

## Key Contributions

- RDC/TMD-anchored RCT-only synthesis of MT for masticatory myofascial pain (5 trials, 279 patients).
- Establishes MT relieves pain consistently but is **not superior** to counseling or botulinum toxin in direct comparison.
- Frames MT as low-cost, reversible, noninvasive — statistically as effective as higher-cost botulinum toxin.
- Emphasizes counseling/patient education as closely tied to MT effectiveness (combination tends to help).
- Documents the weak evidence base: no meta-analysis possible; all included RCTs at unclear risk of bias.

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA-guided SR of randomized/controlled clinical trials; no meta-analysis (heterogeneity + differing comparators).
- **Databases**: Cochrane Library, MEDLINE, Web of Science, Scopus, LILACS, SciELO (3 independent reviewers, Feb–Apr 2019); reference lists hand-checked.
- **Inclusion**: adolescents/adults/elderly with RDC/TMD-diagnosed myofascial pain; ≥1 MT intervention group (mobilization, stretching, isometric resistance, guided opening/closing, massage); ≥1 control group (other treatment or none).
- **Screening**: 167 records → 143 de-duplicated → 23 full-text → **5 included** (18 excluded: 10 not RDC/TMD, 2 non-English/Portuguese/Spanish, 2 MT + medications, 2 book chapter/case-control, 1 duplicate).
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (6 domains); all 5 studies rated overall **Unclear** (blinding of participants/personnel "Unclear" in every study).

## Results

Population: 279 patients (12–69 y); 156 MT alone or MT + counseling, 123 controls (15 botulinum toxin, 20 home physical therapy, 31 no treatment, 57 counseling).

| Study (country) | Comparison | n / group | Follow-up | Main result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Guarda-Nardini (Italy) | MT vs single botulinum toxin (150 U/side masseter+temporalis) | 15 vs 15 | 3 mo | Both improved; MT slightly better subjectively but **NS (P > .05)** |
| Michelotti (Italy) | Counseling only vs MT + counseling | 34 vs 36 | 3 mo | Success 57% vs 77%; **NS (P = .157)** |
| Tuncer (Turkey) | MT + home physical therapy vs home physical therapy alone | 20 vs 20 | 4 wk | Added specialist MT **significantly better** (P < .001) |
| Kalamir 2012 (Australia) | MT vs MT + counseling vs no treatment | 31/31/31 | 6 mo & 1 y | Both interventions > no treatment (P < .05); at 1 y MT+counseling > MT (P = .016) |
| Kalamir 2013 (Australia) | MT vs counseling alone | 23 vs 23 | 6 wk | MT **significantly better** (P < .001) |

Bottom-line pattern:
- MT relieved pain in **all 5 studies**.
- MT **better than no treatment** (Kalamir 2012) and **better than counseling alone** (Kalamir 2013).
- MT + counseling **not statistically better than counseling alone** (Michelotti, P = .157).
- MT alone **not better than botulinum toxin** (Guarda-Nardini, P > .05).
- MT + home therapy **better than home therapy alone** (Tuncer, P < .001).
- All 5 RCTs at **unclear risk of bias**; heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis → conclusions inconclusive, more RCTs needed.
