## One-line Summary

PICOS/PRISMA systematic review of 28 clinical trials mapping the *techniques* (target muscle, substance, dose, navigation, sessions) of intramuscular injection and dry needling in masticatory muscles for myofascial pain — masseter (65%) and temporalis (27%) dominate, BTX is the most-studied substance, palpation is the main navigation method (72.5%), and pterygoid needling is scarce and unstandardized.

## Summary

Nowak et al. (2021) systematically reviewed **28 clinical trials** (evidence level 1–3, English, ≥2011; PROSPERO 272124) describing how needling therapies are *technically performed* within masticatory muscles for myofascial pain. Unlike efficacy-focused meta-analyses, this review deliberately did **not** appraise quality or risk of bias — its outcome metric was effective muscle penetration and the cataloguing of technique parameters. From 367 records, blind dual screening (Cohen's κ = 0.98) and triple full-text review yielded 28 papers, tabulated by target muscle.

Two elementary techniques are distinguished: **wet needling** (injection of a substance) and **dry needling** (solid filiform needle, no fluid). Within wet needling, **botulinum toxin (BTX)** dominates the literature, with local anesthetics (LA) second; novel substances — **platelet-rich plasma (PRP)** and **collagen** — are emerging but supported only by low-quality evidence. The **masseter** and **temporalis** account for the overwhelming majority of interventions because they are large, accessible muscles giving satisfactory results without navigation aids; the **lateral and medial pterygoid** muscles are severely under-studied despite diagnostic/therapeutic relevance for TMJ internal derangement. All included studies claimed effectiveness (pain reduction, increased maximum mouth opening, raised pressure pain threshold), so the authors conclude needling is a simple, safe, accessible supporting/alternative therapy — while urging standardized protocols and high-quality trials of non-BTX substances and pterygoid needling.

## Key Contributions

- **Technique map, not an efficacy pool**: positions itself as the complement to Al-Moraissi et al.'s 2020 network meta-analysis — it answers *how* needling is done (muscle, substance, dose, navigation, sessions), not *which works best*.
- **Quantifies the field**: 24/28 papers (86%) from 2016+; masseter is dominant (65% of interventions; 711 patient cases), temporalis 27% (294), lateral pterygoid 8% (90), medial pterygoid <1% (5).
- **Substance distribution** (masseter): 17 BTX, 7 LA, 4 deep dry needling (DDN), 2 PRP, 1 superficial dry needling (SDN), 1 collagen. Temporalis = all BTX except one lidocaine. Pterygoids = BTX, DDN, percutaneous needle electrolysis (PNE) only.
- **Navigation distribution**: palpation 72.5%, EMG 11.8%, unspecified 9.8%, EPI® electrotherapy 3.9%, MRI 2.0%.
- **Safety flag**: dose-related bone loss in the masticatory complex with repeated/high-dose BTX (Raphael 2014; De la Torre Canales 2020), countered by a no-effect cohort (Raphael 2020) — monitoring advised at high doses.

## Methodology

- **Frameworks**: PICOS eligibility, PRISMA reporting; protocol registered (PROSPERO 272124).
- **Search**: PubMed (MEDLINE/PMC) + BASE, 18 April 2021 → 367 records.
- **Selection**: Rayyan de-duplication; blind title/abstract screening by 2 reviewers (κ = 0.98); blind full-text by 3 reviewers (total convergence) → **28 included**.
- **Population**: adults with myofascial orofacial pain (RDC/TMD, DC/TMD, ICOP). Excluded: animals; fibromyalgia, neuralgia, oromandibular dystonia, cerebral palsy, masseter hypertrophy; induced pain.
- **Intervention**: IM injection into and/or dry needling of masticatory muscles. Excluded: non-masticatory muscle injection, nerve blockade, acupuncture.
- **Outcomes**: primary = effective muscle penetration; secondary = any positive effect. **No quality/RoB appraisal, no own meta-analysis** (acknowledged limitation).
- Multi-muscle/multi-substance papers counted as separate "trials," producing the per-muscle intervention tallies.

## Results

Intervention and dosing parameters extracted across the four muscle tables:

| Target muscle | Interventions | Patient cases | Substances studied | Typical BTX dose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Masseter | 32 (65%) | 711 | BTX (17), LA (7), DDN (4), PRP (2), SDN (1), collagen (1) | 20–150 IU (30–50 IU dominant) |
| Temporalis | 13 (27%) | 294 | BTX (all but one LA) | 10–100 IU (20–25 IU dominant) |
| Lateral pterygoid | 5 (8%) | 90 | BTX (2), DDN (2), PNE (1) | 10 & 20 IU |
| Medial pterygoid | 1 (<1%) | 5 | DDN (1) | — |

Additional technique findings:

- **Injection sites**: 51% of trials specified a number of sites (masseter 2–5, temporalis 2–3); 37% targeted MTPs directly; 12% unspecified. Two-to-three punctures dominated; single punctures rare; Guarda-Nardini used a multi-puncture "chessboard" BTX pattern.
- **Local anesthetic volumes**: 0.2–0.5 mL/puncture (Nitecka-Buchta 2 mL/muscle).
- **Dry needling**: classic Hong "fast in, fast out" with local twitch response (LTR); newer evidence questions whether LTR is necessary (Hakim, Perreault). Ozden found **superficial DN out-performed deep DN** for pain reduction (n=40).
- **Navigation systems**: EMG (functional only — no morphologic tracking; Patel's electrode needle also reached LPM); PNE/EPI® (Lopez-Martos: earlier improvement than DDN, no adverse effects); MRI infrared Brainlab navigation for LPM upper head (Pons: accurate/safe but complex, costly, 4/6 improved). Out-of-eligibility tools noted as promising: CT-derived custom intraoral guides (Oliveira, Yoshida) and ultrasound strain/shear-wave elastography (Sanabria, Olchowy).
- **BTX bone-density caution**: dose-dependent coronoid/condylar volume loss reported; low doses appear safe; high-dose patients should be monitored.

**Conclusion**: needling within masticatory muscles is a simple, safe, accessible supporting/alternative therapy for myofascial pain, but the field lacks standardization; pterygoid needling and non-BTX substances (PRP, collagen) need high-quality trials.
