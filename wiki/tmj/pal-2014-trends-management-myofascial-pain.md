## One-line Summary

Narrative orofacial/maxillofacial management review placing the spray-and-stretch technique within a two-phase treatment algorithm for masticatory myofascial pain — alongside occlusal splints, massage, ultrasound, TENS, LLLT, biofeedback, trigger-point injection (saline/LA/botulinum toxin), muscle relaxants (tizanidine), and tricyclic antidepressants.

## Summary

This maxillofacial-surgery review situates **masticatory myofascial pain** inside the TMD classification (Bell/Okeson) and walks through the full management ladder. Spray-and-stretch (분사신장) appears here not as a standalone therapy but as one of several **physical-therapy / trigger-point modalities** — which is the realistic clinical role of the technique. For a wiki whose other physiotherapy evidence is exercise/manual-therapy SRs and a single immediate-MMO study ([[tmj/koole-2019-spray-stretch-technique-mouth-opening]]), this page supplies the surrounding decision context.

The authors set three management goals — reduce pain, **restore normal mouth opening**, and improve quality of life — and present a two-phase algorithm: conservative physical/occlusal/pharmacologic therapy first, escalating to trigger-point injection and botulinum toxin for refractory cases, with re-evaluation for concomitant TMJ internal derangement and psychogenic perpetuating factors.

> Despite the title's "systematically reviewed" wording, this is a **narrative review** — no PRISMA, no quality grading, no pooled estimates. The text itself repeatedly notes that modalities like ultrasound and spray-and-stretch rest on empirical rather than high-grade evidence.

## Key Contributions

- A single orofacial-context map of myofascial-pain management modalities, with spray-and-stretch positioned among physical therapies.
- Facial trigger-point referral patterns (teeth, alveolar ridge, tongue, palate, temporal/frontal, TMJ, ear, pharynx, occiput) — useful for distinguishing muscle-referred from odontogenic/joint pain.
- Palpation (active vs latent TrP, jump sign, local twitch response) framed as the diagnostic gold standard; instrumented tests (jaw tracking, sonography, vibratography, sEMG) flagged as low-specificity adjuncts.

## Methodology

- Literature search across MEDLINE/PubMed/Google Scholar plus textbooks; keywords "myofascial", "trigger points", "myospasms", "myofascial pain dysfunction syndrome", "facial myalgia", "taut bands".
- No formal inclusion or risk-of-bias criteria → narrative synthesis organized by diagnostic process and treatment modality.

## Results

Modality-level summary (no effect sizes — review):

| Domain | Modalities cited |
|---|---|
| Physical therapy | Deep stroking massage; ultrasound (high-power > conventional for acute); TENS (high-freq/high-intensity); LLLT; biofeedback (EMG frontalis/masseter); chiropractic ischemic compression; **spray-and-stretch** |
| Occlusal | Occlusal splint therapy (neuromuscular harmony) |
| Injection | Trigger-point injection — saline, local anesthetic, botulinum toxin A (US-guided more effective) |
| Pharmacology | Tizanidine (first-line relaxant), topical thiocolchicoside, tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline ≤75 mg/day) |
| Escalation | Re-evaluate etiology / TMJ internal derangement / psychogenic factors → BTX; adjunct yoga/pranayama |
