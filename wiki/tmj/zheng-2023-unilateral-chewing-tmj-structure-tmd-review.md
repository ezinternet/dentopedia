## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Chinese, J Zhejiang Univ Med Sci): chewing-side preference (unilateral chewing) is a major TMD risk factor — preferred-side condyles become shorter and displaced with reduced posterior-superior joint space, articular eminence inclination and glenoid fossa depth increase, TMD patients show higher unilateral-chewing rates (e.g. 56.5% vs healthy), and proposed mechanisms span biomechanical (BMP-2/VEGF condylar remodeling), masticatory-muscle (CaN-driven Type II→I fiber shift, EMG asymmetry) and neurogenic (substance P/CGRP) pathways.

## Summary

Unilateral chewing (chewing-side preference) is defined as one side's chewing-cycle count exceeding the other by >30%, with reported prevalence ~76–87% across dentitions. This narrative review (Zheng 2023) collates human imaging (mainly CBCT), clinical, animal and finite-element studies to argue that chewing-side preference is an important — and mechanistically plausible — risk factor for temporomandibular disorders (TMD), adding a functional behavioral variable to the long-contested occlusion–TMD debate.

The review is organized as a three-level chain: **structure → clinical association → mechanism**.

**Structure.** On the preferred side the condyle tends to be smaller and displaced superiorly, with reduced posterior, posterior-superior, lateral and superior joint spaces and a wider condylar neck; the non-preferred side shows compensatory anterior joint-space enlargement. Articular eminence inclination and glenoid fossa depth are greater (most marked in TMD-with-unilateral-chewing patients), which predisposes to anterior disc displacement during translation. One CBCT volume study (Costa 2021) found no significant condylar difference — the human structural evidence is not unanimous.

**Clinical association.** Across 15 tabulated studies, TMD patients consistently show higher unilateral-chewing rates than controls (e.g. Wu 2021: 56.5% in TMD vs healthy; another series 69.7% vs 54.1%), and prevalence rises monotonically with TMD severity (Yalçın 2017: 36.8%→46.9%→54.6%→65.0% from none to severe). Unilateral chewing correlates with opening pain, joint pain, orofacial pain, limited movement and clicking; TMJOA patients with unilateral chewing show more condylar surface destruction/flattening.

**Mechanism.** Three pathways are proposed: (1) **biomechanical** — altered loading raises BMP-2, VEGF, IL-1β, TNF-α and Type II collagen in chewing-side condylar cartilage (thickening), with rotation-vs-translation differences between sides driving fossa remodeling; (2) **masticatory-muscle** — elevated intracellular Ca²⁺ activates the calcineurin (CaN) pathway, transforming Type II→Type I fibers on the preferred side, with EMG asymmetry and inflammatory muscle damage; (3) **neurogenic** — upregulated trigeminal-ganglion substance P and CGRP enhance nociception and produce TMJ neurogenic inflammation.

**Clinical takeaway:** clinicians should identify unilateral chewing and its causes (missing teeth, caries, periapical disease) in both healthy and TMD patients and correct it early to help prevent or limit TMD. Causality remains uncertain — most mechanistic data are from rat models, and human studies rarely stratify by age, sex, preferred side or duration.

## Key Contributions

- Frames chewing-side preference as a discrete, biomechanically-grounded TMD risk factor distinct from static occlusal interferences — a functional behavioral lens on the occlusion–TMD question.
- Maps the dose–response-like gradient of unilateral-chewing rate against TMD severity (Yalçın 2017) and TMD case status (Wu 2021).
- Integrates a coherent three-axis mechanism (biomechanical → muscular → neurogenic) linking habitual loading to structural remodeling and pain.

## Methodology

Narrative review (综述), not systematic — no formal search, risk-of-bias appraisal, or pooled effect estimates. Evidence base: CBCT/imaging studies of joint morphology, cross-sectional/case-control clinical studies (Table 1, 15 studies, mostly RDC/TMD-referenced), rat experimental models, and finite-element analysis of mandibular movement. Open access (CC BY-NC-ND 4.0), funded by NSFC (82020108011).

## Results

- **Condyle (preferred side):** ↓ posterior/posterior-superior/lateral/superior joint space, ↑ condylar neck width, smaller condyle migrating superiorly; non-preferred side ↑ anterior joint space (compensatory). Conflicting null: Costa 2021 (no volume difference).
- **Eminence/fossa:** ↑ articular eminence inclination on preferred side; TMD-with-unilateral-chewing > TMD-without > healthy for inclination and fossa depth (Ma 2021).
- **Disc:** higher non-reducing anterior disc displacement, pain and joint noise on preferred side in disc-displacement patients (Huang 2021).
- **Prevalence in TMD:** Wu 2021 — TMD prevalence 31.7%, unilateral chewing in 56.5% of TMD (>healthy). Severity gradient (Yalçın 2017): 36.8% / 46.9% / 54.6% / 65.0% (none/mild/moderate/severe).
- **Symptoms:** significant associations with opening pain, joint pain, orofacial pain, limited jaw movement, clicking; TMJOA + unilateral chewing → more condylar destruction/flattening.
- **Mechanisms (mostly rat):** BMP-2/VEGF/IL-1β/TNF-α↑ and cartilage thickening on chewing side; CaN-driven Type II→I muscle-fiber shift + EMG asymmetry; substance P / CGRP↑ in trigeminal ganglion → neurogenic inflammation.
