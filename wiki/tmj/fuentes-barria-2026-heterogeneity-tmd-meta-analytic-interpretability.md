Letter to the Editor (narrative critique) of a TMD arthrocentesis SR+MA: pooling mechanistically distinct comparators (splints, physiotherapy, PRP/HA, arthroscopy, arthrocentesis variants) under I²=85–98% — plus SMD on a mm scale and mixed diagnoses — leaves the pooled estimates without a clinical referent and clinically uninterpretable.

## Summary
This is a Letter to the Editor in *The Japanese Dental Science Review* (2026), not a primary study — `confidence: narrative-review`. Fuentes-Barría critiques a meta-analysis by Valenzuela-Fuenzalida et al. on arthrocentesis effectiveness in temporomandibular disorders (TMD), arguing the analysis is internally valid in form but clinically uninterpretable in substance.

The central charge: the meta-analysis pools comparators with fundamentally different mechanisms, indications, and expected effects — occlusal splints, physiotherapy, intra-articular injections (PRP and hyaluronic acid), arthroscopic surgery, and multiple arthrocentesis variants — into a single summary estimate. This conflates distinct therapeutic questions, so the pooled effect has no clear clinical referent. The point is reinforced by extreme statistical heterogeneity (I² 85–98% across most outcomes): at this level a random-effects model does not restore interpretability, and large significant effects are at high risk of being driven by between-study dispersion rather than a consistent treatment effect, with no adequate pre-specified subgroup analysis to explain it.

Two further appraisal points: (1) using standardized mean difference (SMD) for outcomes already on a common, clinically meaningful scale (millimeters, e.g. MMO/MIO) is hard to justify and can distort effect sizes via dependence on within-study variance; (2) mixing heterogeneous diagnoses (disc displacement, osteoarthritis, arthralgia, other joint conditions) obscures applicability to specific patient groups. The takeaway is a transferable EBM lesson, not a new clinical result.

## Key Contributions
- Names extreme heterogeneity (I² 85–98%) + mechanistically incoherent comparator pooling as the reason a statistically "significant" arthrocentesis pooled estimate can be clinically meaningless.
- Flags SMD-on-millimeters as an inappropriate effect-size choice that reduces clinical interpretability.
- Flags diagnostic-entity mixing (disc displacement / OA / arthralgia) as an unaddressed source of clinical variability.
- Serves as a caution lens for reading the wiki's own high-heterogeneity arthrocentesis SR/MA pages.

## Methodology
Opinion/commentary letter — no new data, no reanalysis. Arguments are conceptual, appraising the target meta-analysis against standard meta-analytic principles (shared-effect assumption for pooling, meaning of I², need for pre-specified subgroups, mean-difference-vs-SMD selection by outcome scale).

## Results
- **Comparator pooling**: distinct mechanisms combined → pooled effect "lacks a clear clinical referent."
- **Heterogeneity**: I² 85–98%; random-effects modeling insufficient; significant large effects likely dispersion-driven.
- **Subgroups**: none robust/pre-specified → heterogeneity source unaddressed.
- **Effect metric**: SMD on mm-scale outcomes unjustified; MMO vs MIO not necessarily comparable across settings.
- **Population**: mixed diagnoses obscure subgroup applicability.
- **Conclusion**: pooled estimates may be statistical aggregation of non-comparable data, not clinically meaningful summaries; need clinically coherent comparisons, rigorous heterogeneity handling, and outcome standardization.
