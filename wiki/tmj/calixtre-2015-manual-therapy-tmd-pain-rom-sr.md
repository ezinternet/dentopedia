## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 8 RCTs (7 high PEDro quality) isolating manual therapy (MT) effects on TMD: masticatory-muscle myofascial release/massage and upper-cervical thrust/mobilisation are more effective than control (GRADE low–high) and as effective as botulinum toxin (moderate), while thoracic manipulation is not effective, for pain, maximum mouth opening (MMO) and pressure pain threshold (PPT).

## Summary

This is the foundational systematic review isolating the effect of **manual therapy (MT)** on **pain**, **maximum mouth opening (MMO)** and **pressure pain threshold (PPT)** in subjects with signs and symptoms of **temporomandibular disorder (TMD)**, restricted to randomised controlled trials. Searching five databases (MEDLINE, Cochrane, Web of Science, SciELO, EMBASE), the authors included **8 RCTs**, of which **7 were of high methodological quality** on the PEDro scale. Methodological quality (PEDro) and overall body-of-evidence quality (GRADE) were both assessed, and per-trial treatment effect sizes were computed for the three outcomes.

The central message is that effectiveness is **technique-dependent**, not uniform. Myofascial release and massage applied to the **masticatory muscles** are more effective than control (low–moderate GRADE) and **as effective as botulinum toxin injections** (moderate GRADE). **Upper cervical spine** thrust manipulation or mobilisation is more effective than control (low–high GRADE). **Thoracic manipulation** is not effective. Overall, MT technique protocols are effective with moderate-to-high evidence, but methodological heterogeneity across trial protocols frequently lowered the quality of the evidence.

## Key Contributions

- First SR to **isolate** the MT effect on MMO and pain in TMD, RCT-only.
- **Technique-stratified GRADE** rather than a single pooled "manual therapy" verdict.
- Identifies the **masticatory-muscle MT ≈ botulinum toxin** equivalence — a clinically important contrast for TMD myogenous pain management.
- Flags **thoracic manipulation as ineffective**, narrowing where MT should be directed (masticatory muscles + upper cervical spine).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review of RCTs (no meta-analysis / pooled estimate; heterogeneity precluded pooling).
- **Databases**: MEDLINE, Cochrane, Web of Science, SciELO, EMBASE.
- **Eligibility**: RCTs of MT for TMD vs other intervention, no intervention, or placebo.
- **Extraction**: two independent reviewers.
- **Risk of bias**: PEDro scale.
- **Evidence grading**: GRADE.
- **Outcomes**: treatment effect size for pain, MMO, and PPT.
- **Included**: 8 RCTs (7 high methodological quality).

## Results

| MT technique | Comparator | Effectiveness | GRADE quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Myofascial release + massage (masticatory muscles) | Control | More effective | Low to moderate |
| Myofascial release + massage (masticatory muscles) | Botulinum toxin | As effective (equivalent) | Moderate |
| Upper cervical spine thrust manipulation / mobilisation | Control | More effective | Low to high |
| Thoracic manipulation | Control | Not effective | — |
| MT technique protocols (overall) | — | Effective | Moderate to high |

- Outcomes improved (technique-dependent): pain, MMO, PPT.
- **Methodological heterogeneity** across protocols was the main driver of downgraded GRADE quality.
- Authors recommend standardised evaluations and stronger designs (validated sham MT, intention-to-treat) for future trials.
