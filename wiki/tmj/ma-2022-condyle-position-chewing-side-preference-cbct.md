## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional CBCT study (n=90 unilateral-symptom TMD patients [69 with chewing-side preference, 21 without] + 20 asymptomatic): condylar position did not strongly differentiate CSP-related from non-CSP-related TMD; TMD-without-CSP patients had significantly more posterior condyles than asymptomatic controls (57.1% vs 30.0%, p<0.05), while CSP patients only trended posterior (53.6% vs 30.0%, p=0.054) — concluding posterior condyle is not a reliable TMD indicator.

## Summary

Chewing-side preference (CSP, habitual unilateral chewing) has been proposed as one aetiology of temporomandibular disorders (TMD) and is known to alter TMJ morphology, but condylar *position* in TMD patients stratified by CSP had not been characterized. This cross-sectional CBCT study (Ma 2022, J Oral Rehabil) compared 90 TMD patients with unilateral symptoms — 69 with CSP and 21 without — against 20 asymptomatic participants, classifying each condyle as anterior, centric or posterior within the glenoid fossa from sagittal joint-space measurements.

The headline finding is essentially **negative / qualifying**: condylar position was not a strong discriminator. Asymptomatic controls distributed nearly randomly (posterior ~30%). TMD patients *without* CSP were the group that diverged most clearly — 57.1% posterior condyles vs 30.0% in controls (p<0.05). TMD patients *with* CSP did not differ significantly from either controls or non-CSP patients (preferred side 50.7% posterior, unpreferred 42.0%, all p>0.05), and their symptomatic-joint posterior shift only trended (53.6% vs 30.0%, p=0.054). Within patients, symptomatic vs asymptomatic joints showed no significant positional difference.

The clinical bottom line stated by the authors: **posterior condyle position should not be viewed as an indicator of TMD**, and condylar position cannot reliably separate CSP-related from non-CSP-related TMD. This is the primary-data CBCT study that the [[tmj/zheng-2023-unilateral-chewing-tmj-structure-tmd-review]] narrative review draws on for the CSP–condyle relationship, and it tempers the older classical literature (and screening habits) that treat posterior condyle position as a diagnostic marker of TMD or internal derangement.

## Key Contributions

- First analysis of condylar position in TMD patients explicitly stratified by chewing-side preference, filling the gap that "condylar position in TMD patients with CSP is unknown."
- Direct intra-patient comparison of preferred vs unpreferred chewing-side condyles, and symptomatic vs asymptomatic joints.
- Evidence-based pushback on posterior condyle position as a TMD diagnostic indicator — finds it neither specific to TMD nor able to distinguish CSP-related from non-CSP-related disease.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional comparative CBCT study (case-control structure). 90 unilateral-symptom TMD patients (69 CSP, 21 non-CSP) + 20 asymptomatic participants imaged by CBCT. Condyle classified anterior/centric/posterior from sagittal joint-space measurements. Intergroup (CSP vs non-CSP vs asymptomatic) and intra-group (preferred vs unpreferred side; symptomatic vs asymptomatic joint) comparisons.

## Results

| Group / joint | Posterior condyle % | vs asymptomatic (30.0%) |
|---|---|---|
| Asymptomatic controls | ~30.0% (near-random A/C/P) | — |
| TMD without CSP | 57.1% | significant, p<0.05 |
| TMD with CSP — preferred chewing side | 50.7% | NS, p>0.05 |
| TMD with CSP — unpreferred side | 42.0% | NS, p>0.05 |
| TMD with CSP — symptomatic joints | 53.6% | trend only, p=0.054 |

- Non-CSP patients: significantly more posterior condyles in symptomatic joints than asymptomatic participants (p<0.05).
- No significant symptomatic-vs-asymptomatic joint difference within either CSP or non-CSP patients.
- **Conclusion:** condylar position is not a strong indicator to differentiate CSP-related from non-CSP-related TMD; posterior condyle is not a reliable indicator of TMD.
