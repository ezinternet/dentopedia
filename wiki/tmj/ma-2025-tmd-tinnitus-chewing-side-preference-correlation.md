## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional study (n=285 TMD patients) finding chewing-side preference is significantly more common in TMD patients with tinnitus (90.7% vs 76.0%, χ²=4.613, P<0.05), tinnitus side is ipsilateral to the habitual chewing side (χ²=7.282, P<0.05), and tinnitus correlates with TMJ-area pain (93.0% vs 73.6%) — but not with clicking, limited opening, or disc-displacement type.

## Summary

A single-center retrospective cross-sectional analysis of 285 TMD patients (199 F / 86 M; mean age 32.3 y) from the Chinese PLA General Hospital (Dec 2020–May 2024), split into a tinnitus group (n=43) and a non-tinnitus group (n=242). All patients had TMD confirmed by CBCT + TMJ MRI; tinnitus cases were ENT-confirmed as **unexplained** tinnitus. Chewing-side preference was assessed by a 2-min gum test (≥3/4 sampled observations same side) plus interview. Groups did not differ in age or sex. The study tests whether chewing-side preference and specific TMD symptoms associate with tinnitus, and whether tinnitus is side-concordant with the habitual chewing side.

## Key Contributions

- Chewing-side preference is significantly **more prevalent among tinnitus patients** (90.7% vs 76.0%) and positively correlated with tinnitus.
- **Ipsilateral pattern:** tinnitus side aligns with the habitual chewing side (left tinnitus → mostly left chewers; right tinnitus → mostly right chewers).
- Among TMD symptoms, only **TMJ-area pain** is correlated with tinnitus; clicking and limited opening are not.
- Disc-displacement status (laterality and reducibility) does **not** differ between tinnitus and non-tinnitus groups.
- Clinical implication: consider chewing habits in the workup of unexplained tinnitus; correcting unilateral chewing is proposed as an adjunct preventive/therapeutic target.

## Methodology

- Design: single-center retrospective cross-sectional (PLA General Hospital IRB S2020-327-01).
- n=285 TMD patients; tinnitus n=43, non-tinnitus n=242.
- Inclusion: TMD signs + CBCT/MRI support; ENT-confirmed unexplained tinnitus (with/without chewing-side preference).
- Exclusion: no TMD signs; systemic organic disease; MRI-incompatible; poor MRI quality; moderate–severe anxiety/depression or on anxiolytic/antidepressant treatment.
- Imaging: 1.5 T Siemens Novus, TMJ surface coil; closed/open-mouth oblique-sagittal & coronal PDWI + fat-suppressed T2WI, plane perpendicular to condylar long axis.
- Chewing-side test: 2-min gum chewing, interrupted every 15 s, 7 observations; ≥3 same-side at observations 1/3/5/7 = preference; corroborated by interview. Classified left / right / none.
- Statistics: SPSS 25.0; χ² and Spearman correlation; t-test/ANOVA for quantitative; P<0.05 significant.

## Results

| Comparison | Tinnitus (n=43) | Non-tinnitus (n=242) | Stat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chewing-side preference | 90.70% (39/43) | 76.03% (184/242) | χ²=4.613, P<0.05 |
| TMJ-area pain | 93.02% (40/43) | 73.55% (178/242) | P<0.05 |
| Joint clicking | 76.74% (33/43) | 83.47% (202/242) | P>0.05 (NS) |
| Limited opening | 48.84% (21/43) | 47.11% (114/242) | P>0.05 (NS) |

**Side concordance (39 tinnitus + chewing-side preference patients):**
- Left tinnitus (n=22): left chewers 54.55% (12/22) vs right 45.45% (10/22).
- Right tinnitus (n=11): right chewers 81.82% (9/11) vs left 18.18% (2/11).
- Bilateral tinnitus (n=6): left chewers 83.33% (5/6) vs right 16.67% (1/6).
- χ²=7.282, P<0.05; positive correlation between tinnitus side and chewing side.

**Disc displacement (NS):**
- By laterality (none / unilateral / bilateral): tinnitus 16.28% / 34.88% / 48.84% vs non-tinnitus 18.18% / 38.02% / 43.80%, all P>0.05.
- By reducibility (none / reducible / irreducible): tinnitus 16.28% / 34.88% / 48.84% vs non-tinnitus 18.18% / 28.93% / 52.89%, all P>0.05.

**Conclusion:** TMD with chewing-side preference may be a contributing factor to unexplained tinnitus; tinnitus is correlated with TMJ-area pain.

**Caveats:** retrospective single-center design; very small side-stratified subgroups (n=6–22); correlation only, no causation; mechanism (auriculotemporal nerve compression, inflammation via petrotympanic/petrosquamous fissure, neurovascular changes) hypothesized.
