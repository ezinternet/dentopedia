Narrative review (Part 2, treatment) from an orofacial-pain unit concluding that, among the many trigger-point treatments, the spray-and-stretch technique and direct trigger-point injection are the most effective options — and that chronic cases need psychosocial intervention and carry a poorer prognosis, underscoring early diagnosis/treatment.

## Summary

The second part of Vázquez-Delgado et al.'s two-part orofacial myofascial-pain review focuses on **differential diagnosis and treatment**. Its notable claim — from a university orofacial-pain unit — is selective rather than encyclopedic: among the numerous trigger-point treatments, **spray-and-stretch (분사신장) and direct trigger-point injection are singled out as the most effective**. This is the strongest endorsement of spray-and-stretch among the orofacial reviews the wiki holds, and it pairs with [[tmj/koole-2019-spray-stretch-technique-mouth-opening]]'s direct mouth-opening measurement.

The review also stresses two clinical realities: a **broad differential** (many conditions mimic trigger-point myofascial pain, so careful anamnesis/examination is required), and a **prognostic penalty for chronicity** — mood disorders and anxiety are common in chronic patients and predict poorer outcomes, which is why the authors argue for early diagnosis and treatment.

> Abstract-only ingest. The comparative "most effective" judgment is the authors' synthesis, not pooled data; treat it as expert opinion, not graded evidence.

## Key Contributions

- A selective endorsement of **spray-and-stretch + injection** as the most effective trigger-point treatments in the orofacial context.
- Explicit attention to **differential diagnosis** and to **psychosocial prognosis** in chronic myofascial pain.

## Methodology

- Narrative literature review (Part 2 of a series; Part 1 = epidemiology/etiopathogenesis). No systematic search or quality appraisal.

## Results

- Diagnosis requires good anamnesis + clinical exploration against a broad differential.
- Spray-and-stretch and targeted trigger-point injection = most effective options.
- Chronic cases: high incidence of mood/anxiety disorders → poorer prognosis → early intervention emphasized.
