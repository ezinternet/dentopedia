## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 81 in vitro studies (9 in synthesis) shows peroxide bleaching reduces enamel microhardness by ~11% (ROM = 0.89; 95% CI 0.84–0.94), with exposure time — not concentration — as the main moderator; the reduction is unlikely clinically meaningful and does not support strict concentration-based regulation.

## Summary

This 2026 SR+MA from Semmelweis University (PROSPERO CRD42023472326) addresses whether hydrogen or carbamide peroxide bleaching clinically damages enamel, using ISO 28,399-aligned eligibility criteria. Of 81 eligible in vitro studies, only 9 entered quantitative synthesis after strict ISO alignment. A multilevel random-effects meta-analysis found a pooled ROM of 0.89 (11% microhardness reduction, p < 0.01; moderate heterogeneity I² = 50.8%). Exposure time was a significant moderator (10× increase → 6.8% Knoop hardness decrease), though this became non-significant after exclusion of one influential study. Peroxide concentration was not a significant moderator. The authors conclude that current evidence does not support strict regulatory concentration limits (e.g., EU 6% cap) based on enamel hardness data alone.

## Key Contributions

1. **ROM just below 10% clinical threshold**: The 11% pooled reduction approaches but does not exceed the commonly accepted 10% clinical significance threshold; outlier sensitivity is high
2. **Concentration irrelevant to hardness**: Peroxide concentration was not a significant meta-regression predictor — undermining the regulatory rationale for concentration-based limits
3. **Exposure time is the key lever**: Longer contact times drive more hardness reduction; this aligns with clinical advice to limit contact duration rather than concentration
4. **ISO 28,399 alignment gap**: Only 9/81 studies met strict inclusion criteria, revealing a major methodological quality gap in the field
5. **Time-course paradox**: Larger changes observed at lower concentrations — possibly because low-concentration products require longer exposure times

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review + meta-analysis (multilevel random-effects, log-transformed ROM)
- **Search**: 4 databases, PRISMA 2020 + Cochrane Handbook
- **Outcomes**: Enamel Vickers and Knoop microhardness before and after bleaching
- **Moderators tested**: Peroxide concentration, exposure time, indentation load, dwell time (meta-regression)
- **Evidence registration**: PROSPERO CRD42023472326

## Results

| Outcome | Value |
|---|---|
| Eligible studies | 81 |
| Studies in quantitative synthesis | 9 (ISO 28,399 aligned) |
| Pooled ROM | 0.89 (95% CI: 0.84–0.94; p < 0.01) |
| I² | 50.8% (moderate) |
| Exposure time as moderator | 10× increase → 6.8% Knoop decrease (p = 0.044; outlier-sensitive) |
| Concentration as moderator | Not significant |
| Clinical threshold | ROM < 0.90 (10% reduction) |

**Clinical interpretation**: Bleaching causes statistically detectable but small enamel microhardness reduction that is unlikely clinically meaningful. Current regulatory limits (EU 6% HP cap) are not supported by enamel hardness data. Further ISO 28,399-compliant RCTs are needed.
