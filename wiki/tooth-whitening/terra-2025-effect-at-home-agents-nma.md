## One-line Summary

Bayesian NMA of 53 RCTs (81 eligible) comparing at-home bleaching agents and concentrations found most concentrations equally efficacious — only the lowest (CP 5%, HP 2–4%) and highest (CP 15–37%, HP 14–16%) extremes differ significantly; overall quality of evidence is low.

## Summary

This 2025 SR+NMA from the Loguercio/Reis group at State University of Ponta Grossa, Brazil (PROSPERO CRD42021265220) is the most comprehensive comparison of at-home bleaching agents to date. Of 81 eligible RCTs, 53 entered a Bayesian NMA covering CP (5%–37%) and HP (2%–16%). The main finding: most concentrations are clinically equivalent for efficacy (ΔE and ΔSGU). Only extreme concentrations — the very lowest (CP 5%, HP 2–4%) vs the higher tiers (CP 15–17% or above, HP 14–16%) — showed statistically significant differences. Quality of evidence is low (GRADE), driven by high risk of bias in most included RCTs. This means that within the standard at-home range (10–22% CP, 3–8% HP), product choice should be guided by sensitivity profile and patient compliance rather than expected whitening difference.

## Key Contributions

1. **First Bayesian NMA of all at-home bleaching concentrations**: Allows simultaneous ranking across the entire CP and HP concentration spectrum
2. **Equivalence of intermediate concentrations confirmed**: Products in the 10–22% CP / 3–8% HP range are interchangeable for efficacy
3. **Extreme concentrations differ**: CP 5% and HP 2–4% are inferior; HP 14–16% and CP 35–37% are superior — but only at extremes
4. **Low QoE limits confidence**: High risk of bias pervades this literature; conclusions provisional
5. **Practical implication**: Agent selection for standard at-home bleaching should prioritize sensitivity reduction, cost, and compliance over concentration optimization

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review + Bayesian network meta-analysis
- **Databases**: 7 (PubMed, Cochrane, LILACS/BBO, SCOPUS, Web of Science, EMBASE, grey literature)
- **Eligible / NMA-included**: 81 / 53 RCTs
- **Primary outcomes**: ΔE (spectrophotometric), ΔSGU (shade guide units)
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane tool 2.0 — high RoB in most studies
- **Quality of evidence**: GRADE — Low overall
- **Registration**: PROSPERO CRD42021265220

## Results

**Significant ΔE differences (spectrophotometric):**

| Comparison | Mean Diff (95% CrI) |
|---|---|
| CP 5% vs CP 15–17% | −3.36 (−6.53 to −0.22) |
| HP 2–4% vs CP 15–17% | −1.85 (−3.63 to −0.08) |
| CP 5% vs CP 35–37% | −4.23 (−8.21 to −0.22) |

**Significant ΔSGU differences (shade guide):**

| Comparison | Mean Diff (95% CrI) |
|---|---|
| HP 14–16% vs CP 5% | +4.66 (0.27 to 9.01) |
| HP 14–16% vs HP 2–4% | +4.06 (0.74 to 7.46) |

All other pairwise comparisons: not statistically significant.

**Clinical decision guide:**
- **Standard at-home range (10–22% CP, 3–8% HP)**: All concentrations equivalent — choose based on sensitivity and compliance
- **Very low concentration (CP 5%, HP 2–4%)**: Inferior efficacy — appropriate only if sensitivity is the dominant concern
- **High concentration (HP 14–16%, CP 35–37%)**: Superior efficacy but typically prescription-only; higher sensitivity risk
