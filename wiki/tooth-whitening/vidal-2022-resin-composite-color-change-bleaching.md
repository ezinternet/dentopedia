## One-line Summary

SR+MA with meta-regression (23 in vitro studies) — resin-based composites measurably change color after peroxide bleaching, but the change is not clinically significant; measured ΔE is driven by storage time, measuring device, and background color, NOT by the bleaching agent or application time.

## Summary

This page covers the "what does bleaching do to existing restorations?" question — a routine pre-bleaching counseling point. The reassuring conclusion is that peroxide bleaching does **not clinically meaningfully** alter the color of resin composites; the small ΔE detected in lab studies is largely a methodological artifact (storage time, spectrophotometer vs visual, background). The flip side, well known clinically and consistent with this: bleaching does not *lighten* existing composites, so shade-matched restorations may look darker than the newly whitened surrounding enamel and may need replacement after bleaching stabilizes (typically ~2 weeks). In vitro evidence only, so it speaks to material chemistry, not in-mouth marginal aging.

## Key Contributions

- Meta-analytic answer to whether bleaching discolors composite restorations: measurable but not clinically significant.
- Meta-regression isolates methodological confounders from any true bleaching effect.

## Methodology

- MEDLINE/PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science; English in vitro studies reporting CIELAB ΔE of RBC after HP/CP bleaching.
- 1335 → 66 full-text → 23 included; meta-regression on covariates.

## Results

- RBC color change after bleaching: present but **not clinically significant**.
- Significant moderators of measured ΔE: storage time (p≤0.01), color-measuring device (p≤0.01), background color (p≤0.01).
- Non-significant: bleaching agent, application time (p>0.01).
