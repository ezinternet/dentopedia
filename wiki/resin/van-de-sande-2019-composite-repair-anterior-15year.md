## Summary
A practice-based retrospective 15-year study from a private dental practice (1994вҖ“2009), including 144 patients with 634 anterior direct composite restorations (Class III, Class IV, and direct veneers). The study analyzed survival separately based on whether repair was counted as failure or not. When repair was not considered failure, Class III/IV showed 69% 15-year survival (AFR 2.4%), versus 64% (AFR 2.9%) when repair was counted as failure. The study supports composite repair as a suitable and conservative alternative to replacement.

## Key Contributions
- **Repair vs replacement**: treating repair as non-failure вҶ’ 69% survival, 2.4% AFR at 15 years
- Direct veneers: 10-year survival 74% (repairвү failure) vs 38% (repair=failure)
- Upper jaw restorations failed significantly more than lower (p<0.01)
- Central incisors had higher failure risk than canines (p<0.01)
- Repair benefits: tooth structure conservation, reduced chair time, reduced patient anxiety

## Methodology
- Retrospective cohort; single private practice; 1994вҖ“2009
- 144 patients, 634 restorations (Class III/IV and direct veneers)
- Kaplan-Meier survival analysis; Cox regression; log-rank test
- Two analytical scenarios: repair as failure vs repair as non-failure
