## Summary

Metwaly et al. ran a double-blind, two-arm RCT (n=240) in endodontically treated lower molars with Class II cavities and moderate remaining structure, comparing polyethylene-fiber-reinforced bulk-fill resin composite against plain bulk-fill resin composite. Blinded assessors applied modified USPHS criteria at baseline, 6, 12, and 24 months. Over two years there was no significant between-group difference on any parameter, and both arms held Alpha scores for retention, gross fracture, and secondary caries throughout. The practical takeaway is twofold: plain bulk-fill performs satisfactorily even in structurally compromised ETT molars, and the added step/cost of polyethylene-fiber reinforcement did not yield a measurable clinical advantage at 2 years. This reinforces the favorable bulk-fill picture from the anchor meta-analysis and extends it into a high-risk ETT indication. (Note: this is fiber-reinforced bulk-fill vs plain bulk-fill — both arms are bulk-fill, not an incremental-vs-bulk-fill comparison.)

## Key Contributions

- RCT evidence for bulk-fill composite performance in endodontically treated molars.
- Direct test of polyethylene-fiber reinforcement benefit; finds none at 24 months.
- Supports a simpler restorative protocol (bulk-fill without fiber) for moderate-structure ETT molars.

## Methodology

Two groups of 120. Group 1: polyethylene-fiber-reinforced bulk-fill; Group 2: plain bulk-fill. Participants 18–55 y, ETT lower molars, Class II with 1–2 marginal ridges lost, ≥2 mm wall thickness. Modified USPHS scoring by two blinded assessors at 0/6/12/24 months. Mann-Whitney U, Friedman, Nemenyi post hoc; α=0.05; R. ClinicalTrials.gov NCT05180903.

## Results

- No significant between-group difference on any USPHS parameter at any time point.
- Alpha scores for retention, gross fracture, and secondary caries at all intervals.
- Both restoration types satisfactory over 24 months.
