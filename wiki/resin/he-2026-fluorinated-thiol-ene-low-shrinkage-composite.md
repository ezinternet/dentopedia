## One-line Summary
In-vitro + in-vivo (rat) study of a novel fluorinated methacrylate-thiol-ene composite (DST-1) using thiol-ene step-growth polymerization to reduce shrinkage stress, showing significantly lower microleakage, reduced streptococcal adhesion via low surface free energy, good wear/polish, and no adverse systemic/tissue response in Wistar rats.

## Summary
This Journal of Dentistry (2026) study from Sun Yat-sen University tackles two of the main drivers of resin-restoration failure at once — polymerization shrinkage and biofilm-driven secondary caries — with a single fluorinated methacrylate-thiol-ene composite (DST). The thiol-ene step-growth polymerization delays the gel point, which reduces shrinkage stress; fluorination lowers surface free energy, which reduces bacterial adhesion. DST-1 showed significantly lower microleakage in modified Class II restorations, reduced total and streptococcal adhesion (without altering biofilm metabolic activity), enhanced wear resistance, lower surface roughness, and improved polishability/hydrophobicity, at the cost of a modest reduction in tensile strength. In-vivo Wistar-rat testing found no adverse systemic or tissue responses. The companion precursor paper (He et al., J Mater Chem B 2024, DOI 10.1039/d3tb01556d) established that adding the thiol-ene system (0-40 wt%) lowers volumetric shrinkage and shrinkage stress without sacrificing degree of conversion.

## Key Contributions
- Combines low shrinkage stress (thiol-ene step-growth, delayed gel point) with bacterial anti-adhesion (fluorination, low surface free energy) in one material
- Connects the chain: low shrinkage stress → better marginal sealing (lower microleakage) → reduced secondary-caries risk
- Reduced total + streptococcal adhesion without affecting biofilm metabolic activity
- Adds in-vivo (rat) hematology/biochemistry/histopathology biocompatibility evidence — uncommon for experimental composites
- Trade-off documented: modest ↓ tensile strength but improved polishing and hydrophobicity

## Methodology
DST prepared via a fluorinated methacrylate-thiol-ene step-growth system. Anti-adhesion assessed with saliva-derived human dental plaque biofilms (CFU, CLSM, SEM, crystal violet, MTT). Microleakage in modified Class II restorations (rhodamine B + CLSM). Physicochemical: wear resistance, surface roughness, contact angle, surface free energy, diametral tensile strength. In-vivo biocompatibility in Wistar rats (hematology, biochemistry, histopathology).

## Results
- Significantly reduced total and streptococcal adhesion vs conventional composites; biofilm metabolic activity unaffected
- DST-1: significantly lower microleakage scores; enhanced wear resistance; lower surface roughness; reduced surface free energy
- Modest ↓ tensile strength; improved polishing + hydrophobicity
- No adverse systemic or tissue responses in vivo
- Precursor (2024): thiol-ene addition reduced volumetric shrinkage + shrinkage stress with no conversion-degree loss
