## One-line Summary
Systematic review and meta-analysis (62 in vitro studies, 58 meta-analyzed) of composition strategies for reducing polymerization shrinkage stress in resin-based materials found resin matrix modification contributes most to stress reduction, while alternative photoinitiators alone showed no significant benefit (p=0.29); SDR (Stress Decreasing Resin) chemistry highlighted as a validated approach.

## Summary
This SR+MA (J Mech Behav Biomed Mater 2018, Vol. 82) systematically reviewed the literature up to December 2016 across 7 databases to identify composition strategies for reducing polymerization shrinkage stress in resin-based dental materials. Only direct testing studies were included. Of 6,113 eligible articles, 62 were included in qualitative analysis and 58 in the meta-analysis. Composition strategies were grouped into three subgroups: filler phase modification, coupling agent modification, and resin matrix modification. Random-effects models were used (α = 0.05). Resin matrix modification showed the largest contribution to stress reduction. The only subgroup without statistical benefit was "alternative photoinitiators" (p = 0.29). SDR (Stress Decreasing Resin) technology was highlighted as a validated approach combining optimized matrix and filler to reduce and control stress.

## Key Contributions
- First SR+MA specifically on composition strategies (not technique) for shrinkage stress control in resin-based materials
- Three-subgroup analysis: filler phase, coupling agent, resin matrix — resin matrix modification is most impactful
- Demonstrated that photoinitiator modification alone (alternative photoinitiators) does NOT reduce shrinkage stress (p = 0.29) despite improving depth of cure
- SDR chemistry (urethane dimethacrylate-based, Dentsply Sirona) validated — optimizes both organic matrix and filler phase
- Established mechanistic foundation explaining why low-shrinkage and bulk-fill materials reduce stress: primarily through resin matrix modification
- The most deleterious factor is not volumetric shrinkage itself, but the stress generated at the bonded tooth-restoration interface in a confined cavity

## Methodology
- **Design**: SR+MA (multiple subgroup meta-analyses); PRISMA Statement
- **Databases**: PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, SciELO, LILACS, IBECS, BBO (7 databases)
- **Search cutoff**: December 2016; no year-of-publication restriction
- **Inclusion**: In vitro studies; direct testing of polymerization shrinkage stress only
- **Exclusions**: Reviews, pilot studies, indirect methods (microleakage, cuspal deflection, FEA, mathematical models)
- **Studies**: 6,113 eligible → 62 qualitative analysis → 58 meta-analysis
- **Composition subgroups**: (1) Filler phase, (2) Coupling agent, (3) Resin matrix
- **Statistics**: Random-effects models; α = 0.05; global + subgroup analyses

## Results
| Composition Strategy | Significant Stress Reduction? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Resin matrix modification | Yes | Largest contribution |
| Filler phase modification | Yes | Significant |
| Coupling agent modification | Yes | Significant |
| Alternative photoinitiators | No (p = 0.29) | Only non-significant subgroup |

Clinical consequences of uncontrolled shrinkage stress: restoration-post debonding, cuspal deflection, microleakage, post-operative hypersensitivity.

SDR chemistry: combines optimized urethane-dimethacrylate matrix + filler → reduced and controlled shrinkage stress → forms basis of flowable bulk-fill base materials.

Low-shrinkage materials (silorane, ormocer) and bulk-fill materials primarily act through resin matrix modification mechanism — consistent with this SR+MA's main finding.
