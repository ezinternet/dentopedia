## One-line Summary

Systematic review + meta-analysis of 9 RCTs finding no significant difference in the clinical failure rate of indirect single-tooth restorations cemented with self-adhesive resin cement versus conventional cements — self-adhesive resin cement is a technique-simplifying, recommendable luting option.

## 한줄요약

RCT 9편의 체계적 문헌고찰 + 메타분석 — 간접 단일치 수복물을 자가접착 레진시멘트(self-adhesive resin cement)로 합착해도 기존 시멘트(conventional cement) 대비 임상 실패율에 유의차가 없어, 술식이 단순한 자가접착 레진시멘트를 임상적으로 권장할 수 있다.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1016/j.prosdent.2024.04.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.prosdent.2024.04.027)), this PRISMA systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42020215577) asked whether the cement class chosen for luting changes how often indirect single-tooth restorations clinically fail. Cementation is a high-leverage, error-prone step, and self-adhesive resin cements were introduced specifically to collapse the multi-step etch/prime/bond sequence into one application, reducing operator error. Pooling 9 randomized clinical trials by hazard ratio, the authors found that 8 of 9 trials reported no significant difference in failure between self-adhesive and conventional cements, and the combined estimate was likewise nonsignificant. The single discordant trial reported a higher failure rate for **single-tooth ceramic crowns** luted with self-adhesive resin cement. The clinical bottom line: for everyday indirect single-tooth work, self-adhesive resin cement carries a failure risk similar to conventional cements while being simpler to apply, so it can be recommended — with the caveat that high-esthetic/low-strength ceramic crowns may warrant the more controlled adhesive protocols suggested elsewhere.

This is a **clinical-outcome** complement to the wiki's mostly in-vitro adhesive-cement literature: where [[resin-bonding/borouziniat-2024-self-adhesive-vs-etch-resin-cement-zirconia-sr-ma]] showed self-adhesive ≈ self-etch on **bench bond strength to zirconia**, Alvarenga shows the non-inferiority carries through to **restoration survival** in patients. ## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA restricted to **RCTs** comparing self-adhesive vs conventional cements on the **clinical failure rate** of indirect single-tooth restorations — a clinical-survival question, not a bench bond-strength one.
- 8/9 trials and the pooled estimate show **no significant failure-rate difference** between cement classes.
- Supplies clinical-evidence backing to use self-adhesive resin cement as a **technique-simplifying** default for routine indirect single-tooth restorations.
- Flags one exception signal: a trial with **higher failure for ceramic crowns** under self-adhesive cement, cautioning against blanket generalization across all ceramics.

## Methodology

- Design: systematic review + meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials; PRISMA-guided; prospectively registered (PROSPERO CRD42020215577).
- Search: PubMed, Cochrane Library, EMBASE, Web of Science, LILACS, plus clinicaltrials.gov (grey literature).
- PICO: P = adults indicated for indirect restorations; I = self-adhesive resin cement; C = conventional cement; O = failure rates.
- Risk of bias: Cochrane RoB2. Certainty of evidence: GRADE.
- Synthesis: pooled hazard ratios and standard errors (reported or estimated); 9 RCTs in qualitative and quantitative analysis.

## Results

- 8 of 9 RCTs: **nonsignificant** difference in failure rate between self-adhesive resin cement and conventional cements.
- 1 of 9 RCTs: **significantly higher** failure for single-tooth **ceramic crowns** luted with self-adhesive resin cement (lone discordant result).
- Pooled across all studies: **nonsignificant** difference → self-adhesive resin cement recommendable for indirect single-tooth restorations with failure risk similar to conventional cement.
- Magnitude/precision: exact pooled HR, 95% CI, and GRADE certainty not recoverable from the abstract (full text not retrieved).
