## One-line Summary
In vitro bond-strength study showing saliva contamination of a silane-treated ceramic surface for more than 1 second significantly reduces ceramic–resin cement bond strength (p<0.05); pre-try-in silane application, or recleaning plus silane reapplication after contamination under rubber-dam isolation, is required to protect the bond.

# Silane Application Timing and Saliva Contamination: Bond Strength Impact

**Source**: [[resin-bonding/unknown-2009-effect-of-silane-treatment-timing]]  
**Year**: 2009 | **Study Type**: In Vitro Bond Strength | **Critical Variable**: Moisture contamination

## Clinical Problem Addressed

### Try-In Contamination Risk
When trying in ceramic restorations before final cementation, **saliva exposure compromises the ceramic surface**, weakening subsequent bonding if silane not reapplied properly.

**Real-World Scenario**: Remove restoration after try-in → patient salivates on preparation → attempt to cement without proper silane reapplication → bond failure years later

## Key Finding: Saliva Contamination Threshold

### Critical Timing: >1 Second Exposure
**Saliva exposure for more than 1 second significantly reduces bond strength** (p < 0.05) between ceramic and resin cement.

**Interpretation**: Even brief try-in where patient saliva contacts ceramic = meaningful bond strength reduction if proper moisture control not maintained afterward.

## Silane Protection Mechanism

### How Silane Protects
**Silane coupling agents** (bifunctional molecules) form protective barriers:
- **Silanol groups** (Si-OH) chemically bond to ceramic (inorganic phase)
- **Organic functional groups** bond to resin cement (organic phase)
- **Hydrolysis and condensation** creates siloxane bridges (Si-O-Si)
- **Water-repellent coating** blocks salivary protein penetration

### Why Saliva Damages Bond
**Salivary components** without silane protection:
- Proteinaceous peptides compete with resin for ceramic surface
- Enzymes degrade interface over time
- Organic matrix formation interferes with resin-ceramic contact
- Moisture promotes hydrolytic degradation of resin-ceramic bond

## Clinical Protocol: Silane Timing Strategy

### Option 1: Pre-Try-In Silane (Preferred)
1. **Apply silane to ceramic** before try-in
2. **Air dry thoroughly** (burn off alcohol)
3. **Proceed with try-in** (minimal risk if brief)
4. **Remove restoration** after try-in
5. **No additional silane needed** if try-in time brief and dry maintained
6. **Proceed to final cementation** with confidence

**Advantage**: Silane protection applied when surface pristine  
**Requirement**: Maintain dry conditions during try-in

### Option 2: Post-Try-In Reapplication (Necessary if Contaminated)
1. **Remove restoration after try-in**
2. **Clean ceramic surface** thoroughly
   - Pumice or rubber polishing cup (remove saliva)
   - Alcohol rinse (remove organic residue)
   - Air dry completely
3. **Reapply silane** to cleaned surface
4. **Air dry thoroughly** (alcohol evaporation critical)
5. **Proceed to final cementation** immediately

**Requirement**: Rigorous decontamination + prompt reapplication

### Option 3: Silane During Final Cementation (Acceptable if Moisture-Controlled)
1. **Use rubber dam isolation** before removing temporary restoration
2. **Clean ceramic** in isolated field
3. **Apply silane** in moisture-free environment
4. **Proceed to seating** immediately after air drying

**Requirement**: Complete moisture isolation throughout cementation

## Moisture Control is Non-Negotiable

### Critical Success Factor
**Moisture control after silane application** determines bond success:
- Saliva exposure after silane application = bond compromise
- Even brief intraoral exposure reduces strength
- Reapplication often necessary after contamination
- Final cementation field must remain dry throughout process

### Technique Priorities
1. **Rubber dam placement** before seating (eliminates moisture)
2. **Silane reapplication** if restoration handled without isolation
3. **Immediate cementation** after silane (drying complete)
4. **Avoid delay** between silane application and cement application
5. **Protect from oral fluids** during initial set (minimize moisture exposure)

## Clinical Decision Framework

**Try-in Management Decision Tree**:
```
Ceramic try-in indicated
├─ Will try-in be BRIEF (under 30 seconds) and DRY?
│  ├─ YES → Apply silane before try-in, proceed
│  └─ NO → Skip pre-try-in silane
│
After try-in removal
├─ Was preparation exposed to saliva during try-in?
│  ├─ NO (dry try-in) → Proceed with final cementation (no reapplication)
│  └─ YES (wet try-in) → MUST clean and reapply silane before cementation
│
Final cementation
├─ Use rubber dam isolation (MANDATORY)
├─ Reapply silane if not recently applied
├─ Dry thoroughly
├─ Seat restoration
└─ Protect from oral fluids during initial set
```

## Evidence-Based Recommendations

**Current Best Practice**:
1. **Pre-try-in silane application** when possible (protected surface application)
2. **Minimize try-in time** with casual moisture exposure
3. **Prompt reapplication and recleaning** if contamination occurs
4. **Rubber dam isolation for final cementation** (non-negotiable)
5. **Monitor long-term** restorations with compromised application histories

**Realistic Perspective**: Even suboptimal protocol (post-contamination reapplication under rubber dam) likely achieves acceptable clinical success if moisture control during cementation is absolute.

## Evidence Grade
**In Vitro Laboratory Study** — Demonstrates silane vulnerability to moisture and saliva contamination; clinical long-term outcome data comparing protocols limited
