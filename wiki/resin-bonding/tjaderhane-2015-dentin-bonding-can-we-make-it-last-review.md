Narrative review (abstract-only) arguing that long-term dentin bond loss is driven by endogenous collagenolytic enzymes (MMPs, cysteine cathepsins) and adhesive hydrophilicity, and that preserving collagen-matrix integrity is the key to durable bonding.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.2341/14-095-BL](https://doi.org/10.2341/14-095-BL)), contemporary dental adhesive systems rely on formation of the hybrid layer — a biocomposite of dentin collagen and polymerized resin adhesive — which usually achieves at least reasonable integrity and high immediate bond strength. However, loss of dentin-bonded interface integrity and bond strength is commonly seen after aging both in vitro and in vivo. This review (ingested abstract-only; full text not retrieved) attributes degradation to two parallel causes: (1) endogenous collagenolytic enzymes — matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and cysteine cathepsins — that drive time-dependent loss of hybrid-layer collagen, and (2) the hydrophilic nature of adhesives, which produces insufficient resin impregnation, phase separation, and low polymerization rate, all reducing bonded-interface longevity. The central thesis is that preservation of collagen-matrix integrity by inhibiting endogenous dentin proteases is key to improving durability, and the review surveys three countermeasure families: enzyme inhibitors, increased collagen resistance to enzymatic degradation, and elimination of interfacial water.

Clinically: hybrid-layer bonds fail over time not only from water-driven hydrolysis but from the tooth's own enzymes digesting the collagen scaffold — so durability strategies (e.g., protease-inhibiting agents such as chlorhexidine, collagen cross-linkers, and keeping the interface dry) target the collagen matrix, not just the resin.

## Key Contributions

- Frames the hybrid layer as a collagen + resin biocomposite with strong immediate but poor aged bond strength.
- Names MMPs and cysteine cathepsins as the endogenous proteases responsible for time-dependent hybrid-layer collagen loss.
- Identifies adhesive hydrophilicity (insufficient impregnation, phase separation, low polymerization rate) as a co-equal degradation axis.
- Establishes protease inhibition / collagen preservation as the key principle for durable bonding.

## Methodology

Narrative review (abstract-only ingestion) synthesizing the principles, current status, and future of techniques designed to prevent loss of the hybrid layer and bond strength. No meta-analysis; no risk-of-bias appraisal. Detail beyond the PubMed abstract was not retrieved.

## Results

- **Degradation mechanism 1 — endogenous enzymes:** MMPs and cysteine cathepsins cause time-dependent loss of hybrid-layer collagen (observed in vitro and in vivo).
- **Degradation mechanism 2 — hydrophilicity:** adhesive hydrophilicity yields insufficient resin impregnation, phase separation, and low polymerization rate, reducing bonded-interface longevity.
- **Countermeasure 1 — enzyme inhibitors:** applied separately or incorporated into adhesive resins.
- **Countermeasure 2 — collagen resistance:** increase collagen's resistance to enzymatic degradation (e.g., cross-linking).
- **Countermeasure 3 — water elimination:** remove water from the interface to slow or eliminate hydrolytic loss of hybrid-layer components.
