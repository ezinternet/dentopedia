## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Buonocore Lecture, Operative Dentistry 2015) demonstrating that dentin bond durability is undermined by endogenous MMPs and cysteine cathepsins degrading hybrid-layer collagen, and cataloging five countermeasure families: enzyme inhibitors (esp. chlorhexidine), chemical bonding (10-MDP), collagen cross-linkers, ethanol-wet bonding, and biomimetic remineralization.

## Summary

Contemporary dental adhesive systems form the hybrid layer — a 5–10 µm biocomposite of demineralized dentin collagen and polymerized resin — which achieves high immediate bond strength but degrades over time, both in vitro and in vivo. Tjäderhane's 2015 Buonocore Lecture (Operative Dentistry 40:4–18) is the canonical framing paper for hybrid-layer degradation and its countermeasures.

**Two parallel degradation axes:**
1. **Endogenous collagenolytic enzymes.** Human dentin contains MMP-2 (gelatinase A), MMP-9 (gelatinase B), MMP-8 (collagenase-2), MMP-3 (stromelysin-1), MMP-20 (enamelysin), and cysteine cathepsins B and K. Both etch-and-rinse (ER) and self-etch (SE) adhesive acidity activates these latent enzymes, triggering time-dependent collagen loss in the hybrid layer. Cathepsin K/B and MMP-2/-9 are dramatically elevated in caries-affected dentin.
2. **Adhesive hydrophilicity.** HEMA (present in 77% of commercial adhesives) absorbs water, causing phase separation, water blistering, incomplete resin impregnation, and low monomer conversion — all reducing long-term interface integrity.

**Five countermeasure families:**
1. **Enzyme inhibitors** — Chlorhexidine (CHX) is the reference standard: a pooled analysis of 25 studies (102 groups, ≥0.2% CHX, ≥6 months) showed CHX significantly reduces bond strength loss vs controls. Application: 15–60 sec of 0.2–2.0% CHX after acid etching. Also: benzalkonium chloride (BAC), galardin (0.2 mM), MDPB (polymerizable QAM in Clearfil Protect Bond), DMSO (MMP-inhibiting solvent that also improves collagen wetting), and zinc-containing adhesives.
2. **Chemical bonding (10-MDP).** Mild SE adhesives leave residual hydroxyapatite around collagen fibrils, theoretically keeping enzymes inactive — but bond strength still declines in vitro and in vivo; enzyme inhibition is additionally needed even with 10-MDP.
3. **Collagen cross-linkers.** Carbodiimide (EDC, low cytotoxicity; can be added to primers), proanthocyanidins (grape-seed extract; short treatment times; improves immediate and long-term bond strength), riboflavin (requires UV light — poor clinical acceptability). Mechanism: increase collagen matrix stiffness and allosterically silence collagenolytic enzymes by altering their binding site conformation.
4. **Ethanol-wet bonding.** Ethanol dehydrates demineralized dentin before hydrophobic resin infiltration, reducing water sorption, solubility, and enzymatic hydrolysis. Technically sensitive and time-consuming; not clinically viable in current form.
5. **Biomimetic remineralization.** Polyanions (polyacrylic acid, polyaspartic acid) serve as phosphoprotein analogs to nucleate apatite from amorphous calcium phosphate composites, replacing matrix water with mineral. In vitro data preserve hybrid-layer mechanical properties and bond strength. Still at proof-of-concept stage.

**Clinical bottom line:** CHX can and should be used until other approaches are proven safe and equally effective. Three-step ER and two-step SE adhesives remain the most reliable benchmarks. Separate acid-etching of enamel margins is recommended with SE adhesives to ensure adequate sealing.

## Key Contributions

- Identifies the **two co-equal failure axes** of hybrid-layer degradation (enzymatic + hydrophilic), moving beyond purely mechanical explanations.
- Provides the full enzyme catalogue in human dentin (Table 1: MMP-2/-3/-8/-9/-20, cathepsin B and K) with substrates and proposed hybrid-layer functions.
- Demonstrates via pooled Figure 3 analysis (25 CHX studies) the magnitude and statistical significance of CHX protection across ER and SE systems.
- Introduces **DMSO** as a novel dual-action MMP inhibitor + collagen-wetting solvent.
- Frames **biomimetic remineralization** (returning hybrid layer collagen to its natural mineralized state) as the ultimate long-term goal for dentin bond preservation.

## Methodology

Narrative review synthesizing principles, current status, and future directions. 15 pages, 150 references. Includes graphical pooled display of CHX studies (Figure 3) but no formal SR+MA methodology. Scope: dentin substrate biology → loss of bond strength mechanisms → methods to improve durability.

## Results

| Countermeasure | Best evidence | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| CHX (0.2–2.0%) | In vitro + in vivo, up to 18–20 months | Significantly reduces bond strength loss rate; in vivo preservation of hybrid layer confirmed; long-term clinical evidence still lacking |
| MDPB (QAM) | In vitro + in vivo | Nearly complete MMP inhibition; may be superior to other SE adhesives |
| Galardin (0.2 mM) | In vitro | Reduces bond strength loss as effectively as CHX; lower concentrations (1:20) fail |
| BAC | In vitro | Inhibits dentinal MMPs; maintains immediate bond strength |
| Carbodiimide (EDC) | In vitro | Eliminates collagen degradation and preserves bond strength; treatment time too long for clinic |
| Proanthocyanidins | In vitro | Short treatment times; improved immediate + long-term bond strength |
| DMSO | In vitro | Improved immediate + long-term bond strength; promising |
| Zinc-containing adhesives | In vitro | Reduces collagen degradation; may induce remineralization |
| Biomimetic remineralization | In vitro (proof-of-concept) | Preserves hybrid-layer stiffness and bond strength; not yet clinically applicable |
| Ethanol-wet bonding | In vitro | Reduces water sorption, enzymatic hydrolysis; technique-sensitive, not clinically viable |

Deep dentin bond strength: 30–50% lower than superficial dentin immediately; caries-affected dentin: 20–50% lower than sound dentin.
