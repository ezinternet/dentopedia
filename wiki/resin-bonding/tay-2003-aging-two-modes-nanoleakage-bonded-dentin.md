## One-line Summary

In-vitro TEM study (abstract-only): bonded human dentin aged ≤12 months in artificial saliva, traced with 50 wt% ammoniacal silver nitrate, shows reticular nanoleakage within the hybrid layer early on, progressively replaced by water-tree silver uptake in the adhesive's water-binding domains — defining two aging-dependent nanoleakage modes and marking water-filled channels as hydrolytic-degradation sites.

## 한줄요약

In-vitro TEM 연구(초록만): 인공 타액에서 ≤12개월 노화시킨 접착 상아질을 50 wt% 암모니아 질산은으로 추적하면, 초기 혼성층(hybrid layer) 내 망상형(reticular) 나노누출이 점차 접착제 물 결합 영역의 워터트리(water tree)형 은 침착으로 대체된다 — 노화 의존적 두 가지 나노누출 양상을 정의하고 물 채워진 채널을 가수분해 열화 부위로 지목.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1177/154405910308200710](https://doi.org/10.1177/154405910308200710)), this in-vitro study used ammoniacal silver nitrate as a tracer of hydrophilic domains and water-filled channels to follow how nanoleakage in bonded dentin evolves with aging. Human teeth bonded with an experimental filled-adhesive were aged in artificial saliva (experimental, aqueous) or non-aqueous mineral oil (control) for up to 12 months, then immersed in 50 wt% ammoniacal silver nitrate and examined by TEM. The paper establishes that nanoleakage is expressed in **two distinct modes** — a *reticular* pattern within/along the hybrid layer, and a *water-tree* pattern within the adhesive's water-binding domains — and that aging shifts the dominant expression from the former toward the latter. (Note: this is an **abstract-only** record; full text was not retrieved, so quantitative method detail is unavailable.)

**Clinically:** resin-dentin bonds degrade not only at the collagen/hybrid layer but also through progressive water-tree formation inside the adhesive itself; minimizing residual water and hydrophilicity at the interface (adequate solvent evaporation, hydrophobic coat over simplified adhesives, controlled moisture) targets the water-filled channels that this study identifies as hydrolytic-degradation sites.

## Key Contributions

- Defines **two modes of nanoleakage** in bonded dentin: reticular (hybrid layer) vs water-tree (adhesive water-binding domains).
- Shows **aging shifts** expression from reticular toward water-tree mode over 12 months.
- Validates **50 wt% ammoniacal silver nitrate + TEM** as a tracer linking water sorption to incipient hydrolytic degradation.

## Methodology

- Human teeth bonded with an **experimental filled-adhesive**.
- Aging media: **artificial saliva** (experimental, aqueous) vs **mineral oil** (non-aqueous control).
- Aging duration: **up to 12 months**.
- Tracer: **50 wt% ammoniacal silver nitrate**; imaging by **TEM**.
- Outcome: qualitative change in silver-uptake morphology over time.

## Results

1. **Early:** reticular silver deposits within the **bulk of the hybrid layer** (experimental/artificial-saliva group).
2. **Over time:** bulk-hybrid-layer reticular deposits **gradually reduced**, then **replaced by deposits along the hybrid layer–adhesive interface**.
3. **Adhesive layer:** silver uptake in **water-binding domains increased with aging** → **water tree formation**.
4. **Mechanism:** these **water-filled channels = potential hydrolytic-degradation sites** for resin-dentin bonds.
5. The non-aqueous mineral-oil control isolates **water sorption** as the driver of these aging changes.
