## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1016/j.prosdent.2024.05.038](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.prosdent.2024.05.038)), this systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated how dentin conditioners — pretreatments applied to dentin before the adhesive — affect resin-dentin bond strength, with a focus on bond *durability* (immediate vs long-term) across dry and wet bonding conditions. Searching four databases (PubMed, Web of Science, EMBASE, Cochrane) from 2013 to July 2023, 23 in vitro studies were included qualitatively and 15 entered a random-effects meta-analysis (pooled standardized mean differences, α=.05). Acid-based conditioners significantly improved long-term bond strength under both dry (P<.001) and wet (P=.006) bonding; selective extrafibrillar demineralization conditioners significantly enhanced immediate and long-term bond strength under dry bonding (P<.001, though long-term subgroup was sample-size limited); metal salt-based conditioners improved immediate bond strength only under wet bonding (P=.010).

This page is **abstract-only** — the full text was not retrieved, so risk-of-bias appraisal, GRADE certainty, and individual effect-size magnitudes are not captured here.

Clinically: when bond *durability* is the priority, acid-based dentin conditioning is the most robust pretreatment because its benefit holds under both dry and wet bonding; selective extrafibrillar demineralization is a promising option specifically for dry-bonding protocols but rests on a thinner evidence base. Because all included data are in vitro, these conclusions inform adhesive-protocol selection rather than predict restoration survival directly.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies which **dentin-conditioner classes** improve resin-dentin bond *durability*, not just immediate strength.
- Stratifies by **mechanistic class** (acid-based, selective extrafibrillar demineralization, metal salt-based) crossed with **bonding condition** (dry vs wet).
- Identifies **acid-based conditioners** as the most condition-robust durability strategy.

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review + meta-analysis of in vitro studies.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Web of Science, EMBASE, Cochrane Library.
- **Window**: 2013 – July 2023.
- **Synthesis**: random-effects model; pooled standardized mean differences (SMD); α=.05.
- **Yield**: 23 studies qualitative, 15 quantitative.

## Results

- **Acid-based conditioners**: significantly improved **long-term** bond strength under **both** dry (P<.001) and wet (P=.006) bonding — the most consistent durability effect.
- **Selective extrafibrillar demineralization conditioners** (dry bonding): significantly enhanced **immediate** bond strength (P<.001); also significant for **long-term** strength (P<.001), but long-term subgroup limited by sample size.
- **Metal salt-based conditioners**: improved **immediate** bond strength **only under wet bonding** (P=.010).
- **Overall**: acid-based = robust across conditions; selective extrafibrillar demineralization = effective under dry bonding but data-limited.
