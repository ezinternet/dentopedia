## Summary

According to PubMed (PMID 12744405), this in-vitro transmission electron microscopy (TEM) tracer study tested the hypothesis that water channels exist along the adhesive–dentin interface and can be detected via silver-nitrate tracer penetration. Three total-etch adhesives (Single Bond, Excite, PQ1) and three self-etch adhesives (Prompt L-Pop, Reactmer Bond, an experimental self-etch adhesive) were bonded to dentin and enamel, immersed for 24 h in either 50 wt% conventional or 50 wt% ammoniacal silver nitrate, photodeveloped, and imaged by TEM. All adhesives — regardless of bonding strategy — exhibited nanoleakage within the hybrid layers. Beyond classic in-layer nanoleakage, the authors observed "water trees": interconnecting, dendritic silver deposits along the surface of the hybrid layer that projected perpendicularly upward into the adhesive resin. Ammoniacal silver nitrate additionally revealed isolated, unconnected silver grains within the adhesives.

This paper is the origin of the **water-treeing** concept in dentin adhesion. The term is borrowed directly from the electrical-engineering literature on **polymeric high-voltage cable insulation**: when such insulation is under electrical stress in a moist environment, microscopic water-filled channels grow through the polymer in a branching, tree-like (dendritic) pattern, and these "water trees" progressively degrade the insulation until it fails. By analogy, the dendritic silver-stained channels Tay & Pashley imaged are water-permeable pathways within and above the hybrid layer — the dental adhesive's equivalent of cable-insulation water trees, providing routes for water to enter and hydrolytically break down the resin–dentin bond over time.

Clinically: resin–dentin bonds are inherently permeable to water even when no marginal gap is visible; the hybrid layer is the weak link, and water-tree channels offer a built-in pathway for the long-term hydrolytic degradation that underlies declining bond strength and marginal breakdown. This argues for minimizing residual water at the interface, controlling adhesive solvent/water content, and favoring measures (e.g., reducing interface permeability) that limit water ingress.

## Key Contributions

- Coined and applied "water treeing" to dentin adhesion, importing the cable-insulation degradation analogy.
- Demonstrated TEM tracer evidence of water-permeable channels along the adhesive–dentin interface across both total-etch and self-etch systems.
- Separated classic in-hybrid-layer nanoleakage from dendritic water trees that extend perpendicularly into the adhesive.
- Identified a second tracer pattern (isolated unconnected silver grains) revealed specifically by ammoniacal silver nitrate.
- Framed these channels as a structural pathway for hydrolytic resin–dentin bond degradation.

## Methodology

- **Design**: in-vitro TEM tracer (nanoleakage) study; hypothesis that interfacial water channels are TEM-detectable.
- **Total-etch adhesives**: Single Bond, Excite, PQ1.
- **Self-etch adhesives**: Prompt L-Pop, Reactmer Bond, experimental self-etch adhesive.
- **Substrate**: dentin and enamel.
- **Tracer**: 50 wt% conventional silver nitrate vs 50 wt% basic (ammoniacal) silver nitrate, 24 h immersion.
- **Processing**: photodeveloping solution → TEM specimen preparation and imaging.

## Results

1. With both conventional and ammoniacal silver nitrate, **all** adhesives showed **nanoleakage within the hybrid layers**.
2. **Water trees** — interconnecting, dendritic silver deposits — were seen along the **surface of the hybrid layers**, extending **perpendicularly into the adhesive layers**.
3. With **ammoniacal** silver nitrate, **additional isolated, unconnected silver grains** appeared within the adhesives (a second tracer pattern).
4. The phenomenon was **general across both total-etch and self-etch** strategies, not adhesive-class specific.
