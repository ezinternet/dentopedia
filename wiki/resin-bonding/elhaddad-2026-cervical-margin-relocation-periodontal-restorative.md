## Summary

Cervical margin relocation (CMR) — also called deep margin elevation (DME) or proximal box elevation (PBE) — places a resin-composite base to lift a deep subgingival proximal margin to a more accessible supragingival level before a direct or indirect restoration. This is the first systematic review with meta-analysis to pool both periodontal and restorative outcomes for the technique.

The headline finding is reassurance about the chief clinical worry: a subgingival composite base does **not** worsen periodontal health in periodontally healthy or stabilized patients. Pooled estimates actually show modest improvement in probing depth and bleeding on probing. Restorative success (marginal adaptation), by contrast, is not guaranteed by the technique itself — it depends heavily on the bonding strategy and the restorative material, with adhesively bonded glass-ceramic restorations performing best.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA to quantitatively pool **periodontal endpoints** (PD, BOP) for CMR/DME.
- Demonstrates **periodontal compatibility** of well-executed CMR in healthy/stabilized periodontium.
- Identifies **bonding strategy and restorative material** as the dominant determinants of marginal adaptation, favoring adhesively bonded glass-ceramics.

## Methodology

- PRISMA-compliant search across PubMed, Scopus, Google Scholar, Web of Science using "cervical margin relocation", "deep margin elevation", "proximal box elevation".
- Meta-analyses for probing depth (PD) and bleeding on probing (BOP); heterogeneity via I2.
- Sensitivity analysis excluding one clinically divergent study.
- Marginal adaptation summarized narratively across bonding strategies and materials.

## Results

- **Probing depth (PD)**: sensitivity-analysis MD **-0.96 mm** (95% CI -1.50 to -0.42; I2 = 0%) — significant reduction.
- **Bleeding on probing (BOP)**: pooled MD **-0.52** (95% CI -0.77 to -0.27; I2 = 27%); after removing one divergent study, effect more pronounced and I2 = 0%.
- **Marginal adaptation**: best with adhesively bonded restorations, particularly glass-ceramics, under controlled conditions.
- Composite margin elevation effectively managed deep proximal cavities restored with ceramic onlays, with favorable periodontal outcomes.
- **Bottom line**: CMR is effective for subgingival margins and compatible with periodontal health when appropriate materials/techniques are employed; long-term follow-up still needed for durability.
