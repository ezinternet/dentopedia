## Summary
Literature review screening 26,222 articles → 88 included on Immediate Dentin Sealing (IDS) — the practice of applying a dentin-bonding agent immediately after tooth preparation, before impression. Authors review terminology (synonyms: prehybridization, dual bonding technique, resin coating), step-by-step protocol, and clinical evidence. IDS is advantageous over Delayed Dentin Sealing (DDS) for bond strength, gap formation, bacterial microleakage during provisional phase, and dentin hypersensitivity. Unresolved areas: impression material interaction, provisional cement choice, conditioning protocol before final cementation. No documented contraindications.

## Key Contributions
- Most comprehensive consolidated review of IDS protocol
- Disambiguates terminology synonyms (prehybridization, dual bonding, resin coating)
- Provides step-by-step protocol guidance
- Identifies specific unresolved areas needing research (impression material, provisional, conditioning)

## Methodology
- **Design**: Literature review (not formal SR)
- **Databases**: MEDLINE, Cochrane, Ovid, Scopus
- **Period**: Jan 1990 – Dec 2020
- **Initial hits**: 26,222 → 88 included
- **Two-reviewer screening** (T.S., D.P.) with consensus
- **Inclusion**: Peer-reviewed English on human permanent teeth (in vitro + clinical)

## Results / Protocol Summary
**IDS benefits over DDS** (qualitative consensus):
- ↑ bond strength
- ↓ gap formation
- ↓ bacterial microleakage during provisional phase
- ↓ dentin hypersensitivity
- Hybrid layer maturation prior to occlusal loading

**Recommended IDS protocol**:
1. Apply dentin-bonding agent immediately after preparation (before impression)
2. Thoroughly light-cure
3. Apply glycerin gel before final cure to remove oxygen-inhibition layer (optional)
4. Air abrasion or pumice clean before impression
5. At delivery: clean with pumice/air abrasion → silane → bond → cement

**Unresolved**: impression material interaction, provisional cement choice, conditioning protocol before final luting.

## Clinical Implications
- IDS is recommended for indirect restorations (inlay, onlay, crown) with dentin exposure
- Particularly beneficial when significant dentin is exposed
- No major contraindications
- Operator must understand oxygen-inhibition layer management and pre-cementation conditioning

## Note on Counterevidence
[[overviews/reis-2024-biomimetic-restorative-myths-narrative]] critiques IDS (BRD myth #6), arguing clinical post-op sensitivity and longevity are similar to DDS. Net interpretation: IDS likely beneficial for bond strength (in vitro consensus) but clinical superiority for patient-centered outcomes is unsettled.
