## One-line Summary

In-vitro XRD + TEM study (experimental 10-MDP primer vs Clearfil SE Bond primer) showing self-assembled MDP-Ca nanolayering forms on enamel and dentin under a clinical protocol — significantly greater on dentin than enamel, proportional to 10-MDP concentration, and enhanced by active rubbing.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1016/j.actbio.2011.04.026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.actbio.2011.04.026)), this landmark in-vitro study (abstract-only — full text not retrieved) extends the **adhesion-decalcification (AD) concept** from synthetic hydroxyapatite (HAp) to natural tooth substrate. Following the AD framework, specific functional monomers can primarily *chemically* interact with HAp; for **10-methacryloyloxydecyl dihydrogen phosphate (10-MDP)** this manifests as self-assembled **"nanolayering"** (ordered periodic layers of MDP-calcium salt). Using X-ray diffraction (XRD) plus TEM interfacial ultrastructure, the authors compared an experimental 10-MDP primer with the commercial control Clearfil SE Bond primer (C-SE, Kuraray) on enamel and dentin under a common clinical application protocol.

XRD confirmed that nanolayering occurs on **both enamel and dentin even with clinically realistic application**, but is **significantly greater on dentin than enamel**, **proportional to the 10-MDP concentration**, and **enhanced by actively rubbing** the primer onto the surface. The bottom-line mechanistic insight: the **MDP-Ca nanolayer is the chemical adhesion mechanism** of MDP-based mild self-etch (and universal) adhesives, and the lower chemical reactivity (less nanolayering) with enamel HAp explains, in part, the well-known **lower bonding effectiveness of mild self-etch adhesives on enamel**.

**Clinically:** when using MDP-based mild self-etch / universal adhesives, (1) enamel is the weak link — selectively phosphoric-acid-etch enamel margins to compensate for its lower MDP chemical reactivity; (2) **actively rub/scrub the primer** rather than passively dab, since agitation increases nanolayer formation; and (3) prefer adhesives with adequate 10-MDP content, because nanolayering — and thus chemical bond — scales with MDP concentration.

## Key Contributions

1. Demonstrates MDP nanolayering on **natural enamel and dentin** (not only synthetic HAp), under a clinical protocol.
2. Establishes the **self-assembled MDP-Ca nanolayer as the chemical-bonding mechanism** of mild self-etch / MDP-universal adhesion to tooth structure.
3. Provides the **mechanistic explanation for weaker mild self-etch bonding on enamel** — lower chemical reactivity (less nanolayering) with enamel HAp than dentin.
4. Shows nanolayering is **concentration-dependent** (∝ 10-MDP %) and **technique-dependent** (enhanced by active rubbing).

## Methodology

- **Materials**: experimental 10-MDP primer vs commercial control Clearfil SE Bond primer (C-SE, Kuraray), applied to enamel and dentin per a common clinical protocol.
- **Interface characterization**: X-ray diffraction (XRD) for the periodic nanolayer (MDP-Ca salt); TEM for interfacial ultrastructure.
- **Concentration effect**: XRD across varying 10-MDP concentrations on dentin.
- **Technique effect**: nanolayering with vs without active rubbing.
- **Functional/stability test**: tensile bond strength to enamel and dentin, with vs without an added photoinitiator.

## Results

1. XRD **confirmed nanolayering on both enamel and dentin** under a clinically used protocol.
2. Nanolayering was **significantly greater on dentin than on enamel**.
3. Nanolayering was **greater when the surface was actively rubbed** with the primer.
4. The degree of nanolayering was **proportional to the 10-MDP concentration** in the primer.
5. The experimental primer **needed the photoinitiator to reach dentin tensile bond strength comparable to control C-SE**, but **not on enamel**.
