## One-line Summary

Bayesian network meta-analysis (14 in vitro studies, primary tooth enamel bond strength): 3-step etch-and-rinse ranked best (highest SUCRA); a two-bottle universal adhesive WITH phosphoric acid etching was statistically equivalent to it; within the same number of steps, self-etch ≈ etch-and-rinse — suggesting clinical steps can be reduced for deciduous enamel.

## Summary

According to PubMed, this is the first systematic review and Bayesian **network meta-analysis** ranking dental adhesive systems specifically on **primary (deciduous) tooth enamel** bond strength ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jdsr.2025.11.005)). Primary enamel differs from permanent enamel — it is thinner, less mineralized, and carries a ~30 μm **aprismatic surface layer** that resists acid etching — so permanent-tooth bonding evidence does not transfer directly. From 331 records, 14 in vitro studies (2001–2024, 35 adhesive systems) were included; all were rated **low risk of bias** (QUIN tool).

The clinical question is whether step-count can be reduced in pediatric restorative and preventive (pit-and-fissure sealant) procedures without losing enamel bond strength. The answer is nuanced: the **3-step etch-and-rinse** remains the top-ranked (gold-standard) approach, but a **two-bottle universal adhesive used with prior phosphoric acid etching** reaches statistically equivalent enamel bond strength, and **within a given step-count self-etch and etch-and-rinse perform similarly** — opening the door to simplification.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a **SUCRA ranking** of 8 adhesive treatment nodes vs the 3-step etch-and-rinse reference, on deciduous enamel specifically.
- Quantifies that **adding phosphoric acid etching to universal adhesives** did not significantly change enamel bond strength for either two-bottle or one-bottle formulations.
- Provides pediatric-dentistry-relevant evidence that step reduction (less chair time, better child compliance) may be feasible.

## Methodology

- **PICO**: P = primary tooth enamel; I = any dental adhesive; C = different systems/strategies; O = bond strength (24 h shear/microshear/microtensile).
- Databases: PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus + gray literature (Jan 2025); Rayyan; two calibrated reviewers (κ = 0.83).
- Excluded: pre-treatment before adhesion, primary-vs-permanent comparisons, etching-agent/time variations, bovine enamel, saliva contamination.
- **Statistics**: mixed-treatment-comparison Bayesian NMA, mean difference, random-effects (lower DIC), 3-step etch-and-rinse reference; node-split inconsistency check; SUCRA; MetaInsight. Thermocycled/aged studies excluded from the quantitative pool → 10 studies, 8 nodes.

## Results

| Comparison vs 3-step etch-and-rinse (reference) | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 2-bottle universal + phosphoric acid etch (2b_Univ_PAE) | **Statistically equivalent** |
| 2-step etch-and-rinse | Inferior |
| 2-step self-etch, 1-step self-etch (no PAE) | Inferior (1-step SE worst by SUCRA) |
| 1- & 2-bottle universal (no PAE), 1-bottle universal + PAE | Inferior |

- **SUCRA ranking**: 3-step etch-and-rinse best → 1-step self-etch without phosphoric acid etching worst.
- Within the same number of steps, **self-etch ≈ etch-and-rinse** (no significant difference).
- Adding phosphoric acid etching to universal adhesives did **not** significantly raise enamel bond strength.
- No network inconsistency detected (node-split p > 0.05).

### Clinical caveats
- Only **immediate 24 h in vitro** bond strength entered the quantitative analysis — no hydrolytic/thermal/mechanical/salivary aging. The authors explicitly state this is **not a clinical recommendation**.
- Substrate heterogeneity (aprismatic vs prismatic/sanded enamel) is a recognized variability source.
