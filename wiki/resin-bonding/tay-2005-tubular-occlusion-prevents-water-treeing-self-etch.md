## One-line Summary

In-vitro TEM/silver-impregnation study (experimental single-bottle one-step self-etch adhesive): bonding to caries-affected dentin with occluded tubules eliminated water-treeing and water-droplets, whereas adjacent sound dentin showed both — demonstrating transdentinal water flux (not just residual solvent) drives water-treeing.

## 한줄요약

체외 투과전자현미경(TEM)·은침착(silver-impregnation) 연구 (실험용 단일병 1단계 자가부식 접착제): 세관(tubule)이 폐쇄된 우식이환상아질(caries-affected dentin)에 접착 시 water-treeing과 water-droplet이 모두 사라진 반면, 인접 정상상아질(sound dentin)에서는 둘 다 관찰됨 — 잔류 용매가 아니라 경상아질 수분 이동(transdentinal water flux)이 water-treeing을 유발함을 입증.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1177/154405910508401004](https://doi.org/10.1177/154405910508401004)), this in-vitro study isolated the *source* of water entrapment at resin–dentin interfaces of one-step self-etch adhesives. The authors hypothesized that if water fluxes from dentin are prevented, any remaining water entrapment must be due to incompletely removed adhesive solvent. They tested this by bonding an experimental single-bottle, one-step self-etch adhesive to caries-affected (transparent) dentin with **occluded** dentinal tubules, surrounded by a rim of sound dentin with patent tubules, **with or without pulpal pressure**, then examining the interfaces by TEM after silver-impregnation. Where tubules were occluded, the adhesive layer was devoid of silver despite the substrate being highly porous; over sound dentin, water-treeing and water-droplets appeared.

Because this is an **abstract-only** ingest (full text not retrieved), quantitative details — bond strengths, silver-uptake metrics, sample sizes — are not available; the page reflects abstract-level findings only.

**Clinically:** water-treeing in one-step self-etch adhesives is driven substantially by transdentinal water flux through *patent* tubules, so reducing pulpal water permeation (e.g., bonding to sclerotic/occluded substrates, or controlling pulpal pressure) suppresses this degradation mode — but note the caveat that the porous caries-affected substrate itself may compromise bond durability, so eliminating water-treeing is not a guarantee of a better long-term bond.

## Key Contributions

- A "knock-out" experiment that isolates the water source: occluded tubules remove transdentinal flux while leaving residual solvent and substrate porosity intact.
- Demonstrates occluded dentinal tubules prevent water-treeing and water-droplet formation; patent (sound-dentin) tubules permit both.
- Identifies water-treeing/water-droplets as manifestations of **evaporative and convective** water fluxes, with the convective component requiring patent tubules.
- Shifts the explanation of water entrapment from "residual solvent only" toward **transdentinal water flux** as a primary driver.

## Methodology

- An experimental single-bottle, one-step self-etch adhesive applied to flat **caries-affected (transparent) dentin** with **occluded tubules**, surrounded by **sound dentin** with patent tubules.
- Bonding performed **with or without pulpal pressure** to modulate convective transdentinal water flux.
- Resin–dentin interfaces examined by **TEM** after **silver-impregnation** (silver tracer marks water-filled nanovoids / water-treeing).
- Within-specimen occluded-vs-sound contrast controls for solvent removal and technique, isolating the tubular-flux variable.

## Results

1. Caries-affected dentin was **highly porous**, yet adhesive layers over it were **devoid of silver deposits when tubules were occluded** (no water-treeing).
2. In bonded **sound dentin** (patent tubules), **variable extents of water-treeing and water-droplets** were identified within the adhesive layers.
3. Water-treeing and water-droplet formation — manifestations of **evaporative and convective water fluxes** — **can be eliminated** by bonding to occluded transparent carious dentin.
4. Caveat: the **highly porous** nature of the caries-affected substrate after bonding may lead to **potentially undesirable consequences** (e.g., compromised durability), despite the absence of water-treeing.
