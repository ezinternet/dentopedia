## Summary

This critical narrative review by Breschi et al. (2018) synthesizes decades of research on resin-dentin bonding, organized from dentin collagen architecture through hybrid-layer (HL) formation, degradation, and preservation strategies, culminating in clinical applications. It is a foundational reference for understanding why resin-dentin bonds fail over time and what can be done about it.

**The core argument** is that HL degradation occurs through two main mechanisms — (1) enzymatic degradation of collagen fibrils and (2) leaching/hydrolysis of resin from the HL — both enabled by **residual unbound water** trapped between denuded collagen fibrils at the bottom of the HL. That water activates endogenous dentinal enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases, MMPs; and cysteine cathepsins), which progressively degrade the collagen scaffold. Simultaneously, hydrolytic degradation breaks covalent bonds in the resin polymer, causing mass loss and plasticization, which then allows salivary esterases to further accelerate the process.

Clinically, durable resin-dentin bonds require managing the demineralized collagen interface by (a) minimizing residual unbound water in the bonded zone and (b) inhibiting endogenous MMP/cathepsin activity. A growing number of chemical agents and physical approaches accomplish this. Despite the remaining challenge that full resin envelopment of the deepest collagen fibrils is "very difficult, if not impossible," impressive progress has been achieved.

## Key Contributions

- **Unifying framework**: residual unbound water at the base of the HL is the common enabler of both degradation pathways (enzymatic and hydrolytic).
- **Two-mechanism taxonomy**: (1) enzymatic collagen-fibril degradation (MMPs + cysteine cathepsins), (2) resin hydrolysis/leaching — clearly distinguishes the two degradation modes and their respective targets.
- **Dentin collagen biology**: contextualizes dentin as 30–50 vol% organic matrix (~20 vol% water), explains how proteoglycans (decorin, fibromodulin) bind fibrillar collagens, and why this structure determines HL vulnerability.
- **E&R vs SE strategy comparison**: both etch-and-rinse and self-etch approaches are analyzed for how they interact with dentin collagen and residual water; neither fully envelopes deepest fibrils.
- **Preservation strategy taxonomy**: two families — water removal and enzyme silencing — map directly onto clinically actionable adjuncts and protocols.
- **Historical attribution**: traces collagenolytic-activity discovery from Dayan → Tjäderhane (MMPs) → Pashley (aseptic intrinsic proteases), giving the mechanistic narrative a coherent historical arc.

## Results

**Dentin composition and collagen structure:**
- Dentin is a mineralized collagen matrix with ~30–50 vol% organic material and ~20 vol% water.
- Non-collagenous proteins include proteoglycans (decorin and fibromodulin bind to separate sites on fibrillar collagens).
- This organic-matrix structure dictates both how resin infiltrates during bonding and why the HL remains biologically vulnerable.

**Adhesive strategies (E&R and SE):**
- Both strategies require acid (mineral removal, collagen exposure), primer (hydrophilic monomer infiltration), and adhesive (polymer matrix).
- E&R involves discrete acid-rinse step before primer; SE uses acidic monomers for simultaneous demineralization and infiltration.
- Neither technique achieves complete resin envelopment of the deepest demineralized collagen fibrils — this structural incompleteness is the fundamental vulnerability.

**Resin degradation mechanism:**
- Hydrolytic degradation: water-mediated breaking of covalent bonds between polymer chains → loss of resin mass from HL.
- Hydrolysis is the primary mechanism of resin degradation within the HL.
- Consequences: adhesive plasticization and swelling → increased penetration by salivary esterases → accelerated hydrolysis cycle; and collagen exposure for enzymatic attack.

**Collagen scaffold degradation:**
- Collagenolytic activity in dentin first described by Dayan et al.; attributed to MMPs by Tjäderhane et al.
- Pashley et al. demonstrated aseptic-condition collagen degradation, confirming intrinsic proteases (not bacterial origin).
- MMPs and cysteine cathepsins are hydrolases requiring unbound water to cleave collagen peptides → mechanistic link between residual water and enzymatic degradation.
- Marginal discoloration, marginal leakage, and loss of restoration retention are the clinical consequences.

**Preservation strategies:**
- *Family 1 — Water removal*: eliminating unbound water from inter-fibrillar gaps denies proteases the cofactor needed for collagen cleavage.
- *Family 2 — Enzyme silencing*: chemical agents (MMP inhibitors, chlorhexidine, cross-linking agents) and physical approaches inhibit endogenous MMP/cathepsin activity; the number of available agents is growing.
- Both families have been demonstrated to extend HL longevity in laboratory settings.
