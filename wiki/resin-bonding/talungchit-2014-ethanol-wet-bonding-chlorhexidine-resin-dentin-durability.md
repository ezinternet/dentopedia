## One-line Summary

In-vitro micro-Raman + μTBS + TEM bench study (Talungchit et al. 2014, J Adhes Dent; abstract-only retrieved) showing ethanol-wet bonding raises bis-GMA/TEG-DMA molar concentration and μTBS across the hybrid layer immediately and after aging, with chlorhexidine further preserving collagen and limiting nanoleakage at 1 year.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3290/j.jad.a32695](https://doi.org/10.3290/j.jad.a32695)), this in-vitro study directly tested whether ethanol-wet bonding (EW) improves hydrophobic-monomer infiltration into demineralized dentin and whether EW plus chlorhexidine (CHX) improves resin-dentin bond durability versus conventional water-wet bonding (WW). A three-step etch-and-rinse adhesive (70% bis-GMA / 28.75% TEG-DMA) was applied under EW or WW; micro-Raman spectroscopy quantified bis-GMA and TEG-DMA molar concentrations across the interface immediately and after 7-month buffer storage, while μTBS and TEM morphology (nanoleakage, collagen fibril diameter, interfibrillar width, hybrid layer thickness) were compared ± CHX immediately and after 1-year storage. EW specimens had significantly higher monomer concentrations and μTBS throughout the hybrid layer both immediately and after aging, and maintained interfibrillar width and hybrid layer thickness; CHX further preserved collagen integrity and limited nanoleakage in EW after 1 year.

This page is **abstract-only** — full text was not retrieved, so exact numeric effect sizes (monomer concentration, μTBS in MPa, nanoleakage scores) and statistical detail beyond "significant" are unavailable.

**Clinically:** for etch-and-rinse adhesion where bond longevity matters, an ethanol-wet bonding protocol (replacing residual water with ethanol before applying a hydrophobic adhesive) deepens monomer infiltration and raises bond strength, and a chlorhexidine pretreatment adds collagen-preserving, anti-degradation benefit — supporting EW + CHX as a more durable combination, though this rests on bench/aging data, not clinical trials.

## Key Contributions

- First to use **micro-Raman spectroscopy** to directly quantify bis-GMA and TEG-DMA molar concentrations within the hybrid layer, giving molecular-level proof that EW infiltrates hydrophobic monomers better than WW.
- Links monomer quantification to mechanical (μTBS) and morphological (TEM nanoleakage, collagen, hybrid layer thickness) durability outcomes across immediate and aged timepoints.
- Shows **chlorhexidine** adds collagen preservation and nanoleakage reduction on top of EW after 1-year storage, supporting combined EW + CHX.

## Methodology

- Three-step etch-and-rinse adhesive (resin: 70% bis-GMA / 28.75% TEG-DMA) on coronal dentin under **EW** (clinically relevant ethanol-wet) or **WW** (conventional water-wet).
- **Micro-Raman spectroscopy**: bis-GMA and TEG-DMA molar concentrations measured at multiple positions across the resin/dentin interface; repeated at the same positions after **7-month storage** in phosphate buffer with 0.1% sodium azide.
- **μTBS** and **TEM hybrid-layer morphology** compared with vs without chlorhexidine, **immediately** and after **1-year storage** (nanoleakage, collagen fibril diameter, interfibrillar width, hybrid layer thickness).

## Results

- EW gave **significantly higher monomer molar concentrations and μTBS throughout the hybrid layer**, immediately and after storage — direct evidence of superior hydrophobic-monomer infiltration vs WW.
- EW **maintained interfibrillar width and hybrid layer thickness**, preserving space for resin infiltration and retention.
- **Chlorhexidine further preserved collagen integrity and limited the degree of nanoleakage** in EW after 1-year storage.
- Authors conclude a **more durable resin-dentin bond may be achieved with combined EW + chlorhexidine**.
- Exact numeric values not reported in the abstract (abstract-only retrieval).
