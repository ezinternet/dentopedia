## One-line Summary

In-vitro TEM + thin-film XRD study (7 commercial 10-MDP adhesives, n=6 TEM / n=4 XRD; experimental 15/10/5 wt% primers): profuse ~3.7 nm-periodicity nanolayering appears only at 15% 10-MDP and is rarely seen in commercial adhesives, challenging nanolayering as a clinical bond-longevity mechanism.

## 한줄요약

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1177/0022034515623741](https://doi.org/10.1177/0022034515623741)), this in-vitro study (Tian, Pashley, Tay et al., *J Dent Res* 2016) tested whether the self-assembled 10-MDP nanolayering reported in experimental systems is actually reproduced by **commercialized** 10-MDP self-etch and universal adhesives applied to human dentin. Seven commercial adhesives (Adhese Universal, All-Bond Universal, Clearfil SE Bond 2, Clearfil S3 Bond Plus, Clearfil Universal Bond, G-Premio Bond, Scotchbond Universal) were examined by transmission electron microscopy (TEM, n=6, 30 random unstained sites) and thin-film glancing-angle X-ray diffraction (XRD, n=4), against experimental control primers of 15, 10, and 5 wt% 10-MDP. Profuse, highly ordered nanolayering (~3.7 nm periodicity, with XRD peaks at 2θ 2.40°/4.78°/7.18°) appeared at 15% 10-MDP, diminished at 10% and 5%, and was **rarely** observed in any of the seven commercial adhesives.

**Clinically**: this is the principal counterpoint to the Yoshihara 2011 nanolayering hypothesis. It does not deny that 10-MDP can self-assemble into ordered nanolayers, but it shows the structure is **concentration-dependent and largely absent in the formulations dentists actually use**. The implication is that the durable, well-documented clinical performance of 10-MDP adhesives is more likely driven by the stable, hydrolysis-resistant 10-MDP–calcium ionic bond itself than by abundant nanolayering — so nanolayering should be treated as an interesting interfacial phenomenon, not a clinical selling point for bond longevity. The longevity-vs-nanolayering link remains an open debate (no bond-strength/aging endpoint was measured here).

## Key Contributions

- First systematic check of whether commercial 10-MDP adhesives reproduce the nanolayering seen with experimental 10-MDP solutions.
- Documents a clear concentration dependence: profuse at 15 wt%, reduced at 10% and 5%, rarely present in commercial products.
- Pairs TEM morphology with thin-film XRD crystallographic fingerprints (characteristic 2θ peaks) for objective detection.
- Reframes nanolayering as clinically marginal, shifting the credit for 10-MDP durability toward the 10-MDP–Ca ionic bond.

## Methodology

- **Design**: In-vitro, TEM + thin-film glancing-angle XRD, on human midcoronal dentin.
- **Commercial adhesives (7), self-etch mode per manufacturer instructions**: Adhese Universal (Ivoclar-Vivadent), All-Bond Universal (Bisco), Clearfil SE Bond 2, Clearfil S3 Bond Plus, Clearfil Universal Bond (all Kuraray Noritake), G-Premio Bond (GC), Scotchbond Universal (3M ESPE).
- **Experimental control primers**: 15, 10, 5 wt% 10-MDP (DM Healthcare Products) in ethanol:water 9:8 (by weight) with photoinitiators; 20 s application, then hydrophobic resin layer.
- **TEM**: n=6 per group; specimens covered with flowable composite; 30 random sites examined unstained.
- **XRD**: n=4; thin-film glancing-angle geometry to detect nanolayering peaks.

## Results

- **15% 10-MDP primer**: profuse nanolayering, highly ordered ~3.7 nm periodicity, adjacent to partially dissolved apatite crystallites.
- **XRD at 15%**: three characteristic peaks — 2θ 2.40° (3.68 nm), 4.78° (1.85 nm), 7.18° (1.23 nm).
- **10% and 5% 10-MDP primers**: reduced nanolayering extent and lower-intensity XRD peaks.
- **7 commercial adhesives**: nanolayering and characteristic XRD peaks RARELY observed.
- **Conclusion**: paucity of nanolayering in commercial interfaces challenges its role as a clinical bond-longevity mechanism.
