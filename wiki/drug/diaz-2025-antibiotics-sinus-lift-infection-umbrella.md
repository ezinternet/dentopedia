## One-line Summary

Umbrella review of 7 SR/MAs (>18,900 patients) finds postoperative infection rates of 0.3–11.6% after sinus floor elevation; low-certainty evidence supports antibiotic prophylaxis only in high-risk scenarios (membrane perforation, extensive lateral approach, systemic comorbidities), while routine use in uncomplicated procedures is unnecessary.

## Summary

This umbrella review (Díaz et al. 2025) synthesized evidence from seven SR/MAs published between 2008 and 2024 to evaluate systemic antibiotic (ATB) use for the prevention and management of infections following maxillary sinus floor elevation (SFE). Covering more than 18,900 patients and 28,000 implants placed in sinus-augmented areas, it applied AMSTAR 2 for methodological quality appraisal, GROOVE for primary study overlap quantification, and GRADE for certainty-of-evidence assessment. The review was prospectively registered in PROSPERO (CRD420251061400) and adhered to PRISMA 2020 and PRIOR 2022 reporting guidelines.

Postoperative infection rates ranged from 0.3% to 11.6% across the included reviews, with consistently higher rates for the lateral window approach (1.7–2.9%) compared to the transcrestal technique (0.3–1.0%). Salgado-Peralvo et al. 2023, rated as high quality by AMSTAR 2, found no statistically significant difference in implant failure rates between patients who received ATBs and those who did not. Implant survival remained consistently above 90% across all reviews regardless of ATB regimen, with pooled survival reaching 98.4% (Salgado-Peralvo et al. 2023) and 97.0% even in cases with Schneiderian membrane (SM) perforation (Schiavo-Di Flaviano et al. 2024). The overall primary-study overlap was slight (CCA = 1.33%), confirming that the included reviews contribute largely independent bodies of evidence.

For infection management, Allevi et al. 2022 reported that endoscopic sinus surgery (ESS) combined with systemic ATB therapy was the most effective strategy, achieving clinical resolution in 94.7% of sinusitis cases. Early ATB therapy — typically Amoxicillin/Clavulanate, Clindamycin, or Fluoroquinolones — combined with anti-inflammatory and decongestant therapy was effective in most cases of graft infection when initiated before extensive complications (OAF formation, implant displacement). Patient-related risk factors including smoking, pre-existing sinusitis, and SM perforation exceeding 10 mm were consistently identified as independent modifiers of infection risk, with surgical integrity and patient susceptibility appearing more determinant than the specific ATB regimen employed.

## Key Contributions

1. First umbrella review apprasing all SR/MAs on ATB use specifically for SFE infection prevention and management, using AMSTAR 2 + GROOVE + GRADE triple appraisal
2. Infection rate by technique confirmed: lateral window 1.7–2.9% vs transcrestal 0.3–1.0%; surgical approach is a stronger determinant than ATB regimen choice
3. Implant survival >90% across all techniques and ATB protocols; routine prophylaxis does not meaningfully raise this floor
4. GRADE certainty for ATB prophylaxis vs infection incidence: LOW; for infection management outcomes: VERY LOW; for implant survival comparisons: MODERATE
5. CCA = 1.33% (slight overlap) validates independent evidence contribution of all seven included reviews
6. Clinical recommendation: risk-based ATB prescribing rather than routine coverage; ATBs reserved for high-risk scenarios (SM perforation, extensive lateral approach, comorbidities)
7. Supports antimicrobial stewardship in oral implantology — non-ATB strategies (CHX rinses, meticulous technique, strict asepsis) provide comparable infection control in uncomplicated cases

## Methodology

- **Design**: Umbrella review (systematic overview of SR/MAs)
- **Included reviews**: 7 (5 SR + 2 SR+MA), published 2008–2024
- **Primary studies**: 189 (1997–2022), including 80 retrospective, 46 prospective, 27 case series, 26 RCTs, 5 cohort, 4 clinical trials, 2 case-control
- **Population**: >18,900 patients; >28,000 implants in sinus-augmented areas
- **Search databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Scopus, Web of Science, EBSCO; Google Scholar manual search
- **Search cut-off**: May 5, 2025
- **Inter-rater agreement**: Fleiss kappa = 0.76 (substantial); 100% on final inclusion
- **Quality appraisal**: AMSTAR 2 (2 high, 3 moderate, 1 low, 1 critically low)
- **Overlap quantification**: GROOVE / CCA (1.33% — slight)
- **Certainty of evidence**: GRADE
- **Synthesis**: Descriptive narrative; no de novo meta-analysis (clinical + methodological heterogeneity precluded pooling)
- **Reporting**: PRISMA 2020 + PRIOR 2022

## Results

### Infection rates

| Review (AMSTAR quality) | Technique | Infection rate | n |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tan et al. 2008 (High) | Transcrestal | 0.8% (0–2.5%) | 2,830 pts |
| Pjetursson et al. 2008 (Moderate) | Lateral window | 2.9% (0–7.4%) | 3,716 pts/12,020 implants |
| Hsu et al. 2022 (Moderate) | Lateral | 1.74% | NR |
| Hsu et al. 2022 (Moderate) | Transcrestal | 0.31% | NR |
| Salgado-Peralvo et al. 2023 (High) | Mixed | 0–23.3% (individual studies) | NR |
| Allevi et al. 2022 (Moderate) | Any (sinusitis) | 36.6% sinusitis post-implant | NR |
| Schlund et al. 2022 (Critically low) | Any | 0.5–11.6% | NR |

| Review | Survival | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|

### GRADE certainty summary

| Outcome | Certainty |
|---|---|
| ATB effectiveness: infection prevention | LOW |
| ATB effectiveness: infection management | VERY LOW |
| Implant survival (ATB vs no ATB) | MODERATE |
| Comparative ATB regimen efficacy | LOW |

### Most common ATB regimens

- **Prophylaxis (1st line)**: Amoxicillin 2 g PO 1 h pre-op; Amoxicillin/Clavulanate 7–10 days post-op
- **Penicillin allergy**: Clindamycin 300–600 mg q6–8h
- **Less frequent**: Cephalosporins, Azithromycin
- **Treatment (severe)**: Amoxicillin/Clavulanate or Levofloxacin + nasal corticosteroids + decongestants + CHX; ESS for refractory cases
- **2nd line (resistance)**: Moxifloxacin if no improvement in 7–10 days

### Risk factors for postoperative infection

- SM perforation (especially >10 mm)
- Lateral window approach (vs transcrestal)
- Smoking, pre-existing sinusitis
- Graft material displacement into sinus cavity
- Particulate grafts in lateral approach (higher SM perforation rate)

### Key infectious organisms (post-SFE sinusitis)

- Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis
- Increasing beta-lactam resistance reported
