## Summary
Systematic review and meta-analysis specifically for periodontal and implant-related surgical procedures (open flap debridement, gingival augmentation, implant site development, implant placement). Eighteen RCTs included from a screening pool of 1,995 titles. Meta-analysis compared pre-emptive analgesia vs placebo at multiple post-operative timepoints (1, 3, 6, 8, 24, and 72 hours).

The result: preemptive analgesia significantly reduces post-operative pain at multiple timepoints in periodontal/implant surgical contexts. This *updates* the long-standing negative finding for third molar surgery (Costa 2015 meta-analysis) — context matters.

## Key Contributions
- First periodontal/implant surgery-specific preemptive analgesia SR+MA.
- Quantifies effect across multiple post-op timepoints.
- Refines the preemptive analgesia question by surgical specialty — positive in periodontal/implant, negative/equivocal in third molar.

## Methodology
SR+MA per PRISMA; 18 RCTs included; outcomes pain SMD at 1, 3, 6, 8, 24, 72 h post-op; intervention preemptive analgesia (mostly NSAID, some COX-2, some APAP).

## Results
[근거강함] Preemptive analgesia significantly reduces pain at several post-op timepoints in periodontal/implant surgery (effect sizes in paper).

## Limitations
- Heterogeneity in preemptive drug class, timing, and dose
- Some periodontal/implant studies underpowered individually
- Cannot extrapolate to third molar surgery (negative in Costa 2015)

## Clinical Implication (Korean Practice)
For Korean periodontal and implant surgery — pre-emptive oral ibuprofen 400mg + APAP 1g 30-60 min before procedure (or as the patient finishes the LA waiting period) is supported by the evidence base. For third molar surgery, pre-emptive dosing has weaker evidence; if dosing pre-emptively for patient convenience, the timing matters less than ensuring the analgesic peak coincides with the post-LA pain emergence.
