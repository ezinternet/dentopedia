## One-line Summary

Umbrella review of 44 SRs (221 meta-analyses) on systemic and local antibiotics in periodontal therapy: statistically robust estimates exist for systemic antibiotics (CAL gain ~0.3 mm), but overall evidence grades as weak due to critically low methodological quality in 66% of underlying SRs, supporting antibiotic stewardship over routine use.

## Summary

This umbrella review, registered on PROSPERO (CRD42024527222) and reported per PRISMA guidelines, systematically appraised 44 systematic reviews with meta-analysis — encompassing 221 meta-analytic comparisons — on the adjunctive use of systemic and local antibiotics in periodontal therapy (surgical and non-surgical). The search covered PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, EMBASE, CENTRAL, and LILACS through March 2024. Each included SR was evaluated using AMSTAR-2 for methodological quality (4 tiers: High/Moderate/Low/Critically Low), and each meta-analysis was graded for evidence strength using a published hierarchical framework (Strong/Highly Suggestive/Suggestive/Weak). These two dimensions were then combined into a single "Overall Grading."

The central finding is a striking disconnect between meta-analytical signal and methodological quality. Four meta-analyses achieved "Strong" evidence for systemic antibiotics (combined types) on key clinical endpoints: CAL gain of 0.3 mm (95% CI: 0.2–0.4), 0.4 mm in moderate-to-deep pockets, bleeding on probing reduction of 6.6% (95% CI: 4.2–9.1), and pocket closure improvement of -14.5% (95% CI: -17.9 to -11.1). A further 15 meta-analyses reached "Highly Suggestive" and 10 reached "Suggestive" levels. However, the SRs generating these estimates had low methodological quality by AMSTAR-2, collapsing the overall grade to "Weak" for all. Across all 221 comparisons, 192 had weak meta-analytic strength from the outset. The local antibiotic evidence was similarly constrained — two highly-suggestive estimates exist for PPD and CAL, but insufficient high-quality SRs support confident clinical recommendation.

Rosenberg's Fail-Safe Number (FSN) analysis of 215 meta-analyses showed that for 65.5% of suggestive-to-strong estimates, the FSN exceeded the number of included studies — meaning the statistical direction is unlikely to reverse with more research. Nevertheless, the authors argue this does not elevate the overall clinical recommendation, because the evidence quality issue is in SR methodology (transparency, risk-of-bias accounting, funding disclosure), not in the aggregate direction of the effect. The article concludes with an explicit antibiotic stewardship call: given the 23.6% worldwide prevalence of severe periodontitis and the public health risk of antimicrobial resistance, routine antibiotic use in periodontal therapy is not supported. Antibiotics should be reserved for aggressive or severe periodontitis, and assessed for efficacy beforehand when feasible.

## Key Contributions

1. Provides the highest-level evidence synthesis (umbrella review) for antibiotics in periodontal therapy, covering 44 SRs and 221 meta-analytic comparisons through March 2024.
2. Demonstrates that strong/highly-suggestive meta-analytic estimates for systemic antibiotics exist (CAL gain ~0.3–0.4 mm, BoP reduction ~6.6%), but these derive from low-quality SRs, making the overall evidence grade "weak."
3. Finds that 65.6% of included SRs are of "critically low" AMSTAR-2 quality, with the most common failures being non-disclosure of study funding (93.4%), lack of study design selection rationale (82.0%), and failure to account for risk-of-bias when interpreting results (57.4%).
4. Shows FSN ≥ number of included studies in 65.5% of suggestive-to-strong meta-analyses, indicating the aggregate direction will not change — but quality of the SR field must improve.
5. Formally recommends antibiotic stewardship: discourages routine adjunctive antibiotic use in periodontal therapy; reserves antibiotics for aggressive/severe periodontitis only.
6. Critiques over-reliance on average continuous outcomes (mean PPD/CAL change) vs. threshold metrics (% sites achieving clinically meaningful improvement).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Umbrella review (PRISMA-compliant, PROSPERO CRD42024527222)
- **Search**: PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, EMBASE, CENTRAL (Cochrane), LILACS; up to March 2024; no language/date restrictions
- **Inclusion**: SRs with meta-analysis assessing systemic and/or local antibiotics as periodontal therapy adjuncts; human studies; pooled estimates with heterogeneity statistics
- **Exclusion**: SRs without pooled estimates, without controls, without I²/heterogeneity data; other umbrella reviews; country-specific SRs; secondary analyses of prior SRs
- **Included**: 44 SRs, 221 meta-analyses
- **Quality tool**: AMSTAR-2 (16 items, 7 critical domains); inter-examiner kappa 0.88
- **Evidence grading**: 4-tier framework (Strong/Highly Suggestive/Suggestive/Weak) based on n, p-value, I², prediction interval, small-study effects
- **Overall grading**: Combined AMSTAR-2 × evidence tier matrix
- **FSN**: Rosenberg's method, Meta-Essentials package; computed for 215 MA
- **SR scope**: 72.1% systemic only, 18.0% local only, 9.8% both

| Pocket closure % (systemic) | -14.5% | -17.9 to -11.1 | Strong |

| Tier | n | % |
|---|---|---|

### FSN (for 29 suggestive-to-strong MA)
- Median FSN: 35 (range 0–175)
- 65.5% have FSN > number of studies included (robust signal direction)
