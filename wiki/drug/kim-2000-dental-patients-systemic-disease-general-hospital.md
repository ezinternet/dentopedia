## Summary

Retrospective chart review at **분당제생병원 (Pundang Jesaeng Hospital DMC) OMFS** clinic, January through December 1999. Of **3,506 dental outpatients** screened, **427 (12.2%)** had a documented systemic disease. Mean age 51.2 y (range 1–88); female-predominant (M:F = 0.79:1).

The findings give a 1999 snapshot of the Korean general-hospital dental practice mix and — more usefully for present-day clinical risk thinking — a small but illuminating case series of **4 severe complications**, each tied to a specific failure in pre-procedural medical screening.

The most common systemic disease was **hypertension**, followed by **diabetes**, **heart disease**, and **liver disease**. The most common dental diagnosis in this cohort was **periodontitis** (n=130), then **caries** (106), **impacted tooth** (47), and **abscess/cellulitis** (25). The most common *definitive* dental treatment was **extraction**, followed by endodontic therapy, periodontal therapy, and prosthetics.

The systems-of-care finding worth flagging: of 427 patients with systemic disease, only **114 received internal-medicine consultation** before treatment. The majority were managed with "counselling only" (n=232), medication (80), simple periodontal therapy (42), extraction (23), endodontic therapy (9), and I&D (4) *without* prior medical clearance.

[claude해석] Read against current standards, this is exactly the gap the modern medically-compromised-patient guidelines were written to close — but the data also reflect the practical truth that most "systemic disease" patients with well-controlled stable disease can in fact be safely treated under standard dental protocols, and over-referral creates its own access-to-care problem. The 4 complications occurred specifically where screening was inadequate or surgery was elective in a high-risk patient.

## Key Contributions

1. **Baseline Korean prevalence figure**: 12.2% of general-hospital dental outpatients carried a documented systemic disease in 1999. Useful as a denominator for clinical-risk planning and as a historical benchmark against present-day numbers.
2. **Frequency ranking of systemic diseases in this dental population** (HTN > DM > heart > liver) is consistent with general Korean adult chronic disease prevalence — confirms the dental population is not unusually risk-enriched.
3. **Documents the consultation gap** (114 consults across 427 systemic-disease patients): identifies a quality-improvement target that remains relevant.
4. **4-case complication series** as a teaching set:
   - **Toxic hepatitis death** after GA-based maxillofacial surgery in an elderly liver-disease patient — internal medicine had flagged surgery as high-risk; patient and family elected to proceed after informed consent; no medicolegal dispute, but a preventable death.
   - **Prolonged gingival bleeding** in a 40-year-old male on **warfarin** after simple scaling/root planing — pre-procedural history and coagulation studies were not performed; the authors classify this as "the operator's major error." Managed with Surgicel + Gelfoam local hemostasis, neurology consult, warfarin held, repeat CBC and coagulation panel until normalized.
   - **2 post-operative infections** (details abbreviated in the OCR'd text).
5. **Disease-specific dental protocol summary** for hypertension, diabetes, asthma/COPD, tuberculosis, and renal disease — practical bedside guidance condensed for general-hospital OMFS staff.

## Methodology

Retrospective chart review + structured questionnaire of all dental outpatients at one general-hospital OMFS clinic over a 12-month window (1999). Outcomes: prevalence of systemic disease, dental diagnoses, treatments delivered, internal-medicine consultations, and complications.

[claude해석] The questionnaire-plus-chart design depends on patient self-report at intake for systemic disease that did not appear in the hospital's own EMR. This very likely **underestimates** true prevalence (asymptomatic well-controlled HTN/DM often unreported). The 12.2% should be read as a floor estimate.

### Disease-specific practical recommendations (from Discussion)

| System | Practical recommendation |
|---|---|
| Hypertension | Routine BP measurement at intake; pre-op internal-medicine referral for uncontrolled cases; in unavoidable uncontrolled HTN, only conservative/emergency care; minimize stress and pain. Stable controlled HTN — any dental procedure permitted. Epinephrine in vasoconstrictor-containing LA contributes to BP rise. |
| Diabetes | Morning appointments, short visits; no mealtime delay (hypoglycemia risk); minimize epinephrine (hyperglycemic effect); oral glucose or sugar available for hypoglycemic shock. |
| Asthma / COPD / emphysema | Avoid respiratory-depressant practices (e.g., bilateral mandibular block, rubber dam if compromise); upright seating; bronchodilator + inhaler accessible. |
| Tuberculosis | Treat as potentially infectious by default; if active treatment <18 mo, or recent course <9 mo — internal medicine evaluation before elective work. |
| Renal disease | If uncontrolled — emergency only; avoid aspirin and nephrotoxic drugs; close BP monitoring; hemodialysis patients require internal medicine coordination. |

## Limitations (Critical Reading)

[claude해석]
1. **Single-center, 12-month design, n=3,506** — captures one general-hospital OMFS clinic's mix in 1999; not generalizable to private clinic.
2. **No control group / no relative risk** — cannot tell whether the 4 complications represent excess risk attributable to systemic disease vs background rate.
3. **Systemic disease definition relies on chart documentation + self-report** — likely undercounts; the 12.2% is a floor.
4. **1999 pharmacological era** — the warfarin case here predates DOACs entirely; current evidence (Inchingolo 2024 SR, Moldovan 2023 SR) supports *not* interrupting anticoagulation for routine dental procedures and instead managing locally. The lesson from the warfarin case (pre-procedural coagulation screening) survives; the management algorithm has moved on.
5. **GA-based maxillofacial surgery in advanced liver disease**: the toxic-hepatitis death case underscores that internal medicine flagging "high risk" is a hard stop for elective work, not a checkbox to overcome with informed consent. Modern Korean medicolegal climate would treat this case differently.
6. **OCR-extracted text** — for any specific drug-name, dose, or numeric claim, re-read the original PDF; some image regions were not cleanly captured.
7. **No long-term follow-up / no MRONJ / no DOAC / no implant** — predates the 2003 first bisphosphonate ONJ report (Marx); the systemic-disease landscape in dentistry has shifted substantially since 1999.

[wiki-living-document] Use this paper as a historical anchor and a teaching case set, not as a current protocol reference. The disease-specific recommendations were good clinical sense in 1999 and most are still defensible; the anticoagulation guidance specifically is **outdated** and should be replaced with the SR-level evidence linked below.

## See Also

- The bleeding case in this paper would today be analyzed differently: the operator's error is **not** "treated a patient on warfarin without stopping it" (modern practice is generally to continue, controlling locally), but rather "did not perform pre-procedural history and coagulation screening before non-emergent periodontal therapy."
