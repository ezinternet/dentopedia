## One-line Summary
In vitro with human platelets: metamizole (dipyrone) active metabolite MAA forms a hydrogen bond with COX-1 serine-530 → blocks aspirin acetylation → antiplatelet effect lost; same mechanism as ibuprofen and naproxen.

## Summary
This in vitro study from Heinrich-Heine-Universität Düsseldorf is the first to show that dipyrone (metamizole, INN), a widely used pyrazolinone analgesic, interferes with aspirin's antiplatelet effect. The key agent is **MAA (4-methylaminoantipyrine)**, dipyrone's primary active metabolite. MAA blocks aspirin's irreversible acetylation of platelet COX-1 by forming a hydrogen bond with serine-530 — the same residue aspirin must acetylate. Aggregation studies (arachidonic acid- and collagen-induced), thromboxane B2 measurement, P-selectin flow cytometry, and cell-free COX enzyme assays all converged on the same conclusion: pyrazolinones preserve platelet thromboxane synthesis that aspirin would otherwise suppress. Computational docking confirmed the molecular basis.

## Key Contributions
- First demonstration that dipyrone/metamizole blocks aspirin's antiplatelet effect
- Identified MAA as the responsible metabolite; class effect extends to other pyrazolinone derivatives
- Molecular docking: MAA → strong hydrogen bond with serine-530 of COX-1 → steric blockade of aspirin acetylation
- Ibuprofen and naproxen served as positive controls, confirming consistency with established literature
- Clinical message: dipyrone should be considered an aspirin antagonist and avoided in patients on antiplatelet therapy for cardiovascular protection

## Methodology
- Human platelet-rich plasma (PRP) from healthy volunteers
- Aggregation: arachidonic acid (AA)- and collagen-induced; MAA pre-incubated before aspirin addition
- Thromboxane B2 (TXB2) measurement: ELISA in PRP supernatant
- P-selectin expression: flow cytometry
- Cell-free COX-1 activity: luminol-enhanced luminescence, human platelet microsomes
- Computational docking: crystal structure of COX-1; binding energy and hydrogen bond characterization for MAA, aspirin, ibuprofen

## Results
- MAA largely attenuated or completely abolished AA-induced platelet aggregation inhibition by aspirin
- TXB2 suppression by aspirin was prevented by pre-incubation with MAA
- P-selectin reduction by aspirin was blocked by MAA
- Cell-free COX enzyme: MAA attenuated aspirin's COX-1 inhibitory effect at the microsomal level
- Other pyrazolinone derivatives: same pattern as MAA
- Ibuprofen and naproxen: same interference (positive controls)
- Docking: MAA hydrogen-bonds serine-530 in the COX-1 hydrophobic channel, blocking covalent acetylation by aspirin
