## One-line Summary

SR+MA (5 studies, n=162 children <8 years): short-course doxycycline (median 8.5 days) caused dental staining in only 1/162 children (0.62%; 1 premature neonate only), with pooled adverse event proportion of 0.21 (95% CI: 0.13–0.28) and I²=0%, supporting revised AAP/CDC guidance permitting doxycycline use in young children for life-threatening indications such as RMSF.

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42023494713) evaluated the dental safety of doxycycline — a tetracycline-class antibiotic — when used short-term in children under 8 years. The historical basis for the contraindication in young children traces back to 1958 studies linking tetracycline to tooth discoloration through calcium chelation and incorporation into developing dentin. Doxycycline shares this class concern, yet its pharmacokinetics differ meaningfully: it has a significantly lower calcium-binding affinity (19%) compared to tetracycline (39.5%), and requires less frequent dosing and lower total daily doses. Despite the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) and Centers for Disease Control (CDC) revising guidance to endorse doxycycline as first-line therapy for Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (RMSF) in children of any age, many clinicians remain hesitant — contributing to preventable pediatric RMSF mortality.

Across 325 deduplicated citations, only 5 studies (published 1969–2017) met inclusion criteria: three retrospective cohort/case-control studies from the USA, UK, and Finland examining RMSF and CNS infections; one Israeli single-blind RCT for atypical pneumonia; and one Italian retrospective study in premature infants. The combined sample was 162 children exposed to doxycycline before age 8. The median treatment duration was 8.5 days (IQR: 6–12.5), and median dose was 2.3 mg/kg/day. All five studies used formal dental examination methods (spectrophotometry, intraoral photography, direct inspection with shade guides, or fluorescence under Wood's light) with follow-up periods ranging from 1 year to over 22 years post-treatment.

The key finding is that dental staining attributable to doxycycline occurred in only 1 of 162 children (0.62%). This single case was a premature infant under 2 months old (Grossi et al., Italy), which was also the only study rated at critical risk of bias. Among the 137 children old enough to have permanent dentition examined at follow-up, zero cases of staining were found. The two RMSF-focused studies (Todd et al., USA; Vohra et al., UK), both with low/moderate bias, reported 0 staining events in 68 RMSF-treated children. Enamel hypoplasia was observed in 2/68 children (1.23%) in the RMSF cohort, but this was not clearly attributable to doxycycline versus the severity of the underlying illness. The pooled proportion of any dental adverse event was 0.21 (95% CI: 0.13–0.28, I²=0%, fixed-effect model), with no evidence of publication bias (fail-safe N=49, Egger test p=0.454).

## Key Contributions

1. Provides the first SR+MA quantifying dental staining risk from short-course doxycycline specifically in children under 8 years, filling the evidence gap underlying prescriber hesitancy
2. Demonstrates that tooth discoloration from short-course doxycycline (≤10–12 days) is exceedingly rare: 0.62% overall, 0% in permanent dentition, 0% in RMSF studies with low/moderate risk of bias
3. Mechanistically explains the lower staining risk vs. older tetracyclines: doxycycline's calcium-binding affinity is 19% vs. tetracycline's 39.5%, reducing dental incorporation
4. Supports the 2021 AAP Red Book and CDC recommendations endorsing doxycycline as the drug of choice for RMSF in children of all ages, including those under 8
5. Identifies the critical evidence gap: only 5 eligible studies in 50+ years; all are retrospective or small-scale; no prospective study specifically designed to capture dental endpoints

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis; PRISMA guidelines; PROSPERO CRD42023494713
- **Search**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Google Scholar; inception to November 2023
- **Population (PICOT)**: Children <8 years, any ethnicity/sex, treated with doxycycline (alone or in combination)
- **Comparator**: Placebo or other antibiotics
- **Outcomes**: Dental adverse events — tooth discoloration/staining, enamel hypoplasia
- **Follow-up minimum**: ≥1 year
- **Eligible designs**: RCT, cohort, case-control, clinical trials in hospital or community settings
- **Screening**: Two-stage (title/abstract then full-text); dual independent review; third senior reviewer for disagreements; Covidence platform
- **Statistical method**: Freeman–Tukey double arcsine transformation for proportion pooling; fixed-effect model (low heterogeneity); random-effects if heterogeneity significant
- **Bias tools**: Cochrane RoB2 (RCTs); JBI Critical Appraisal (observational studies)
- **Software**: Jamovi 2.4.14

## Results

| Outcome | Finding |
|---|---|
| Tooth staining (all) | 1/162 (0.62%) — premature infant only |
| Tooth staining (permanent teeth, n=137) | 0/137 (0%) |
| Tooth staining (RMSF only, n=68) | 0/68 (0%) |
| Enamel hypoplasia | 2/68 (1.23%) — RMSF cohort |
| Pooled adverse event proportion | 0.21 (95% CI: 0.13–0.28) |
| Heterogeneity I² | 0% (Q=0.954, p=0.917) → fixed-effect |
| Publication bias | None significant (Kendall's Tau p=0.233; Egger p=0.454) |
| Fail-safe N | 49 |

**Pharmacological context**:
- Doxycycline calcium-binding: 19% vs tetracycline 39.5% → lower dental incorporation risk
- Median dose: 2.3 mg/kg/day (IQR: 1.525–5.438)
- Median duration: 8.5 days (IQR: 6–12.5)
- Median age at exposure: 4.25 years (IQR: 2.065–5.563)

**Risk of bias across 5 studies**: 1 low (Todd), 3 moderate (Vohra, Peltola, Amsden), 1 critical (Grossi — the only study with any positive staining finding)

**Clinical implication**: Prescribers should not withhold doxycycline from children under 8 for life-threatening indications (RMSF, scrub typhus, leptospirosis) based on staining concerns — the evidence base does not support that risk for short courses. For non-life-threatening common pediatric infections, alternatives remain appropriate per AAP guidance.
