## One-line Summary
Narrative review: in inflammatory arthritis (RA/AS), NSAIDs do NOT increase cardiovascular (CV) risk as they do in the general population and may be cardioprotective via suppression of systemic inflammatory burden — indirect support for not over-restricting dental NSAID prescribing in RA/AS patients on CV-risk grounds.

## Summary
Narrative review examining whether NSAIDs increase cardiovascular (CV) risk specifically in patients with inflammatory arthritis (rheumatoid arthritis [RA] and ankylosing spondylitis [AS]). In the general population, NSAIDs are associated with increased CV risk — well established. However, in inflammatory arthritis patients, NSAID use does NOT appear to increase CV risk and may be cardioprotective: by reducing systemic inflammatory burden (elevated in RA/AS), NSAIDs may mitigate the inherent CV morbidity associated with these diseases. Oslo RA registry data show coordinated RA treatment (MTX + TNFi) reduces mortality; NSAID use remains higher in RA/AS patients than in the general population even post-biologic therapy, reflecting ongoing pain despite inflammation control.

## Key Contributions
- Clearly distinguishes CV risk profile of NSAIDs in general population vs inflammatory arthritis
- RA and AS patients: NSAID use not associated with increased CV risk; potential cardioprotective mechanism via anti-inflammatory effect
- Pain in RA is weakly correlated with synovial inflammation — justifies ongoing NSAID use beyond disease control
- First author has industry affiliation (MSD) — interpret conclusions with appropriate caution
- Dental relevance: RA/AS patients commonly present to dental clinic; NSAID prescribing for postoperative pain does not carry the excess CV risk assumed by the general population data

## Methodology
Narrative review. Registry data (Oslo RA registry) and other observational data. No systematic search protocol. No quality appraisal.

## Results
| Population | NSAIDs + CV Risk |
