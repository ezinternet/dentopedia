## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 7 RCTs (5 quantitatively pooled): ketorolac significantly outperforms comparator analgesics for post-endodontic pain at 6 h (SMD -0.82, p<0.00001) and halves rescue-medication need (RR 0.50), but the advantage vanishes by 12 and 24 h.

## Summary

Ping et al. (2026) conducted the first systematic review + meta-analysis isolating **ketorolac** (rather than NSAIDs as a class) for the control of pain after root canal treatment. From 386 records they included 7 RCTs (published 1994–2024) qualitatively and pooled 5 quantitatively. The bottom line is a **time-resolved efficacy window**: ketorolac is significantly better than comparator analgesics (dexamethasone, ibuprofen, tapentadol, etodolac, prednisolone) at **6 h** post-administration, but by 12 and 24 h the difference is no longer significant — consistent with ketorolac's ~6 h plasma half-life and faster time-to-peak than its comparators. Independently, fewer patients needed rescue medication after ketorolac (RR 0.50). The authors support ketorolac as a favorable option for early post-endodontic pain but caution that the evidence base is small and heterogeneous.

## Key Contributions

- **Drug-specific synthesis**: first SR+MA on ketorolac alone for post-endodontic pain, spanning oral, local-injection (periapical/intracanal/IM), and intranasal routes.
- **Early-window superiority quantified**: SMD -0.82 at 6 h (significant); NS at 12 h and 24 h.
- **Rescue-medication benefit**: RR 0.50 (I²=0%) — the most robust pooled estimate in the review.
- Highlights **intranasal ketorolac (Sprix)** as the only nasally-delivered NSAID and a noninvasive endodontic-pain option.

## Methodology

- PRISMA-compliant; pre-registered PROSPERO CRD42024535882.
- 10 databases (PubMed, WoS, ScienceDirect, Cochrane, Embase, Scopus, SinoMed, CNKI, Webvpn, Wanfang); search deadline ~August 2025.
- RCTs only (PICOS). Cochrane Handbook 5.1.0 risk-of-bias (7 domains), dual independent review.
- RevMan 5.3: inverse-variance random-effects model with **SMD** for continuous outcomes; **RR** fixed-effect Mantel-Haenszel for dichotomous (rescue medication). Heterogeneity via I² / Cochran Q.
- Flow: 386 → 290 (dedup) → 13 (screen) → 7 qualitative → 5 quantitative.

## Results

| Outcome | Studies (n trials) | Effect | 95% CI | p | I² |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pain at **6 h** | 3 | SMD = **-0.82** | -1.18 to -0.46 | <0.00001 | 47% |
| Pain at 12 h | 2 | SMD = -0.29 (NS) | -1.36 to 0.78 | 0.59 | 92% |
| Pain at 24 h | 3 | SMD = -0.25 (NS) | -1.07 to 0.56 | 0.54 | 90% |
| **Rescue medication** needed | 3 | RR = **0.50** | 0.35–0.73 | 0.0002 | 0% |

- **Qualitative**: 5/7 favored ketorolac; Rogers et al. found no difference vs ibuprofen/dexamethasone; Praveen et al. found prednisolone more sustained over time.
- **Risk of bias**: 3 low-risk studies (Akhlaghi, Praveen, Sekhar); allocation concealment unclear in 4; Rogers high-risk for blinding (differing administration methods).
- **Mechanism**: ketorolac inhibits cyclooxygenase → reduces prostaglandins; rapid onset (IM/IV ~10 min, IN peak ~45 min, oral 30–60 min) explains 6 h superiority; ~6 h half-life explains 12 h convergence.
- **Caveat**: the 12 h and 24 h pooled estimates carry I² ≈ 90%, so their null findings are unstable.
- **Side effects** (only Sethi reported): mild headache, dizziness, sweating, injection-site pain.
