## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 3 case-control studies in pediatric CHD patients found very low GRADE evidence that antibiotic prophylaxis before dental procedures prevents infective endocarditis (OR 3.63, ns) but does reduce bacteremia (pooled OR 0.24, p<0.05).

## Summary

This systematic review (Loper et al. 2025, *Special Care in Dentistry*) addresses a specific gap in the literature: while prior reviews on antibiotic prophylaxis (AP) for infective endocarditis (IE) prevention focused on adults, no systematic review had examined the evidence specifically for **pediatric patients with congenital heart disease (CHD)** undergoing dental procedures. Six major databases were searched through April 2024 with no date or language restrictions; 1,981 records were identified, 1,774 screened after deduplication, and ultimately only 3 case-control studies met inclusion criteria — revealing a stark evidence vacuum in this clinically important population.

The three included studies evaluated outcomes in two separate streams. For IE (primary outcome), only one nested case-control study (Sun et al., Taiwan, n=24,729 CHD children) was available, finding that dental procedures were not significantly associated with IE risk regardless of AP status. The OR for IE when AP was given before dental procedures was 3.63 (95% CI: 0.98–13.47; p=0.05) — non-significant but trend-unfavorable, likely reflecting confounding by indication (sicker patients receiving both more dental procedures and AP). For bacteremia (secondary outcome), two older Irish studies using blood culture after extractions found AP significantly reduced bacteremia: pooled OR 0.24 (95% CI: 0.14–0.42; p<0.05), with zero heterogeneity (I²=0%).

The GRADE certainty for both outcomes was rated **very low** (⊕◯◯◯), downgraded due to serious risk of bias in study designs, imprecision (optimal information size not met), and the absence of any RCTs. No RCTs were identified, and the authors note that conducting a RCT withholding AP from high-risk CHD children raises insurmountable ethical barriers. The review concludes that despite the empirical evidence being insufficient to make a formal recommendation for or against AP in pediatric CHD, continued guideline recommendations in high-risk patients are probably justified by the severity of IE consequences, pathophysiological plausibility, and expert consensus — essentially a precautionary stance.

## Key Contributions

1. First SR focused exclusively on pediatric CHD patients and AP for dental procedures — prior SRs either targeted adults or used children as a subgroup.
2. Identified that only 3 eligible studies exist despite comprehensive 6-database search, quantifying the evidence vacuum in this population.
3. Demonstrated that bacteremia after dental extractions is significantly reduced by AP (OR 0.24; I²=0%) — but noted that bacteremia is not a validated surrogate marker for IE.
4. Confirmed that IE as an outcome in pediatric CHD cannot be meta-analyzed (1 study only) — OR 3.63 (ns), likely confounded by indication.
5. GRADE analysis: very low certainty for both outcomes — guideline recommendations currently rest on expert consensus and ethical/practical considerations, not empirical RCT-level evidence.
6. Identified that AP antibiotic regimens in the two bacteremia studies are outdated (penicillin/erythromycin era), limiting applicability to current amoxicillin 2g pre-op protocols.

## Results

### Included Studies

| Study | n | Dental Procedure | Antibiotic Used | Outcome | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Elliott & Dunbar (Ireland) | 217 | Extractions | Benzylpenicillin (primarily) | Bacteremia | AP group: 11% positive cultures vs 36% healthy controls |
| Coulter et al. (Ireland) | 58 | Extractions | IM/PO/IV penicillin, amoxicillin, IV erythromycin | Bacteremia | AP group: 35% positive vs 63% non-AP controls (p<0.05) |
| Sun et al. (Taiwan) | 24,729 CHD children; 237 IE cases, 4,725 controls | Extractions, endo, perio, oral surgery | Not specified | IE (nested case-control, 1997–2010) | No significant association between dental procedure ± AP and IE incidence |

### Primary Outcome — IE

- Single eligible study (Sun et al.): adjusted OR for IE with AP = **1.31** (95% CI: 0.64–2.66; p=0.459) vs no dental procedure
- Adjusted OR without AP = 0.35 (95% CI: 0.11–1.17; p=0.088)
- When pooled as AP vs no-AP: OR = **3.63** (95% CI: 0.98–13.47; p=0.05) — not significant
- GRADE: ⊕◯◯◯ Very low

### Secondary Outcome — Bacteremia

- Pooled OR (Mantel-Haenszel, fixed effects): **0.24** (95% CI: 0.14–0.42; p<0.05)
- Heterogeneity: I² = 0.00% (p=0.56) — homogeneous
- GRADE: ⊕◯◯◯ Very low

### Context: Comparison with Adult SR

- Sperotto et al. 2024 (JAMA Cardiology, adults): pooled RR for IE with AP = **0.41** (95% CI: 0.29–0.57) in high-risk cardiac patients — protective effect
- Adult guideline review: 3/4 major guidelines (ESC, AHA, Japanese Circulation Society — not NICE) support AP for high-risk patients
- NICE 2008 (UK): removal of AP recommendation associated with ~35 additional IE cases/year in high-risk patients in England
