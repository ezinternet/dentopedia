## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional survey of 280 Singapore dentists (2024): appropriate antibiotic prescribing ranged 6.5-97.7% across scenarios (lowest periodontal 30.4%, oral surgery 34.0%), 71.2% over-prescribed prophylaxis after third-molar surgery and 73.5% before implants, public-sector/locally-trained/junior dentists prescribed more appropriately, and AMR-mechanism knowledge was consistently poor (59.3% unaware resistant bacteria spread easily).

## Summary

The first comprehensive Singapore study of dental antibiotic prescribing, knowledge, and attitudes. Globally, dentists issue ~10% of all antibiotic prescriptions and up to 80% are inappropriate, yet a 2024 multinational systematic review found no Singapore data. This 2024 online survey of registered Singapore dentists (n = 280, 10.4% of the workforce) measured appropriateness against prescribing guidelines and identified clinician characteristics predicting appropriate prescribing.

The headline finding is wide variability and systematic over-prescription in surgical/periodontal contexts: appropriate prescribing ranged from 6.5% to 97.7% across scenarios, lowest for periodontal (30.4%) and oral surgery (34.0%) and highest for restorative (58.3%) and medically complex patients (56.6%). Prophylaxis was the worst offender — 71.2% routinely prescribed after third-molar surgery in healthy patients and 73.5% before implant placement, both unsupported by evidence. Despite an 80% positive attitude toward AMR, AMR-mechanism knowledge was poor across all groups (59.3% unaware resistant bacteria transmit easily). Public-sector, locally-trained, and lower-experience dentists prescribed more appropriately. Nearly all (88.2%) wanted further training, preferring local guidelines (83.6%) and mobile apps (70.4%).

## Key Contributions

- First Singapore (and a rare Asian) comprehensive dental antibiotic prescribing + AMR-knowledge survey; uniquely assesses AMR-mechanism knowledge, not measured in prior surveys.
- Maps appropriate-prescribing variability (6.5-97.7%) onto clinical categories and pinpoints prophylaxis over-prescription as the dominant problem.
- Identifies modifiable predictors (practice sector, experience, training location, sex) via multivariable logistic regression to target future interventions.
- Documents dentists' preferred intervention modalities (local guidelines > mobile apps > conventional lectures).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Cross-sectional online survey (Qualtrics), 2024, convenience sampling via SDA / CGDP / specialist association mailing lists; anonymous, voluntary.
- **Instrument**: 5 sections (~50 items) — demographics, prescribing preferences, clinical & nonclinical scenarios (restorative / oral surgery / periodontal & implant / medically complex), AMR knowledge & attitudes, knowledge sources & training needs.
- **Appropriateness**: scored against an earlier study, local guidelines, and scenario-specific references; per-respondent % per category (excluding "do not do procedure").
- **Analysis**: t-tests / one-way ANOVA (Bonferroni) for group comparisons; multivariable logistic regression for per-scenario predictors. SPSS v27, p < .05.
- **Sample**: target 337 (population 2690, 95% CI, 5% margin); achieved **n = 280** (72 incomplete excluded; ~5.5% margin with finite population correction).

## Results

**Respondents (n = 280):** 79.6% under 50 yo; 60.4% female; 75% locally trained; 34.6% public sector; 32.9% specialists.

**Prescribing preferences:**
- 5-day course most common (86.8%).
- Amoxicillin / Augmentin most prescribed (97.1%).
- Multidrug regimens always/often by 14.9%; most common combo amoxicillin + metronidazole (89.0%).

**Appropriate prescribing by category (mean ± SD):**

| Category | Appropriate % |
|---|---|
| Restorative | 58.3% ± 28.6% (highest) |
| Medically complex patients | 56.6% ± 18.0% |
| Oral surgery | 34.0% ± 29.0% |
| Periodontal | 30.4% ± 37.5% (lowest) |
| Across all scenarios | range 6.5% - 97.7% |

**Over-prescription hotspots:**
- 71.2% always/often prescribe prophylaxis after impacted third-molar removal in healthy patients (a local intervention cut use 84%→21% with no rise in infection).
- 73.5% routinely prescribe prophylaxis before implant placement (absolute risk reduction only 2.9%; no benefit in healthy patients).
- Inappropriate for pulp necrosis with acute apical abscess 40.1%; localised periodontal abscess 24.7%.

**Medical-indication prophylaxis (4 conditions, all needing none):** only 25.4% correct; mitral valve prolapse misidentified by 52.9%, unrepaired septal defect by 62.5%.

**Nonclinical pressures:** only 21.2-37.5% consistently appropriate under patient request / time pressure / anaesthesia difficulty / diagnostic uncertainty; 6.8% defensive prescribing.

**Predictors:** public-sector, locally-trained, and fewer-experience dentists generally more appropriate; senior dentists (≥21 yrs) more often inappropriate (esp. prophylaxis: total joint replacement OR 0.30, p=.001) but more defensive (OR 0.38). Public-sector exceptions: implant placement (OR 0.16, p<.001), head/neck radiotherapy extraction (OR 0.34).

**AMR knowledge & attitudes:** 80% positive attitude; 59.3% unaware resistant bacteria spread easily; mechanism knowledge poor across all groups.

**Training:** only 25.7% had prior training; 88.2% want more; preferred local guidelines (83.6%) and mobile apps (70.4%). Local CPG (Academy of Medicine Singapore) released mid-collection but poorly known. Authors note guideline dissemination alone is insufficient — COM-B / audit + personalised-feedback strategies needed.
