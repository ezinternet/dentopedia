## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 23 studies (1997–2023) shows antibiotic stewardship interventions — especially audit+feedback and audit+education — reduce inappropriate antibiotic prescribing among dentists by 70% (95% CI 33.3%–86.4%), with RCT-only subgroup showing 63.9% reduction; evidence quality is low due to predominance of uncontrolled pre-post designs.

## 한줄요약

항생제 청지기 SR+MA (23개 연구, 1997–2023): 감사+피드백 (Audit and Feedback, A&F) 및 감사+교육 기반 중재가 치과의사의 부적절 처방을 70% 감소시키며, 이는 임상에서 실행 가능한 가장 효과적인 항균제 내성 (Antimicrobial Resistance, AMR) 대응 전략임.

## Summary

Dentists account for approximately 10% of all antibiotic prescriptions worldwide, with inappropriate prescribing rates estimated between 50% and 80%. Given the growing global burden of antimicrobial resistance (AMR), stewardship interventions targeting dental prescribers have become a public health priority. This systematic review and meta-analysis by Méndez-Romero et al. (2025) provides the first quantitative synthesis of the effectiveness of interventions designed to improve antibiotic use among dentists, updating a 2016 non-quantitative systematic review by Löffler et al. The authors searched six major databases (MEDLINE, SCOPUS, EMBASE, COCHRANE CENTRAL, LILACS, BBO) through 2023 and included 23 studies in qualitative synthesis and 10 in the meta-analysis.

The meta-analysis found that interventions overall reduced inappropriate antibiotic prescribing by 70% (95% CI: 33.3%–86.4%). Stratified by study design, pre-post studies (n=8) showed a 71% reduction (I²=99.2%), while the two RCTs showed a 63.9% reduction (I²=0%). Stratified by intervention type, audit+education achieved 73.3% reduction (95% CI 44%–87.4%) and audit+feedback achieved 75.0% (95% CI 33%–91.4%). These magnitudes are clinically impressive, though the very high heterogeneity in pre-post analyses (I²=99.2%) and the small number of RCTs substantially limit confidence in the estimates.

The quality of the evidence is rated as low. Twenty of 23 studies used pre-post designs without concurrent control groups, making them susceptible to regression to the mean, Hawthorne effects, and secular trends. Only three RCTs were identified, two of which provided quantitative data for meta-analysis. Most studies originated from the United Kingdom, limiting global generalizability. The authors note that Latin American countries — which have among the highest rates of inappropriate antibiotic prescribing — have not published intervention studies, representing a critical gap. Despite these limitations, the consistency of the directional finding across diverse settings, time periods, and intervention formats supports the conclusion that dentists are receptive to behavioral change through stewardship programs, and that audit-based interventions with active feedback are the most effective delivery mechanism.

## Key Contributions

1. **First meta-analysis** specifically evaluating the effect of stewardship interventions on antibiotic prescribing by dentists.
2. Quantified a **70% overall reduction** in inappropriate antibiotic prescribing (95% CI: 33.3%–86.4%) across intervention types.
3. Identified **audit+feedback (75%)** and **audit+education (73.3%)** as the most effective individual intervention categories.
4. Demonstrated **RCT-level efficacy of 63.9%** (I²=0%) — more reliable estimate from only 2 RCTs.
5. Highlighted that **active/personalized feedback** (face-to-face, individual report cards) outperforms passive strategies (email, guidelines alone).
6. Identified **emerging interventions** not yet evaluated in dentistry: nudge strategies, AI-based decision support, e-health prescribing tools.
7. Underscored the need for **higher-quality RCTs** and **studies from under-researched regions** (Latin America, Africa).

## Methodology

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design | Systematic review + meta-analysis (PRISMA) |
| PROSPERO | CRD42023474664 |
| Databases | MEDLINE, SCOPUS, EMBASE, COCHRANE CENTRAL, LILACS, BBO |
| Search period | Inception to 2023 |
| Studies included | 23 qualitative; 10 in meta-analysis |
| Study designs | 3 RCTs; 20 pre-post; retrospective cohorts |
| Setting | Predominantly general dentistry (2 studies: oral surgery, endodontics) |
| Population | Dentists (not patients) |
| Intervention | Stewardship interventions (audit, education, feedback, digital tools, government strategies) |
| Primary outcome | Appropriate antibiotic prescriptions (pre vs. post) |
| Statistical model | Random effects; I² for heterogeneity |
| Effect measure | Relative risk reduction (analogous to vaccine efficacy %) |
| Risk of bias tool | The Evidence Project (RCT and non-RCT compatible) |

## Results

**Overall meta-analysis:**

| Subgroup | Reduction in Inappropriate Prescribing | 95% CI | I² |
|---|---|---|---|
| All interventions (n=10) | 70% | 33.3%–86.4% | High |
| Pre-post studies (n=8) | 71% | 28.8%–88.1% | 99.2% |
| RCTs (n=2) | 63.9% | 41%–78.1% | 0% |
| Audit + education | 73.3% | 44%–87.4% | 69% |
| Audit + feedback | 75.0% | 33%–91.4% | 99% |

**Key individual study results:**

| Study | Country | N | Design | Intervention | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Steed & Gibson 1997 | UK | 320 Rx | Pre-post | Consensus | 50% reduction in prescriptions |
| Palmer 2001 | UK | 175 | Pre-post | Audit+feedback | 57.4% → 70.5% appropriate |
| Seager 2005 | UK | 97 | RCT | Guidelines vs. pharmacist visits | 37% → 67% appropriate (pharmacist arm) |
| Chate 2006 | UK | 212 | Pre-post | Audit+education+feedback | 43.6% reduction |
| Elouafkaoui 2016 | UK | 795 | RCT | Audit+feedback ± message | Significant reduction |
| Kim 2017 | Korea | 22,098 Rx | Pre-post | Guidelines + removal of prescription button | Behavioral change + reduced rates |
| Gross 2019 | USA | — | Pre-post | Multimodal | 72% decrease (urgent care) |
| Teoh 2020 | Australia | 26 | Pre-post | Education + online prescribing tool | 44.6% reduction |
| Goff 2022 | USA | 15 | Pre-post | Expert audit + individual feedback | 19% → 88% appropriate |
| Lund 2020 | Sweden | 1,276,203 Rx | Retrospective | Government strategies | Sustained reduction over 8 years |

**Background context**: Dentists prescribe ~10% of all antibiotics globally. Inappropriate prescribing rates: 50%–80%. Clindamycin carries the highest rate of fatal and non-fatal adverse drug reactions among dental antibiotics.

**Prophylaxis dosing referenced**: Amoxicillin 2g PO 1h pre-procedure; clindamycin 600mg for beta-lactam allergy.
