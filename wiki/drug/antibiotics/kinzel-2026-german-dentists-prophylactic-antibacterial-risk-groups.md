## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional online survey of 537 German dentists (~0.5% of the profession): prophylactic antibacterial-drug use is generally restrained but inconsistent — a positive trend only in endocarditis-risk and immunosuppression/organ-transplant groups, a negative trend to negative consensus in diabetes and joint replacement; amoxicillin first-line and clindamycin second-line across all four groups.

## Summary

Kinzel & Seifert surveyed German dentists on how often they prescribe **systemic prophylactic antibacterial drugs (ABD)** across four medical risk groups and a range of dental procedures, then benchmarked the answers against German-language guidelines. From 1070 participants, **537 fully completed questionnaires** were analyzed (~0.5% of Germany's ~101,344 dentists). Each item used a 6-level frequency scale; the authors classified responses as **positive/negative consensus** (≥80% in one direction) or **trend** (50–80%).

The dominant finding is **restrained but highly inconsistent** prescribing. The four risk groups split cleanly:

- **Higher prophylaxis (positive trends):** endocarditis risk and immunosuppression/organ transplant.
- **Lower prophylaxis (negative trend → negative consensus):** diabetes and joint replacement.

The **only positive consensus in the entire survey** was for prosthetic heart valves (72% always). Drug choice was uniform regardless of group: **amoxicillin first-line, clindamycin second-line**, matching guideline recommendations (clindamycin reserved for penicillin allergy). Notably ~75% of dentists reported feeling confident with prophylactic ABD despite the heterogeneous behavior. The authors echo Italian, Swiss, and Saudi surveys: guidelines are deviated from in practice, procedures lacking guidelines invite precautionary over-administration, and binding RCT-based guidance is needed.

## Methodology

- **Design**: cross-sectional, online-only self-report survey (SoSci platform), Oct 2023–Mar 2024.
- **Sample**: 537 complete responses / 1070 participants; recruited via 7 state dental associations.
- **Instrument**: 4 sections (endocarditis risk, joint replacement, diabetes, immunosuppression/organ transplant), each crossing condition subtypes with dental-procedure types; frequency scale never→always plus "not specified"; most/second-most ABD; confidence (yes/no); state-exam year.
- **Analysis**: descriptive; ≥80% agreement = consensus, 50–80% = trend, else inconclusive; compared to DGZMK, DGK, AE, and German Diabetes Society guidance.

## Results

**By risk group (prophylaxis tendency):**

| Group | Overall signal | Guideline benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Endocarditis risk | Positive consensus (prosthetic valve only); positive trend for survived IE, heart transplant | Largely follows DGZMK/DGK (AHA-derived); deviates for gingival manipulation, LA injection |
| Immunosuppression / organ transplant | Positive trend (disease, meds, post-transplant; +extraction, implantation, bone) | DGZMK S2k recommends ABD before dentoalveolar procedures; not followed for periodontal/trauma |
| Joint replacement | No positive signal; negative consensus for soft-tissue/minor procedures | Discrepancy vs AE (single preop 2 g amoxicillin for invasive procedures) |
| Diabetes | Most negative-consensus items; negative against ABD in type 1 & 2 DM | Only implantation guideline (DGZMK + Diabetes Society) recommends ABD — deviated |

**Procedure pattern (consistent across groups):** higher prophylaxis for extraction, implantation, periodontal, and bone-involving procedures; **consensus against** ABD for postoperative suture removal, local-anesthetic injection, mucosal/gingival injury, and PA-pocket probing (dentists perceive low bacteremia risk for these).

**Drug choice:** amoxicillin most frequent, clindamycin second, in all four groups. German S2k/AE dosing where specified = single 2 g amoxicillin (0.6 g clindamycin as alternative).

**Endocarditis specifics:** prosthetic valve 72% always; survived bacterial endocarditis 68%; heart transplant 69%. Despite AHA/DGZMK/AE guidance, <50% would treat in most cyanotic defects, congenital cyanotic disease, acquired valvular dysfunction, and pulmonary shunts.

**Implantation caveat:** no group reached consensus, but ~¼ answered "not specified" (many dentists place no implants); excluding non-placers often yields at least a positive trend, aligning with German implant guidelines.

**Limitations:** only ~0.5% coverage; voluntary/anonymous/unmonitored (self-selection, social-desirability, possible multiple responses); self-reported behavior rather than actual prescriptions; ABD choices capped at 10 options.
