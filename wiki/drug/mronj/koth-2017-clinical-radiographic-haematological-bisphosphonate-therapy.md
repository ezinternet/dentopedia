## One-line Summary

Retrospective BRONJ vs non-BRONJ comparison in bisphosphonate users: alkaline phosphatase and ESR were significantly higher in BRONJ, with associations to smoking, tooth extraction, anaemia and leukocytosis, plus radiographic persisting alveolar socket, osteolysis, bone sclerosis and mandibular canal narrowing; CTX, PTH, calcium and phosphorus did not differ.

## Summary

This retrospective study (Dentomaxillofacial Radiology, 2017) examined how clinical, radiographic, and haematological/biochemical features interrelate in patients on bisphosphonate therapy, comparing those who developed bisphosphonate-related osteonecrosis of the jaw (BRONJ) against those who did not. The central diagnostic message is that BRONJ is multifactorial: no single marker is definitive, but a cluster of laboratory, clinical, and panoramic/periapical findings tends to co-occur in affected patients.

Notably, the resorption marker **CTX** — frequently invoked for MRONJ risk stratification — did **not** significantly differ between groups, while **alkaline phosphatase** and **ESR** did. This cautions against over-reliance on CTX alone and points to inflammation/bone-activity markers as additional signals.

> Content is grounded in the paper's structured abstract only (full text paywalled). No sample size or effect-size statistics were recoverable; findings are reported as the significant vs non-significant variables stated by the authors.

## Key Contributions

- Cross-correlates three diagnostic domains in a single bisphosphonate cohort, separating discriminating features from non-discriminating ones for BRONJ.
- Flags **alkaline phosphatase** and **ESR** as elevated in BRONJ, and reports **CTX, PTH, calcium, phosphorus, and fasting glucose** as non-discriminating in this sample.
- Provides a panoramic/periapical interpretation checklist: which radiographic signs carry diagnostic weight for BRONJ.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Retrospective record review of bisphosphonate patients, grouped by BRONJ occurrence.
- **Imaging**: Panoramic + periapical radiographs scored for bone sclerosis, osteolysis, persisting alveolar socket, mandibular-canal narrowing, periodontal-ligament-space widening, periradicular radiolucency, sequestrum, and lamina dura thickening.
- **Labs**: CBC, fasting serum glucose, ESR, and serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, PTH, CTX.
- Sample size, bisphosphonate type/route/duration, and statistical detail not stated in available abstract.

## Results

| Domain | Significantly associated with BRONJ | No significant difference |
|---|---|---|
| Biochemical/Haematological | Alkaline phosphatase ↑, ESR ↑, anaemia, leukocytosis | Fasting glucose, CTX, PTH, calcium, phosphorus |
| Clinical | Smoking, tooth extraction | — |
| Radiographic | Persisting alveolar socket, osteolysis, bone sclerosis, mandibular-canal narrowing | Lamina dura thickening, periapical radiolucency, PDL-space widening, sequestrum |

**Conclusion**: BRONJ is a multifactorial, high-morbidity disease; the authors call for experimental studies to clarify the mechanistic role of the reported risk factors and radiographic signs to improve diagnosis.

## Clinical Bottom Line

- In bisphosphonate patients, **raised ALP/ESR plus anaemia/leukocytosis** alongside panoramic signs of **persisting (non-healing) sockets, osteolysis, sclerosis, or mandibular-canal narrowing** should raise suspicion of BRONJ — especially with a recent extraction or smoking history.
- **CTX alone is insufficient** for risk stratification on this evidence; interpret it within a broader clinical-radiographic picture.
