## Summary
Single-author narrative review by Cas Weykamp (MCA Laboratory, Netherlands) covering the full landscape of HbA1c assays. The review groups methods into two principles — **separation methods** (Ion Exchange Chromatography, Capillary Electrophoresis, Affinity Chromatography) based on physicochemical differences between glycated and non-glycated Hb, and **chemical reaction methods** (Immunoassay, Enzymatic) based on specific binding or enzymatic cleavage. It describes the dual standardization architecture (IFCC Reference Measurement Procedure as gold standard, NGSP-traceable to DCCT as clinical anchor) and the resulting dual reporting in % (NGSP) vs mmol/mol (IFCC). Clinical sections cover diagnostic application (≥6.5% threshold), pregnancy use, and Point-of-Care (POC) testing strengths (convenience) and weaknesses (variable quality from non-laboratory staff).

## Key Contributions
- **Method classification framework**: separation vs chemical reaction, each with sub-method strengths/weaknesses tabulated.
- **Hb variant interference catalog**: 99% of variants fall into S/C/D/E categories with distinct geographic prevalence; HbF and β-thalassemia produce method-specific interference.
- **Standardization architecture**: NGSP (US, %) traced to DCCT vs IFCC (international, mmol/mol) RMP — explains dual reporting.
- **Quality management blueprint** for clinical labs: accreditation, internal QC, EQA/PT participation, pre/post-analytical controls.
- **POC testing critique**: convenience benefit vs reliability concerns — directly relevant to dental chairside use.

## Methodology
- Single-author narrative review, no systematic search reporting (no PRISMA, no quality assessment).
- Five analytical methods described qualitatively with strengths/weaknesses table.
- Hb variant interference reviewed with reference to NGSP method-specific evaluation reports (https://ngsp.org).
- Clinical applications section covers diagnosis, monitoring, pregnancy, and POC settings.

## Results
**Method-specific characteristics**:

| Method | Detects variants | Throughput | POC suitable | Key weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IEC (HPLC) | Yes | High | No | Variant interference; standalone instrument |
| CE | Yes | Moderate | No | Parallel capillary consistency |
| AC (boronate) | No | High | Possible | Total glycated Hb, not specifically HbA1c |
| Immunoassay | Partial | High | Yes | Two parallel tests required |
| Enzymatic | No | High | Yes | Variant-independent (advantage in mixed populations) |

**Reference ranges**:
- Normal: 4–6% (20–42 mmol/mol)
- Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4% (39–46 mmol/mol)
- Diabetes diagnosis: ≥6.5% (≥48 mmol/mol)

**Hb variant prevalence (99% of all variants)**:
- S: Black African/American, Mediterranean, Indian
- C: Black African/American
- E: Southeast Asian
- D: globally distributed

**HbF interference**: with Immunoassay HbA1c decreases by ~1%; with AC glycation rate ~1/3 of HbA.

**Analytical performance goal**: total CV <2%, bias from RMP <2%.

## Clinical Implications for Dentistry
- **Chairside HbA1c POC testing**: convenient for diabetic dental patient screening before surgery; however review explicitly flags quality concerns when non-laboratory staff operate POC devices. Practical recommendation — use POC for screening, refer to lab for diagnostic-grade measurement before high-risk surgery (implant, multiple extractions).
- **Population-specific variant interference**: for patients of Southeast Asian (HbE), African (HbS/C), or Mediterranean/Indian (HbS) descent, choose enzymatic or BAC-based POC over IEC/IA to avoid variant-induced bias.
- **Standardization matters**: when interpreting external lab results, verify whether reported in NGSP (%) or IFCC (mmol/mol) — conversion: NGSP = 0.09148 × IFCC + 2.152.
