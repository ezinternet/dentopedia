## One-line Summary

Narrative review (12 included studies) of oral manifestations and dental management in pediatric CKD, hemodialysis, and renal transplantation — paradoxically LOW caries (high salivary urea/buffering) but HIGH enamel defects (31-83%), gingival/periodontal disease, calculus, xerostomia, and drug-induced gingival overgrowth; key management levers are dialysis-day-after timing, bleeding-risk control, fluoride contraindication, and the 6-month post-transplant elective-care moratorium.

## Summary

This narrative review (Elhusseiny & Saleh, 2024; 12 studies synthesized through a PRISMA-style funnel) opens the renal/CKD axis of medically-compromised dentistry for the wiki. It addresses three overlapping populations — children with chronic kidney disease (CKD), those on hemodialysis, and renal-transplant recipients — and frames oral health as an under-recognized component of holistic CKD care.

The central paradox is **caries**: despite high-carbohydrate protein-sparing diets, CKD children show *lower* caries and more caries-free individuals than healthy peers. The mechanism is salivary chemistry — elevated salivary urea is hydrolyzed to ammonia + CO₂, raising oral pH above the enamel-demineralization threshold; salivary buffering capacity is high; urea is mildly antibacterial; and cariogenic flora (S. mutans, lactobacilli) is reduced. This protection is **not durable**: after successful transplant the pH normalizes and biofilm shifts cariogenic, so previously caries-free children become at-risk.

Against that low-caries backdrop sits a **high burden of other oral disease**: developmental enamel defects/hypoplasia (31-83% incidence, from hypocalcemia, low active vitamin D, and high phosphate/PTH/fluoride during mineralization), gingival and periodontal disease, heavy calculus (salivary urea/pH/phosphorus precipitating calcium phosphate/oxalate), delayed tooth eruption, xerostomia, uremic odor/metallic taste, and characteristic radiographic findings (loss of lamina dura, ground-glass bone, hypercementosis from renal osteodystrophy).

The management half of the paper is the clinically actionable core — see Results.

**Scope caveat:** the evidence is pediatric and the authors flag the small included-study count and selection bias as the chief limitations; some included studies span well into adulthood (e.g., 16-70 y), so apparently "pediatric" thresholds rest on mixed-age data.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes the salivary-urea mechanism behind paradoxically low caries in CKD, and warns it reverses post-transplant.
- Quantifies the high-prevalence manifestations (enamel defects 31-83%, gingival bleeding up to 95.8%, periodontal pockets 70.3%).
- Delivers a staged dental-management protocol (pretreatment + treatment + hemodialysis-specific + transplant-specific).
- Codifies the two highest-yield clinical rules: **treat the day after dialysis** and **no elective dental care in the first 6 months post-transplant**.

## Methodology

- **Design**: narrative review with PRISMA-flow-documented selection (2 independent reviewers); narrative synthesis, no meta-analysis.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar.
- **Funnel**: 54 records → 18 duplicates removed → 36 screened → 22 eligibility-assessed → 10 excluded → **12 included**.
- **Inclusion**: pediatric CKD/hemodialysis/renal-transplant oral manifestations; peer-reviewed; English. **Exclusion**: adult-only, case reports/series, off-topic.

## Results

**Oral-manifestation benchmarks (from the 12 included studies)**

| Manifestation | Representative findings |
|---|---|
| Caries (protective) | Caries-free 40% (CRF) vs 8.5% (controls); DMFT 0.5-0.85; salivary urea 11.6 vs 3.6 mmol/L; buffering pH 6.4 vs 5.6 |
| Enamel defects | 31% (Koch) to 83% diffuse opacities (Nunn); 57-58% defects; primary-canine hypoplasia 19% vs 3% |
| Gingival/periodontal | Gingival bleeding 95.8%; gingival index 1.88 vs 0.92; periodontal pockets 70.3%; gingivitis tied to anemia (p=.0002) & dialysis duration (p=.007) |
| Gingival overgrowth | CsA-induced GO ∝ total cyclosporine dose in first 6 post-transplant months; drug-induced GO 16.7% |
| Transplant infections | Oral HSV 0-11.3%; oral candidiasis 4-43% |

**Dental-management protocol**

- *Pretreatment*: thorough medical history + nephrology consult; assess coagulation before bleeding procedures (uremic platelet dysfunction); modify drug doses for reduced renal clearance; antibiotic prophylaxis before invasive procedures; corticosteroid cover for patients on steroids; amide local anesthetic (lidocaine); avoid nephrotoxic aminoglycosides/tetracyclines/doxycycline.
- *Analgesics*: paracetamol preferred; **avoid aspirin** (antiplatelet) and **NSAIDs** (prostaglandin suppression → hypertension) in severe renal failure; opioids liver-metabolized, usually no dose change.
- *Restorative*: bonded composite or full-coverage for enamel hypoplasia; **fluoride supplementation contraindicated** (retained in renal impairment); not all DDE teeth need filling.
- *Hemodialysis*: **treat the day after dialysis** (heparin effect lasts only 3-5 h; intravascular volume optimal); layered hemostasis (topical thrombin, sutures, oxidized regenerated cellulose, microfibrillar collagen, desmopressin, conjugated estrogens, tranexamic acid 10-15 mg/kg/day rinse); HIV/HBV/HCV screening + LFTs before surgery; avoid tight chair postures (vascular access); 3-6-month recall.
- *Renal transplant*: eliminate all oral infection foci pre-transplant; **avoid elective care in first 6 months post-transplant**; manage gingival overgrowth (cyclosporine A, nifedipine) by cyclosporine→tacrolimus substitution + rigorous hygiene; mTOR-inhibitor (sirolimus/everolimus) stomatitis managed with topical steroids; HSV (acyclovir) and candidiasis (chlorhexidine/nystatin/fluconazole) treatment; heightened oral-cancer (Kaposi sarcoma, SCC) surveillance.

**Limitations**: small pediatric evidence base, narrative (non-pooled) synthesis, acknowledged selection bias, wide prevalence ranges from heterogeneous classification systems, and mixed-age included studies despite the pediatric framing.
