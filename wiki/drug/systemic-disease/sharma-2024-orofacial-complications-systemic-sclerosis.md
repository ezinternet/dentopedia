## One-line Summary

Narrative review (J Dent Res, 3-author) of orofacial complications in systemic sclerosis: microstomia (52–80%), xerostomia (sicca 70%; secondary Sjögren ≤14%), more severe periodontitis (stage III/IV in 90% dcSSc vs 60% lcSSc), condylar resorption (10–50%), trigeminal neuralgia, dysgeusia; GCF MMP-9/CXCL-4 track early disease; implants can succeed with vasculopathy/bone-quality risk assessment.

## Summary

Systemic sclerosis (전신경화증, Systemic Sclerosis, SSc / scleroderma) is an autoimmune fibrosing connective tissue disease driven by vascular injury, hypoxia (HIF-1α), and fibrogenic chemokines (TGF-β, ET-1, IL-1α) that push fibroblasts toward contractile myofibroblasts and collagen-rich extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling. Although SSc research has focused on lung and skin, **orofacial manifestations affect up to 80% of patients** — and because of their characteristic oral presentation, SSc patients are often first diagnosed by dentists. This narrative review (Journal of Dental Research) consolidates that understudied orofacial burden for a dental audience. There is no disease-modifying therapy; care is symptom-by-symptom.

SSc is classified as limited cutaneous (lcSSc, 64%), diffuse cutaneous (dcSSc, 28% — higher mortality), or sine scleroderma (8%). The dominant orofacial lesion is **microstomia** (소구증, reduced mouth opening) from perioral skin fibrosis, seen in 52–80% of patients and 2.5× more handicapping (MHISS) in dcSSc. Microstomia impairs mastication/deglutition (→ malnutrition), oral hygiene, and the dentist's ability to examine and treat. Critically, the review notes that **microstomia — not skin-thickness score — correlates with oral complications**, arguing it should become a clinical-trial end point.

## Key Contributions

- **Maps the full orofacial picture of SSc**: facial skin/muscle rigidity (mask-like face) and microstomia, telangiectasia, periodontitis/gingival inflammation, salivary gland dysfunction, dysgeusia, TMJ involvement, mandibular/facial bone resorption, and trigeminal neuropathy.
- **Distinguishes lcSSc from dcSSc** where data allow: advanced periodontitis (stage III/IV) 90% in dcSSc vs 60% in lcSSc; secondary Sjögren's associated with lcSSc; microstomia worse in dcSSc.
- **Proposes oral biomarkers and end points**: GCF MMP-9 and CXCL-4 correlate with early-onset (<2 y) disease and periodontal parameters, whereas IL-6 does not — suggesting them as predictive biomarkers, and microstomia as a trial outcome.
- Notes drug-induced oral side effects in SSc management: candidiasis (corticosteroids), mouth ulcers (methotrexate), calcium channel blocker–induced gingival hyperplasia.

## Methodology

Narrative (non-systematic) review — no search strategy, pooled estimates, or quality appraisal. Organized by SSc subtype and orofacial anatomical site, with a closing molecular-mechanism synthesis (interleukins, CXCL-4, MMP-9). Cited primary evidence is small and described by the authors as "scant" and low-quality.

## Results

**Salivary / xerostomia.** Sicca syndrome (gland fibrosis) in 70%; secondary Sjögren's syndrome (American-European Consensus Group criteria) in ≤14%, associated with lcSSc. Xerostomia (구강건조증) raises caries, erosion, and periodontitis risk — compounded by SSc-associated gastroesophageal reflux reducing oral pH buffering.

**Periodontitis.** Worse than controls (attachment loss, recession, higher plaque/gingival indices, raised biofilm/GCF inflammatory markers). PDL widening in >1/3 of patients (collagen excess and/or increased occlusal force from bulky masticatory muscles). Notably gingiva does NOT develop the skin's fibrotic phenotype — attributed to scar-less gingival healing and gingival fibroblast resistance to myofibroblast differentiation. Etiology multifactorial: xerostomia, hygiene restriction from microstomia, microbiome alterations, microangiopathy, immune dysregulation, ECM remodeling.

**TMJ and bone.** Restricted mandibular movement, TMD, jaw pain; condylar resorption prevalence 10–50%, with reported secondary apertognathia (anterior open bite). Resorption concentrates at muscle-attachment sites (mandibular angle, condylar head, coronoid process, zygomatic arch). Distinguishing TMJ pathology from soft-tissue fibrosis as the cause of impaired function is difficult.

**Neuropathy / taste.** Trigeminal neuralgia more prevalent than in the general population and may precede SSc diagnosis (presenting symptom). Dysgeusia (bitter/sour/metallic) reported but unstudied mechanistically.

**Management.** Mouth exercises improve interincisal distance; severe microstomia may need commissurotomy (risk of scarring/recurrence) or foldable silicone prostheses. Xerostomia: sialagogues (pilocarpine, anetholtrithione, salivary substitute). **Dental implants** can be placed successfully — survival reported unaffected by SSc and can improve OHRQoL — but require thorough preoperative risk assessment and follow-up, and may be contraindicated with severe vasculopathy. Facial/mandibular bone resorption has no treatment (follow-up only); surgical TMJ reconstruction after condylar resorption shows promise.
