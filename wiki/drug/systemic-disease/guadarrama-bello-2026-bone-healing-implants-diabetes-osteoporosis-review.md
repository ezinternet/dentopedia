## One-line Summary

Narrative review (in vitro/in vivo/clinical) of how diabetes (hyperglycemia, AGE-RAGE, oxidative stress, prolonged inflammation, impaired angiogenesis) and osteoporosis (resorption-shifted remodeling, low BMD) impair peri-implant bone healing, and how titanium surface modifications—especially nanotopography (<25 nm)—can rescue deficient healing pathways.

## Summary

According to PubMed, this Advanced Healthcare Materials review ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/adhm.202504701)) explains *why* diabetes and osteoporosis—the two most prevalent metabolic conditions impairing bone—compromise osseointegration, and what surface engineering can do about it.

Two distinct pathophysiologies are contrasted. **Diabetes** alters bone *repair* through metabolic dysregulation: chronic hyperglycemia impairs osteoblast function, sustains inflammation, and delays angiogenesis. Advanced glycation end-products (AGE-RAGE), oxidative stress, and elevated inflammatory cytokines diminish osteoblast activity and increase osteoclastogenesis—so bone may form but fails to integrate properly with the implant. **Osteoporosis** instead directly degrades bone *quantity and quality*: a remodeling balance shifted toward resorption (heightened osteoclast activity, insufficient osteoblast function) yields low-density, mechanically weak peri-implant bone that cannot sustain loading.

Preclinical models confirm both: diabetic pigs and STZ rats show reduced BIC/BMD (with dysregulated turnover), while OVX rats show decreased BIC, BV/TV, and removal-torque/push-out strength. Clinically, poorly controlled diabetics face higher peri-implantitis and marginal bone loss risk, and osteoporotic patients show lower primary stability—yet well-managed diabetics approach healthy success rates and osteoporotic implant survival often exceeds 90% with appropriate protocols. The shared driver is systemic inflammation (TNF-α, IL-1β, RANKL/OPG imbalance) at the interface.

The therapeutic thesis is surface modification: physico-chemical and biological surfaces can modulate macrophage polarization (M1→M2), osteoblast differentiation, oxidative-stress response, and ECM organization through Wnt/β-catenin, BMP/Smad, MAPK, PI3K/Akt, VEGF, and Nrf2 pathways. Nanoscale topography is highlighted—nanoporous titanium (<25 nm) reduced inflammation and provided alternative adhesion in genetically impaired cells via intrinsic mechanotransductive signaling, without bioactive coatings—as a promising way to "reprogram" deficient cells in compromised hosts.

## Key Contributions

- Separates the two mechanisms: diabetes impairs *repair* (metabolic); osteoporosis degrades *structure* (quantity/quality).
- Links preclinical readouts (BIC, BMD, BV/TV, removal-torque) to clinical outcomes (survival, marginal bone loss, peri-implantitis) and to shared inflammatory drivers (TNF-α, IL-1β, RANKL/OPG).
- Maps surface-modification strategies to specific rescuable molecular pathways; positions <25 nm nanotopography as a coating-free rescue strategy.

## Methodology

Narrative review integrating in vitro, in vivo (STZ diabetic rats/pigs; OVX osteoporosis rats), and clinical evidence, structured around normal vs impaired healing and surface-modification rescue. No pooled estimates or formal risk-of-bias assessment.

## Results

- **Diabetes (in vivo)**: lower BIC and BMD at 4 and 12 weeks (pigs); increased peri-implant bone volume but reduced direct BIC (STZ rats) — bone forms but fails to integrate.
- **Osteoporosis (OVX)**: decreased BIC, BV/TV, mechanical anchorage; delayed new bone formation; progressive trabecular deterioration.
- **Clinical**: uncontrolled diabetes → higher failure/peri-implantitis risk; well-controlled approaches healthy rates. Osteoporotic survival often >90% with patient selection, though lower primary stability and slightly higher marginal bone loss.
- **Surface rescue**: nanoporous Ti (<25 nm) lowered inflammatory response and gave alternative adhesion in impaired cells; nanostructured implants sustained bone formation under loading.
