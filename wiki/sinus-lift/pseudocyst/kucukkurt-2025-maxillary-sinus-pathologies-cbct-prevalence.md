## One-line Summary

Retrospective CBCT study (n=1,000 scans) found 68% of dental implant candidates had maxillary sinus pathologies, with mucosal thickening (~47%) most prevalent and strongly linked to periapical lesions and periodontal disease.

## 한줄요약

임플란트 후보 1,000명 CBCT 연구: 68%에서 상악동 병변, 점막비후(~47%)가 가장 흔하며 치근단 병변·치주질환과 유의한 연관.

## Summary

This is the largest single-center CBCT-based study examining maxillary sinus pathology prevalence in dental implant candidates. A retrospective analysis of 1,000 CBCT scans (500 males, 500 females) revealed that approximately 68% of sinuses showed at least one pathologic finding. Mucosal thickening (defined as Schneiderian membrane thickness >3 mm) was the most prevalent pathology at approximately 47%. Other findings included mucus retention cysts, polyps, mucoceles, and non-specific opacifications.

Key findings:
- **Pathological threshold**: >3 mm for Schneiderian membrane thickening (current consensus)
- **Odontogenic associations**: periapical lesions and periodontal disease were significantly and independently associated with mucosal thickening
- **Demographic**: male/female prevalence similar
- Clinicians need nuanced preoperative CBCT assessment — not all thickening represents infection requiring treatment delay

## Key Contributions

- Largest published CBCT prevalence study in implant candidates
- Statistical analysis of odontogenic risk factors (not just frequency counts)
- Reinforces >3mm threshold for pathological mucosal thickening

## Methodology

- Retrospective CBCT review, 1,000 scans (500M/500F)
- Dental implant candidates only
- Pathology categories: mucosal thickening, retention cysts, polyps, mucoceles, opacifications
- Logistic regression for odontogenic risk factor associations

## Results

| Pathology | Prevalence (approx.) |
|---|---|
| Any pathology | ~68% |
| Mucosal thickening (>3mm) | ~47% |
| Retention cysts | 2nd most common |
| Other (polyps, mucoceles) | less common |

- Periapical lesions: significantly associated with mucosal thickening (OR not specified in available text)
- Periodontal disease: significantly associated
- No significant gender difference
