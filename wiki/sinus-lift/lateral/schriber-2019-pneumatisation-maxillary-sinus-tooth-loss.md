Retrospective CBCT volumetric study (100 posterior maxillae: 50 dentate vs 50 edentulous) found no significant difference in maxillary sinus volume, surface area, or maximum diameter between dentate and edentulous sides, indicating that post-extraction vertical bone loss in the posterior maxilla is driven by alveolar crest resorption rather than sinus pneumatisation; males had significantly larger sinuses than females.

## 한줄요약
후향적 콘빔 컴퓨터단층촬영 (Cone-Beam Computed Tomography, CBCT) 체적 분석 (유치악 50명 vs 무치악 50명 후방 상악): 상악동 부피·표면·최대 직경에서 유의차 없음 → 발치 후 후방 상악의 수직 골량 감소는 상악동 함기화 (Pneumatisation)가 아닌 치조정 흡수 (Alveolar Crest Resorption)에 기인함을 시사. 남성이 여성보다 상악동이 유의하게 큼.

## Summary
A retrospective CBCT volumetric study of **100 posterior maxillae (50 dentate + 50 edentulous)** at the University of Bern tested the long-held assumption that the maxillary sinus **pneumatises** (expands downward) after posterior tooth loss. Two observers traced planar curves of each sinus in three planes, and a custom software computed volume, surface area, and maximum diameter. **No significant difference** in any sinus dimension was found between dentate and edentulous sides. Males had significantly larger sinuses than females; sides did not differ. Intra- and inter-observer agreement was strong, and each analysis took <4 minutes. The authors conclude that the apparent vertical bone loss in the edentulous posterior maxilla is driven primarily by **alveolar crest resorption**, not by sinus pneumatisation — challenging a piece of long-standing clinical orthodoxy and reframing the rationale for sinus elevation and ridge preservation in the posterior maxilla.

## Key Contributions
- Direct radiographic refutation of "post-extraction sinus pneumatisation" as a meaningful driver of vertical bone loss in the posterior maxilla.
- Volumetric sinus normative data stratified by sex.
- Demonstrates a fast, reproducible CBCT volumetric workflow (<4 min/case).

## Methodology
- 100 CBCTs from adult patients (>30 years), 50 dentate + 50 edentulous posterior maxillae.
- Two observers drew planar curves in frontal, sagittal, and axial planes.
- Custom software computed volume (mm³), surface area (mm²), maximum diameter (mm).
- Outcomes: dentition state effect (primary); age, sex, side, observer agreement, time per case (secondary).

## Results
- Sinus volume, surface, diameter: no significant difference between dentate and edentulous.
- Sex: males > females (p < 0.05).
- Side: no significant difference.
- Intra-/inter-observer Pearson correlations strong; <4 min per case.
- Clinical reframing: posterior vertical bone loss = crestal resorption, not sinus expansion.
