## Summary

Wang et al. (2019) conducted a Cochrane-methodology SR+MA restricted to RCTs with ≥3 years follow-up to answer: in severely damaged endodontically treated teeth (ETT) with ≤2 remaining coronal walls, do fiber posts or metal posts perform better clinically?

From 1,511 screened records, 4 RCTs (all low risk of bias) met criteria, covering follow-up periods of 3–7 years. The key finding: fiber posts showed significantly higher overall survival (RR 0.57, 95% CI 0.33–0.97, P=.04), meaning the risk of failure was ~43% lower with fiber posts than metal posts. However, no statistically significant differences were detected in success rates, post debonding rates, or root fracture rates. GRADE confidence was high for survival and moderate for success rates.

## Key Contributions

- **Survival advantage confirmed for fiber posts** in the hardest clinical scenario (≤2 coronal walls)
- **RR 0.57** is the best-available point estimate for fiber vs metal survival in severely damaged ETT
- **GRADE high** for the primary survival endpoint — actionable for clinical guideline use
- No difference in root fracture — counters older concern that stiffer metal posts protect against root fracture in compromised teeth

## Methodology

- Systematic review + meta-analysis per Cochrane Handbook
- Inclusion: RCTs only, follow-up ≥3 years, severely damaged ETT (≤2 coronal walls), fiber vs metal post comparison
- 1,511 records → 4 RCTs included; all low risk of bias
- Follow-up: 3–7 years (medium-term)
- Primary outcome: overall survival rate; secondary: success rate, post debonding, root fracture
