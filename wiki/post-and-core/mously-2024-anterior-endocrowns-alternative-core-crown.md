## One-line Summary

Systematic review (12 in-vitro/FEA studies, narrative — no meta-analysis): endocrowns gave comparable-or-greater fracture resistance and more repairable failures vs post-core-crown in anterior ETT; ferrule was the dominant fatigue-resistance factor, lithium disilicate the best material.

## Summary

This PRISMA systematic review (Mously 2025, *International Dental Journal*; PMC full text) compares the biomechanical performance of endocrowns versus post-core-crown (PCC) restorations specifically for anterior endodontically treated teeth (ETT) with severe coronal loss — a niche under-served by mostly posterior-focused reviews. From 1307 records, 12 studies were included (8 in-vitro fatigue + load-to-failure, plus FEA), all at medium risk of bias (QUIN 50-70%); heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis, so synthesis is narrative.

The review reframes the post's role: its principal benefit is **retention of the core**, not reinforcement of the tooth. Most studies found endocrowns delivered comparable-or-greater fracture resistance than PCC, with more **repairable** failure patterns, whereas PCC (especially with ferrule) reached higher fracture resistance but failed more **catastrophically** (vertical root fracture). The recurring dominant variable was the **ferrule** (1-2 mm), which governed fatigue resistance regardless of technique; absent a ferrule, an endocrown with 3 mm intracanal extension gave the best stress distribution. Material mattered: lithium disilicate (flexural ~400 MPa, toughness 3.3 MPa·m^1/2) gave the best biomechanics; resin nano-ceramic produced ~7% fewer catastrophic failures owing to its lower modulus; rigid zirconia reached the highest fracture resistance but its high modulus (210 GPa vs dentin 18.6 GPa) concentrated root stress and drove catastrophic failures. Recommended intracanal extension is 3-5 mm; deeper extension raises pull-out bond strength but worsens marginal gap, and long (10 mm) posts gave the most non-repairable fractures. The only RCT (1-year, e.max endocrown vs e.max PCC) found no significant gross-fracture difference but the authors still favored PCC for anterior ETT.

## Key Contributions

- Anterior-specific synthesis of endocrown vs post-core-crown biomechanics.
- Reframes the post as a core-retention device, not a tooth-reinforcement device; flags rigid (metal/zirconia) restorations as drivers of catastrophic root fracture.
- Identifies ferrule as the dominant fatigue-resistance factor and lithium disilicate as the best material; gives intracanal-extension depth guidance (3-5 mm).

## Methodology

1307 records → 1107 unique → 30 full-text → 12 included. QUIN risk-of-bias tool for in-vitro studies. Narrative qualitative comparison across fracture resistance, failure pattern, pull-out bond strength, and FEA stress distribution (meta-analysis not feasible due to heterogeneity).

## Results

- Endocrowns ≈ or > PCC fracture resistance in most studies; endocrown failures more repairable, PCC failures more catastrophic.
- **Ferrule (1-2 mm)** dominant for fatigue resistance regardless of material/technique; without ferrule, endocrown + 3 mm intracanal extension gave best stress distribution.
- **Lithium disilicate** best biomechanics; **resin nano-ceramic** ~7% fewer catastrophic failures (lower modulus); **zirconia** highest fracture resistance but rigid → root stress → catastrophic failures.
- Intracanal extension **3-5 mm**; deeper raises pull-out bond strength but worsens marginal gap; long (10 mm) posts gave most non-repairable fractures.
- 1-yr RCT: no significant gross-fracture difference (endocrown vs PCC); endocrown 33% marginal discrepancy (NS); authors still recommended PCC for anterior ETT.
