## One-line Summary

Rat split-mouth study (n=14 male Sprague-Dawley, unilateral maxillary molar extraction, 8 wk) showing occlusal hypofunction decreases BMD and BV/TV and increases osteoclast number, with upregulated sclerostin and RANKL and downregulated β-catenin — implicating sclerostin-driven inhibition of Wnt/β-catenin signaling as the molecular mechanism of disuse alveolar bone loss.

## 한줄요약

Rat split-mouth 연구(수컷 Sprague-Dawley 14마리, 편측 상악 대구치 발치로 교합저하 유도, 8주) — 교합저하 측에서 골밀도(BMD)·BV/TV 감소와 파골세포 증가가 sclerostin·RANKL 상승, β-catenin 저하와 동반됨을 보여, sclerostin이 Wnt/β-catenin 신호를 억제해 disuse 치조골 소실을 일으키는 분자 기전임을 시사.

## Summary

Tooth loss removes occlusal load from the surrounding alveolar bone, producing a "disuse" (unloading) state that accelerates bone loss — clinically important for the elderly and for prosthetic rehabilitation. The molecular driver had not been defined in jaw bone. Xu et al. (2016) used a rat split-mouth model: the left maxillary molars were extracted (ipsilateral upper incisors abraded) to create occlusal hypofunction, with each rat's non-extraction side serving as its own paired control. After 8 weeks, the hypofunction side showed clear bone loss and architectural deterioration on radiography, micro-CT, and H&E. The molecular signature — increased sclerostin (an osteocyte-secreted Wnt antagonist) and RANKL, with decreased β-catenin and unchanged OPG — points to **sclerostin-mediated inhibition of canonical Wnt/β-catenin signaling** (suppressing bone formation) combined with **RANKL-driven osteoclastogenesis** (enhancing resorption) as the mechanism of occlusal-hypofunction alveolar bone loss. The authors extend a known long-bone unloading mechanism (tail-suspension) to alveolar bone and propose anti-sclerostin therapy as a candidate for protecting against disuse jaw-bone loss.

## Key Contributions

- First demonstration of **sclerostin upregulation in an occlusal-hypofunction (jaw-bone unloading) environment**, extending the long-bone unloading mechanism to alveolar bone.
- A within-animal **split-mouth occlusal-hypofunction model** (paired t-test, contralateral control) that isolates the unloading effect.
- Couples the disuse phenotype (↓BMD, ↓BV/TV, ↑osteoclasts) to a coherent signaling signature (↑sclerostin, ↑RANKL, ↓β-catenin, OPG unchanged).
- Frames **anti-sclerostin antibody** as a potential adjunct in dental prosthetic rehabilitation to counter occlusal-hypofunction bone loss.

## Methodology

- **Animals**: 14 male Sprague-Dawley rats (10 wk), powder diet, ethics-approved.
- **Model**: left maxillary molars extracted + ipsilateral upper incisors abraded → occlusal hypofunction; non-extraction side = paired control. Endpoint: 8 weeks.
- **Outcomes**: BMD (Faxitron MX-20 radiography); BV, TV, BV/TV (micro-CT, µCT 35 Scanco); H&E histology; osteoclast count (TRAP); IHC for sclerostin, β-catenin, OPG, RANKL quantified as mean optical density (Image-Pro Plus 6.0).
- **Statistics**: mean ± SD, paired t-test, SPSS 17.0, P<0.05.

## Results

| Outcome (hypofunction vs control side) | Direction | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| BMD | ↓ markedly | — |
| BV/TV | ↓ | P<0.001 |
| Osteoclast number (TRAP) | ↑ | P<0.001 |
| Sclerostin (IHC) | ↑ | — |
| RANKL (IHC, osteocytes) | ↑ | P<0.001 |
| β-catenin (IHC) | ↓ | — |
| OPG (IHC) | no significant change | NS |

- Net effect: occlusal hypofunction → ↑sclerostin → antagonism of Wnt/β-catenin (↓β-catenin) → ↓bone formation; plus ↑RANKL → osteoclastogenesis → ↑resorption → alveolar bone loss.
- OPG unchanged (with qualitatively stronger staining at the hypofunction side) is interpreted as a negative-feedback attempt to restrain RANKL-driven resorption.

## Limitations

- Small n (14), single 8-wk time point, rat-only, males-only; associative IHC rather than causal (no sclerostin knockout/antibody intervention to prove "essential").
- Occlusal unloading is distributed across the tooth–PDL–alveolar bone complex; PDL atrophy / PDL-fibroblast mechanoresponses (not assayed) may also contribute.
