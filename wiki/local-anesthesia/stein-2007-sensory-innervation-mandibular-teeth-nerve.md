## One-line Summary

Narrative review: the nerve to the mylohyoid — traditionally classified as purely motor — carries accessory sensory innervation to mandibular teeth; because of variable branching location and the barrier formed by the pterygomandibular fascia/sphenomandibular ligament, it may escape anesthesia during a standard inferior alveolar nerve block, contributing to IANB's high failure rate.

## Summary

Dental students are taught that the **nerve to the mylohyoid** is a purely motor branch of the inferior alveolar nerve, supplying only the mylohyoid muscle and anterior belly of digastric. This review challenges that dogma, synthesizing dissection and clinical evidence that the nerve also carries **accessory sensory fibers to mandibular teeth**. The nerve branches from the IAN within the infratemporal fossa at a location that varies between individuals; anatomical barriers — the **pterygomandibular fascia** and **sphenomandibular ligament** — can prevent local anesthetic from a standard IANB injection from reaching it, leaving a sensory pathway to the mandibular teeth unblocked even when the "main" IANB appears clinically successful (lip numbness present). The review outlines alternative techniques aimed at ensuring this nerve is specifically blocked.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes the nerve to the mylohyoid as a **mixed motor-sensory** nerve rather than purely motor — a distinct accessory-innervation pathway from the cervical-plexus route described elsewhere.
- Identifies the anatomical mechanism (variable branching point + fascial/ligamentous barriers) by which this nerve can escape a technically correct IANB.
- Reviews supplemental techniques for achieving blockade of this specific nerve when standard IANB is inadequate.

## Methodology

- Narrative (non-systematic) synthesis of prior dissection studies and clinical anesthesia-failure literature.
- Abstract-only ingest — full text not retrieved.

## Results

- Nerve to the mylohyoid **can** be anesthetized by IANB when its branch point/course falls within the anesthetic field, but variability in branching location means this is inconsistent.
- Proposed as a contributing (not sole) mechanism behind IANB's well-documented high failure rate.
- Alternative injection approaches are reviewed for ensuring blockade specifically of this nerve.
