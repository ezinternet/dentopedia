## One-line Summary

Review: the Gow-Gates mandibular nerve block (open-mouth, anesthetic deposited near the mandibular condyle neck close to the trigeminal nerve's exit from the foramen ovale) and the Akinosi-Vazirani closed-mouth block (anesthetic fills the pterygomandibular space) are reliable alternatives to standard IANB, particularly valuable when a patient has a history of IANB failure from anatomical variability or accessory innervation.

## Summary

Given the standard IANB's well-documented unreliability, this review describes two established alternatives. The **Gow-Gates technique** requires the patient's mouth to be open wide; the clinician deposits anesthetic just anterior to the neck of the mandibular condyle, close to where the mandibular division of the trigeminal nerve exits the skull at the foramen ovale — effectively blocking the nerve trunk more proximally (and more broadly, potentially including accessory branches) than a standard IANB targets. The **Akinosi-Vazirani technique** requires the mouth to be closed and fills the pterygomandibular space with anesthetic, making it useful for patients with limited mouth opening. Both are indicated for any mandibular dental procedure, but the review specifically highlights their value for patients with a **history of standard IANB failure attributable to anatomical variability or accessory innervation** — directly relevant to the mechanisms described by the cervical-plexus and mylohyoid-nerve papers in this cluster.

## Key Contributions

- Details the Gow-Gates block's more proximal (condylar-neck) targeting of the trigeminal mandibular division, which may capture accessory branches missed by a standard IANB.
- Details the Akinosi-Vazirani closed-mouth alternative for limited-opening patients.
- Explicitly recommends both techniques for patients with known/suspected accessory innervation or anatomical variability driving prior IANB failure.

## Results

- Both techniques indicated for any mandibular-arch procedure.
- Both framed as more reliable than standard IANB in patients with anatomical variability/accessory innervation.
- Clinician proficiency in both techniques broadens the ability to achieve consistent mandibular anesthesia.
