## One-line Summary

Systematic review (44/185 papers, 1922–2015): cervical plexus accessory innervation (via transverse cervical nerve, great auricular nerve) may explain persistent sensation after "successful" mandibular anesthesia, but evidence quality is mostly low (LOE 4–5 dominate; only 4 LOE-1 studies).

## Summary

This systematic review addresses a specific clinical puzzle: why patients sometimes report persistent sensation, pain, or discomfort during mandibular procedures **even after an inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB) that should have been technically successful**. The authors screened 185 papers (1922–March 2015) and retained 44 addressing the **cervical plexus (CP)** as a source of **accessory innervation (AI)** — sensory branches from C1–C4 (great auricular nerve, transverse cervical nerve, lesser occipital, supraclavicular nerves) that may supply parts of the mandible/lower face not covered by the trigeminal (inferior alveolar) distribution.

Using a methodological scoring index (MSI), included studies were graded by level of evidence (LOE 1=highest to 5=lowest). The evidence base skews heavily toward low quality: only 4 LOE-1 (RCT-level) and 1 LOE-2 study exist, versus 20 LOE-4 and 14 LOE-5 (mostly case reports/expert opinion). The authors conclude the mechanism is plausible and clinically relevant but under-studied, and that cervical plexus anatomy itself needs re-examination in the anatomical literature.

## Key Contributions

- Systematic (not narrative) synthesis specifically on cervical plexus accessory innervation as a mechanism for dental anesthesia failure.
- Methodological Scoring Index (MSI) applied to grade 44 studies by LOE, quantifying how thin the high-quality evidence actually is.
- Explicit call for renewed anatomical study of the cervical plexus in dentistry.

## Methodology

- Ovid/Medline search, 1922–March 2015, any language.
- Inclusion: human/animal/cadaver studies with anatomical, anesthetic-technique, or clinical approach to CP branches in dentistry.
- Exclusion: maxillary buccal infiltration studies, no abstract available, non-CP-branch studies, duplicates.
- 185 screened → 44 included; each graded LOE 1–5 via MSI.
- Abstract-only ingest — full text not retrieved.

## Results

- Study types among the 44: 1 RCT, 3 comprehensive reviews, 1 cohort, 5 case series/reports, 16 poor-quality cohort/case series, 18 review/case-report/expert-opinion.
- LOE distribution: LOE 4 (n=20) > LOE 5 (n=14) > LOE 3 (n=5) > LOE 1 (n=4) > LOE 2 (n=1).
- Bottom line: CP accessory innervation is a real, clinically cited explanation for anesthesia-failure persistent sensation, but is supported mostly by low-LOE literature — treat as **plausible mechanism, not firmly proven**.
