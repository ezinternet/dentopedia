## Summary

This introductory article to a 2011 JADA supplement asks whether the traditional inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB), despite being the most commonly used mandibular anesthesia technique, has become unreliable enough that dentists should routinely reach for alternatives. It frames IANB failure as **multifactorial** rather than attributable to a single cause: adult cortical bone thickness, soft-tissue thickness at the injection site (which increases needle deflection), the inherent difficulty of precisely locating the inferior alveolar nerve, and — as this cluster's other papers examine in depth — **accessory innervation** from sources outside the expected trigeminal distribution. As a striking illustration of how unreliable IANB can be, the paper cites an **81% failure rate in lateral incisors**. This framing motivates the supplement's subsequent papers (e.g., on Gow-Gates and Akinosi-Vazirani techniques) as practical alternatives.

## Key Contributions

- Frames IANB failure as multifactorial: cortical bone thickness, soft-tissue-driven needle deflection, nerve-location difficulty, and accessory innervation.
- Cites the 81% lateral-incisor IANB failure rate as a concrete benchmark of unreliability.
- Positions accessory innervation as one of several — not the sole — mechanisms behind IANB's poor success rate, complementing the anatomically-focused papers in this cluster.

## Methodology

- Narrative review / introductory supplement article synthesizing prior literature on IANB failure causes.
- Abstract-only ingest — full text not retrieved.

## Results

- Four named contributing factors to IANB failure: cortical bone thickness, soft-tissue thickness (needle deflection), nerve-location difficulty, accessory innervation.
- Lateral-incisor IANB failure rate cited at 81%.
- IANB remains the most commonly used mandibular anesthesia technique despite this unreliability.
