## Summary

This Italian study (Bambino Gesù Children's Hospital, Rome) directly compares the Wand STA computerized local anesthetic delivery system against a traditional Carpule syringe in 67 pediatric/adolescent patients (7-15y) using a within-patient split-mouth crossover design — each child received one injection type per side, in two separate appointments, blinded to which device was used (eyes closed, Wand sound muted). The Wand won on every outcome measured: lower pain (VAS -1.09, P=0.0003), smaller heart-rate rise (-3.4 bpm, P=0.028), more children showing fully relaxed behavior on the modified Venham scale (P=0.019), and higher satisfaction (P=0.0003). Both devices achieved clinically effective (painless) anesthesia in experienced hands — the Wand's edge is in perceived pain and behavioral cooperation, not anesthetic success rate. The crossover design (vs. earlier parallel-group trials that found no difference) is highlighted by the authors as the likely reason this and other crossover studies detect a Wand advantage that parallel-group RCTs (e.g., Kandiah & Tahmassebi) miss — within-subject comparison controls for each child's individual anxiety/pain-threshold baseline.

## Key Contributions

- Split-mouth crossover comparison (not parallel-group) of Wand STA vs traditional syringe in children — controls for inter-patient anxiety/pain-sensitivity variability.
- Reports pain (VAS), physiologic stress (heart rate), behavior (modified Venham), and satisfaction together in one cohort.
- Blinded outcome assessment (patient eyes closed, Wand audible cue disabled).
- Explicit discussion of why crossover vs. parallel-group designs yield divergent Wand-vs-syringe pain results in the pediatric literature.

## Methodology

Observational crossover split-mouth study, n=67 (29F/38M), age 7-15y (mean 9.37, SD 2.04), Bambino Gesù Children's Hospital, Rome, June-Oct 2015. Each patient received both a Wand STA injection (STA-intraligamentary mode, 0.005 mL/sec, 30G extra-short needle) and a traditional syringe injection (30G needle) on opposite sides of the arch, in randomized order across two visits, using the same anesthetic (1.8 mL mepivacaine 20 mg/mL + adrenaline 1:100,000). Outcomes: 10-point VAS pain, pre/post heart rate, modified Venham scale (0-5, rated by 3 kappa-calibrated pediatric dentists, kappa 0.78-0.90), and 1-10 satisfaction. ANOVA for quantitative outcomes (adjusted for sequence and treatment type), Kruskal-Wallis for qualitative outcomes.

## Results

- Pain: Wand mean VAS reduction of 1.09 points vs syringe (P=0.0003); 38/67 rated syringe more painful vs 12/67 rating Wand more painful; no sex difference.
- Heart rate: Wand associated with 3.4 bpm smaller mean increase (P=0.028); 41/67 had a larger increase with syringe.
- Behavior: significantly more patients scored 0 (fully relaxed) on modified Venham with the Wand (Fisher exact P=0.019); median score 0 with both.
- Satisfaction: Wand favored by 1.09 points mean (P=0.0003); median satisfaction 9 with both.
- Sequence and treatment type (conservative vs extraction) had no significant effect on any outcome.
- Limitation: small single-center sample; excludes children <7y and precooperative children — the population most likely to be affected by injection device choice.
