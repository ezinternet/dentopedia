## One-line Summary

RCT (n=80) comparing computer-controlled Single Tooth Anesthesia (STA) system versus traditional local infiltration for maxillary posterior restorative treatment: no significant difference in perceived pain (p=0.59) or systolic BP (p=0.09) during the anesthetic injection itself, but STA patients reported significantly less pain during the restorative procedure (p<0.001) and had significantly better treatment experience and future preference (p=0.04), despite significantly higher heart rate throughout (already elevated at baseline).

## Summary

This parallel-arm RCT (n=80, Riyadh, Saudi Arabia) tested whether the STA System — a computer-controlled local anesthetic delivery system (CCLAD) that delivers intra-ligamentary anesthesia with dynamic pressure feedback — outperforms traditional needle-syringe infiltration for maxillary posterior restorative dentistry (class I/II restorations). Patients were randomized 1:1 to STA (2% lidocaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine, intra-ligamentary mode) or traditional infiltration (same anesthetic, standard technique), with blinding achieved by keeping the STA unit powered on (audible cue disabled) in both arms.

The headline finding is a dissociation between injection-pain and procedure-comfort: STA conferred **no advantage during the injection itself** (pain p=0.59, systolic BP p=0.09 between groups) but delivered **significantly less pain during the actual restorative procedure** (p<0.001 within STA group; p=0.008 between groups) and **significantly better post-procedure satisfaction and future preference** (p=0.04 for both). Heart rate was significantly higher in the STA group at every timepoint, but this difference was already present at baseline (before any anesthetic was given), and the proportion of patients showing an HR rise during anesthesia was similar between groups (22/40 STA vs 25/40 infiltration) — suggesting comparable anxiety levels rather than a device- or drug-specific cardiovascular effect.

## Key Contributions

- Isolates STA's actual clinical benefit to intra-procedural comfort and patient-reported satisfaction rather than injection-pain reduction, in contrast with some earlier STA literature that reported injection-pain advantages (potentially confounded by inconsistent comparator technique — nerve block vs infiltration — and topical anesthetic use).
- Uses a parallel-arm (not split-mouth) design specifically to avoid carryover bias from a first injection influencing rating of a second, while sacrificing within-subject control.
- Provides multi-timepoint physiologic (HR, SBP) and patient-reported (Wong-Baker FACES, satisfaction questionnaire) data across a full restorative visit, not just the injection moment.
- First (per authors) STA-vs-infiltration comparison specifically for non-surgical, everyday restorative treatment rather than surgical/nerve-block-comparator contexts.

## Methodology

- Parallel RCT, computer-generated randomization, n=80 (40 STA / 40 traditional infiltration).
- Inclusion: adults >18y, ASA ≤2, needing class I/II restorations on maxillary premolars/molars, healthy periodontium, no periapical pathology.
- STA arm: STA System (Milestone Scientific), 2% lidocaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine, intra-ligamentary injection mode at 0.005 mL/s, 30-gauge extra-short needle; 1.8 mL for multi-rooted teeth, 0.9 mL for single-rooted teeth.
- Control arm: same anesthetic solution via standard infiltration technique.
- Blinding: STA device left on (audible feedback disabled) in both groups so patients could not identify technique by sound.
- Outcomes measured before/during/after injection and during the restorative procedure: Wong-Baker FACES pain scale, heart rate, systolic blood pressure; anesthetic efficiency via electric pulp testing every 10 min for up to 60 min; validated post-procedure questionnaire (treatment experience, anxiety, numbness, future preference, 1–10 scale).
- Statistics: paired t-test (within-group), unpaired t-test (between-group).

## Results

| Outcome | STA vs Infiltration |
|---|---|
| Injection pain (before/during/after) | NS: p=0.72 / p=0.59 / p=0.19 |
| Pain during restorative procedure | STA significantly lower, p=0.008 (between-group); STA within-group drop p<0.001 vs infiltration NS p=0.18 |
| Systolic BP (before/during/after) | NS: p=0.39 / p=0.09 / p=0.11 |
| Heart rate (before/during/after) | STA significantly higher throughout: p<0.001 / p=0.03 / p<0.001 (but baseline imbalance present pre-injection) |
| Post-procedure treatment experience | STA significantly better, p=0.04 |
| Future method preference | STA significantly higher, p=0.04 |
| Anxiety / achieved numbness | NS: p=0.2 / p=0.08 |
| Pulp-test responsiveness (early re-anesthesia need) | Infiltration: 3 responsive at 10 min, 1 at 20 min; STA: 2 responsive at 10 min (suggests longer STA duration of effect) |

Bottom line: STA is not "less painful to inject" than traditional infiltration, but it produces a more comfortable, better-tolerated restorative visit overall, with higher patient satisfaction and preference — consistent with a longer/more reliable duration of pulpal anesthesia rather than a superior injection experience.
