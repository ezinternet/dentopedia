## One-line Summary

Single-blinded RCT (n=68, Barcelona) among upper third molar extraction patients found that a detailed verbal explanation of The Wand computerized anesthesia system before injection did NOT significantly reduce dental anxiety (ISAR/MDAS/DFS/STAI-S) or perceived pain (VAS) vs no information; need of intraoperative re-anesthesia (42.6%) was unrelated to anxiety but significantly related to longer operative time (p=0.007).

## Summary

[근거중간] A single-blinded RCT at the University of Barcelona Dental Hospital randomized 68 patients (34/arm) undergoing upper third molar extraction to receive, or not receive, a standardized verbal explanation of **The Wand** — a computerized, pressure-regulated, footswitch-activated local anesthesia delivery system — before injection. All patients received identical anesthesia (The Wand, supraperiosteal infiltration, 1.6mL buccal + 0.2mL palatal, 4% articaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine). The trial isolates the psychological effect of *informing* patients about a computerized device's mechanism, not the device's intrinsic anesthetic performance. Contrary to the study hypothesis, detailed explanation produced **no significant reduction** in anxiety (measured by four separate instruments: ISAR, MDAS, DFS, STAI-S) or in perceived pain (100mm VAS at buccal/palatal puncture and infiltration). Need for intraoperative re-anesthesia (42.6% of patients) was unrelated to anxiety level or to whether an explanation was given, but was significantly associated with longer operative time.

## Key Contributions

- Directly tests the "information reduces anxiety" hypothesis specifically for a computerized/unfamiliar anesthesia delivery device, rather than assuming information transfer generalizes from conventional-syringe contexts.
- Uses four concurrent validated anxiety instruments (ISAR, MDAS, DFS, STAI) plus 4-point VAS pain — a stronger multi-instrument capture than typical single-scale dental-anxiety trials.
- Reports intraoperative re-anesthesia rate and its correlates for adults treated with a computerized delivery system — data largely absent from prior Wand literature (which is more pediatric-focused for this specific outcome).
- Null result that stands in tension with prior work (Wang interview study; Heaton CARL desensitization RCT) suggesting information/explanation reduces dental fear — suggesting the effect of "more information" may not transfer uniformly to unfamiliar computerized equipment, where novelty (device beeps, pen-shaped handpiece) could itself be anxiogenic even as the explanation intends to reassure.

## Methodology

- Single-blinded, two-arm parallel RCT, computer-generated randomization (randomization.com); CONSORT-compliant; University of Barcelona ethics approval (Protocol 11/2016).
- Population: 18–45y, upper third molar extraction (any impaction status), asymptomatic, ASA I–II; dental professionals/students excluded (prior device familiarity confound).
- Experimental group (n=34): standardized verbal explanation of The Wand's mechanism and target anesthesia area before injection. Control group (n=34): no specific information.
- All patients anesthetized identically: The Wand, supraperiosteal infiltrative technique, 1.6mL buccal + 0.2mL palatal, 25mm 30G needle, 4% articaine + 1:100,000 epinephrine, delivered by 3 blinded calibrated researchers.
- Anxiety assessed post-anesthesia via ISAR, MDAS, DFS, STAI-S (STAI-T/trait anxiety collected at a prior baseline visit); pain via 4× 100mm VAS (buccal/palatal × puncture/infiltration), all administered simultaneously by a separate investigator.
- Sample size: G*Power, α=0.05, power=0.9, d=0.8 → 68 total. Analysis: SPSS 22.0, Shapiro-Wilk normality, non-parametric U-Mann Whitney / Fisher exact, significance p<0.05. No topical anesthesia used (to avoid confounding VAS pain data).

## Results

- 85 screened → 17 lost to follow-up (cancelled surgical visit) → 68 analyzed. 47 women (69.1%), 21 men (30.9%), mean age 28.8±9.3y. Mean operative time 19.2±11.7 min; 55 conventional extractions (Parant type 1), 13 needed ostectomy (type 2).
- **No significant between-group difference** in any anxiety measure (ISAR, MDAS, DFS, STAI-S) — detailed Wand explanation did not reduce measured anxiety. Overall anxiety scores were low across the sample; few highly-anxious individuals were captured.
- **No significant between-group difference** in VAS pain at buccal or palatal puncture/infiltration.
- **Re-anesthesia**: 29/68 (42.6%) needed intraoperative re-anesthesia — significantly associated with longer operative time (15.5 min SD 8.8 vs 24 min SD 13.3, p=0.007), but NOT with anxiety level (any instrument), prior explanation (p=0.806), radiological difficulty, or intervention type.

## Limitations and Future Work

- Underrepresentation of highly-anxious patients limited power to detect an information effect in the subgroup most likely to benefit.
- Trainee-performed surgeries ("Master fellows") may have inflated the re-anesthesia rate relative to specialist-performed cases.
- Single-center, single-procedure design (upper third molar extraction only); no conventional-syringe comparison arm — isolates only explanation-vs-no-explanation within Wand-anesthetized patients.
- Contradicts prior literature suggesting information/explanation reduces dental fear (Wang, Heaton) — authors note the format/amount of information (and specifically its target: an unfamiliar computerized device with sounds/beeps) may behave differently from general procedural information.
