## One-line Summary

RCT (n=200, 5-arm, single-blind) comparing conventional syringe, manual pressure syringe (MCJ), spring-activated pressure syringe (PCJ), needle-free jet injector (NFI), and CCLAD for palatal infiltration in adults: no clinically meaningful difference in post-injection pain across devices (p=0.380, all Hedges' g<0.20), but the spring-activated pressure syringe (PCJ) achieved the largest fear reduction and CCLAD produced the largest transient pulse-rate rise.

## Summary

This RCT is the first head-to-head comparison of all five common anesthetic delivery systems for palatal infiltration — conventional dental syringe (CDS), manual pressure syringe (MCJ, Citoject-type), spring-activated push-button pressure syringe (PCJ), needle-free jet injector (NFI), and computer-controlled local anesthetic delivery (CCLAD) — in 200 healthy adults undergoing maxillary molar extraction. All systems delivered 0.4 mL of 4% articaine with epinephrine 1:100,000 to a standardized palatal site with no topical anesthetic, isolating the device's mechanical/psychological contribution from pharmacology.

Bottom line: pain outcomes were statistically and clinically similar across all five devices (overall p=0.380; all pairwise Hedges' g<0.20 except MCJ, which trended slightly higher). What differed meaningfully was the psychophysiological profile — fear reduction was greatest with PCJ, and CCLAD produced the largest (though still physiologically safe) transient pulse-rate increase, likely reflecting its markedly longer injection time (~52s vs <1s for NFI). SpO2 was unaffected by device choice in all groups. No adverse events occurred.

## Key Contributions

- First RCT to compare CDS, MCJ, PCJ, NFI, and CCLAD simultaneously under identical palatal-infiltration conditions (prior literature only ever compared 2-3 systems at a time).
- First to differentiate the manual (MCJ) vs spring-activated push-button (PCJ) Citoject variants, which are often treated as interchangeable in practice — PCJ significantly outperformed MCJ on fear reduction.
- Combines subjective VAS pain/fear with objective physiological monitoring (pulse, SpO2) and applies k-means cluster analysis to reveal distinct patient response phenotypes, including a "silent high-stress" cluster (high subjective distress but blunted autonomic response) — cautions against relying on vital signs alone as a comfort proxy.
- Demonstrates that needle-free jet injection (NFI), despite eliminating needle-related visual/tactile cues, gave the *smallest* fear reduction among the four active/technological devices in the palatal region — attributed to the high-pitched acoustic discharge and abrupt tissue distension against firm palatal mucosa.

## Methodology

- Prospective, randomized, parallel-arm, single-blind, CONSORT-adherent superiority RCT (Sept 2023 – Mar 2025, Istanbul Aydın University OMFS dept).
- n=200 healthy adults (ASA I/II), block-randomized 1:1:1:1:1 (n=40/arm) with concealed allocation (opaque numbered envelopes).
- Single calibrated operator for all injections; assessors and statistician blinded to allocation (operator/participant blinding not feasible due to device-specific mechanical/acoustic differences).
- Standardized dose (0.4 mL 4% articaine + epi 1:100,000), standardized injection site (~2mm anterior to greater palatine foramen, adjacent to first molar), no topical anesthetic.
- Device protocols: CDS 27G/50mm manual 15-20s; MCJ (ASPIJECT) 30G/16mm manual 15-20s; PCJ (PAROJECT) spring push-button, two 0.2mL doses; NFI (Comfort-in) <1s jet at 4-5 bar, no aspiration; CCLAD (CALAJECT Program I) 30G/16mm, AutoFlow 0.006-0.009 mL/s, auto-aspiration at 5s.
- Primary outcome: post-injection pain (10cm VAS). Secondary: fear (10cm VAS pre/post, ΔVAS), pulse rate and SpO2 at baseline/during/3-min-post.
- Sample size powered (G*Power) for 1.0cm VAS MCID at 80% power, α=0.05.
- Statistics: ANOVA/Kruskal-Wallis + adjusted post hoc, repeated-measures/Friedman, chi-square, regression (with 95% CI, Hedges' g), ANCOVA adjusting for injection duration, k-means clustering, Spearman correlation, mediation analysis; intention-to-treat.

## Results

| Outcome | Best performer | Worst / notable | Statistical significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Post-injection pain (VAS) | CCLAD (3.14) / NFI (3.17) | MCJ (4.05, highest) | Overall NS (p=0.380); all effect sizes small (g<0.20), below MCID |
| Fear reduction (ΔVAS) | PCJ (-2.70) | CDS (smallest reduction) | Significant overall (p=0.001); PCJ vs CDS g=-0.95, p<0.001 |
| Injection duration | NFI (~1s) | CCLAD (52±1.7s, longest) | p<0.001 |
| Pulse rate rise (T1-T0) | MCJ (most stable) | CCLAD (largest rise, +6.2 bpm vs CDS, p=0.025) | p=0.001 |
| Pulse recovery (T2-T1) | MCJ (best recovery) | CCLAD (worst recovery, -9.8 bpm diff vs MCJ, p<0.001) | p<0.001 |
| SpO2 | All groups stable (97.3-98.8%) | — | NS |

- Regression: PCJ independently predicts greater fear reduction (β=+2.08, p<0.01); MCJ independently predicts higher pain vs CDS (β=+0.89, p<0.05).
- Cluster analysis identified 3 phenotypes: (A) low pain/fear + elevated pulse — PCJ/CCLAD/NFI; (B) moderate balanced responders; (C) high fear/pain + blunted autonomic reactivity — mostly older males, MCJ group.
- In high-baseline-anxiety subgroup: CCLAD and MCJ gave greatest fear reduction, PCJ still had lowest pain.
- Zero adverse events across 200 completers; all devices judged clinically safe.
- Clinical interpretation: device choice should be individualized — PCJ/CCLAD favored for anxious patients (fear reduction, tactile/visual neutrality), NFI reserved for pronounced needle phobia despite modest anxiolytic benefit in this cohort, CDS remains a viable low-cost option when skillfully used.
