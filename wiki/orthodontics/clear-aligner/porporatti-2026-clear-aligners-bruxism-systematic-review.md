## One-line Summary

Systematic review (11 studies, n=818, 72.8% female; meta-analysis infeasible) concluding clear aligner therapy has a predominantly **neutral** effect on bruxism — frequently reducing tonic contractions (clenching) but with inconsistent/transient effects on phasic activity (grinding) and no change in overall sleep-bruxism index; aligners cannot be classified as protective or harmful (GRADE moderate for RCTs, very low for non-randomised).

## Summary

This is the **first systematic review** of the interaction between clear aligner therapy (CAT) and bruxism, examining whether aligners act as a neutral factor, protective agent, or risk factor across sleep bruxism (SB), awake bruxism (AB), and mixed/self-reported phenotypes. Eleven studies (818 patients, 72.8% female; mostly Italian) were synthesised qualitatively — heterogeneity in diagnostic method, appliance type (active vs passive/retainer), and follow-up (8 days to 12 months) made meta-analysis impossible.

The dominant signal is **neutrality**. No study showed a change in the overall **SB index**. A recurring nuance is that aligners tend to reduce **tonic** contractions (clenching / occlusal load) while their effect on **phasic** activity (grinding) is inconsistent — one RCT even reported a transient *increase* in phasic contractions. Most EMG effects appear in the first month and then fade, consistent with functional adaptation rather than a sustained therapeutic benefit. Awake bruxism was generally unaffected. Self-reported outcomes were the most contradictory, driven by individual psychological/behavioural factors (occlusal hypervigilance, anxiety, somatization) rather than the device itself.

The authors caution that aligners can be defined as **neither protective nor harmful**, and that the "risk" signals came exclusively from cross-sectional self-report studies without baseline data (which cannot establish temporality). GRADE certainty was moderate for the randomised studies and very low for the non-randomised ones.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic, critical appraisal of the aligner–bruxism relationship (PROSPERO CRD420251053793; PRISMA 2020; GRADE).
- Bottom line: **neutral** overall effect; aligners cannot be classified as protective or harmful.
- Clarifies the tonic-vs-phasic dissociation: clenching (tonic) frequently reduced, grinding (phasic) inconsistent/transient.
- Stratifies evidence by diagnostic tier (instrumental EMG = definite, EMA = definite, questionnaire = possible/probable), exposing diagnostic heterogeneity as a core methodological flaw.
- Proposes (as expert reasoning, not data-derived) a gradual wear-adaptation protocol for high-hypervigilance patients.

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review, qualitative synthesis (no meta-analysis), GRADE certainty rating.
- **Search**: EMBASE, PubMed/Medline, Scopus, Web of Science + Google Scholar/OpenGrey, through 15 April 2025; reference hand-search. 857 records → 408 de-duplicated → 14 full-text → 11 included.
- **PECOS**: P = children/adults under orthodontic treatment; E = clear aligners; C = fixed orthodontics or none; O = presence/modification of SB or AB; S = RCTs, observational, case series >10.
- **Included designs**: 5 RCTs; 6 non-randomised (2 cohort, 2 cross-sectional, 1 case-control, 1 quasi-experimental).
- **Diagnostic methods**: portable EMG±ECG (Bruxoff) for SB; smartphone ecological momentary assessment (BruxApp) for AB; Oral Behaviour Checklist (OBC) and other questionnaires for self-report.
- **Risk of bias**: Joanna Briggs Institute tools → 1 moderate, 10 high.
- **A priori classification**: each study labelled neutral / protective / risk per outcome direction.

## Results

**Sleep bruxism (instrumental):**
- No study altered the overall **SB index** (Bargellini 2017/2024, Castroflorio 2018 RCT, Manfredini 2018 RCT).
- Tonic contractions reduced: Bargellini 2017 (1, 3 mo) and Bargellini 2024 (6, 12 mo).
- Castroflorio 2018 (RCT, n=60, full-coverage CAT vs occlusal splint vs placebo): **increased phasic** contractions (transient), no SB-index change.
- Pittar 2023 (passive Zendura FLX aligners, n=31): reduced contraction **amplitude**, but frequency and duration unchanged — intensity effect, not occurrence.
- Manfredini 2018 (passive invisible retainers, n=19): no change in SB index (3.0±1.5 without vs 3.6±1.9 with) or sMMA events.

**Awake bruxism (EMA / EMG):**
- Colonna 2024 (RCT, F22, EMA): no AB-frequency difference across no-aligner/passive/active sessions (relaxed jaw 64.9% / 63.0% / 60.0%).
- Pereira 2021 (RCT, n=38): AB ~53.5% aligner vs 51.3% fixed — no difference over 6 months.
- Liu 2017 (RCT, n=23 females, Invisalign): ↓ OBC scores at 3 and 6 mo vs baseline, but ↑ temporalis/SCM EMG (recruitment shift, not AB-frequency change).

**Mixed / self-reported:**
- Heleiwa-Ferioli 2024 (quasi-experimental, n=100, Invisalign): ↓ clenching/grinding sensation, ↓ muscle pain, ↓ TMJ pain at 3-6 mo; no headache change. Bruxism most frequent at age 28-36.
- Saccomanno 2021 (cross-sectional, n=236): **higher** self-reported bruxism in aligner users vs metal braces (no baseline — hypothesis-generating).
- Saccomanno 2022 (cross-sectional, n=175, Invisalign 95.4%): 50.3% reported clenching/bruxism during therapy; 24% TMJ pain, 26.9% headache, 5.8% significant pain. No baseline.

**Certainty (GRADE):** moderate (randomised), very low (non-randomised).

**Clinical takeaway:** Aligners are, on current evidence, neither a reliable bruxism treatment nor a clear risk factor. For patients with high occlusal hypervigilance/anxiety who perceive worsening, the authors *hypothesise* a gradual wear-up protocol (2-3 h/day, ramping over 1-2 weeks) and pre-screening with validated hypervigilance/anxiety/catastrophizing questionnaires — but this is expert reasoning, not tested in the included trials.
