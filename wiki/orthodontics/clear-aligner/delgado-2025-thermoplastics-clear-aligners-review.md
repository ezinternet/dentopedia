## One-line Summary

Narrative material-science review of clear-aligner thermoplastics (PETG/PET, hTPU, EVA, PP, PC, multilayers, blends) and emerging shape-memory polymers and direct-3D-print resins, mapping optical, mechanical, stress-relaxation, thermal, and biocompatibility properties to guide material selection.

## Summary

This narrative review surveys the polymers used in clear aligner treatment (CAT) and the property trade-offs that drive material selection. **PET/PETG** (amorphous polyester) is the most common material; **high-hardness thermoplastic polyurethane (hTPU)** is second. EVA and PP are relegated to mouthguards; polycarbonate (PC) has been abandoned for aligners (hydrolysis, cost, BPA reputation). Two efficiency-driven strategies extend the base palette: **multilayers** (enabled by Invisalign patent expiry; soft + hard layers combined) and **blends** (PC proposed as a compatibilizer between PETG and TPU). Two emerging classes — **shape memory polymers (SMP)** and **direct-3D-print resins** — promise faster, better-fitting, lower-waste workflows but currently underperform on force retention and biocompatibility data.

The mechanistic core: aligners deliver force by operating in the elastic range near the **yield point**, and because plastics are viscoelastic they undergo **stress relaxation** (a viscous force drop toward a stable settling force). hTPU's broader elastic ("security") range gives more uniform, lower, more comfortable force; PETG's higher modulus and lower yield point give a painful first cycle then comfort, with greater force overall.

## Key Contributions

- **Material taxonomy + selection logic.** Rigid PETG for small strains (aligned arches needing expansion/retraction); softer plastics for crowded teeth (gentle force over longer time). No clear guideline yet on when to switch rigid→soft staging or how many of each a patient needs.
- **Quantified force gentleness.** Multilayer force is **2–3× lower than PETG** and **1.5–2× lower than hTPU** in models; **3D resins enact almost no force**.
- **PETG vs TPU movement contrast.** PETG produces larger **root** movements (higher force) and far higher **bonded-attachment wear**; TPU produces mainly **crown** movement with little root movement and less attachment wear.
- **Emerging-material reality check.** 3D resin TC-85 retains only ~10% of force in stress relaxation vs ~80% for PETG, and the only approved direct-print resin has Tg ≈ 37°C — explaining its low efficiency and forces. SMP shape-memory effect degrades after a limited number of cycles with accumulating residual strain.
- **Biocompatibility nuance.** Most materials are low/null cytotoxic; BPA is mostly undetectable except higher in Invisalign's TPU; **all BPA-free polyesters (including PCTG) showed detectable in-vitro estrogenic activity whereas PETG did not**; Invisalign's isocyanate-containing TPU can provoke histologic reactions.

## Methodology

Descriptive narrative synthesis organized by property domain — optical (spectrophotometry, colorimeter, scanner), aging/chemical resistance (water/saliva immersion, "Mustard test"), mechanical (3PB, uniaxial tensile, indentation; force/strain via OMSS, DIC, force-films, capacitive/piezoelectric sensors; stress relaxation & creep), thermal (Tg by DSC/DMTA, storage/loss modulus), biocompatibility (in-vitro cytotoxicity, bisphenol/estrogenic leaching by LC, biofilm; in-vivo irritation), and environment/recyclability. No PRISMA protocol, registered search, risk-of-bias appraisal, or pooled estimates — hence `narrative-review`.

## Results

- **Optical**: PETG, TPU, PC, multilayers similarly transparent when naive; thermoforming roughens and reduces transparency; transmittance falls after ~7 days; **TPU is most stain-susceptible** (water sorption + porosity); **coffee** is the most damaging agent.
- **Aging**: higher water sorption → mechanical decline; PC hydrolyzes badly; TPU among the most hygroscopic; blends/stacking reduce water uptake; worn-aligner modulus drops by day 7, stabilizes by day 14.
- **Mechanical**: PC highest yield stress + modulus; hTPU comparable yield stress at lower modulus; PETG higher modulus, lower yield. Hardness: PETG/PC/hTPU monolayers similar > multilayers > PP lowest.
- **Activation**: standard **0.25 mm**; higher activation only yields transient over-recommended force that decays to the same threshold.
- **Thermal Tg**: PETG **75–80°C**, TPU **80–95°C**; approved direct-print resin Tg ≈ **37°C**.
- **Prescription/aiders (perspectives)**: some evidence aligners can change every 10 or 7 days with equal efficiency (no firm guideline); vibrational and piezoelectric aids improve predictability/pain, optical aids only reduce aligner count.
- **Environment**: aligners are medical devices → not recyclable, must be incinerated; no full life-cycle assessment exists; plant-based PEF/PCTG and enzymatic degradation are early-stage options.
