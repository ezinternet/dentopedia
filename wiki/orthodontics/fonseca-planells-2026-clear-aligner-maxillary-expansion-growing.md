## One-line Summary

SR+MA (15 studies, 7 pooled) in growing patients: clear aligners can achieve maxillary expansion but produce significantly LESS transverse gain than conventional expanders — intermolar (6-6) −1.77 mm, palatal volume −460.6 mm³, arch perimeter −1.75 mm in favor of conventional expanders; aligner expansion is mainly dentoalveolar (anterior, deciduous teeth), evidence mostly retrospective / moderate RoB.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1186/s40510-026-00620-7](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40510-026-00620-7)), this is the first clear-aligner page in the wiki (the orthodontics category had been TAD/miniscrew- and tooth-movement-biology-only). It is a PROSPERO-registered SR+MA on whether clear aligners can produce maxillary transverse expansion in growing patients, and how they compare to conventional expanders.

The answer is nuanced: clear aligners **can** expand the maxilla, but **significantly less** than conventional expanders. Pooling 7 of 15 included studies, conventional expanders outperformed aligners on intermolar distance (6-6) by **1.77 mm (95% CI 0.97–2.57; p<0.0001)**, total palatal volume by **460.6 mm³ (182.9–738.3; p=0.0011)**, and arch perimeter by **1.75 mm (0.80–2.71; p=0.0003)** (the abstract reports these as negative differences in the aligner direction). Mechanistically, aligner-driven expansion is largely **dentoalveolar** — more pronounced anteriorly and at the deciduous teeth — whereas conventional expanders deliver greater **skeletal/posterior** transverse change, the advantage widening in patients with pronounced transverse deficiency.

Clinically: for mild dentoalveolar transverse needs in a growing/mixed-dentition patient, aligners (e.g. Invisalign First) are a plausible esthetic option; for true skeletal transverse deficiency, conventional expanders remain superior. The authors stress the evidence is mostly retrospective with moderate risk of bias, so the conclusions are provisional pending prospective RCTs. Because the population is growing patients, this paper also sits at the intersection with pediatric dentistry.

## Key Contributions

- First quantitative aligner-vs-expander transverse comparison in the wiki.
- Aligners under-expand relative to conventional expanders (intermolar −1.77 mm, palatal volume −460.6 mm³, arch perimeter −1.75 mm).
- Aligner expansion is predominantly dentoalveolar, not skeletal.

## Methodology

SR+MA (PROSPERO CRD420250608558). 5-database search; retrospective + prospective + 1 RCT, Jan 2021–Oct 2025; ROBINS-I + RoB2; I² heterogeneity; Trim-and-Fill publication-bias check. 267 screened → 15 included, 7 meta-analyzed. Confidence `sr+ma`; PMC OA full text retrieved.

## Results

- Intermolar (6-6): −1.77 mm (95% CI −2.57, −0.97; p<0.0001) vs conventional.
- Palatal volume: −460.63 mm³ (−738.32, −182.94; p=0.0011).
- Arch perimeter: −1.75 mm (−2.71, −0.80; p=0.0003).
- Aligner expansion mainly dentoalveolar (anterior, deciduous); conventional better for skeletal/pronounced deficiency.
- Evidence mostly retrospective, moderate RoB.
