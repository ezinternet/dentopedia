## One-line Summary

Systematic-search hybrid review (4 databases, 2403→13 included studies, mostly case reports/series + retrospective, 1 prospective) concluding elastodontic appliances intercept mild-to-moderate dentoskeletal malocclusion in mixed dentition (mild class III, class II with high overjet/overbite, functional posterior crossbite, anterior crowding, atypical swallowing) but on weak evidence — caution before substituting conventional functional appliances.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3390/children10111821](https://doi.org/10.3390/children10111821)), this systematic narrative hybrid review (systematic search across Web of Science, Embase, Scopus, and PubMed up to September 2023, PICO eligibility, Cohen-kappa inter-rater agreement, narrative domain-based synthesis without meta-analysis) screened 2403 citations down to 13 included studies (3 case reports, 3 case–control, 1 case series, 5 retrospective, 1 prospective) to answer "which malocclusions are amenable to elastodontic devices?". Elastodontic appliances (EAs) are prefabricated removable silicone-elastomer myofunctional devices that deliver light biological elastic forces through the neuromuscular system rather than direct active force, working within the lip–tongue–cheek "elastodontic space" with fewer dentoalveolar compensations than fixed appliances. The review maps EA indications across six domains — sagittal discrepancies (mild class III, class II with high overjet/overbite), transversal discrepancies (functional posterior crossbite, anterior crowding), atypical swallowing/bad habits/teeth malposition, conventional two-phase orthodontics, and lack of teeth retention (e.g. dentinogenesis imperfecta). The protocol is an active phase of 6–8 months (max 1 year, night + 1–2 h daytime) followed by a 12–16 month night-only retention phase. Clinically: EAs are a comfortable, prefabricated, low-anchorage option for intercepting mild-to-moderate malocclusion and bad habits in mixed dentition, but the evidence is weak (case-level designs dominate), so they should not yet be treated as a proven substitute for conventional functional appliances.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic-search overview defining the **six-domain indication map** for elastodontic appliances in interceptive orthodontics.
- Frames EAs mechanistically: light elastic forces via the neuromuscular system, vestibular/lingual flanges, four-plane orthopedic action (vertical, transverse, sagittal, torsional), "elastodontic space" neutral zone.
- Historical lineage — Kesling positioner (1945) → Soulet & Besombes activator (1950) → Bergersen Occlus-O-Guide / eruption guidance appliance (EGA) (1975).
- Documents standard protocol and tolerability profile (prefabricated, cheaper, no impression/construction bite, not caries-susceptible, no root resorption).

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic narrative hybrid review (HR) — systematic search/selection + narrative synthesis; **no meta-analysis**.
- **Databases**: Web of Science, Embase, Scopus, PubMed (to September 2023) + reference-list hand-search; librarian-assisted strategy.
- **Eligibility (PICO)**: P growing humans; I elastodontic device treatment; C before/after outcomes; O occlusal/radiographic dental and/or skeletal outcomes. Reviews excluded; no year/language limits.
- **Selection/extraction**: EndNote X9; two reviewers (V.R., S.L.R.); third-author adjudication (G.P.); Cohen kappa for agreement.

## Results

- **Screening**: 2403 citations → 2168 after de-duplication → 2029 removed on title/abstract → 120 full texts → **13 included** (3 case reports, 3 case–control, 1 case series, 5 retrospective, 1 prospective). *(Limitations section states "16" — internal inconsistency in the paper.)*
- **Background**: interceptive orthodontic treatment corrects ~15% and improves ~49% of mixed-dentition disorders.
- **Mild class III (sagittal, 2 studies)**: significant skeletal change (ANB) — driven mainly by **decreased SNB** (mandibular postural change after anterior crossbite correction) + small non-significant SNA increase + significant vestibular inclination of upper incisors → negative overjet corrected.
- **Class II (sagittal, prospective + case series)**: SNA unchanged at 1 year; **SNB significantly increased**; ANB reduced to physiological values; increased lower anterior + total facial height and mandibular length; no change in cranio-mandibular vertical relationship; minor counterclockwise mandibular growth-rotation tendency (2 studies); effective for high overjet.
- **Functional posterior crossbite (transversal, 1 prospective)**: IMW and ICW significantly increased; crossbite side gained more than non-crossbite side, narrowing the inter-side difference post-treatment.
- **Anterior crowding (transversal, 1 retrospective + 1 prospective + 1 case series + 2 case reports)**: anterior elastic channel promotes transverse dentoalveolar growth.
- **Atypical swallowing / bad habits (retrospective + case–control)**: prevents bilabial contact, repositions tongue, instructs correct swallowing; improves breathing/posture in class II oral-breathers.
- **Two-phase orthodontics (case report)**: 13-yr-old, class I malocclusion + severe crowding on skeletal class II base, overjet 4 mm / overbite 4.5 mm, deep curve of Spee, upper midline shifted right → after **4 months** active EA + myofunctional therapy: overbite reduced to edge-to-edge, anterior teeth aligned, mild mesial first-molar tipping; subsequent fixed therapy faster.
- **Lack of teeth retention (case report)**: effective in dentinogenesis imperfecta via minimum tooth anchorage.
- **Airway/OSAS**: class II have smaller airway volumes; 2 studies show improved deglutition/phonation/respiratory function vs untreated controls + inferior hyoid shift → proposed for pediatric OSAS.
- **Incidental finding**: pontilicus posticus reported in 12.6% of patients, more frequent in skeletal class II.
- **Protocol/tolerability**: active 6–8 months (max 1 yr, night + 1–2 h day); retention 12–16 months night-only; mild, transient side effects (salivation, pain).
