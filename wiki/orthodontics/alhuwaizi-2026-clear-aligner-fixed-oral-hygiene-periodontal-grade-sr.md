Qualitative SR with GRADE (6 RCTs, 2015–April 2025): periodontal/hygiene parameters significantly better with clear aligners than fixed appliances, but 5/6 RCTs at high RoB and certainty only very-low (oral hygiene) to low (periodontal) — the CA advantage is highly compliance-dependent and no firm conclusion is possible.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1093/ejo/cjaf100](https://doi.org/10.1093/ejo/cjaf100)), this systematic review pairs RoB 2 with a GRADE certainty assessment to test whether clear aligners (CA) genuinely protect oral hygiene and periodontal health better than conventional fixed appliances (FA). It is the GRADE-graded counterpart to the periodontal effect-size literature: where pooled meta-analyses report CA favored on plaque/gingival/bleeding indices, this review asks how much we should trust that signal.

From 1098 screened records, only **6 RCTs** (2015–April 2025) met inclusion, and meta-analysis was not performed — synthesis is qualitative. Qualitatively, periodontal parameters were **significantly better with CA than FA**. But the appraisal is sobering: **5 of 6 RCTs are at high risk of bias**, with inconsistencies in oral hygiene instructions, patient age, follow-up duration, diagnosis, and measurement methods. GRADE certainty came out **very low for oral hygiene level** and **low for periodontal health**. The authors conclude that the CA advantage is **highly compliance-dependent**, and that heterogeneity plus high RoB plus low/very-low certainty prevented any firm conclusion.

This was ingested abstract-only (paywalled); Summary and Results reflect the PubMed structured abstract, not the full text.

Clinically: do not promise patients that aligners "protect the gums" as an inherent property of the device. The periodontal benefit is real in the trials but rests on weak evidence and, crucially, on the patient — wear time, removal for cleaning, and hygiene adherence. A non-compliant aligner patient can lose the advantage; a meticulous fixed-appliance patient can erase the gap. Counsel on compliance, not hardware.

## Key Contributions

- First CA-vs-FA oral-hygiene/periodontal SR to apply a **dual RoB 2 + GRADE** appraisal.
- Converts the narrative "CA is gentler on the gums" claim into formal certainty grades (very-low / low).
- Reframes the periodontal edge as **compliance-dependent**, not intrinsic to the appliance.

## Methodology

- Qualitative SR (no meta-analysis), RCTs only. Databases: Scopus, PubMed, Web of Science, Google Scholar; 2015 to April 2025.
- Independent eligibility, extraction, filtering; RoB 2 for trial quality; GRADE for certainty of evidence.
- Primary outcome: oral hygiene / periodontal health by clinical periodontal indices.
- 1098 records → 6 RCTs included. PROSPERO CRD420251120933. Confidence `sr`; abstract-only ingest.

## Results

- Qualitative synthesis: periodontal parameters **significantly better with CA than FA**.
- **5 of 6 RCTs at high RoB**; inconsistencies in OHI, age, follow-up, diagnosis, and measurements.
- GRADE certainty: **very low** (oral hygiene level), **low** (periodontal health).
- No meta-analysis performed (heterogeneity).
- Bottom line: CA advantage is **highly compliance-dependent**; firm conclusion impeded by heterogeneity, high RoB, and low/very-low certainty.
