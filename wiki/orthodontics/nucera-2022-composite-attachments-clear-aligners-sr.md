## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.3390/ma15020533](https://doi.org/10.3390/ma15020533)), this SR isolates one of the three predictability determinants for aligners (alongside anchorage and trimline): the **composite attachment**. Synthesizing 5 clinical trials (all medium risk of bias) across six movement categories, it asks which movements attachments actually improve.

The answer: attachments **mostly increase** treatment effectiveness, with the clearest benefit for **anterior root torque, rotation, mesio-distal movement**, and **posterior anchorage** — exactly the movements aligners express poorly without purchase. Results were **contradictory or non-significant** for some movements; **intrusion** may improve but the evidence is weak; **extrusion** evidence is lacking; and **no studies** examined posterior bucco-lingual tipping/expansion. The review cannot yet specify the optimal attachment number, size, shape, or position.

Clinically: attachments are a predictability tool targeted at torque/rotation/derotation and anchorage, not a universal fix — and the evidence base for intrusion/extrusion remains thin, so plan those movements conservatively. This complements the distalization (anchorage) and trimline (force-delivery/retention) determinants.

## Key Contributions

- Maps attachment benefit by movement: torque, rotation, M-D, anchorage strongest.
- Identifies evidence gaps: intrusion weak; extrusion and posterior expansion unstudied.

## Methodology

SR (no MA). 8 databases to Mar 2020; two-author selection/extraction/RoB; 5 clinical trials (medium RoB); analysis across 6 movement groups. Confidence `sr`; PMC OA full text.

## Results

- Improve anterior root torque, rotation, M-D movement; increase posterior anchorage.
- Intrusion possibly improved (weak); extrusion lacking; posterior expansion unstudied.
- Attachment geometry/position effects unresolved.
