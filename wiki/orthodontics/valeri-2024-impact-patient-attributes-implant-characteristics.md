## One-line Summary

SR+MA (15 studies, 1981 patients, 3272 orthodontic mini-implants in young adults): success consistent across demographics — gender and age not significant — but failure higher in males and maxilla; insertion site and implant size not significant; higher applied force correlated with lower failure.

## Summary

This young-adult-focused SR+MA examines what actually drives orthodontic mini-implant (OMI) failure. Across 15 studies (1981 patients, 3272 OMIs), success was broadly consistent regardless of patient demographics: neither gender nor age (adolescents vs young adults) was a significant predictor, though failure trended higher in males and when implants were placed in the maxilla. Notably, insertion site and implant size were not significant for failure, and a negative correlation emerged between applied force magnitude and failure (higher force → lower failure). This refines the older site-dependence conclusion: in the young-adult subgroup, anatomical site and size matter less than the broader literature implied, while jaw (maxilla) and sex remain signals.

## Key Contributions

- Tests adolescent-vs-adult age effect head-on (not significant).
- Univariate + multivariate meta-analysis isolating single and joint factor effects.
- Counter-intuitive: higher applied force associated with lower failure.

## Methodology

- PRISMA; PROSPERO CRD42023411955; PubMed-MEDLINE, Scopus, Cochrane, WOS, ScienceDirect (focus 2015–2023).
- 599 records → 15 included (10 retrospective, 5 prospective cohorts); 1981 patients, 3272 OMIs.
- Random-effects (Cohen's method for categorical), leave-one-out sensitivity, I², Egger's test; weighted regression for numerical variables; ROBINS-I + Robvis.

## Results

- Gender: NS overall; failure higher in males.
- Age: NS (adolescents ≈ young adults).
- Jaw: failure higher in maxilla.
- Insertion site & implant size: NOT significant.
- Force: negative correlation with failure (higher force → lower failure).
- Implant range: diameter 1.2–4.1 mm, length 4–14 mm.
