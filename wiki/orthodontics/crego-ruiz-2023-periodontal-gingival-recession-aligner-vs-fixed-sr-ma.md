## One-line Summary

SR+MA (12 studies, 8 in MA, 612 patients; Cochrane RoB) finding clear aligners only slightly favor periodontal health — significant only for plaque index mid-term (MD −0.99) and pocket depth long-term (MD −0.93 mm), both at I²=99% — so evidence is insufficient to claim CA maintains better periodontal health than fixed appliances.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.4317/medoral.25760](https://doi.org/10.4317/medoral.25760)), this systematic review and meta-analysis pooled 12 studies (3 RCTs, 8 prospective cohorts, 1 retrospective; 612 patients — 291 clear aligner [CA], 321 fixed appliance [FA]), 8 of which entered quantitative analysis, to compare periodontal-health maintenance and gingival-recession development between CA and FA. Plaque index (PI), pocket probing depth (PPD), gingival index (GI) and bleeding on probing (BoP) were stratified by short- (≤2–3 mo), mid- (6–9 mo) and long-term (≥12 mo) follow-up. Risk of bias was assessed with ROBINS-I and RoB 2.0.

The data show a consistent *trend* toward better periodontal indices with CA, but the trend rarely crosses significance. Only two pooled estimates were significant: PI at mid-term (MD −0.99; 95% CI −1.94 to −0.03; P=.04) and PPD at long-term (MD −0.93 mm; 95% CI −1.16 to 0.7; P<0.0001), both favoring CA. Critically, **both estimates carry extreme heterogeneity (I²=99%)**, the long-term PPD result rests on a single study, and PI/GI are ordinal so sub-1-point mean differences are unlikely to change a clinical score. Gingival recession was reported by only one study (Madariaga et al.): FA developed significant recession by 3 months while CA did not — but this is preliminary single-study evidence. Only one study (Chhibber et al., 18-mo RCT) was at low risk of bias.

Clinically: do not over-sell aligners as periodontally superior. When adequate oral-hygiene promotion is in place, appliance type itself has little-to-null effect on periodontal health; the small CA advantage seen here is statistically fragile (extreme heterogeneity, ordinal-scale and sub-millimeter differences, sparse long-term data) and there is **insufficient evidence** to conclude CA maintains better periodontal health or causes fewer gingival recessions than FA. Patient oral hygiene — not appliance choice — remains the dominant lever.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA to jointly examine periodontal health **and** gingival recession under CA vs FA, correcting a prior MA (Jiang et al.) that had pooled non-CA studies.
- Follow-up-window stratification reveals significance is window-specific (PI mid-term, PPD long-term), not a uniform CA advantage.
- Explicitly flags the **clinical-vs-statistical gap**: ordinal indices and <1 mm PPD differences make the significant results clinically marginal.
- Documents that gingival-recession evidence is essentially absent (1 study, 3-month follow-up).

## Methodology

- **Databases**: MEDLINE, Scopus, Cochrane Library, Web of Science to 30 Sept 2022 + grey literature + manual screening; PROSPERO CRD42020175280, PRISMA.
- **PICOS**: orthodontic patients; CA vs buccal FA; outcomes PI/PPD/GI/BoP + gingival recession; RCTs/CCTs/cohorts. Excluded cross-sectional, <2-mo follow-up, no control, case series, lingual appliances, retainers, orthognathic surgery.
- Two independent reviewers (κ=0.914); change-from-baseline as outcome; random-effects pooling; GI/PI treated as continuous; missing SDs imputed.
- MA only when ≥2 studies shared the same index/window; publication bias and sensitivity not assessed (≤4 studies per MA).
- RoB: ROBINS-I (non-randomized) + RoB 2.0 (RCTs). Stata 14 / RevMan 5.3, α=0.05.

## Results

- **Yield**: 129–145 potential → 12 included → 8 in MA. 612 patients (291 CA / 321 FA); mostly 15–30 y (Han et al.: older periodontitis patients, mean 52.9 y).
- **PI (6 studies)** — mid-term **MD −0.99 (95% CI −1.94 to −0.03; P=.04; I²=99%)** favors CA; short/long-term NS (trend favors CA).
- **GI (4 studies)** — NS at all windows; trend favors CA, strongest long-term (MD −0.46; 95% CI −1.03 to 0.11; P=0.11; I²=96%).
- **PPD (5 studies)** — short/mid-term NS; long-term **MD −0.93 mm (95% CI −1.16 to 0.7; P<0.0001)** favors CA, **but single-study**.
- **BoP (8 studies, no MA)** — most studies favor CA (except Miethke, Vogt); significance timing varies (12 mo for Karkhanechi/Srinath; 9 mo but not 18 mo for Chhibber).
- **Gingival recession (1 study, Madariaga)** — FA: significant recession by 3 mo (−0.85 → −0.67 mm); CA: unchanged (−1.16 mm at both timepoints).
- **RoB**: only Chhibber et al. (18-mo RCT) low risk; outcome-assessor blinding impossible (FA not removed); 2 studies had age mismatch.
- **Bottom line**: insufficient evidence that CA maintains better periodontal health or produces fewer recessions than FA; **all MAs I²>90%**.
