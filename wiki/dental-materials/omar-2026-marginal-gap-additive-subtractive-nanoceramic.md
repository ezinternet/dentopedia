## One-line Summary

In-vitro comparative study (20 implant fixtures): milled Nanoksa G-Plus hybrid nanoceramic crowns showed significantly smaller marginal gaps (34.08→37.76 µm before/after thermomechanical aging) than 3D-printed Nanoksa BioGuard crowns (59.71→63.83 µm) — milling superior for this novel hybrid nanoceramic material class, though both stayed within the classic 120 µm clinical acceptability threshold.

## Summary

This in-vitro study compared marginal gap of implant-supported crowns fabricated from a novel hybrid nanoceramic material family (nano-zirconia + nanocarbon-reinforced polymer matrix) using two CAD/CAM manufacturing routes: subtractive milling (Nanoksa G-Plus discs) versus additive 3D printing (Nanoksa BioGuard resin, SLA/DLP-compatible). Twenty implant fixtures with stock straight titanium abutments were divided into milled (n=10) and 3D-printed (n=10) crown groups, using an identical digital design and standardized cementation protocol. Marginal gap was measured via stereomicroscope at six points per crown, both before cementation and after thermomechanical accelerated aging (5,000 thermal cycles + 75,000 mechanical loading cycles, ~6 months clinical equivalent each). Milled crowns showed significantly smaller marginal gaps than 3D-printed crowns at both timepoints (p<0.001), and both groups showed a significant gap increase after aging, attributed to cement degradation during thermocycling. All values remained under McLean & von Fraunhofer's 120 µm clinical acceptability benchmark.

## Key Contributions

- Direct head-to-head comparison of milling vs 3D printing within a single novel hybrid nanoceramic product family (Nanoksa), filling a gap where prior CAD/CAM marginal-fit comparisons used different material chemistries across groups.
- Milling produced significantly lower marginal gap both before cementation (34.08 vs 59.71 µm) and after thermomechanical aging (37.76 vs 63.83 µm), p<0.001 at each stage.
- Both fabrication methods showed statistically significant gap widening after aging (milled: +3.67 µm; printed: +4.12 µm, both p<0.001), consistent with cement layer degradation under thermocycling.
- All observed marginal gaps (34–64 µm) fell within the widely cited 120 µm clinical acceptability threshold, so both techniques are usable, but milling offers a superior safety margin.

## Methodology

- 20 dental implant fixtures (Implance, 4.8×10 mm) embedded in bone-mimicking epoxy resin (elastic modulus ~16.8 GPa) using a surveyor-aligned custom metallic mold.
- Stock straight titanium abutments (7 mm height, 2 mm collar) torqued at 25 Ncm.
- Group I (n=10): milled from Nanoksa G-Plus CAD/CAM discs (i mes-icore milling machine).
- Group II (n=10): 3D printed with Nanoksa BioGuard resin (Inox S2 SLA/DLP printer), ethanol-cleaned and UV/N2-post-cured.
- Identical digital workflow for both groups: DOF Freedom HD extraoral scanner → STL → ExoCad design with standardized 60 µm circumferential cement space (starting 1 mm above margin) and fixed molar crown geometry (10×10×10 mm).
- Marginal gap measured with a stereomicroscope (35X) at 6 standardized reference points per crown (mesio-/mid-/disto-buccal and -lingual), by a single blinded, coded examiner; per-crown mean used for analysis.
- Cementation: airborne-particle abrasion (50 µm Al2O3), Z-primer + silane primer, Duo-Link resin cement, 5 kg (49 N) static seating load × 10 min, standardized across all specimens.
- Accelerated aging: 5,000 thermal cycles (5–55°C) then 75,000 mechanical chewing-simulation cycles (50 N, 1–1.6 Hz, wet) — each ~6 months clinical equivalent.
- Statistics: paired t-test (within-group, before vs after aging) and unpaired Student t-test (between-group), SPSS v25, significance at p<0.05.

## Results

| Stage | Milled (Nanoksa G-Plus) | 3D-printed (Nanoksa BioGuard) | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| Before cementation | 34.08 ± 0.85 µm | 59.71 ± 0.79 µm | <0.001 |
| After thermomechanical aging | 37.76 ± 0.64 µm | 63.83 ± 0.57 µm | <0.001 |
| Within-group increase (aging effect) | +3.67 ± 0.86 µm (p<0.001) | +4.12 ± 1.08 µm (p<0.001) | — |

Milling was significantly superior to 3D printing at every timepoint; the null hypothesis of no difference between fabrication methods was rejected. Both groups' gaps widened significantly after aging, attributed to cement layer degradation during thermocycling, but the milled group retained its relative advantage throughout.
