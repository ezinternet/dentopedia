Narrative review (Saudi Dental Journal 2020, PubMed search 2010–2019) classifying dental ceramics by composition (glass-based, glass-infiltrated, polycrystalline) and fabrication method (stacking, heat-press, slip-cast, dry-press, CAD/CAM), with flexural strength benchmarks and clinical indication guidance.

## Summary

Warreth and Elkareimi (Saudi Dental Journal 2020) provide a comprehensive narrative overview of dental ceramics for clinical material selection. The review covers porcelain-fused-to-metal (PFM) restorations as baseline comparison, then systematically addresses all-ceramic restorations through two complementary classification frameworks: composition-based and fabrication-based.

The composition-based classification divides ceramics into three main groups. Glass-based ceramics span from feldspathic porcelain (weakest, 70–90 MPa, highest aesthetics) through moderately filled leucite-containing ceramics, to highly filled lithium disilicate ceramics (300–500 MPa, suitable for short-span bridges). Glass-infiltrated ceramics (In-Ceram group: Alumina, Spinell, Zirconia) occupy an intermediate position. Polycrystalline ceramics — alumina (~650 MPa) and zirconia (800–1500 MPa) — offer the highest strength with lower translucency.

The fabrication-based classification distinguishes: (1) conventional stacking/sintering, (2) heat/hot-pressing lost-wax technique (IPS Empress), (3) slip-casting with glass infiltration (In-Ceram), (4) dry-pressing (Procera), and (5) CAD/CAM in soft-machining (partially sintered, requires post-mill sintering, e.g., IPS e.max ZirCAD, Lava) versus hard-machining (fully sintered blocks; superior fit but more tool wear) modes. The review concludes that ceramic success depends on material selection, restoration design, occlusion, and cementation media.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a clear **two-axis classification system** (composition × fabrication) for dental ceramics enabling systematic material selection
- Provides **flexural strength benchmarks** for each ceramic group (70–90 MPa feldspathic → 300–500 MPa lithium disilicate → 800–1500 MPa zirconia) for clinical risk stratification
- Maps ceramic types to **clinical indications** (veneers, inlays, onlays, single crowns, anterior/posterior FPDs) and appropriate jaw region (anterior vs. posterior)
- Explains **monolithic vs. bi-layered** design trade-offs: bi-layered offers superior aesthetics but risks veneer delamination; monolithic is more durable for posterior use
- Explains **soft-machining vs. hard-machining** CAD/CAM distinction for polycrystalline ceramics
- Summarizes PFM long-term failure data (4% @ 5y, 12% @ 10y, 32% @ 15y — Valderhaug 1991) as historical context

## Methodology

- **Study type**: Narrative review (no formal PRISMA/systematic protocol)
- **Search**: PubMed (MEDLINE), restricted to January 2010 – December 2019, English only
- **Keywords**: Ceramics, all-ceramics, dental porcelain, polycrystalline, PFM, ceramo-metal, Procera, e.max, zirconia, In-ceram, inlays, onlays, overlays, endocrown; combined with survival rate/success rate/clinical outcomes/classification; restricted to humans
- **Yield**: 2,173 PubMed articles + additional Google Scholar + manual searches; most relevant selected narratively
- **Limitation of design**: No formal inclusion/exclusion criteria reporting; no risk of bias assessment; article selection not blinded

## Results

**Composition classification — flexural strengths:**

| Ceramic class | Particle content | Flexural strength | Key examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Feldspathic (predominantly glass) | <17% | 70–90 MPa | VM13 (Vita), All-Ceram (Degudent) |
| Leucite-based (moderately filled) | 17–25% | Improved vs. feldspathic | VMK 95, Ceramco II, IPS d.SIGN |
| Leucite-based (highly filled) | 45–70% | 120–160 MPa | IPS Empress Aesthetic, Optec OPC |
| Lithium disilicate (highly filled) | 45–70% | 300–500 MPa | IPS e.max Press/CAD |
| In-Ceram Alumina (glass-infiltrated) | ~70% alumina + 30% glass | ~600 MPa | In-Ceram Alumina (Vita) |
| In-Ceram Spinell | MgAl₂O₄ + trace alumina | ~350 MPa | In-Ceram Spinell (Vita) |
| Alumina (polycrystalline) | 99.9% | ~650 MPa | Procera Alumina |
| Zirconia Y-TZP (polycrystalline) | 99.9% | 800–1500 MPa | IPS e.max ZirCAD, Lava |

**Clinical indication mapping:**
- Feldspathic: Veneers, inlays, onlays, veneering layers for all-ceramic or PFM frameworks
- Leucite/lithium disilicate: Full range — veneers, inlays, onlays, crowns, anterior short-span FPDs
- In-Ceram: Single crowns, 3-unit anterior FPDs (Alumina/Zirconia); anterior crowns only (Spinell)
- Zirconia: Monolithic posterior crowns; also core/framework for bi-layered restorations

**PFM failure benchmark** (Valderhaug 1991 cited): 4% at 5 years, 12% at 10 years, 32% at 15 years.

**PFM disadvantages**: Light opacity (dark appearance, especially cervically); metal allergy risk (nickel); requires adequate tooth reduction to mask metal.
