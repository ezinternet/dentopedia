## Summary
Largest real-world dataset comparing amalgam and composite resin restoration survival. Retrospective cohort using electronic health records from 58 dental clinics (440 dental units) in the Maccabi Healthcare Fund (Israel), 2014–2021. Primary outcome: restoration failure defined as replacement, root canal treatment, or extraction within follow-up. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis and Cox proportional hazards regression with adjustment for age, sex, number of surfaces, tooth type, and operator.

Annual failure rate: amalgam 3.5% vs composite 4.5% (HR 1.29, p<0.001). Single-surface restorations showed no significant difference between materials. Multi-surface composite restorations had significantly higher failure rates than amalgam equivalents.

## Key Contributions
- Largest real-world dataset on amalgam vs composite survival (>650,000 patients; ~260,905 restored patients over 2014–2021)
- Composite HR 1.29 overall (p<0.001), driven by multi-surface restorations
- Single-surface: no significant material difference — key clinical take-away
- Provides evidence in the context of Minamata Convention mercury restrictions

## Methodology
- **Design**: Retrospective cohort; electronic health records
- **n**: >650,000 patients; ~260,905 with restorations placed 2014–2021
- **Setting**: 58 clinics, 440 dental units, Maccabi Healthcare Fund, Israel
- **Failure definition**: Restoration replacement, RCT, or extraction
- **Analysis**: Kaplan-Meier + Cox regression; confounders: age, sex, surface number, tooth type, operator
