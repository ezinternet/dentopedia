## One-line Summary

SR+MA (21 studies, October 2024 search) of resin matrix ceramic (PICN and RNC) restorations: RMC crowns had higher complication rates than conventional ceramics; laminate/occlusal veneers and inlays/onlays showed comparable outcomes to lithium disilicate; overall performance is restoration-type dependent.

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis (PRISMA 2020) evaluated clinical complications, success, and survival rates of resin matrix ceramic (RMC) restorations — specifically polymer infiltrated ceramic network (PICN) and resin nano ceramic (RNC) materials — compared to conventional ceramic and direct composite alternatives. PubMed, Scopus, and Cochrane were searched through October 2024, yielding 21 included studies. Risk of bias was assessed using Cochrane RoB 2 (RCTs) and ROBINS-I (non-randomized studies). Meta-analysis used inverse variance with fixed effects model, results expressed as risk ratios (RR) with 95% CI.

Key finding: RMC clinical performance is strongly **restoration-type dependent**:
- **Full and partial crowns**: RMC showed **higher complication rates** than conventional ceramics (lithium disilicate). Poorer success and survival rates for crown geometries.
- **Laminate and occlusal veneers**: RMC demonstrated **comparable outcomes** to lithium disilicate — a favorable indication.
- **Intracoronal restorations (inlays/onlays)**: RMC showed complications **similar to lithium disilicate**; better than direct composite restorations.

Clinical implication: RMC/hybrid ceramics are NOT preferred for crown restorations but show favorable and clinically acceptable performance for veneers and intracoronal (inlay/onlay) restorations. Material selection must match the biomechanical demands of the restoration design.

## Key Contributions

- Comprehensive SR+MA covering all RMC restoration types in a single analysis — crowns, veneers, inlays/onlays, implant-supported restorations
- Stratified results by restoration type: first clear quantitative evidence that RMC "good indications" (veneers, intracoronal) differ from "poor indications" (crowns)
- RMC vs composite comparison: RMC intracoronal restorations outperform direct composite, supporting indirect RMC as a composite upgrade
- RMC vs lithium disilicate comparison: crowns favor LD; veneers and inlays are equivalent — provides material selection algorithm
- Identifies key future research need: long-term (≥5 year) RCTs for crown-geometry RMC

## Methodology

- **Framework**: PRISMA 2020
- **Databases**: PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane (search: October 2024)
- **Inclusion**: Clinical studies (RCTs + prospective/retrospective observational) evaluating PICN or RNC complications
- **Materials**: PICN (e.g., Vita Enamic, Lava Ultimate) and RNC (e.g., Cerasmart, Ceram.x)
- **Risk of bias**: RoB 2 (randomized trials), ROBINS-I (non-randomized studies)
- **Statistical model**: Inverse variance, fixed effects; results as Risk Ratio (RR) with 95% CI; α ≤ 0.05
- **Studies included**: 21 studies
- **Comparators**: Lithium disilicate glass-ceramic, feldspathic ceramic, direct composite resin

## Results

### By Restoration Type

| Restoration Type | RMC vs Ceramic | RMC vs Composite |
|---|---|---|
| Full/partial crown | **Higher** complication rate (worse) | — |
| Laminate veneer | Comparable to LD | — |
| Occlusal veneer | Comparable to LD | — |
| Inlay/onlay | Similar to LD | **Fewer** complications (better) |

### Clinical Complication Patterns

- Crown failures in RMC: predominantly fracture/chipping, debonding — consistent with lower flexural strength of PICN (~150–200 MPa) vs lithium disilicate (~400 MPa) under heavy occlusal loading in crown geometry
- Veneer success of RMC: thin geometry and adhesive bonding regime reduces stress concentration; PICN's elastic modulus closer to dentin (~40 GPa) may absorb flexural stress
- Intracoronal RMC: conservative cavity preparation retains more tooth structure, reducing fracture risk; PICN's moderate elastic modulus advantageous vs stiff ceramic in deep inlays

### Success and Survival

- Overall success and survival rates: **dependent on restoration size and cavity design**
- Poorer success/survival: full and partial coverage crowns
- Acceptable/good success/survival: veneers and intracoronal restorations

### Implant-Supported RMC

- PICN/RNC on implant hybrid abutment crowns: comparable crestal bone levels and periodontal parameters vs lithium disilicate in one included RCT
- Adhesive bonding of RNC to zirconia abutments: some bond failures reported (especially with resin cement alone, without silane/primer protocol)
