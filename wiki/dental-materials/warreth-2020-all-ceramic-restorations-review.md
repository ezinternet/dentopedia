## Summary
Narrative review (PubMed 2010–2019, 2173 articles identified) covering dental ceramic classification, construction methods, and clinical indications. Comprehensively categorizes glass-based (feldspathic, leucite, lithium disilicate) vs polycrystalline (alumina, zirconia) ceramics. Compares PFM vs all-ceramic. Discusses zirconia generations and expanding clinical indications.

## Key Contributions
- Comprehensive classification: glass-based vs polycrystalline dental ceramics
- Lithium disilicate: optimal strength-esthetics balance; most versatile
- Zirconia: high strength for posterior bridges; translucency improving with new generations
- PFM remains clinically reliable; esthetic limitations drive all-ceramic adoption
- CAD/CAM fabrication improved dimensional accuracy and predictability
- Adhesive cementation essential for glass-ceramics; conventional for zirconia crowns

## Methodology
Narrative review. PubMed database search January 2010–December 2019 (2173 articles). Keywords: ceramics, all-ceramics, dental porcelain, polycrystalline, PFM, e.max, zirconia, In-Ceram, inlays, onlays, overlays, endocrown. Topics: microstructure, fabrication (pressing, CAD/CAM, heat-pressing), surface treatment, cementation, clinical indications, failure modes.

## Results
- Glass-based ceramics: feldspathic (lowest strength), leucite, lithium disilicate (highest in group)
- Polycrystalline: alumina (historical), zirconia (3Y-TZP: dominant clinical choice in 2020)
- Lithium disilicate: flexural strength ~400 MPa; can be etched with HF + silane
- Zirconia: flexural strength >900 MPa; requires mechanical/tribochemical surface pretreatment
- CAD/CAM improved marginal fit; digital workflow reduces chair time
- Failure modes: cohesive fracture (glass), delamination (veneered zirconia)
