In vitro fractographic study (n=168 specimens, 7 zirconia materials, 3–5 mol% Y) showing CAM-milling defects dominate as-sintered strength while polishing shifts failure to intrinsic subsurface pores and grain clusters.

## Summary

Kwon et al. (2024) investigated the flexural strength, Weibull reliability, and fracture origins of seven commercially available CAD/CAM full-contour zirconia materials (3Y, 4Y, 5Y yttria contents) comparing as-sintered (AS) vs. high-polished (HP) specimens using four-point bend testing and systematic fractography (FE-SEM + EDS).

**Core finding:** Two distinct flaw populations control zirconia strength depending on surface state.

**As-sintered specimens** fail from **extrinsic (CAM-milling) defects**:
- Surface gouges and scratches from milling bur contact
- Edge chips with associated microcracks
- These are the weakest flaws and dictate AS strength (lower mean but more consistent → higher Weibull modulus)

**High-polished specimens** fail from **intrinsic (processing/blank) defects**:
- Subsurface or volume pores
- Large grain clusters (abnormal grain growth during sintering)
- Foreign inclusions detected by EDS (Al, Si, Ca, Fe — likely powder contamination or bur wear debris)
- Corner-located critical flaws
- Higher mean strength than AS (surface defects removed) but lower Weibull modulus (more variable intrinsic flaw population)

## Key Contributions

1. **Two-population flaw model** for dental CAD/CAM zirconia: extrinsic (milling) vs. intrinsic (blank/sintering) — both independently contribute to clinical fracture risk
2. **Polishing paradox**: polishing increases mean strength but often decreases reliability (Weibull modulus) — replacing controllable surface flaws with random intrinsic ones
3. **5Y-PSZ heterogeneity**: two out of three 5Y materials showed no significant effect of polishing (milling damage may self-heal or be less severe during sintering); one 5Y material showed significant strength gain but reliability loss after polishing
4. **EDS-identified contaminants** at fracture origins suggest powder/blank quality variation between manufacturers — clinically opaque manufacturing differences matter for reliability

## Methodology

- 7 commercial zirconia blanks: 3Y, 4Y, 5Y (including 3 high-translucent 5Y grades)
- Bend bars: 2.0 × 4.0 × 25.0 mm³, milled from pre-sintered pucks (5-axis CAM, DWX-51D Roland)
- Sintering: 1530–1550°C, 2 h per manufacturer instructions
- Groups: AS (n=12/material) vs. HP (n=12/material) — 3-step chairside polishing + goat-hair brush + Zircon-Brite paste
- Four-point flexure (1/4-point, inner/outer span 10/20 mm)
- Fractography: FE-SEM + EDS at fracture origins
- Statistics: Weibull analysis (characteristic strength σ₀, modulus m)

## Results

| Condition | Fracture Origin Type | Strength | Weibull Modulus |
|---|---|---|---|
| As-sintered (AS) | Extrinsic: milling gouges, scratches, edge chips | Lower | Higher (more consistent) |
| High-polished (HP) | Intrinsic: pores, grain clusters, inclusions, corner flaws | Higher | Generally lower |

- 5Y-PSZ Group A&B: polishing → no significant change in strength or reliability
- 5Y-PSZ Group C: polishing → significant strength increase + significant reliability decrease
- Clinical implication: un-polished (glazed) full-contour zirconia restorations retain milling-introduced defects that can act as fracture origins under occlusal loading
