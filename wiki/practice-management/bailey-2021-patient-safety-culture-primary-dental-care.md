## One-line Summary

Narrative review/perspective (Prim Dent J 2021) arguing that patient safety must move from secondary-care/academic research into primary dental care — where ~95% of care is delivered — using human factors, second-victim awareness, restraint on over-regulation, and a non-punitive culture as the tools to build a patient safety culture in practice.

## Summary

Patient safety should sit at the heart of any healthcare service: systems, teams, individuals, and the working environment all have to operate in tandem to produce safety and quality. Bailey and Dungarwalla note that dental patient-safety research is still in its infancy and that most of it originates from **secondary care and academia** — yet **approximately 95% of dental care is delivered in primary care**. The evidence base, in other words, is being applied in a setting where it was largely not generated. The paper gives an overview of that evidence base and offers the primary-care clinician a set of practical tools for building a patient safety culture in their own practice.

## Key Contributions

- **Reframes the locus of dental safety** — shifts attention from secondary-care/academic research to the primary-care setting where the overwhelming majority (~95%) of dental care actually happens.
- **Human factors** — positions error as emerging from system, environment, and team interactions rather than individual fault alone.
- **Second-victim concept** — recognizes that the clinician or team involved in an adverse event is themselves harmed (distress, guilt, eroded confidence) and needs support; a supportive, non-punitive response is part of, not separate from, a safety culture.
- **Caution against over-regulation** — warns that excessive prescriptive regulation can be counterproductive, suppressing the open reporting and learning that a genuine safety culture depends on.
- **Building a patient safety culture** — synthesizes the above into actionable culture-building (shared values, psychological safety, open incident reporting, learning over blame) for the dental practice.

## Methodology

Narrative review / perspective. Synthesizes the existing patient-safety evidence base — drawn largely from general healthcare and secondary care — and re-contextualizes it for primary dental care. No systematic search or quantitative pooling; sits low on the evidence hierarchy and is best read as an orienting framework rather than a source of effect estimates.

## Results

No quantitative outcomes (narrative, abstract-only). Headline qualitative claims:

- **~95%** of dental care is delivered in primary care, where most safety research has not been conducted.
- Errors are systemic (human-factors view), not merely individual.
- Clinicians involved in incidents are **second victims** requiring support; punitive responses undermine reporting.
- **Over-regulation** risks suppressing the open learning that safety culture requires.
- A patient safety culture is built on shared values, open reporting, and learning rather than blame.
