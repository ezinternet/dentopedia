## Summary

> abstract-only — full text not retrieved

This conceptual primer sets out the principles of economics and applies them to the promotion, protection, and restoration of oral health in populations and to the planning, management, and delivery of oral health care. It is foundational scaffolding for the practice-management theme of cost, insurance, and behavioral economics: the empirical studies in this category (utilisation, cost-sharing, workforce) are concrete instances of the demand/supply, market-failure, and workforce-planning concepts framed here.

## Key Contributions

- **Economic determinants of oral health.** Oral health outcomes are shaped not only by clinical inputs but by economic factors (income, prices, insurance, access), which the paper uses as the analytic entry point.
- **Demand and asymmetric information.** Demand for oral health care is examined with particular reference to **asymmetric information** between patient and provider: the patient cannot fully judge the need for or quality of care, so the provider acts as an agent and shapes demand.
- **Market failure → efficiency and equity.** The paper explains why (oral) health-care markets fail and the implications for **efficiency** (resources not reaching highest-value care) and **equity** (utilisation driven by ability to pay rather than need).
- **Economic evaluation under resource constraints.** Economic evaluation (e.g., cost-effectiveness analysis) is positioned as the method for allocating constrained resources to maximise oral health gains.
- **Behavioural aspects & supplier-induced demand.** Both patients' demand for and dental professionals' provision of services have behavioural dimensions; provider agency under asymmetric information creates scope for **supplier-induced demand**.
- **Workforce planning.** Outlines methods for planning the dental workforce in ways that reflect system goals rather than supply inertia.

## Methodology

Narrative review / conceptual primer. No empirical dataset; the paper organises established health-economics theory and maps it onto oral health and dental care delivery. Methodological detail beyond the abstract was not retrieved (abstract-only ingest).

## Results

Not applicable — conceptual primer, no quantitative results (abstract-only — full text not retrieved). Conceptual takeaways:

- Demand for dental care is distorted by asymmetric information, giving providers an agency role and creating scope for supplier-induced demand.
- Market failure in oral health care undermines both efficiency and equity.
- Economic evaluation is the mechanism for choosing among interventions when resources are constrained.
- Workforce planning should be derived from system goals (need/demand), not supply.
