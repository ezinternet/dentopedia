## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional questionnaire study (n=294 parents, pediatric dental clinic, Navi Mumbai, India) found a 52% missed-appointment rate; forgetfulness and exams were the top reasons for missing, reliance on memory (and higher socioeconomic status) were associated with missing (p<0.05), and 48.3% preferred a reminder call from the dentist the day before.

## Summary

This cross-sectional, self-administered questionnaire study examined why children miss appointments at a pediatric dental clinic in a dental college in Navi Mumbai, Maharashtra, India. Among **294 parents** of children in active treatment (surveyed Jan–Jul 2015), **52% reported having missed an appointment**. The most frequent reasons for *missing* were forgetfulness and the child's exams; the most frequent reasons for *canceling* were the patient being unwell and parents' commitments to other work. Reliance on memory to track appointments was significantly associated with no-shows (p=0.024), and the abstract reports socioeconomic status as significantly correlated with missing (p<0.05) — though notably the upper-middle class, not the lowest, had the highest no-show rate (58.8%), suggesting busy schedules and low prioritization rather than cost. Child age, gender, and parental education were not significant correlates. The single most preferred intervention was a **reminder phone call from the dentist** a day before the appointment (48.3%). The authors recommend a flexible, accessible appointment system with a reminder/recall component, established at the patient's first visit.

This India-based pediatric cohort triangulates with the Jordan-based [[practice-management/khries-2024-identifying-barriers-pediatric-dental-appointments]] (n=265, 44% missed): both find pediatric no-show driven largely by forgetfulness and family/parent commitments rather than cost, and both point to reminder systems as the headline lever.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies pediatric dental no-show burden in an Indian setting: **52% missed-appointment rate** (153/294).
- Identifies **memory-reliance** as a significant, modifiable correlate of no-shows (p=0.024) — patients tracking appointments by memory alone had the highest failure rate.
- Surfaces a **counter-intuitive socioeconomic pattern**: higher (upper-middle) SES had the highest no-show rate (58.8%), implying time/prioritization barriers, not financial barriers.
- Establishes patient-preferred intervention hierarchy: **dentist phone call (48.3%) > appointment card (25.9%) > SMS (23.8%) > e-mail (2%)**.
- Profiles the highest-risk no-show patient: young, memory-reliant, high SES, male, with prior broken appointments.

## Methodology

- **Design**: cross-sectional, self-administered questionnaire (English, translated to the regional language; pretested and refined).
- **Setting / sample**: pediatric dental clinic of a dental college, Navi Mumbai; 294 complete questionnaires from parents in the waiting area whose children were in active treatment, over 6 months (Jan–Jul 2015).
- **Domains**: sociodemographics; methods of remembering appointments; reasons for canceling/missing; satisfaction with past clinic experience.
- **Six analysis variables**: child age, child gender, parent education, family socioeconomic status (Kuppuswamy's scale, 1976), reason for canceling, method of remembering.
- **Statistics**: Kendall's Tau-B correlation, α=0.05.

## Results

- **Missed an appointment**: 52% (153/294); not missed 48%.
- **Reasons for missing**: exams 17.0%, forgot 17.0%, too ill 15.3%, symptoms improved 15.3%, parent commitments 9.5%, child's fear 3.7%, not doctor of choice 3.7%, other 4.1%.
- **Reasons for canceling**: patient unwell 27.9%, parent commitments 15.0%, scheduling problem 12.2%, long clinic wait 10.5%, child not in mood 9.2%, financial 6.1%, traffic 1.7%.
- **Method of remembering** (SIGNIFICANT, p=0.024): memory 52.0% (highest no-show), diary 24.8%, mobile 19.7%, other 3.4%.
- **Age** (NS, p=0.951): 7–12 yr 53.4% > 1–6 yr 51.6% > 13–18 yr 50% (missed rate decreased with age).
- **Gender** (NS, p=0.089): males 54.4% vs females 45.6%.
- **Socioeconomic status**: upper-middle 58.8% (highest), lower-middle 38.2%, upper 35.7%, upper-lower 18.8% (abstract: significant p<0.05; SES table reports p=0.142 — internal inconsistency, treat as suggestive).
- **Preferred reminder**: dentist call 48.3%, card 25.9%, SMS 23.8%, e-mail 2.0%.

> [!note] Data caveat
> The abstract states socioeconomic status was significantly correlated with missing (p<0.05), but the SES results table reports p=0.142 (not significant). The robust significant correlate is the *method of remembering* (memory-reliance, p=0.024).
