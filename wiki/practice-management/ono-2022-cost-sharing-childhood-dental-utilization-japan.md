## One-line Summary

Retrospective difference-in-differences study of Japanese administrative dental claims (1108 children, 6276 visits, 455 clinics) finding that discontinuing a childhood dental subsidy at age 10 produced NO significant short-term (12-month) change in utilization, cost, or acute severe dental conditions.

## Summary

This study asks a health-economics question with direct practice-management relevance: does reducing patient cost-sharing (here, a public childhood dental subsidy) actually increase dental care utilization and improve oral-health outcomes? Most prior evidence on the cost-sharing → utilization relationship comes from the US, where out-of-pocket exposure is large; the authors test whether it holds in a **universal-healthcare** system (Japan), where baseline patient charges are already minimal.

Using a Kumamoto Prefecture administrative claims database (2014–2015), they exploit a policy discontinuity — municipalities that **discontinued the childhood dental subsidy at age 10** versus those that retained it — as a natural experiment, estimated with **difference-in-differences (DiD)**. Among 1108 eligible children (6276 visits, 455 clinics; 230 children, 50.5%, in subsidy-discontinued municipalities), removing the subsidy produced **no significant short-term change** in any outcome.

The bottom line should be read **cautiously**: this is a 12-month, single-prefecture, universal-coverage finding where residual cost-sharing was already minimal — so the price change being tested was small. The null result says the *current* subsidy policy has limited marginal value in this context, not that price is universally irrelevant to dental demand.

## Key Contributions

- Brings cost-sharing → dental utilization evidence into a **universal-healthcare** (non-US) context.
- Uses a clean **policy-discontinuity natural experiment** (age-10 subsidy cutoff) with **difference-in-differences** on administrative claims, rather than self-report.
- Reports a **null short-term effect**, framing the current subsidy as having limited marginal value for promoting utilization or preventing caries in school-aged Japanese children.

## Methodology

- **Design**: retrospective natural experiment, difference-in-differences on administrative dental claims.
- **Setting / data**: Kumamoto Prefecture, Japan, 2014–2015; municipalities with subsidy upper age limit ≥9 years.
- **Comparison**: children in municipalities that discontinued the subsidy at age 10 (treated, n=230, 50.5%) vs those that retained it (control), adjusting for area income and the minimal residual user charges.
- **Sample**: 1108 children, 6276 visits, 455 clinics.
- **Outcomes**: first visits, total visits, cost per visit (utilization); treatment for acute severe dental conditions (caries-prevention-failure proxy).

## Results

(abstract-only — full text not retrieved; values as reported in abstract)

| Outcome | Effect estimate | 95% CI | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| First visits | mean ratio 1.01 | 0.97–1.04 | no significant change |
| Total visits | mean ratio 1.01 | 0.98–1.05 | no significant change |
| Acute severe dental conditions | mean ratio 1.06 | 0.90–1.24 | no significant change |
| Cost | coef 0.7 USD | −0.2–1.6 | no significant change |

- Every interval spans the null (1.00 for ratios, 0 for the cost coefficient): discontinuing the subsidy at age 10 had **no detectable 12-month effect** on utilization, cost, or acute severe conditions.
- **Interpretation caveats**: short horizon (12 months); universal-coverage setting; minimal baseline cost-sharing (the price change tested was small); single prefecture; claims-based proxy for caries. The authors conclude the *current* subsidy policy may have limited value for promoting utilization and improving oral health among school-aged children in Japan — not that cost-sharing is irrelevant in general.
