## One-line Summary

Umbrella review of 10 systematic reviews (AMSTAR-2 appraised): teledentistry is widely assumed cost-saving with positive oral-health signals, but every included review was low/critically-low quality and the 10 reviews drew on only 5 distinct economic-evaluation studies — so there is insufficient evidence for true cost-effectiveness or long-term clinical effectiveness.

## Summary

Scheerman et al. (2024) is the **first umbrella review** to synthesize systematic reviews specifically on teledentistry's effects on **costs and oral-health outcomes**. Searching seven databases (plus JBI/DARE/PROSPERO) through April 2023 and appraising with AMSTAR-2, the authors included 10 systematic reviews. The headline is a strong evidence-gap message: teledentistry is *commonly perceived* as cost-saving and the oral-health data are not unfavorable, but the conclusions rest on assumptions and low-quality primary studies. Every included review rated **low or critically low** on AMSTAR-2, and across all 10 reviews only **five distinct primary studies** carried any economic evaluation — none of them a cost-benefit, cost-utility, or incremental analysis. The clinical bottom line is that teledentistry remains promising but evidentially under-supported, and the field needs high-quality experimental designs and standardized terminology before cost-effectiveness claims can be sustained.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes the first cross-review synthesis of teledentistry cost and oral-health evidence.
- Classifies interventions by the **IADR e-oral health network consensus definition** rather than authors' inconsistent terms (m-health / tele-oral medicine / telehealth), tackling terminology confusion head-on.
- Makes the evidence scarcity concrete: 10 reviews → only 5 unique economic-evaluation studies; cost-effectiveness assertions are assumptions, not findings.
- Documents uniformly low methodological quality (AMSTAR-2) of the existing teledentistry review literature.

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA-consistent umbrella review (Aromataris methodology); analytical unit = the review. PROSPERO CRD42023363204.
- **Search**: seven databases (PubMed/MEDLINE, EMBASE, CINAHL, Web of Science, Scopus, Cochrane CDSR, NHSEED & HTA) inception → 24 Apr 2023, plus JBI/DARE/PROSPERO and citation tracking; EN/NL/ES/DE.
- **Eligibility**: peer-reviewed systematic reviews whose primary studies met the e-oral health network teledentistry definition (synchronous or asynchronous communication between provider and patient/caregiver, or between providers, separated by distance). Excluded training-only telehealth and unidirectional interventions (e.g. automated reminder texts).
- **Appraisal**: AMSTAR-2 (7 critical + 9 non-critical domains); two independent reviewers, third for consensus.
- **Synthesis**: narrative only; meta-analysis precluded by heterogeneity.

## Results

- 880 records after deduplication → 171 full texts → **10 reviews included** (abstract: 749 identified).
- **Oral-health outcomes** (2 reviews): Ben-Omran 2021 — no significant GOHAI/OHRQoL difference vs control; Estai 2018 — teledentistry comparable to or better than non-telemedicine on DFS/periodontal/oral-hygiene indices. Both: evidence not yet conclusive for long-term effectiveness.
- **Costs** (10 reviews): all indicated cost-saving, but only 5 reported costs as an outcome of interest, and collectively the reviews used just **5 distinct economic-evaluation primary studies** (cost description, cost analysis, cost-minimization, time-effectiveness). Asynchronous store-and-forward reported as least costly, then real-time, then face-to-face (Aquilanti 2020); real-time better on cost outcomes (Joshi 2020). No cost-benefit / cost-utility / incremental analyses anywhere.
- **Quality**: all 10 reviews **low or critically low** on AMSTAR-2; weakest domains Q3, Q7, Q8, Q13, Q14; none reported primary-study funding (Q10).
- **Context**: teledentistry far behind telemedicine in economic evidence (Bergmo: 33 telemedicine economic evaluations 1990–2007); consistent with Eze et al. telemedicine umbrella review (92% low/critically-low quality).
- **Gap**: no experimental teledentistry effectiveness study exists for special-needs populations (disability, mental illness, institutionalized/older people) despite their high oral-disease burden.
