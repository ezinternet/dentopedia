## One-line Summary

Systematic review (11 studies: 8 before–after + 3 controlled trials; general healthcare) finds MODERATE evidence that the SBAR hand-off tool improves patient safety — 8 of 26 outcomes significantly improved, strongest for structured nurse-to-physician telephone communication, with overall low study quality.

## Summary

> **Framing — general-healthcare evidence extrapolated to dentistry.** This is NOT a dental study. It is a general-healthcare systematic review of nurses and physicians in hospitals, a rehabilitation centre and nursing homes. It is held in the dental wiki's `practice-management` category to **anchor the dental team-communication axis**: SBAR is a concrete, transferable intra-team communication tool, and structured hand-offs become more critical the more a dental practice delegates tasks across its team (dentist, hygienist, assistant, front desk). Treat the evidence as a direct framework import, not a dental outcome.

SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) is a four-part structured hand-off framework, widely adopted across healthcare as "best practice" for delivering information in critical situations. Its value for *soft* outcomes (employee satisfaction, communication completeness, interdisciplinary teamwork) was already documented; what was unclear — and what this review tests — is whether SBAR improves *hard* patient-safety outcomes (incidence of adverse events).

Eleven studies (2006–2016) met inclusion: **8 before–after designs, 2 controlled clinical trials, 1 RCT**, across hospitals (7), nursing homes (3) and a rehabilitation centre (1). SBAR was deployed for three purposes: team communication in general, patient hand-offs (nurse-to-nurse or interprofessional), and structured nurse-to-physician telephone calls (anticoagulation management, patient deterioration).

Across the 11 studies, **26 distinct patient outcomes** were measured: **8 significantly improved** (in 5 studies), **11 were described as improving without any statistical test** (4 before–after studies), **6 were unchanged**, and **1 study reported a descriptive increase** in adverse events (overall falls rose while fall severity fell — plausibly a reporting-awareness artefact). None showed a significant *reduction* in safety. The authors graded this as **MODERATE evidence** for improved patient safety, **strongest when SBAR structures communication over the phone**.

The evidence quality is the key caveat: only the single RCT (Field) was rated strong and one CCT (Randmaa) moderate; the remaining 9 studies were weak. No study performed a power calculation, roughly half the outcomes lacked any statistical test, and no study measured communication *process* measures — so the causal chain (SBAR -> better communication -> changed conduct -> better outcome) is asserted rather than demonstrated. The authors caution that unreflected adoption of a tool presumed to "solve" communication may paradoxically stall further improvement.

## Key Contributions

- First SR to isolate SBAR's effect on **hard patient-safety outcomes** rather than soft satisfaction/perception measures.
- Identifies the **highest-yield use case**: structured nurse-to-physician **telephone** hand-off for deteriorating patients (significant reductions in unexpected death, hospital transfers, readmissions).
- Quantifies the **evidence-quality gap** behind a near-universally adopted tool: 9 of 11 studies weak, none powered, ~half of outcomes untested statistically.
- Provides a transferable structural template (S/B/A/R) for any team hand-off, including the dental team.

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review (narrative synthesis; meta-analysis impossible due to heterogeneity of designs, outcomes, settings and SBAR variants).
- **Search**: PUBMED, EMBASE, CINAHL, Cochrane Library, PsycINFO (OvidSP), Jan 2017, + reference screening; terms SBAR/ISBAR/SBAR-R/ISBARR/ISOBAR; no language/date/type limit; no grey literature; no protocol.
- **Eligibility**: SBAR implemented into routine, SBAR the primary objective, ≥1 patient outcome. Excluded when SBAR was a minor part of a larger bundle.
- **Quality**: EPHPP tool (Cochrane-recommended), 18 criteria / 6 domains, strong/moderate/weak; two reviewers + tiebreak.
- **Agreement**: inclusion ICC 0.90 (95% CI 0.86–0.94); quality-rating ICC 0.85 (95% CI 0.78–0.90).

## Results

- **Inclusion flow**: 1053 -> 701 de-duplicated -> 94 full-text -> **11 included**.
- **Outcomes (n=26)**: 8 significantly improved · 11 improved without statistical test · 6 unchanged · 1 descriptive increase in adverse events.
- **Significant improvements (selected)**:
  - **Field 2011** (RCT, nursing home, telephone nurse→doctor): INR time-in-target +4.5% (95% CI 3.1%–8.7%); warfarin-related AE not significant (OR 0.9, 95% CI 0.5–1.4).
  - **Randmaa 2014** (CCT, anaesthesiology hand-off): communication-error CIRS events 31% → 11%.
  - **De Meester 2013** (before–after, telephone deterioration): unexpected death 0.99 → 0.34 per 1000 admissions (p<0.001).
  - **Devereaux 2016** (before–after, nursing home telephone): 30-day readmissions 0.12 → 0.04 (p=0.012); avoidable hospitalisations 0.15 → 0.05 (p=0.007).
- **Descriptive-only (no test)**: Christie & Robinson 2009 — mortality −11%, MRSA bacteraemia −83%, adverse events −65%; also Haig 2006, Andreoli 2010, Freitag & Carroll 2011.
- **Null / counter**: Telem 2011 (sentinel events NS), Jarboe 2015 (transfers NS, longer follow-up), Andreoli 2010 (overall falls incidence ↑ with severity ↓).
- **Quality**: 1 strong (RCT), 1 moderate (CCT), 9 weak; no power calculations; no process measures.
- **Verdict**: **moderate** evidence for improved patient safety; **strongest for telephone communication**; high-quality research still lacking.
