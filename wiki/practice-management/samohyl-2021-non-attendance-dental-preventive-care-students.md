## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional school-based survey (Slovakia, n=515 students aged 15-22 + 681 parents): male gender, older age, incomplete family, low household income, and home stress predicted non-attendance at dental preventive care; a parent's own avoidance of preventive care was the strongest independent predictor of the child's avoidance, and skipping pediatric care co-occurred with skipping dental care (AOR 5.14, 95% CI 2.40-10.99).

## Summary

This Slovak cross-sectional school-based survey (the Youth and Parents Risk Factor Behavior Survey, Bratislava) examined why adolescents and young adults aged 15-22 avoid dental preventive care. Of 515 students, 57 had not attended a preventive check-up in the previous year (study group) versus 458 who had (control). By linking students to their parents' questionnaires, the study isolates a **parental behavior model**: a parent who avoids their own preventive care independently predicts the child's non-attendance. This reframes non-attendance from an individual patient failing into a family and socioeconomic pattern — a clinically actionable insight for recall and prevention targeting.

## Key Contributions

- Establishes **parental avoidance as an independent predictor** of an adolescent's dental non-attendance: maternal non-attendance AOR 4.19 (95% CI 1.50-11.71, p < 0.01) and paternal non-attendance AOR 3.41 (95% CI 1.24-9.32, p < 0.05).
- Shows that not attending **pediatric** preventive care strongly co-occurs with skipping **dental** care (AOR 5.14, 95% CI 2.40-10.99) — non-attendance is a general health-behavior trait, not tooth-specific.
- Identifies a family/SES cluster of avoiders: male gender, older adolescent/young-adult age, incomplete (divorced/single) family, low household income (<EUR 900), and home stress.
- Recommends extending free twice-yearly preventive check-ups beyond age 18, since the 19+ group loses the under-18 entitlement yet carries the highest treatment need.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional school-based survey using two CDC-derived (BRFSS/YRBSS) standardized questionnaires — one for students, one for parents — pilot-validated in Slovak. 2384 questionnaires distributed (798 student, 1586 parent); response rates 64.0% / 66.1%. Final sample: 515 students (15-22 y, 37.9% male) from 8 randomly selected Bratislava secondary schools, plus 390 mothers and 291 fathers. Outcome was self-reported non-attendance at dental preventive care in the prior year. Statistics: two-sample t-test (age), chi-square / Fisher exact (categorical), and multiple logistic regression mutually adjusted for age, gender, family completeness, and parental dental preventive care, reporting adjusted odds ratios (AOR) with 95% CI; significance p < 0.05. Missing data were treated as random and excluded.

## Results

- **Sex**: males avoided preventive care more than females (54.4% vs 45.6%, p < 0.01); male gender remained significant in the multivariate model.
- **Age**: older students avoided more (18-19 y: 21.8%; 19+ y: 20.0%) in bivariate analysis, but age lost significance after adjustment.
- **Family / SES (bivariate)**: incomplete family 56.1%; home stress 17.5%; children of divorced mothers and mothers with household income < EUR 900 showed higher avoidance.
- **Cross-care pattern**: >34.5% attended neither dental nor pediatric preventive care; joint non-attendance AOR 5.14 (95% CI 2.40-10.99).
- **Parental behavior (multivariate independent predictors)**: maternal non-attendance AOR 4.19 (1.50-11.71); single/divorced/widowed father AOR 3.73 (1.21-11.54); paternal non-attendance AOR 3.41 (1.24-9.32). Fathers in the exposed group were significantly more likely to skip their own preventive care (47.8%, p < 0.05).

Bottom line: non-attendance is socially patterned and intergenerational — engaging the parent's own care behavior is a lever for improving adolescent recall attendance. As a cross-sectional, single-city, self-reported study with a small (n=57) non-attender group, it is hypothesis-generating rather than causal.
