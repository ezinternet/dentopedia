## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional survey (N=91 rural Latina/o/x adults, southwest Virginia) found 57% had no interest in teledentistry even if available; only higher household income (>$24k) and lacking dental insurance were significantly associated with acceptability — availability alone will not close access gaps without explicit health-equity integration.

## Summary

This is the first study to examine teledentistry acceptability specifically among Latina/o/x adults living in a rural U.S. area — a fast-growing, oral-health-underserved population. A COVID-era cross-sectional REDCap survey recruited 91 self-identified Latina/o/x adults in 14 rural southwest Virginia ZIP codes (May 2020–Feb 2021).

Contrary to prior literature reporting positive pre-experience attitudes toward telehealth among Latinos and rural residents, **57.1% reported no interest** in video/internet dental consultations even if available. In bivariate (chi-squared) analysis, only two factors were significantly associated with acceptability: **household income >$24,000 (p=.04)** and — counter-intuitively — **not having dental insurance (p=.01)**, with the uninsured being *more* receptive (interpreted as viewing teledentistry as a cheaper, more accessible care route). Self-perceived oral-health need was not associated with acceptability. Non-receptive participants were disproportionately Spanish speakers (61.5%) and foreign-born (63.5%), implicating acculturation and language as barriers.

The clinical/policy takeaway: broad teledentistry availability may leave acculturation- and language-barriered rural Latino subgroups behind and perpetuate access inequities unless health equity is explicitly built into design, marketing, and implementation (equity-centered, person-centered care; bilingual/bicultural workforce; community partnerships). Teledentistry alone will not solve dental-access inequity.

## Key Contributions

- First teledentistry-acceptability study in rural U.S. Latina/o/x adults (an understudied population, 9% of rural U.S. populations).
- Empirical patient-side counterweight to access/cost optimism in teledentistry reviews: majority non-interest despite availability.
- Identifies **cost-related enabling factors** (income, insurance) — not perceived need — as the correlates of acceptability, with uninsured patients more receptive.
- Frames teledentistry expansion as a potential **equity risk** requiring explicit health-equity integration.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Cross-sectional REDCap survey (80 items), May 2020–Feb 2021 (COVID-19 pandemic).
- **Population**: Self-identified Latina/o/x adults ≥18 in 14 rural southwest Virginia ZIP codes (Census-selected); English/Spanish option; $10 incentive.
- **Outcome**: Acceptability = yes/no interest in video/internet dental consultations after a telehealth definition was provided.
- **Covariates**: Sociodemographics (income <$24,999 vs ≥$25,000, education, age, sex), acculturation proxies (survey language, nativity, years in U.S.), oral-health variables (dental insurance, past-year utilization, self-rated oral health).
- **Analysis**: Descriptive statistics + chi-squared bivariate tests (SAS 9.4); 8 incomplete cases excluded → N=91. No multivariable modeling (sample-size limited). VCU IRB approved.

## Results

- **Sample (N=91)**: 70.3% female, 63.7% >30 y, 54.9% Spanish-speaking, 58.2% foreign-born; 57.2% ≤high-school education; 56% income <$24,000; **58.2% no dental insurance**; **67.3% no past-year dental visit**; 71.4% self-rated oral health good+.
- **57.1% reported no interest in teledentistry** if available.
- Interested respondents skewed toward women (66.7%), English speakers (53.8%), good+ self-rated oral health (76.9%).
- **Significant correlates of acceptability**: income >$24,000 (p=.04); **lacking dental insurance** (p=.01) — uninsured *more* receptive.
- Self-perceived oral health **not** correlated with acceptability.
- Non-receptive group disproportionately Spanish speakers (61.5%) and foreign-born (63.5%).
- **Limitations**: small convenience sample, descriptive-only (no prediction modeling), women over-represented (70.3%), single yes/no acceptability item with possible interpretation variance.
