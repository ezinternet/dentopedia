## Summary

Medical errors are among the leading causes of death in the US, and patients can be harmed during care, making a robust culture of safety imperative. This cross-sectional study evaluated the patient safety culture among providers at 4 US dental institutions and compared the findings with medical counterparts (2016). Using the Medical Office Survey on Patient Safety Culture (MOSPSC) modified for dentistry, the authors found that dentistry trails medicine on most safety-culture measures, though dental clinics show relative strength in organizational learning and teamwork. The authors conclude that safety culture has progressed over time but remains heterogeneous across academic dental, private (nonacademic), and medical clinics, and that administering this survey in dental clinics is a key measure for identifying improvement areas.

## Key Contributions

- Adapts the AHRQ Medical Office Survey on Patient Safety Culture (MOSPSC) for use in dental settings.
- Provides a direct dental-vs-medical benchmark, identifying where dentistry lags its medical counterparts.
- Flags organizational learning and teamwork as the relative strengths of surveyed dental clinics.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Cross-sectional survey.
- **Instrument**: MOSPSC, modified for dentistry.
- **Setting**: 4 US dental institutions, administered 2016; compared with medical-counterpart data (2016).
- **Sample**: All dental team members invited; **656 of 1,615 responded (40.6% response rate)**.

## Results

- Medical institutions outperformed dental institutions on **9 of 10** safety-culture dimensions.
- Medical outperformed dental on **6 of 6** overall quality items and **8 of 9** patient-safety/quality issues.
- Surveyed dental institutions' strongest average percentage-positive scores: **organizational learning 85%** and **teamwork 79%**.
- Conclusion: safety culture has progressed over time, but heterogeneity remains within academic dental, private (nonacademic), and medical clinics.
