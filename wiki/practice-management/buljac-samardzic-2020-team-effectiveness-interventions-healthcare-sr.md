## One-line Summary

SR (GRADE; 297 studies, 2008–Jul 2018, 7 databases) of healthcare team-effectiveness interventions organized into a 3+1 taxonomy — training (CRM/TeamSTEPPS/simulation), tools (SBAR/(de)briefing checklists/rounds/technology/monitoring), organizational redesign, and combined programmes; training is the most-studied and **principle-based + simulation-based training offer the greatest chance of improving (mostly non-technical) team functioning**, predominantly in acute hospital settings on low-to-moderate evidence.

## Summary

> **Framing note — general-healthcare evidence extrapolated to dentistry.** This is NOT a dental study. Buljac-Samardzic et al. reviewed team-effectiveness interventions across all of healthcare (operating theatres, ICUs, EDs, nursing homes). It is held in the dental wiki because a dental practice is itself a small healthcare team subject to the same teamwork-and-handoff vulnerabilities, and this is the broadest single catalogue of *how* team effectiveness is actually improved. Apply its taxonomy and evidence ranking to the dental clinic with the usual caveat that none of the 297 included studies was conducted in a dental setting.

This systematic review (Human Resources for Health, 2020) updates the authors' own 2008 review and screens **6025 articles** (7 databases, English, 2008–July 2018, PRISMA, three independent reviewers) down to **297 included studies**, each GRADE-rated for evidence level. It deliberately imposes **no restriction** on intervention type, healthcare setting, or research design — making it the broadest available map of healthcare team-improvement interventions.

The headline finding: of all approaches, **principle-based training (CRM and TeamSTEPPS) and simulation-based training provide the greatest opportunity** for improving team functioning, with **(de)briefing checklists** the best-supported tool. Most interventions target **non-technical skills** (communication, situational awareness, leadership, decision-making, task management), are concentrated in **acute hospital settings**, and rest on **low-to-moderate evidence** overall.

## Key Contributions

- A unified 3+1 taxonomy of healthcare team-effectiveness interventions spanning a decade and all settings/designs.
- A GRADE-based ranking of which interventions are best-supported by evidence.
- Identification of four research gaps: non-hospital settings, long-term/sustainability effects, contextual reporting, and under-used outcomes (notably professional well-being).

## Methodology

- **Design**: systematic review with GRADE evidence rating (no meta-analysis).
- **Search**: 7 databases, four-area keyword strategy (team × healthcare × intervention × outcome), 2008–July 2018, English peer-reviewed.
- **Selection**: 6025 screened → 639 after title/abstract → **297 included** (two reviewers + third tiebreaker; PRISMA).
- **Categorization**: developed over three iterations (50 → 50 → 197 articles), peer-reviewed.

## Results

**The 3+1 intervention taxonomy:**

| Category | Subcategories | Best-supported example | Evidence note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Training** | principle-based (**CRM**, **TeamSTEPPS**); method-based (**simulation-based training**); general team training | CRM + TeamSTEPPS + simulation | Most-studied category; simulation most-researched (69 studies, 16 moderate-to-high); TeamSTEPPS ~half moderate-to-high |
| **2. Tools** | structuring (**SBAR**, **(de)briefing checklists**, rounds); facilitating (communication technology); triggering (monitoring/feedback, dashboards) | (de)briefing checklists (~1/3 high/moderate) | SBAR, rounds, triggering tools mostly (very) low evidence |
| **3. Organizational (re)design** | restructuring pathways, schedules, roles/responsibilities, dedicated teams | — | Mostly (very) low; only 4 studies moderate |
| **4. Programme** | combination of training + tools + redesign (e.g. CUSP, medical team training/MTT) | CUSP, MTT | Diverse, mostly positive; evidence high to very low |

- **Most-studied**: Training; simulation-based training the single most-researched training type.
- **Setting**: predominantly **acute hospital** (ED, OR, ICU, surgical unit); primary care / nursing homes / long-term care under-represented.
- **Outcomes**: mostly **non-technical skills**, mostly subjective measures (SAQ, NOTECHS).
- **Bottom line**: **principle-based training (CRM, TeamSTEPPS) and simulation-based training offer the greatest opportunity** for improving team functioning; (de)briefing checklists also well-supported; combined programmes promising.
- **Caveat (Whyte)**: briefings can paradoxically mask knowledge gaps, disrupt positive communication, reinforce professional divisions, create tension, and perpetuate a problematic culture — so a tool is not automatically beneficial.

**Dental extrapolation.** SBAR — held in this wiki as a dedicated handoff-communication topic — is just *one* structuring tool inside the larger "Tools" branch here; this review situates it within the full menu (training generally outranks isolated tools on evidence). For a dental practice, the transferable signal is that **structured team training (CRM/TeamSTEPPS-style) and simulation** are the highest-yield investments, while standalone communication tools (SBAR, checklists) help but rest on weaker evidence.
