## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 31 acute-care studies (1390 teams): teamwork process correlates with clinical performance at r=0.28 (≈OR 2.8, medium effect), robust across team composition, size, task type, and patient realism (moderators non-significant).

## Summary

**This is general-healthcare (acute-care) evidence, not dental-specific.** The included studies come from surgery, anaesthesia, emergency/resuscitation, obstetrics, and neonatal/intensive care — short, high-task-interdependence episodes. It is filed in `practice-management` and extrapolated to the dental practice team by analogy, to anchor the dental workforce / team-delegation / communication axis with a quantitative effect size the dental literature itself does not supply. The extrapolation caveat is real: the authors explicitly note their results may not generalise to long-term care or mental health, and dental outpatient care differs structurally from acute-care teams. Treat the magnitude as a directional anchor, not a transplanted dental estimate.

Schmutz, Meier & Manser conducted the first **quantitative meta-analysis** of the teamwork→clinical-performance relationship (prior reviews were qualitative or examined *team training* rather than teamwork itself). Across **31 studies / 1390 teams**, the sample-size weighted mean correlation between a teamwork process (coordination, communication, leadership, non-technical skills) and clinical performance was **r = 0.28 (95% CI 0.20–0.35)** — a medium-sized effect the authors translate into an **OR of 2.8** (teams engaging in teamwork are ~2.8× more likely to achieve high performance).

The headline is the **null moderator test**: an omnibus mixed-effects test of six pre-specified moderators (professional composition, team familiarity, team size, task type, patient realism, performance-measure type) was **not significant** (QM=0.18, p>0.20). Teamwork's payoff held regardless of whether teams were interprofessional or uniprofessional, familiar or ad hoc, large or small, handling routine or non-routine tasks, or observed with real vs simulated patients. The practical corollary: teamwork matters in **routine** care, not only crises — so team training should not be confined to crisis-resource-management (emergency) scenarios. For the dental analogy, this is the load-bearing claim — it argues teamwork's value is not an artifact of a particular team configuration.

## Key Contributions

- First meta-analytical quantification of teamwork↔clinical-performance in healthcare: **r=0.28, OR≈2.8** (medium effect).
- Effect is **context-robust** — none of six moderators significantly altered the average effect.
- Reframes team training to include routine, not just crisis, situations.
- Demonstrates **simulation generalises to real settings** (real-patient r=0.28 = simulated r=0.28).

## Methodology

- PRISMA SR + random-effects meta-analysis (REML); PubMed (no date limit, last search Jun 2018) + manual backward/forward searches + author-solicited unpublished work.
- 2002 records → 31 studies in the meta-analysis (one outlier, Carlson 2009, excluded by studentized deleted residuals).
- Inclusion: ≥1 teamwork *process* linked to a *performance* measure in an **acute care** setting, team-level effect sizes only; long-term care excluded.
- Heterogeneous effect metrics converted to correlation r; within-sample correlations averaged via Fisher's z; sample-size weighted (not reliability-corrected).
- No publication bias (random-effects Egger z=1.79, p=0.074).

## Results

- **Overall**: N=1390, k=31, **r=0.28** (95% CI 0.20–0.35); heterogeneous (Q=53.7, I²=46%).
- **Moderator omnibus NOT significant** (QM=0.18, df=6, p>0.20). Subgroups all r≈0.25–0.30:
  - Interprofessional 0.28 (k=27) ≈ uniprofessional 0.28 (k=4)
  - Real teams 0.29 (k=21) ≈ experimental 0.25 (k=10)
  - Routine 0.27 (k=14) ≈ non-routine 0.29 (k=16)
  - Real patient 0.28 (k=16) = simulated 0.28 (k=15)
- One gradient of note: **process performance r=0.30 (k=27) vs outcome performance r=0.13 (k=4)** — weaker but still significant for hard patient outcomes (mortality, complications, bloodstream infection), with the caveat of only 4 outcome studies.
