## One-line Summary

Difference-in-differences study on MEPS 2001–2014 (DHPPI-coded state regulations): granting dental hygienists *moderate* practice autonomy raises total dental visits through more *preventive* care, while the *highest* autonomy level reduces *treatment* use — both effects larger in provider-shortage areas.

## 한줄요약

MEPS 2001–2014 자료를 이용한 이중차분(difference-in-differences) 연구. 치과위생사(dental hygienist)에게 *중간 수준*의 진료 자율성을 부여하면 예방치료(preventive care) 증가로 전체 치과 방문이 늘지만, *최고 수준* 자율성에서는 치료(treatment) 이용이 줄며, 두 효과 모두 치과의료 취약지(shortage area)에서 더 크게 나타난다.

## Summary

This study sits on the **delegation / skill-mix economics axis** of practice management: it asks what happens to the *volume* and *mix* of dental care when the law expands what dental hygienists may do without dentist supervision. Loosening supervision rules is a workforce-policy lever — the same lever that scope-of-practice rulings adjudicate — and this paper supplies causal evidence on its downstream effect on patient utilization.

Using the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS) for 2001–2014 and an extended Dental Hygiene Professional Practice Index (DHPPI) that captures within-state regulatory change over time, the authors apply a difference-in-differences (DiD) design. The central finding is **non-monotonic**: a *moderate* increase in hygienist autonomy raises total dental visits by increasing preventive care, but the *highest* autonomy tier is associated with a *decrease* in dental treatment utilization. Both effects are stronger in dental-care provider shortage areas, where visits shift toward hygienists and hygienist-performed tasks (cleanings, exams) increase.

## Key Contributions

- Provides causal (quasi-experimental DiD) evidence linking dental hygienist scope-of-practice regulation to dental care utilization.
- Extends the DHPPI to 2001–2014 so that *within-state* regulatory changes — not just cross-state differences — identify the effect.
- Decomposes the effect by care type (preventive vs. treatment) and by autonomy level (moderate vs. highest), revealing a non-monotonic pattern that a single "more autonomy = more care" framing would miss.
- Documents access-context heterogeneity: effects amplify in provider-shortage areas, where care reallocates toward hygienists.

## Methodology

- **Data**: MEPS, 2001–2014 (nationally representative U.S. survey of health care use and expenditure).
- **Exposure**: DHPPI, extended by the authors to score the strength of state DH practice-autonomy regulation across 2001–2014, capturing within-state changes over time.
- **Design**: Difference-in-differences on selected states (pre/post regulatory relaxation vs. unchanged states).
- **Outcomes**: total dental visits; preventive vs. treatment visits; hygienist-performed tasks; subgroup analysis by provider-shortage area.

## Results

| Regulatory change | Effect on utilization |
|---|---|
| Moderate hygienist autonomy (relaxed supervision) | ↑ total dental visits, driven by ↑ **preventive** care |
| Highest autonomy level | ↓ dental **treatment** use |
| Provider-shortage areas | Both effects larger; visits shift to hygienists; ↑ hygienist tasks (cleanings, exams) |

- The shift toward preventive care under moderate autonomy is the headline access gain.
- The reduction in treatment use at the highest autonomy tier is reported as a counter-effect; the abstract does not explain the mechanism (possible substitution away from dentist-delivered treatment, referral friction, or measurement).
- Point estimates and significance are not available in this abstract-only record.
