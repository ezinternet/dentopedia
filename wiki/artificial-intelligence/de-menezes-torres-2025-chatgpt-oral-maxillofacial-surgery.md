## Summary

According to PubMed, de Menezes Torres et al. (2025, Br J Oral Maxillofac Surg) systematically reviewed (PRISMA; PROSPERO CRD42024625882) the large language model (LLM) ChatGPT in oral and maxillofacial surgery across 10 studies. **GPT-4 achieved the highest MCQ accuracy (76.8%)** but was variable, weaker in pharmacology and complex clinical scenarios. ChatGPT **excelled at generating informed-consent documents** — outperforming other AI models and human residents on accuracy, completeness, and readability — and gave accurate, supportive postoperative follow-up responses. It was limited for personalised advice in cases like oral cancer and orthognathic surgery. The authors conclude ChatGPT can **complement but not replace** human judgment, requiring supervision and integration with clinical databases. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bjoms.2025.03.006)

This is the LLM (text-reasoning) counterpoint to the image-diagnosis CNN papers: unlike 2D cephalometric landmarking — which already clears its clinical bar — LLMs are usable for communication/documentation drafting but not for autonomous complex decisions, mirroring the staged-adoption framing of the AI overview.

## Key Contributions

- Dentistry-specific (OMFS) LLM evidence vs general-healthcare ChatGPT umbrellas.
- Maps where ChatGPT helps (consent, patient education, follow-up) vs fails (pharmacology, complex decisions).
- Reinforces the human-supervision-required boundary.

## Methodology

- PRISMA-guided SR; 4 databases; 10 studies; no meta-analysis (heterogeneity).
- Domains: clinical decision-making, surgical planning, patient education, research.
