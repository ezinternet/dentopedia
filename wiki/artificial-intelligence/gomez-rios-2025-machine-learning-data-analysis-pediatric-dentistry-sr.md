## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA, QUADAS-2; 20 of 1945 screened articles) showing machine learning in paediatric dentistry is dominated by caries prediction (14/20 studies; socio-demographic/behavioural/biological predictors), with ML cost analyses favouring sealants + fluoride and authors calling for ML-specific methodological guidelines and quality scales.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.23804/ejpd.2025.2288](https://doi.org/10.23804/ejpd.2025.2288)), this systematic review assessed whether applying machine learning (ML) to database analysis improves how oral diseases are approached in the paediatric population. Searching PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and Lilacs, the authors screened 1945 records and included 20 articles, appraised for quality with the QUADAS-2 scale under a PRISMA-conformant protocol. The field is heavily skewed toward caries prediction (14 of 20 studies), which leans on socio-demographic, behavioural, and biological predictors; remaining studies addressed oral-health-related quality of life in adolescents, predictors of the need for a second treatment under deep sedation, and the effectiveness of preventive dental services. ML-driven health-economic analysis found that children with early caries lesions incur higher insurer costs, while those receiving sealants and fluoride show greater cost savings. The authors conclude ML can surface patterns in large datasets but that ML-specific methodological guidelines and quality scales are still needed.

**This wiki record is abstract-only — full text was not retrieved.** Numbers and claims are limited to the PubMed abstract; no per-model algorithm types, sample sizes, or pooled performance metrics (AUC, sensitivity/specificity) were available.

Clinically: in paediatric practice, ML's near-term, evidence-backed value is in caries risk stratification (driven by socio-demographic, behavioural, and biological data) and in reinforcing the cost case for preventive sealants + fluoride — but ML outputs should be read cautiously until task-specific reporting standards and quality scales mature.

- Design: systematic review, PRISMA-conformant.
- Databases searched: PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, Lilacs.
- Quality appraisal: QUADAS-2.
- Screening: 1945 records screened → 20 included.
- Topics spanned: caries prediction; adolescent OHRQoL; predictors of second treatment under deep sedation; preventive-service effectiveness.
- (Abstract-only; no quantitative pooling or per-study model detail available in the retrieved text.)

## Results

- 1945 records screened; 20 articles met eligibility and were analysed.
- 14 of 20 articles focused on caries prediction.
- Caries-prediction predictors clustered as socio-demographic, behavioural, and biological.
- ML cost analysis: children with early caries lesions incur higher insurer costs.
- ML cost analysis: children receiving sealants and fluoride showed greater cost savings.
- Background figure cited: dental caries affects 514 million children worldwide.
- Conclusion: ML identifies patterns in large datasets and is recommended for integration into research/education; ML-specific methodological guidelines and quality scales are needed.
