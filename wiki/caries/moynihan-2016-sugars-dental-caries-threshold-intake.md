## One-line Summary
Narrative review of 54 studies (8 cohort studies being best evidence) showing moderate-quality dose-response evidence that limiting free sugars to ≤10% of energy intake reduces dental caries — and low-quality ecologic evidence for additional benefit below 5% energy — underpinning the 2015 WHO free-sugars guideline.

## Summary
Moynihan synthesizes the evidence commissioned for the 2015 WHO free-sugars guideline update, reviewing 65 papers from 54 studies across all ages and countries. The strongest evidence (8 cohort studies, GRADE moderate) consistently shows lower caries with free sugars ≤10% of energy: e.g., UK adolescents consuming >10%E developed 0.9 more decayed surfaces per year (Rugg-Gunn 1984); Brazilian 3-year-olds at 16%E were ~3× more likely to develop high caries than those at ≤10%E (Rodrigues 1999). Crucially, ≤10%E does not eliminate caries. Ecologic data (GRADE low) suggest further caries reduction when intake falls below 5%E, supporting the WHO conditional recommendation. The paper frames caries as a lifelong cumulative disease: <3 DMFS at age 12 can grow to 15 DMFS by age 32 (Dunedin cohort), making early dietary control imperative.

## Key Contributions
- WHO free-sugars classification: "free sugars" = all mono-/disaccharides added to foods + sugars in honey/syrups/fruit juices; intrinsic sugars in whole fruits, vegetables, milk do not importantly contribute to caries.
- 42/50 child studies and 5/5 adult studies showed ≥1 positive association between sugars amount and caries.
- Establishes dose-response benchmarks from cohort studies.
- Clarifies that ≤10%E reduces but does not eliminate caries — a key caveat for clinical communication.
- Frames caries as a progressive, cumulative lifelong disease demanding early-life intervention.

## Methodology
Narrative review reporting the supporting evidence for the 2015 WHO guideline; underlying formal SR by Moynihan & Kelly (Caries Res 2014). Evidence base: 65 papers from 54 studies; n = 50 child studies, n = 5 adult studies; no RCTs found; best design: 8 cohort studies; GRADE applied for quality assessment. No date or language restriction; all countries.

## Results
- Cohort studies (GRADE: moderate): 7/8 showed lower caries with lower sugars; all 5 cohort studies with ≤10%E vs. >10%E comparison showed lower caries at ≤10%E.
- Rugg-Gunn 1984 (UK, ages 12–14): >10%E group → 0.9 more decayed surfaces/year.
- Rodrigues 1999 (Brazil, age 3): 16%E → ~3× risk of high caries vs. ≤10%E.
- Stecksen-Blicks 1986 (Sweden, age 13): low-caries group ~9.8%E; high-caries group 14.5%E.
- Burt 1988 (US, ages 10–15): each 5 g sugars → 1% increase in probability of developing caries.
- Ecologic data (GRADE: low): lower caries when free sugars <5%E vs. 5–10%E.
- ≤10%E does not eliminate caries (residual disease documented across studies).
