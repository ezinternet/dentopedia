## One-line Summary

Systematic review of RCTs (2 eligible trials, no quantitative pooling) finding that silver diamine fluoride (SDF) may yield favourable short-term (≤12 mo) caries-arrest and treatment-failure outcomes for cavitated dentinal lesions in primary molars versus conventional restorative treatment — but randomised evidence is limited.

## Summary

This PRISMA 2020-compliant systematic review by Garcia-Miralles et al. set out to synthesise and critically appraise RCTs that **directly compare silver diamine fluoride (SDF) with conventional restorative treatments** for **cavitated dentinal caries in primary molars** — a deliberately narrower PICO than the broader SDF literature, which often mixes non-cavitated lesions and preventive comparators. Searches of MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, and Scopus up to October 2025 identified **2 eligible RCTs** out of 433 records: Zhi et al. 2012 (China) and the CariedAway cluster-RCT (USA, 2025). Heterogeneity in outcome definitions and study designs precluded quantitative pooling; a qualitative synthesis with GRADE assessment was performed instead.

**Primary outcome — caries arrest (≤12 months):** Zhi et al. found 38% SDF significantly superior to high-fluoride glass ionomer cement (GIC) — RR 1.57 (95% CI 1.13–2.17; p < 0.05) at the lesion level; annual and semi-annual SDF schedules showed no clinically relevant difference. GRADE certainty: **low** (⬤⬤◯◯).

**Secondary outcome — treatment failure/retreatment (≤12 months):** CariedAway (n = 1,668 participants) showed SDF reduced treatment failure versus atraumatic restorative treatment (ART) — 45.5% vs 53.3%, RR 0.85 (95% CI 0.78–0.94). GRADE certainty: **moderate** (⬤⬤⬤◯).

Both trials were rated low risk to some concerns on RoB 2. SDF consistently favoured over comparators across both outcomes.

**Clinical positioning:** SDF is a viable **minimally invasive, no-drill, low-cost** option for cavitated dentinal caries in primary molars, especially for young/uncooperative children and community/school-based programmes. Black staining of arrested lesions is the key drawback; parental acceptance is generally favourable with clear communication. Evidence is thin (2 RCTs, ≤12 mo) — further well-designed long-term RCTs are needed before SDF can be positioned as equivalent to conventional restoration in all clinical contexts.

## Key Contributions

- Narrows the SDF question to the most decision-relevant comparison: **SDF vs conventional restoration**, RCTs only, **cavitated dentinal lesions in primary molars** — filling a gap left by reviews that pool non-cavitated and cavitated lesions together.
- Provides specific RR estimates from each trial: caries arrest RR 1.57 (GIC comparator; Zhi 2012) and treatment failure RR 0.85 (ART comparator; CariedAway).
- Applies GRADE to quantify certainty: low for caries arrest, moderate for retreatment reduction.
- Honestly documents the evidence gap — only 2 trials, no pooling feasible, short follow-up — positioning SDF as promising but not yet definitively equivalent to conventional restoration.
- Clinical significance statement: SDF's simplicity, low cost, and lack of requirement for anaesthesia or rotary instrumentation make it particularly relevant for uncooperative children and public health programmes.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review of RCTs; PRISMA 2020 compliant; PROSPERO pre-registered; qualitative synthesis (meta-analysis not feasible).
- **Databases**: MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Scopus — search to October 2025; no language/date restrictions; manual reference-list screening.
- **PICO**: Population — children ≤12 yr with cavitated dentinal lesions in primary molars; Intervention — SDF (any concentration/frequency); Comparator — ART, GIC, composite, stainless steel crowns, Hall technique; Primary outcome — caries arrest ≤12 mo; Secondary — treatment failure/retreatment ≤12 mo; Design — RCTs only.
- **Screening**: 433 records → 391 duplicates removed → 42 title/abstract screened → 48 full-text assessed → **2 RCTs included**.
- **Data extraction**: piloted form; two independent reviewers; lesion-level and participant-level data extracted separately; RR with 95% CI as effect measure.
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane RoB 2 instrument; five domains; two independent reviewers.
- **Analysis**: R 4.3.1; random-effects HKSJ method planned; cluster-RCT adjusted by ICC design effects; GRADE evidence certainty applied.
- **Pooling**: not performed — heterogeneity in outcome definitions (lesion-level vs participant-level) and designs (individual RCT vs cluster-RCT).

## Results

| Outcome | Trial | N | Effect (RR, 95% CI) | GRADE certainty |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Caries arrest (≤12 mo) | Zhi et al. 2012 | 649 lesions | **1.57 (1.13–2.17)** favouring SDF vs GIC | Low ⬤⬤◯◯ |
| Treatment failure / retreatment | CariedAway 2025 | 1,668 participants | **0.85 (0.78–0.94)** favouring SDF vs ART | Moderate ⬤⬤⬤◯ |

- **RoB 2 (Zhi et al.)**: low risk overall (some concern: outcome measurement domain).
- **RoB 2 (CariedAway)**: some concerns overall (cluster design, baseline imbalances, outcome measurement).
- **Annual vs semi-annual SDF (Zhi et al.)**: no clinically relevant difference in caries arrest — both schedules effective.
- **Treatment failure rates (CariedAway)**: 45.5% (SDF) vs 53.3% (ART) — absolute risk difference ~7.8 percentage points in favour of SDF.
- **No high-risk-of-bias studies** included.
- **Consistent direction**: both trials favour SDF across their respective primary outcomes.
- **Limitations of body of evidence**: 2 RCTs only; no pooling; ≤12 mo follow-up; single cultural/healthcare context per trial (China; USA school-based).
