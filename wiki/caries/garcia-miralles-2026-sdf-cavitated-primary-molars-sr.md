## One-line Summary

Systematic review of RCTs (2 eligible trials, no quantitative pooling) finding that silver diamine fluoride (SDF) may yield favourable short-term (≤12 mo) caries-arrest and treatment-failure outcomes for cavitated dentinal lesions in primary molars versus conventional restorative treatment — but randomised evidence is limited.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106703](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106703)), this systematic review set out to synthesise and critically appraise RCTs that **directly compare silver diamine fluoride (SDF) with conventional restorative treatments** for managing **cavitated dentinal caries in primary molars**. Searches across MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, and Scopus up to October 2025 (plus reference-list screening) yielded only **2 eligible RCTs**. Because of heterogeneity in outcome definitions and study design, **quantitative pooling was not feasible** — the synthesis is qualitative. The limited randomised evidence **suggests SDF may provide favourable short-term (≤12 month) outcomes** for caries arrest and treatment failure/retreatment, with effect estimates planned as risk ratios with 95% CI. (Note: this page is built from the **abstract only — full text not retrieved**; per-trial sample sizes and point estimates are not available.)

**Clinically:** for a cavitated dentinal lesion in a primary molar — especially in a young, hard-to-treat child or a community/school setting — SDF is a defensible **minimally invasive, simple, low-cost** alternative to conventional restoration, with short-term outcomes that appear at least comparable. Manage expectations on the black staining and recognise the evidence is thin (2 trials, ≤12 mo); reserve conventional restoration where esthetics, pulpal involvement, or durable function demand it, and re-evaluate beyond 12 months.

## Key Contributions

- Narrows the SDF question to the most decision-relevant comparison: **SDF vs conventional restoration**, RCTs only, in **cavitated dentinal lesions of primary molars**.
- Pre-specified clinical outcomes — **caries arrest** and **treatment failure / need for retreatment at ≤12 months** — with **risk ratios (95% CI)** as the planned effect measure.
- Transparently documents the evidence gap: **only 2 RCTs**, **no pooling possible**, short follow-up — positioning SDF as a promising minimally invasive option, not a proven equivalent.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review of RCTs (qualitative synthesis; meta-analysis not feasible).
- **Databases**: MEDLINE (PubMed), Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Scopus, up to **October 2025**, plus manual reference-list screening.
- **PICO**: Population — children with cavitated dentinal lesions in primary molars; Intervention — SDF; Comparator — conventional restorative treatments; Design — RCTs only.
- **Outcomes**: caries arrest; treatment failure or need for retreatment at ≤12 months; effect estimates as risk ratios with 95% CI.
- **Result of screening**: 2 trials met eligibility; quantitative pooling not feasible due to heterogeneity.

## Results

- **Eligible RCTs**: only **2** met inclusion criteria.
- **Meta-analysis**: **not performed** — heterogeneity in outcome definitions and study design precluded quantitative pooling.
- **Effect direction**: limited randomised evidence **suggests SDF may give favourable short-term (≤12 mo) outcomes** vs conventional restorative treatment for cavitated dentinal caries in primary molars.
- **Effect measure**: risk ratios with 95% CI (specific point estimates not reported in the retrieved abstract).
- **Practical positioning**: SDF framed as a **minimally invasive, simple, low-cost** option, **particularly relevant for young children and community-based settings**.
- **Authors' caveat**: further well-designed RCTs with **longer follow-up** are needed.
