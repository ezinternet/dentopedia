## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 23 cost-effectiveness studies (836 screened): pooled incremental net monetary benefit (INMB) of fluoride varnish vs other caries-preventive interventions in children was $124.1 (I²=0%), showing NO significant economic difference — cost-effectiveness of FV remains uncertain in high-income countries from the healthcare provider/payer perspective.

## Summary

This is a **health-economic** systematic review and meta-analysis — it pools the **cost-effectiveness**, not the clinical efficacy, of professional fluoride varnish (FV) for preventing dental caries in children. The clinical caries-preventive effect of FV is established elsewhere; this paper asks a different question: **is FV economically worth it relative to other preventive options?**

From 836 screened studies, **23 economic evaluations** were included. FV was compared against oral health education (OHE, n=5), treatment as usual (TAU, n=3), pit-and-fissure sealant (n=5), toothbrushing (n=5), and no intervention (n=7). Pooling was restricted to **model-based FV studies in high-income countries (HICs)**, from a **healthcare provider/payer perspective**, vs OHE/TAU/no intervention.

The pooled total **incremental net monetary benefit (INMB) was $124.1** with a confidence interval crossing zero and **I²=0%**, i.e. **no statistically significant difference in cost-effectiveness** between FV and the other caries-preventive interventions. The authors conclude the cost-effectiveness of FV is **uncertain** in HICs from the provider/payer perspective, while noting potential economic value especially in **settings that lack robust local cost-effectiveness data**.

**Clinical reading:** this does NOT say FV fails to prevent caries — it says the available economic evidence cannot show FV is more (or less) cost-effective than alternatives in high-income settings. Sparse, imprecise economic data (wide CI despite I²=0%) is the limiting factor.

## Key Contributions

- First SR+MA to synthesize the **economic value** (INMB) of childhood-caries fluoride varnish rather than its clinical efficacy.
- Pooled total INMB = **$124.1**, I²=0%, CI crossing zero → no significant cost-effectiveness difference vs comparators.
- Comparator map: OHE, TAU, pit-and-fissure sealant, toothbrushing, no intervention.
- Registered PROSPERO CRD42023483985; risk of bias by modified Economic Evaluations Bias (ECOBIAS) checklist; two reviewers; no funding.

## Methodology

- **Screening:** 836 studies → **23 included** economic evaluations.
- **Comparators:** OHE (n=5), TAU (n=3), pit-and-fissure sealant (n=5), toothbrushing (n=5), no intervention (n=7).
- **Pooling restriction:** only **model-based FV studies, in HICs, healthcare provider/payer perspective, vs OHE/TAU/no intervention** were eligible for meta-analysis.
- **Databases:** PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, Cochrane — to January 2024.
- **Outcome metric:** total incremental net monetary benefit (INMB).
- Source artifact: abstract-only (no PMC open access); body limited to abstract-level detail.

## Results

- Overall pooled FV vs other interventions: **total INMB $124.1** (95% CI crosses zero; reported as −1.3×10 to 1.3×10), **I²=0%** → **no significant cost-effectiveness difference**.
- Cost-effectiveness of FV is **uncertain** in HICs from the provider/payer perspective.
- Potential economic value of FV is highlighted especially in **settings lacking robust cost-effectiveness data**.

> **Scope note:** This page concerns **economic cost-effectiveness**, not clinical caries-prevention efficacy. FV's clinical efficacy is documented in other wiki pages and is not contradicted here.
