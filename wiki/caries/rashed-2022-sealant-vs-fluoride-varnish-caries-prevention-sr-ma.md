## One-line Summary

SR+MA (4 RCTs, 1249 children, all 24-month follow-up): resin pit-and-fissure sealant vs fluoride varnish shows **no significant difference** in caries prevention of first permanent molars (caries-increment RR 0.65, 95% CI 0.31-1.35, p=0.26, I²=89%; DMFS MD 0.09, 95% CI -0.03 to 0.21) → FV favored as cheaper and easier.

## Summary

This systematic review + meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42022146807) compares **resin-based pit-and-fissure sealant (PFS)** against **fluoride varnish (FV)** for preventing caries in the **first permanent molars (FPM)** of schoolchildren aged 6-12. Four RCTs (published 1996-2014, **1249 subjects** aged 6-8) met strict criteria: RCTs only, **≥24-month follow-up**, resin sealant only (GIC/glass-ionomer sealant comparisons excluded for low retention), and **split-mouth excluded** because FV carryover contaminates the contralateral half. Pooled results show **no statistically significant difference** between PFS and FV on either caries incidence or DMFS increment. Because FV is cheaper, faster, and easier to apply in a school setting (PFS requires a steady child plus proper isolation), the authors emphasize FV.

## Key Contributions

- Head-to-head **PFS vs FV** SR+MA restricted to resin sealants and a uniform 24-month follow-up — explicitly built to resolve conflict among earlier MAs that mixed follow-up times, included GIC-vs-FV, or pooled 5-yr with 2-yr trials without subgroups.
- Quantifies equivalence at 2 years: no significant difference in caries increment or DMFS, despite very high heterogeneity.
- Translates the null finding into a pragmatic recommendation favoring FV on cost and ease-of-application.

## Methodology

- **PICO**: schoolchildren 6-12 yr with sound occlusal FPM; PFS (resin-based) vs FV; outcomes = caries incidence on FPM surfaces and DMFS increment.
- **Databases**: Embase, Google Scholar, Cochrane CENTRAL, MEDLINE via Ovid (Jan 1980-May 2022).
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane Collaboration tool (7 domains).
- **Statistics**: RevMan 5.4; RR + 95% CI for dichotomous, mean difference (MD) + 95% CI for continuous; heterogeneity I²; **random-effects model** when I² > 50%.
- Split-mouth, quasi-randomized, non-randomized, and observational designs excluded.

## Results

- **Screening**: 231 records → 140 duplicates removed → 64 excluded on title/abstract → 27 eligibility-assessed → **4 RCTs included**.
- **Primary (caries incidence, FPM)**: 3 trials, **2,622 FPMs**, all 24-month follow-up. **RR 0.65; 95% CI 0.31-1.35; p=0.26; I²=89%** — not significant, very high heterogeneity. (Earliest trial favored PFS; the more recent trial showed no difference.)
- **Secondary (DMFS increment)**: 2 studies. Bravo et al. favored PFS (MD -0.64; 95% CI -1.07 to -0.21); Salem reported higher occlusal caries increment with PFS. Pooled (dominated by Bravo's large n): **MD 0.09; 95% CI -0.03 to 0.21** — not significant; heterogeneity Chi²=11.69, **I²=91%**.
- **Risk of bias**: selective reporting low across all; half high-risk / half unclear for blinding of participants and personnel; randomization method undescribed in Bravo and Salem.
- **Limitations**: very high heterogeneity, uncontrolled confounders (fluoride exposure, SES, parental education), only 4 studies all at 2-year follow-up → long-term efficacy unestablished.
