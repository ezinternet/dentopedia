## One-line Summary

Cochrane SR + network meta-analysis (27 RCTs, 3350 participants, 4195 teeth, search to 2020): across carious-tissue-removal strategies, conventional complete removal (CR) carried the highest probability of failure for deep lesions vs selective (SE), stepwise (SW), and Hall technique (HT); in primary teeth HT outperformed CR (CR-vs-HT failure OR 8.35) and NRCC, while SE ranked best for permanent deep lesions — most certainty low/very-low.

## Summary

This Cochrane review answers a single clinical question that crosses the seal-vs-restore divide: when a lesion is cavitated or extends into dentine, how much carious tissue should you actually remove? It compares six strategies — conventional restoration (CR, complete/non-selective removal), selective excavation (SE), stepwise removal (SW), sealing with sealant materials, the Hall Technique (HT, preformed metal crown sealing without removal), and non-restorative cavity control (NRCC) — using a composite **failure** outcome (pulp exposure, endodontic therapy, extraction, restorative complications). For the deep-lesion subgroup it adds a **network meta-analysis** that ranks the strategies.

The consistent direction: removing **less** carious tissue does at least as well as, and often better than, complete removal. For deep lesions the NMA put CR at the highest failure probability versus SE, SW and HT. In primary teeth, sealing the lesion under a Hall crown beat both CR and NRCC. The headline caveat is certainty — all 27 trials were at high risk of bias, samples were small, and failure events were few, so only the permanent-tooth CR-vs-SW and SW-vs-SE comparisons reached moderate GRADE certainty; nearly everything else is low or very low.

## Key Contributions

- Highest-tier (Cochrane) synthesis ranking carious-tissue-removal strategies, including the **first Cochrane NMA** for deep-lesion management.
- Establishes the minimal-intervention principle for *managing* (not just preventing) cavitated dentine lesions: SE and SW for permanent deep lesions, HT and SE for primary teeth.
- Quantifies the seal-vs-restore evidence gap: sealant-material comparisons are all single/double-study and very imprecise.

## Methodology

- RCTs only; vital pulps; lesions conventionally judged to need a restoration (cavitated/micro-cavitated, or non-cavitated occlusal extending radiographically into dentine). Primary + permanent teeth.
- Outcome = composite **failure**; ORs with 95% CI; pairwise meta-analysis, plus **NMA for failure in the deep-lesion subgroup** (CR/SE/SW/HT).
- 27 RCTs, 3350 participants, 4195 teeth, 11 countries, 1977–2020; 24 parallel-group, 3 split-mouth. All at **high risk of bias**; GRADE per comparison.
- 17 trials compared CR vs a less-invasive arm: SE (8), SW (4), HT (2), sealant (4), NRCC (1). Follow-up mostly 12–24 months (range: none to 120 months).

## Results

**Deep lesions (NMA + pairwise):** failure probability **greatest for CR** vs SE, SW, HT.
- CR vs SW (permanent): OR **2.06** (1.34–3.17), 3 studies/398 teeth — **moderate certainty**, CR worse.
- SW vs SE (permanent): OR **2.25** (1.33–3.82), 3 studies/371 teeth — **moderate certainty**, SW worse than SE → **SE ranks best**.
- CR vs SE (permanent): OR **11.32** (1.97–65.02), 2 studies/179 teeth — very low, CR worse.
- CR vs SE (primary): OR 4.43 (1.04–18.77), 4 studies/265 teeth — very low, CR worse.
- CR vs SW (primary) OR 2.43 (0.65–9.12) and SW vs SE (primary) OR 2.05 (0.49–8.62) — both very low, inconclusive.

**Cavitated-not-deep, primary teeth:**
- CR vs HT: OR **8.35** (3.73–18.68), 2 studies/249 teeth — **low certainty**, CR fails more (HT better).
- HT vs NRCC: OR 0.19 (0.05–0.74), 84 teeth — very low, HT better than NRCC.
- SE vs HT OR 8.94 (0.57–139.67) and CR vs NRCC OR 1.16 (0.50–2.71) — very low, insufficient.

**Sealing with sealant materials (non-cavitated / cavitated-not-deep)** — all very-low, insufficient:
- Sealing vs CR: OR 5.00 (0.51–49.27), 41 permanent teeth.
- Sealing vs SE: OR 3.11 (0.11–85.52), 82 primary teeth.
- Sealing vs no treatment: OR 0.05 (0.00–2.71), 103 permanent teeth.

**Clinical bottom line:** vs CR, fewer failures with **HT and SE in primary teeth** and **SE and SW in permanent teeth**; SE is the best-ranked excavation strategy for permanent deep lesions. Confidence is limited by high risk of bias and imprecision.
