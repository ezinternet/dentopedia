## One-line Summary

36-month split-mouth RCT (38 children, 64 pair-matched primary incisors / 128 crowns) — GIC (95.3% retention) vs self-adhesive resin cement (100% retention) for luting paediatric zirconia crowns showed no statistically significant difference in retention or gingival health; GIC is a clinically acceptable alternative.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106762](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106762)), this 36-month split-mouth randomised controlled trial compared two luting agents — Glass Ionomer Cement (GIC) and self-adhesive resin cement — for cementing paediatric zirconia crowns on carious primary incisors. Thirty-eight children (aged 3–5 years) contributed 64 pair-matched incisor pairs (128 teeth); within each child, paired teeth were randomly assigned to GIC or resin cement, then evaluated for retention and gingival condition across seven recalls from 1 week to 36 months. Three debonding events occurred in the GIC arm (95.3% retention at 36 months) versus zero in the resin arm (100%), but the difference was not statistically significant (p > 0.05; 95% CI for difference −0.5% to +9.9%), and gingival index scores did not differ at any timepoint (effect size r = 0.02–0.11).

This page is **abstract-only** (full text not retrieved); methodological detail beyond the abstract (crown brand, surface treatment, cement products, blinding) is unavailable, and the small sample limits power to detect a true retention difference.

Clinically: GIC is a clinically acceptable alternative to self-adhesive resin cement for luting paediatric zirconia crowns on primary anterior teeth — both deliver high retention and favourable gingival health at 36 months, so the simpler/fluoride-releasing GIC is a reasonable default, while the wide CI means a small resin retention advantage cannot be fully excluded.

## Key Contributions

- First direct head-to-head RCT (per authors) of **GIC vs self-adhesive resin cement** for paediatric zirconia crowns — fills the luting-agent evidence gap left by crown-type studies.
- Split-mouth design controls patient-level confounders (oral hygiene, diet, caries risk).
- Mid-term 36-month follow-up with structured 7-point recall schedule.
- Practical message: GIC is an acceptable, simpler alternative to resin cement for this indication.

## Methodology

- **Design**: split-mouth randomised controlled trial.
- **Participants**: children aged 3–5 with carious primary incisors needing zirconia crowns.
- **Sample**: 38 patients; 64 pair-matched incisors = 128 teeth.
- **Intervention**: paired incisors randomised within-child to GIC vs self-adhesive resin cement.
- **Outcomes**: crown retention; gingival condition (gingival index).
- **Recalls**: 1-week, 1-, 3-, 6-, 12-, 24-, 36-month.
- **Statistics**: McNemar's test (retention), Wilcoxon signed-rank test (gingival index); p < 0.05.

## Results

- **Enrolment**: 38 patients, 64 pair-matched incisors (128 teeth).
- **GIC retention**: 3 debonding events → 96.9% (12-mo), 95.3% (24-mo), 95.3% (36-mo).
- **Resin-cement retention**: 0 debonding events → 100% throughout.
- **Retention difference**: not statistically significant (p > 0.05; 95% CI −0.5% to +9.9%).
- **Gingival index**: no significant difference between cements at any timepoint (p > 0.05; effect size r = 0.02–0.11).
- **Conclusion**: both cements gave high retention and favourable gingival health; GIC is a clinically acceptable alternative for luting zirconia crowns in primary teeth.
