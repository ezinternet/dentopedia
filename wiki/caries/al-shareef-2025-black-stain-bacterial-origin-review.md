## Summary

Dental black stain (BS) is an external cervical discoloration appearing as dark lines or incomplete coalescent spots on the cervical third of teeth. This 2025 narrative review synthesizes contemporary evidence on the etiology, microbiology, salivary biochemistry, epidemiology, and treatment of bacterially-derived black stain.

The unifying mechanistic hypothesis is that Actinomyces spp. and associated anaerobes produce hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) via sulfur metabolism, which reacts with elevated salivary iron (Fe³⁺) to precipitate insoluble ferric sulfide (FeS) — the black pigment. Individuals with black stain show distinctively altered salivary profiles: higher iron, sodium, copper, and calcium/phosphate concentrations; elevated pH; and reduced salivary flow. The elevated pH and calcium/phosphate are thought to underlie the well-documented inverse association between black stain and dental caries.

Prevalence is 2.4–20%, with equal gender distribution and occurrence across all age groups. Treatment is primarily repeated professional ultrasonic scaling. Alternative modalities with limited evidence include photodynamic therapy, virgin coconut oil, peroxide therapy, lactoferrin, and oral probiotics. All treatments must be balanced against potential enamel damage.

## Key Contributions

- Proposes consolidated iron-sulfide precipitation mechanism: bacterial H₂S + salivary Fe³⁺ → ferric sulfide black deposits
- Confirms Actinomyces spp. as the most consistently dominant organisms in black stain biofilm across studies
- Documents salivary chemistry signature: elevated iron, sodium, copper, Ca/P; higher pH; lower flow rate
- Synthesizes inverse caries association and proposes salivary pH + mineral supersaturation as the protective mechanism
- Reviews evidence for alternative treatments; none yet demonstrated superiority over ultrasonic scaling
- Cautions against aggressive mechanical removal due to enamel wear risk

## Methodology

- Study design: Narrative review (no systematic protocol, no PRISMA)
- Scope: Bacterial-origin black stain only — explicitly distinct from extrinsic staining from tobacco, food, chlorhexidine, iron supplements
- Covered domains: clinical presentation, epidemiology, microbiology, salivary biochemistry, treatment options
- No formal risk-of-bias assessment; evidence synthesis is qualitative

## Results

| Variable | Finding |
|---|---|
| Prevalence | 2.4–20%; both genders equally; all ages |
| Chief organism | Actinomyces spp. (most abundant across microbiome studies) |
| Caries relationship | Inverse — black stain → lower caries incidence |
| Salivary iron | ↑ in BS individuals |
| Salivary pH | ↑ in BS individuals |
| Salivary flow | ↓ in BS individuals |
| Other elevated ions | Na, Cu, Ca, phosphate |
| Pigment chemistry | Fe³⁺ + H₂S (bacterial) → FeS (ferric sulfide) |
