## Summary

Black stain (BS) is an extrinsic dark pigmentation appearing as a line or coalescent dots on the cervical third of teeth. This narrative review synthesizes the literature from 2001–2014 to address etiology, prevalence, microbiological profile, chemical composition, and relationship to dental caries.

Prevalence ranges from 2.4% to 18% with roughly equal sex distribution. The microflora of BS deposits is dominated by *Actinomyces* species rather than the cariogenic *Streptococcus mutans* and *Lactobacillus*, conferring lower acidogenic potential than regular dental plaque. Chemically, iron/copper and sulfur compounds (including ferrous sulfide) are responsible for the characteristic dark color — dietary chromogens such as tea polyphenols interact with these metal-sulfur complexes.

In patients with BS, saliva shows higher calcium concentrations and higher buffering capacity, creating an environment less conducive to demineralization. The majority of reviewed studies confirm that BS is associated with lower caries experience (DMFT/dmft). Contributing factors include dietary habits (tea/coffee consumption), iron supplementation, and socioeconomic context.

## Key Contributions

- Consolidated framework for BS etiology covering microbiological, chemical, salivary, and dietary dimensions
- Documents the inverse BS–caries relationship across the available literature
- Identifies *Actinomyces* dominance in BS deposits as a key explanation for reduced cariogenic potential
- Implicates iron/sulfur chemistry (ferrous sulfide) as the primary chromogenic mechanism
- Highlights salivary differences (Ca²⁺, buffering capacity) as a plausible protective pathway

## Methodology

Narrative review. Literature: English-language studies published 2001–2014 reporting on black stain prevalence, etiology, microbiota, or caries association — primarily pediatric populations. No formal PRISMA protocol or risk-of-bias assessment reported.

## Results

| Domain | Finding |
|---|---|
| Prevalence | 2.4–18%; equal sex distribution |
| Dominant microorganism | *Actinomyces* spp. |
| Cariogenic bacteria | Lower *S. mutans* and *Lactobacillus* vs non-discolored plaque |
| Chromogenic chemistry | Fe/Cu + S complexes (FeS) |
| Salivary calcium | Higher in BS patients |
| Salivary buffering capacity | Higher in BS patients |
| Caries association | Majority of studies: BS → lower DMFT/dmft |
| Contributing factors | Tea/coffee intake, iron supplementation, SES |
