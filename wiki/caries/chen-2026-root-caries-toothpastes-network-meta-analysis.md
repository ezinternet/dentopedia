## One-line Summary

SR + network meta-analysis (Cochrane, 9 databases; 13 in vitro + 3 in situ + 12 clinical studies): high-concentration sodium fluoride toothpaste (5000 ppm) most effectively reduces root-caries lesion depth, hardens lesions, and prevents new caries — bioactive Ca/P and arginine+fluoride pastes are alternatives, while standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient in high-risk patients.

## Summary

Chen, Li, Hill, and Baysan (Queen Mary University of London / Zhengzhou University) conducted this systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) to determine which toothpaste active ingredients best manage root caries, integrating in vitro, in situ, and clinical evidence. Literature was searched across nine databases (up to 31.01.2024); PROSPERO CRD42024512171. A total of 28 studies met eligibility: 13 in vitro, 3 in situ, and 12 clinical RCTs. Cochrane RoB 2.0 was used for clinical studies; a modified GRADE tool appraised certainty of evidence (all clinical outcomes rated low quality).

Active toothpaste significantly outperformed placebo on lesion depth (MD −55.75; 95% CI −90.70 to −20.80; p=0.002) and mineral loss (MD −1.18; 95% CI −1.85 to −0.51; p=0.0006) in in vitro analyses. In clinical studies, the NMA cumulative ranking ranked high-concentration NaF (5000 ppm) as first, bioactive calcium/phosphate-releasing toothpaste as second, and arginine+fluoride as third, with standard NaF and stannous/amine fluoride at fourth and fifth. The star-shaped NMA topology (no direct head-to-head trials between test agents) means all relative rankings are based on indirect evidence via the standard-fluoride reference node. Overall clinical lesion-hardening was non-significant (RR 1.60, CI 0.92–2.79, I²=93%), but high-fluoride toothpastes specifically achieved RR 2.55 (1.68–3.86) and bioactive toothpastes achieved RR 1.81 (1.39–2.36) in participant-level hardening.

**Clinical takeaway**: 5000 ppm NaF toothpaste (by prescription) can be considered first-line non-invasive therapy for arresting active root caries; OTC bioactive (Ca/P) or arginine+fluoride toothpastes are suitable alternatives for patients who cannot tolerate or decline high-fluoride prescriptions. Standard 1450 ppm NaF alone is likely insufficient for high-risk patients with active root caries.

## Key Contributions

- **First NMA** to rank toothpaste active ingredients specifically for root-caries management across in vitro, in situ, and clinical study types.
- Confirms the NMA ranking order: **5000 ppm NaF > bioactive Ca/P > arginine+fluoride > standard NaF > stannous/amine fluoride** (clinical lesion hardening).
- Documents a statistically significant benefit of bioactive (RR 1.81, 1.39–2.36) and high-fluoride (RR 2.55, 1.68–3.86) toothpastes over standard NaF for lesion hardening.
- Quantifies that arginine+fluoride hardened 61.7–70.5% of root carious lesions vs 56–58.1% with standard NaF (two low-risk RCTs).
- Establishes clinically relevant prescription vs OTC decision framework for root caries management.

## Methodology

**Search and eligibility**: 9 databases (PubMed, MEDLINE via Ovid, Scopus, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane CENTRAL, Google, Open Grey, ClinicalTrials.gov); search to 31.01.2024; no language/date limits. Inclusion: permanent teeth (clinical) or extracted human root dentine (lab); RCTs, non-RCTs, controlled/prospective/retrospective trials; root-caries arrest/reversal outcomes. Exclusion: deciduous teeth, animal studies, hydroxyapatite pellets, dentine powders, case reports, expert opinion, systematic reviews.

**Screening flow**: 487 records → 246 duplicates removed → 241 screened → 80 full-text reviewed → 28 included (13 in vitro, 3 in situ, 12 clinical). One ongoing trial excluded.

**Risk-of-bias**: In vitro: 7-parameter quality tool (4 high, 6 medium, 3 low). In situ: Cochrane tool plus appliance-construction parameters (all 3 studies: low selective-reporting bias but unclear/high for allocation and blinding). Clinical: Cochrane RoB 2.0 (8/12 low risk, 4/12 some concerns; 3 flagged high risk due to missing outcome data — dropout 7.5–49.1%).

**Statistical approach**: MD (random-effects) for lesion depth, mineral loss, root-caries increments; RR for lesion hardness (teeth and participants) and new lesions. I² thresholds: <25% low, 25–50% moderate, >50% high. Subgroup analyses by duration and fluoride concentration. NMA conducted using cumulative ranking probabilities with standard NaF as central reference node.

**Toothpaste categories**:
- High fluoride: >1500 ppm NaF (typically 5000 ppm)
- Standard fluoride: 1000–1500 ppm NaF or NaMFP
- Arginine + fluoride: 1.5% arginine + 1450 ppm Na2PO3F
- Bioactive: Ca/P-releasing systems (10% HA; 5% HX-BGC; NaF + CaSO4 + NH4H2PO4; Fluoro Calcium Phospho Silicate)
- Stannous/amine fluoride: 1100 ppm SnF2 or 1400 ppm AmF/SnF2

## Results

### In vitro (13 studies; 10 in meta-analysis)

| Outcome | MD (95% CI) | p | I² |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lesion depth (toothpaste vs control) | −55.75 (−90.70 to −20.80) | 0.002 | 94% |
| Mineral loss (toothpaste vs control) | −1.18 (−1.85 to −0.51) | 0.0006 | 71% |

Subgroup analysis by duration (lesion depth): <7 days: MD −25.81 (−39.15 to −12.47), p=0.0002, I²=0%; 7–14 days: MD −78.28 (−151.41 to −5.16), p=0.04, I²=96%; >14 days: MD −8.20 (NS). Subgroup differences not significant (p=0.61 for lesion depth; p=0.43 for mineral loss). Funnel plots indicate potential publication bias.

Bioactive toothpastes (10% HA and 5% HX-BGC) showed statistically significant and consistent mineral loss reduction (MD −1.06, −1.59 to −0.53; p<0.0001; I²=0%); high NaF failed to reach significance for mineral loss vs placebo in this in vitro subgroup (MD −0.30, NS).

### In situ (3 studies; 2 in meta-analysis)

1000 ppm NaF vs no-brushing control (5-week studies): MD lesion depth −33.54 (−65.48 to −1.61), p=0.04, I²=86%.

### Clinical (12 studies; 10 in meta-analysis)

**Lesion hardening (teeth level) — overall**: RR 1.60 (95% CI 0.92–2.79), p=0.10, I²=93% (non-significant overall due to heterogeneity).

Key subgroup results:
- **Fluoride >1450 ppm vs standard**: RR **2.55 (1.68–3.86)**, p<0.0001
- **Arginine+Na2PO3F vs standard**: RR 1.21 (1.01–1.46), p=0.04
- **Stannous/amine fluoride vs standard**: RR 0.83 (0.54–1.26), NS
- **<1-year follow-up** (5000 ppm NaF + arginine formulations): RR 1.96 (1.03–3.73)
- **1–2 year follow-up** (SnF2 formulations): RR 0.83, NS

**Lesion hardening (participant level)** — bioactive (1100 ppm NaF + 1% CaSO4 + 1.1% NH4H2PO4) or arginine+1450 ppm F vs standard NaF: **RR 1.81 (1.39–2.36)**, I²=0%, p significant.

**New root carious lesions** — 5000 ppm vs 1450 ppm NaF: RR 0.43 (0.14–1.32), p=0.14, I²=97% (NS).

**Root caries increments** — Ca/P or 1100 ppm NaF vs standard/placebo: MD −0.74 (−2.08 to 0.60), p=0.28, I²=59% (NS).

**GRADE certainty**: all outcomes rated low (⊕OOO) — serious risk of bias in most; inconsistency high; evidence base small.

### Network meta-analysis rankings

NMA star topology with standard NaF as central node; all comparisons indirect. Cumulative ranking (clinical, lesion hardening):

| Rank | Toothpaste type | NMA probability comment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | High-concentration NaF (5000 ppm) | Dominant probability curve |
| 2 | Bioactive (Ca/P delivery) | Second highest curve |
| 3 | Arginine + fluoride | Third |
| 4 | Standard NaF (1450 ppm) | Fourth |
| 5 | Stannous/amine fluoride | Lowest |

No statistically significant difference between toothpastes in head-to-head NMA comparisons (all indirect evidence; small study network).

### Clinical context

- Arginine hardened 61.7–70.5% of root carious lesions vs 56–58.1% in NaF control (two low-risk RCTs: Hu 2013, n=141 arginine vs 134 control; Souza 2013, n=129 vs 124; 6-month).
- Bioactive toothpaste (Papas 2008, n=29 vs 28; 12 months): 1100 ppm NaF + Ca/P significantly more lesions becoming hard than standard NaF.
- 5000 ppm NaF clinical evidence: 5 RCTs (Baysan 2001, Ekstrand 2008, Ekstrand 2013, Srinivasan 2014, León 2019); follow-up 6–24 months; populations predominantly older adults (≥56–81.7 years).
