## Summary

This systematic review with network meta-analysis (J Dent 2026; Chen, Li, Hill, Baysan) evaluated toothpastes containing different active ingredients for the management of **root caries**, integrating laboratory-based (in vitro), in situ, and clinical evidence. It was conducted per Cochrane guidelines, with literature searches across **nine databases** up to 31.01.2024. In vitro and in situ studies were appraised with a quality assessment tool, and clinical studies with **Cochrane RoB 2.0**; two reviewers independently performed selection, extraction, risk-of-bias appraisal, and **GRADE** certainty assessment. Eligible: **13 in vitro + 3 in situ + 12 clinical** studies.

Active toothpaste significantly outperformed placebo on lesion depth and mineral loss. Among clinical comparisons, **high-concentration sodium fluoride (5000 ppm)** emerged as the most effective single agent for reducing lesion depth, hardening lesions, and preventing new caries. **Bioactive calcium/phosphate** and **arginine + fluoride** toothpastes were positioned as alternatives, whereas **standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient** to arrest active root caries in high-risk patients. Bottom line: 5000 ppm NaF toothpaste (prescription) can be first-line non-invasive therapy for arresting active root caries; OTC fluoride+arginine or bioactive pastes are alternatives for patients who cannot tolerate or decline high-fluoride prescriptions.

## Key Contributions

- First SR + network meta-analysis to **rank toothpaste active agents specifically for root-caries management**, combining in vitro, in situ, and clinical evidence.
- Confirms a significant overall benefit of active toothpaste vs placebo on root-caries lesion depth and mineral loss.
- Positions **5000 ppm sodium fluoride** as the most effective single agent across the three outcomes (lesion depth, hardening, new-lesion prevention).
- Identifies **bioactive Ca/P** and **arginine + fluoride** pastes as alternatives — useful when prescription high-fluoride is not tolerated or declined.
- Cautions that **standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient** for active root caries in high-risk patients.

## Methodology

- Conducted per **Cochrane** guidelines.
- Literature searches across **nine databases** (up to 31.01.2024).
- Quality assessment tool for in vitro/in situ studies; **Cochrane RoB 2.0** for clinical studies.
- Two reviewers independently did selection, extraction, risk-of-bias, and **GRADE** certainty.
- Eligible studies: **13 in vitro + 3 in situ + 12 clinical**.
- Mean differences (MD) for lesion depth, mineral loss, and root-caries increments; risk ratios (RR) for changes in lesion hardness and new root carious lesions; **random-effects** model.

## Results

| Comparison | Outcome | Estimate (95% CI), p |
|---|---|---|
| Toothpaste vs placebo | Root-caries lesion depth | MD −55.75 (−90.70 to −20.80), p=0.002 |
| Toothpaste vs placebo | Mineral loss | MD −1.18 (−1.85 to −0.51), p=0.0006 |
| High NaF (5000 ppm) or 1.5% arginine + 1450 ppm NaPOF vs standard 1450 ppm NaF | Hardening / reversing lesions | RR 1.60 (0.92 to 2.79) |
| Bioactive (1100 ppm NaF + 1% CaSO4 + 1.1% NH4H2PO4) vs standard toothpaste | Lesions becoming hard | RR 1.81 (1.39 to 2.36) |

- **High-concentration sodium fluoride** toothpaste was most effective at reducing lesion depth, improving hardness, and preventing new caries.
- The high-fluoride/arginine vs standard comparison (RR 1.60) had a 95% CI crossing 1 (0.92 to 2.79) — benefit not statistically conclusive in that pooled estimate; the bioactive vs standard estimate (RR 1.81, 1.39 to 2.36) did not cross 1.
- **Clinical conclusion**: 5000 ppm NaF toothpaste by prescription can be **first-line non-invasive therapy** for arresting active root caries; **OTC fluoride+arginine or bioactive (Ca/P) toothpastes** are alternatives for patients who cannot tolerate or decline high-fluoride prescriptions. Standard 1450 ppm NaF may be insufficient in high-risk patients.
