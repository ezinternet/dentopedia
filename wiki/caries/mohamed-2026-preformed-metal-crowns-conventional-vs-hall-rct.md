## One-line Summary

12-month non-inferiority RCT (n=80 carious primary molars, Qatar; 73 returned): preformed metal crowns by conventional technique (CT) vs Hall technique (HT) both achieved 100% "Successful" clinical outcomes and 100% restoration survival at 12 months (P=1.0); HT-induced OVD increase (mean 1.18±0.68 mm) resolved to baseline within 1 month (P=0.32).

## Summary

This primary care-based, open-label, two-arm parallel-group non-inferiority RCT (ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06350578) was conducted in Paediatric Dental Clinics of the Primary Health Care Corporation (PHCC), Qatar. It randomized 80 carious primary molars (1 tooth per child, 40 per arm) in cooperative children aged 3–9 years to receive PMCs either by the Conventional Technique (CT: local anesthesia, full caries removal, occlusal/proximal preparation, cementation) or the Hall Technique (HT: no anesthesia, no caries removal, no preparation — direct cementation over carious tooth). Both arms used 3M ESPE stainless steel crowns and Fuji Triage low-viscosity GIC.

At 12 months, 73 of 80 (91.3%) participants returned (CT: 36/40; HT: 37/40; P=0.28). Every PMC-restored tooth in both arms was graded "Successful" — no Major or Minor failures in either group (McNemar P=1.0). Restoration survival was 100% in both arms by Kaplan-Meier analysis. In the HT arm, OVD increased by a mean of 1.18±0.68 mm immediately after PMC seating, returning to pre-treatment values within one month (baseline 3.45±0.85 mm; post-HT 4.63±1.0 mm; 1-month 3.5±0.91 mm; P=0.32). Multilevel regression found no association between OVD changes and age, gender, tooth type, or jaw.

This paper is Part 2 of a two-part RCT. Part 1 (Mohamed et al. Eur Arch Paediatr Dent 2025) demonstrated HT superiority on procedural time, child pain perception, and parental acceptability. Taken together: HT is less invasive, better tolerated, and clinically equivalent to CT at 12 months.

**Clinical takeaway**: The non-invasive Hall technique is a viable alternative to conventional PMC placement for carious primary molars. The post-seating "bite step" (OVD increase) that concerns parents and clinicians resolves within one month and is not a reason to avoid HT.

## Key Contributions

- Non-inferiority RCT directly comparing CT vs HT PMC placement for carious primary molars at 12 months — one of the few prospective head-to-head studies.
- Both techniques achieved 100% clinical success and 100% restoration survival at 12 months (P=1.0).
- Quantified transient OVD elevation after HT (mean +1.18±0.68 mm) with confirmed resolution to baseline within 1 month (P=0.32); no association with age, gender, tooth or jaw type.
- Supports the position paper assertion that HT should be considered a routine — not radical — clinical option.
- Part of an ongoing Qatar-based RCT series (Part 1: procedural outcomes; Part 2: 12-month clinical outcomes).

## Methodology

- **Design**: Non-inferiority, primary care-based, open-label, two-arm parallel-group RCT (non-inferiority margin 15%; assumed 94% success in both arms; 80% power, α 0.05 → minimum 64, enrolled 80 to account for attrition).
- **Setting**: PHCC Paediatric Dental Clinics, Qatar; single operator (HM).
- **Participants**: Healthy cooperative (Frankl 3–4) children ages 3–9 years with vital primary molars showing active occluso-proximal dentinal caries (asymptomatic or reversible pulpitis; ≥1 mm sound dentine preoperatively; no periapical/inter-radicular pathology). One tooth per child.
- **Randomization**: Permuted block; off-site sequence generation; SNOSE allocation concealment.
- **CT arm (n=40)**: Local anesthesia; caries excavation; occlusal/proximal preparation; 3M ESPE PMC cemented with Fuji Triage GIC.
- **HT arm (n=40)**: No anesthesia; no preparation; 3M ESPE PMC cemented with Fuji Triage GIC directly over carious tooth; bite-down on cotton roll until set.
- **Follow-up**: 1, 6, 9, 12 months (clinical outcome); 1 month (OVD, HT arm).
- **Outcomes**: Clinical grade (Successful / Minor / Major failure per Innes et al. 2011 criteria); restoration survival (functional presence at 12 months without major failure); OVD measurement (UNC CP15 probe, canine-to-canine distance).
- **Statistics**: McNemar exact test (clinical outcomes); Kaplan-Meier + log-rank (survival); multilevel linear regression (OVD); Cohen's kappa (reliability); SPSS v24; P<0.05 threshold.

## Results

**Retention**: 73/80 returned at 12 months (CT 36/40, HT 37/40; P=0.28 for dropout difference).

**Clinical outcome at 12 months**:

| | CT (n=36) | HT (n=37) | P |
|---|---|---|---|
| Successful | 36 (100%) | 37 (100%) | 1.0 |
| Minor failure | 0 | 0 | — |
| Major failure | 0 | 0 | — |

Intra-examiner kappa = 1.0 for clinical outcomes.

**Restoration survival**: CT 100%, HT 100%; flat Kaplan-Meier curves for both groups.

**OVD resolution (HT arm, n=40)**:

| Timepoint | Mean OVD (mm) |
|---|---|
| Baseline | 3.45 ± 0.85 |
| Immediately post-HT | 4.63 ± 1.0 |
| 1-month follow-up | 3.5 ± 0.91 |

- Mean OVD increase immediately post-HT: **1.18 ± 0.68 mm**
- Baseline vs 1-month: **P = 0.32** (no significant difference)
- No association with age, gender, tooth type (1st vs 2nd primary molar), or jaw (maxillary vs mandibular)
- Intra-examiner kappa for OVD: 0.95–0.97

**Literature context**: Prior studies reported 12-month success of 91.3–100% for CT and 88.6–100% for HT; 24-month success 85.2–86.5% for CT and 86.3–88.1% for HT. This study's 100%/100% at 12 months is at the top end of both ranges. Prior OVD data show re-equilibration within 2–4 weeks consistent with this paper's 1-month resolution finding.
