## One-line Summary

In-vitro photoelastic + ISO 4049 study of 10 ion-releasing polymeric restoratives (RMGIC, compomer, giomer) showing material-dependent polymerization contraction stress relaxed (and sometimes reversed to expansion stress) by hygroscopic water sorption; Ionosit (compomer) had the highest water sorption (~3 wt%) and produced significant radial pressure after 56 days.

## 한줄요약

이온유리 폴리머 수복재 10종(RMGIC·컴포머·지오머)을 광탄성 + ISO 4049로 평가한 in-vitro 연구 — 중합수축응력이 수분흡수(hygroscopic expansion)로 완화·역전됨이 재료별로 다르며, Ionosit(컴포머)이 전 시험재 중 최고 수분흡수(~3 wt%)·56일 후 유의한 radial pressure를 보임.

## Summary

Sokolowski et al. (2018, *Polymers*) tested 10 fluoride/ion-releasing polymeric restoratives across three classes — RMGIC (Fuji II LC, Ionolux), compomer/polyacid-modified composite (Dyract eXtra, Compoglass Flow, **Ionosit**, Glasiosite, Twinky Star), and giomer (Beautifil Bulk Fill Flow, Beautifil Flow Plus F00, Beautifil Flow F02) — to measure how water sorption modifies the stress state at the restoration–tooth interface. Using a modified photoelastic method (epoxy plates with 3 mm × 4 mm cavity-mimicking orifices, Timoshenko-equation stress, polariscope) plus ISO 4049 water sorption/solubility/absorption, they tracked materials from 30 min to 2016 h (84 days). All materials shrank during hardening then relaxed under hygroscopic expansion; four developed net expansion stress. Effects were strongly material-dependent and tied to filler content.

## Key Contributions

- **Named Ionosit (DMG) bench data**: Ionosit is classified as a **compomer (polyacid-modified composite)**; it had the **highest water sorption of all tested materials** (~3 wt%) and **produced significant radial (expansion) pressure after 56 days** of water storage, with post-polymerization volume increase compensating polymerization contraction.
- Quantifies the polymerization-contraction → hygroscopic-expansion transition: **absolute stress changes 6.2–21.4 MPa**, with most materials reaching 100% contraction-stress reduction and four reaching net expansion stress.
- Shows higher filler content lowers sorption/solubility (reduced free volume); low-sorption materials (Beautifil Bulk Fill 13.6 µg/mm³, Dyract eXtra ≤1.6 MPa at 21 d, Glasiosite, Twinky Star) did NOT reach total stress reduction.
- Documents the photoelastic method's inadequacy for RMGIC (Fuji II LC, Ionolux) due to persistent contraction isochromes during the acid-base remodeling transition.

## Methodology

- In-vitro laboratory study; 10 commercial ion-releasing materials.
- Water absorption dynamic, water sorption & solubility per **ISO 4049** (n=5, 15 mm × 1 mm specimens; desiccator constant-mass → 7-day 37 °C immersion).
- Modified **photoelastic analysis** (n=3/material): birefringent epoxy plates with 3 mm diameter × 4 mm deep orifices mimicking a cavity; circular transmission polariscope FL200; stress via Timoshenko equation.
- Time points: 0.5, 24, 72, 96, 168, 240, 336, 504, 672, 1344, 2016 h. Light cure 1250 mW/cm², 2 mm increments.

## Results

- **Ionosit (compomer)**: highest water sorption among all materials; absorption up to ~3 wt%; significant radial pressure after 56 days (echoing McCabe's F2000 compomer behavior).
- Beautifil Flow F02 (giomer): highest swelling/radial pressure via S-PRG pre-reacted-glass osmotic effect; also ~3 wt% absorption.
- RMGIC water sorption >115 µg/mm³; lowest sorption = Beautifil Bulk Fill Flow (13.6 µg/mm³).
- Overall contraction-stress change range **6.2–21.4 MPa**; most materials → 100% contraction-stress reduction, four → net expansion stress.
- No significant overall difference between giomers and compomers; differences driven by composition/filler load. Low-sorption materials (Beautifil Bulk Fill, Dyract eXtra, Glasiosite, Twinky Star) lacked total stress reduction.
