## One-line Summary

Systematic review + meta-analysis (6 RCTs; periodontal-treatment n=124 vs control n=131) showing non-surgical periodontal therapy in patients with both periodontal disease and rheumatoid arthritis significantly reduces RA activity (DAS28 mean difference −0.56, 95% CI −0.82 to −0.31, p=0.001; I²=39%) and improves clinical attachment level (CAL −0.41 mm, 95% CI −0.70 to −0.12, p=0.006; I²=7%); trim-and-fill–adjusted to −0.39 DAS28 points and −0.30 mm.

## Summary

This PRISMA systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42020192179) asks the **interventional** version of the periodontitis–rheumatoid-arthritis (RA) question: in patients who have *both* periodontal disease and RA, does treating the periodontal disease reduce RA disease activity? It pools 6 randomized controlled trials of non-surgical periodontal treatment (scaling/root planing plus oral-hygiene reinforcement), comparing treated patients (n=124) with untreated controls (n=131) over a roughly 2-month window.

Two outcomes were pooled with a random-effects inverse-variance model: **DAS28** (a 0–9.4 composite RA disease-activity score) and **clinical attachment level (CAL, mm)** for periodontal status. Both moved significantly in favor of periodontal treatment. DAS28 dropped by a mean of −0.56 (95% CI −0.82 to −0.31; z=−4.33, p=0.001; slight heterogeneity I²=39%), shifting enrolled patients from moderate toward low RA activity, while control groups worsened. CAL improved by −0.41 mm (95% CI −0.70 to −0.12; z=−2.73, p=0.006) with essentially no heterogeneity (I²=7%). Trim-and-fill adjustment for publication bias added three studies and retained significance for both endpoints (−0.39 DAS28 points; −0.30 mm CAL).

The clinical message: controlling periodontal infection and gingival inflammation through SRP and plaque control in moderate periodontal disease can contribute to reducing the signs and symptoms of active RA, supporting a multidisciplinary model where RA patients are screened and treated periodontally alongside conventional antirheumatic therapy.

> [!note] On the "null hypothesis supported" wording
> The paper's discussion states the null hypothesis (no association) is "supported," but its own significant DAS28/CAL treatment effects and its conclusion ("control of periodontal disease can reduce the severity of RA... supports a pathogenic association") argue the opposite. Read this as an internal inconsistency: the data and conclusion endorse a treatable association. Also note the abstract/discussion swap the −0.56 / −0.41 labels between DAS28 and CAL; the internally consistent values (Results §3.4) are CAL −0.41 mm and DAS28 −0.56.

## Key Contributions

- Pools the **treatment** direction of the periodontitis↔RA link (does treating PD lower RA activity), complementing risk-direction meta-analyses.
- Restricts to RCTs and reports both an RA outcome (DAS28) and a periodontal outcome (CAL).
- Provides publication-bias-adjusted (trim-and-fill) estimates that stay significant for both endpoints.

## Methodology

- **Design**: PRISMA SR+MA of RCTs; PROSPERO CRD42020192179.
- **Search**: PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane up to March 2024. 29 records → 6 RCTs after de-duplication and screening. Sample sizes <100/study; no dropouts.
- **PICO**: PD+RA patients; non-surgical periodontal treatment; treated vs untreated; outcomes CAL (mm) and DAS28.
- **Synthesis**: random-effects inverse-variance mean differences; z test; Q/I² heterogeneity; trim-and-fill for publication bias. Risk of bias by Jadad scale (most not double-blind; only Silvestre-Rangil and Thilagar double-blind).
- **Confidence**: `sr+ma` — but tempered by 6 small short-follow-up RCTs with mixed blinding.

## Results

| Outcome | Treatment vs control | Mean difference (95% CI) | z; p | I² | Trim-and-fill adjusted |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CAL (mm) | n=124 vs n=131 | −0.41 (−0.70 to −0.12) | −2.73; 0.006 | 7% | −0.30 mm (−0.55 to −0.046; p=0.021) |
| DAS28 | n=124 vs n=131 | −0.56 (−0.82 to −0.31) | −4.33; 0.001 | 39% | −0.39 pts (−0.46 to −0.31; p<0.001) |

- DAS28 bands: low ≤3.2, moderate 3.2–5.1, high >5.1; enrolled at moderate, treated shifted toward low.
- CAL ≥4 mm marks active periodontitis; controls progressed, treated improved.
- Both endpoints remained significant after publication-bias adjustment.
