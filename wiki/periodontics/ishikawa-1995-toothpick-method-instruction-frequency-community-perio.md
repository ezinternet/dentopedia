## One-line Summary

Community comparative intervention (n=31 three-time vs n=40 single Watanabe toothpick-method instruction, 1-year follow-up): repeated instruction significantly reduced gingival redness/swelling and gave higher CPITN improvement, while single instruction did not change gingival inflammation at 1 year — repeated instruction outperformed single.

## Summary

This classic Japanese community study (Okayama University Dental School; **T. Watanabe**, originator of the toothpick method, is a coauthor) asks a practical public-health question: how often must you re-teach a brushing technique for it to actually improve periodontal health? Two non-randomized courses in a small town compared **three monthly toothbrushing instruction sessions** (n=31, mean age 48.7 ± 9.2 yr) against a **single instruction session** (n=40, mean age 55.2 ± 8.3 yr), both teaching the **Watanabe Toothpick Method**. Both groups shared the same first visit: a dental health education class, self-observation of their own plaque under a **phase-contrast microscope**, baseline periodontal recording, and toothpick-method instruction. At 1 year, only the repeated-instruction group resolved gingival inflammation, supporting **reinforced, repeated oral-hygiene instruction over a one-shot session**.

*This page is abstract-level only — the paper is in Japanese and only the English abstract (PubMed-truncated at 250 words) was retrieved; numbers beyond those stated below are not available and were not invented.*

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates an **instruction-frequency (dose) effect** on periodontal outcomes for the Watanabe toothpick method, not just a single-instruction snapshot.
- Couples the clinical effect to **behavior change**: repeated-instruction subjects self-reported brushing more actively and were more satisfied with the program.
- A **community-program** (not clinic-RCT) demonstration of motivational tools — phase-contrast self-observation of plaque + repeated reinforcement.

## Methodology

- **Design:** community comparative intervention, two non-randomized courses, 1-year follow-up.
- **Groups:**
  - Three-time instruction: **n=31**, mean age **48.7 ± 9.2** yr — three monthly instruction sessions.
  - Single instruction: **n=40**, mean age **55.2 ± 8.3** yr — one instruction session.
- **Shared first visit (both groups):** dental health education class → self-observation of dental plaque under a **phase-contrast microscope** → baseline recording of teeth and periodontal tissue → instruction to brush by the **Toothpick Method**.
- **Outcomes:** percentage of sites with gingival redness and swelling; percentage of sites with bleeding on probing (BOP); CPITN code improvement; 1-year self-report questionnaire (brushing activity, satisfaction). The three-time group's gingival inflammation was also checked at 1 and 2 months.

## Results

- **Gingival redness/swelling:** significantly decreased at **1 month, 2 months, and 1 year** in the three-time group; **no change at 1 year** in the single-instruction group.
- **Bleeding on probing (BOP):** significantly decreased in **both** groups.
- **CPITN improvement ratio:** **higher in the three-time group** than in the single group.
- **Questionnaire:** three-time group brushed more actively and reported greater program satisfaction.
- **Conclusion:** repeated (three-time) instruction was more effective than single instruction.
- *Caveats:* non-randomized; the single group was older (55.2 vs 48.7 yr); small samples; effect sizes/p-values beyond "significant" not reported in the truncated abstract; BOP improved in both groups, so part of the benefit may stem from the shared baseline education rather than the extra sessions alone.
