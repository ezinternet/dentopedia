## One-line Summary

26-year prospective longitudinal cohort (160 Norwegian males; 615 control vs 98 test mesial sites) showing subgingival filling margins drive higher plaque/gingival inflammation and increased attachment loss vs supragingival/sound surfaces, detectable 1–3 years after placement with a later "burn-out" effect.

## Summary

This is one of the longest prospective datasets on the perio-restorative interface. Drawing on the Norwegian natural-history-of-periodontitis cohort, the authors tracked mesial sites of premolars/molars in 160 well-maintained males across 7 examinations over 26 years (1969–1995). Sites that acquired a subgingival filling margin (>1 mm subgingival) between 1969 and 1971 (test, n=98) were compared with sites that stayed sound or kept margins >1 mm from the gingival margin throughout (control, n=615). Subgingival margins were associated with significantly higher plaque and gingival inflammation at nearly every survey and with greater loss of attachment. The damage was not immediate: it began slowly and became clinically detectable 1–3 years after the restoration was placed, then showed a "burn-out" (plateau) effect. The study confirms the long-held clinical principle that subgingival restoration margins are detrimental to periodontal tissues, primarily via plaque retention.

## Key Contributions

- 26-year prospective confirmation that subgingival restoration margins harm gingival and periodontal health.
- GI elevated at subgingival-margin sites vs controls at all 7 surveys (p<0.001).
- Time course: attachment loss detectable 1–3 years post-placement, then a "burn-out" plateau.

## Methodology

- Prospective longitudinal cohort, 7 examinations 1969–1995 (single examiner, pointed probe).
- Test = 98 mesial sites acquiring a >1 mm subgingival filling margin by 1971; Control = 615 sound/supragingival sites at all surveys.
- Indices: Plaque Index, Gingival Index, Loss of Attachment, Gingival Restoration Index (Löe 1967).
- Limitations: single demographic (Scandinavian males, good hygiene), heavy dropout, pre-modern materials.
