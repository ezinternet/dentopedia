## One-line Summary

Narrative (systematically-searched) review concluding that contemporary tooth-supported restorations/FDPs are compatible with periodontal health under good hygiene — subgingival/intracrevicular margins per se do not cause periodontitis, while large overhangs and connective-tissue-attachment invasion drive inflammation and bone loss.

## Summary

This review (a companion/update to Ercoli & Caton's 2018 World Workshop tooth-related-factors paper) searches PubMed systematically and synthesizes the periodontal consequences of tooth-supported restorations and fixed dental prostheses across four domains: direct restorations, indirect restorations, biologic width / supracrestal tissue attachment, and tooth preparation / finish-line design. The central, clinically important nuance is that *intracrevicular margins do not cause periodontal disease* — what drives inflammation and bone loss are large overhangs (direct or indirect) and invasion of the connective tissue attachment. Removing the connective tissue attachment produces histological change, apical shift, and subsequent re-establishment. Restorative materials differ in tissue response when seated in the sulcus or attachment; finish-line design does not produce clinically different periodontal responses; gingival displacement during impression can cause recession; and emergence profile spans a range of values not inherently pathologic. The bottom line: with adequate patient education and self-performed oral hygiene, contemporary restorative procedures and materials are compatible with periodontal health, and periodontal diagnostic criteria should be reviewed before fixed restorative treatment.

## Key Contributions

- Reframes the older blanket warning: subgingival/intracrevicular margins alone ≠ periodontitis; large overhangs and attachment invasion are the harmful variables.
- Four-domain organization (direct, indirect, biologic width/SCTA, preparation/finish-line).
- Finish-line design: no clinically different periodontal response across designs.

## Methodology

- Narrative review with a systematic PubMed search methodology (expanded database); filters for case reports, trials, reviews, guidelines, RCTs, meta-analyses, SRs, English.
- Narrative synthesis; no pooled meta-analytic estimates.
- Limitations: heterogeneous decades-spanning evidence; conclusions assume maintained oral hygiene.
