## Summary

This systematic review (confidence: sr) compiles in-vitro evidence on how the three principal professional instrumentation modalities — hand instruments, ultrasonic scalers, and air-polishing devices — affect dental hard tissue, measured as material (substance) loss and surface roughness on human cementum and dentine. The clinical question is which modality is least destructive to root structure during repeated debridement, relevant to supportive periodontal therapy where the same surfaces are instrumented many times over a patient's lifetime.

The key ranking: per stroke, hand instruments cause the greatest substance loss, while per unit time (per second) air-polishing with low-abrasive powders causes the most. Ultrasonic scalers sit between the two on both metrics. Surface-roughness findings were inconsistent across studies, and high heterogeneity precludes firm pooled comparison.

## Key Contributions

- Provides side-by-side numeric ranges of root substance loss per stroke and per second across hand, ultrasonic, and air-polishing instrumentation.
- Scopes air-polishing evidence to low-abrasive powders (glycine or erythritol), excluding other powders and combined treatments.
- Frames a least-invasive selection logic: stroke-count-limited tasks favour ultrasonic over hand curettes; time-limited continuous application makes air-polishing the most aggressive.

## Methodology

- Design: systematic review of in-vitro studies.
- Databases: MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane Library.
- Inclusion: human cementum and dentine; ≥6 human tooth specimens.
- Exclusion: powders other than glycine or erythritol; combined treatments.
- Outcomes: substance (material) loss and surface roughness.

## Results

Root substance loss (quoted from abstract):

| Modality | Per stroke (µm) | Per second (µm) |
|---|---|---|
| Ultrasonic scalers | 0.28–14.73 (mean 4.89) | 0.33–8.20 (mean 2.24) |
| Hand instruments | 0.86–20.60 (mean 6.0) | — |
| Air polishing (low-abrasive powders) | — | 0.12–23.40 (mean 8.65) |

- Most studies reported higher substance loss with hand curettes than with ultrasonic scalers.
- Substance loss per second was highest with air-polishing devices vs ultrasonic.
- Substance loss per stroke was greatest with hand instruments vs ultrasonic scalers.
- Surface roughness: mixed — one study found no increase with air polishing; others reported both increases and decreases.
- Conclusion: heterogeneity limits direct comparison between modalities.
