## One-line Summary

Case report (n=1, 58-year-old man) of an acute periodontal abscess on a vital mandibular left central incisor that radiographically and clinically mimicked an acute apical abscess; resolved with antibiotics (amoxicillin + metronidazole), chlorhexidine, scaling/drainage rather than endodontic therapy — emphasizing pulp vitality testing for differential diagnosis.

## Summary

A 58-year-old man presented with severe sharp pain, swelling, reddish gingiva, and bleeding in the mandibular anterior region, with fever (38.0°C) and tender submandibular lymph nodes. A periapical radiograph showed radiolucency around the apex of the mandibular left central incisor, and the clinical picture initially suggested an acute apical (endodontic) abscess. However, thermal and electric pulp sensibility testing confirmed all mandibular incisors were vital, and heavy plaque/calculus with localized lingual gingival recession pointed to a periodontal origin. The final diagnosis was an acute periodontal abscess. The lesion was managed non-surgically with chlorhexidine irrigation, systemic antibiotics (amoxicillin + metronidazole) and naproxen, followed by scaling and drainage once pain subsided — not endodontic therapy. The case underscores that periodontal and apical abscesses can look identical radiographically, and pulp vitality testing is the decisive differentiator.

## Key Contributions

- Illustrates a periodontal abscess presenting with periapical radiolucency that mimicked an endodontic abscess on a vital tooth.
- Establishes pulp vitality (thermal + electric) testing as the key diagnostic discriminator, preventing unnecessary endodontic intervention.
- Contrasts management pathways: acute periodontal abscess → periodontal therapy (scaling, debridement, drainage) + systemic antimicrobials; acute apical abscess → pulp extirpation ± incision/drainage, with no significant role for systemic antibiotics.
- Advises case-by-case use of systemic antimicrobials, avoiding them when a confirmed diagnosis is not possible.

## Methodology

- Single-patient case report (n=1). 58-year-old Saudi man, healthy, nonsmoker/nondrinker.
- Workup: extraoral/intraoral exam, vitals, periapical radiograph, thermal and electric pulp sensibility testing.
- Treatment protocol: 0.2% chlorhexidine supragingival irrigation; amoxicillin 500 mg TID ×7d; metronidazole 500 mg TID ×7d; naproxen 550 mg PRN; 0.12% chlorhexidine rinse BID ×2 weeks; scaling and pus drainage at day 3.
- Follow-up: clinical at 1 week, radiographic at 3 months.

## Results

- Day 3: pain markedly reduced, enabling scaling and pus drainage.
- 1 week: complete healing — no redness, swelling, lymphadenopathy, or bleeding.
- 3 months: periapical radiograph showed healing in progress.
- Tooth retained; favorable prognosis once correctly diagnosed and treated for periodontal etiology.
