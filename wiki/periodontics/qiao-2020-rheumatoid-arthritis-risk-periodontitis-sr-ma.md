## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis — the first to pool case-control studies exclusively and include comprehensive subgroup analyses — assessed whether periodontitis is a risk factor for rheumatoid arthritis (RA). Thirteen studies from Asia (6), Europe (4), and North America (3), published between 2008 and 2019, were pooled. The total study population included 706,611 periodontitis patients and 349,983 controls (1,056,594 participants total), with 7,154 RA patients identified in the periodontitis group.

**Overall result**: periodontitis patients had a 69% greater RA risk compared with controls (OR 1.69, 95% CI 1.31–2.17, P<0.0001). High heterogeneity was present (I²=89%), but sensitivity analysis did not substantially change the pooled estimate (OR 1.48 after removing the largest study, 95% CI 1.20–1.83, P=0.0003), and no significant publication bias was detected (Egger test P=0.052).

The biological plausibility rests on shared pathophysiology: (1) *Porphyromonas gingivalis* (Pg) carries peptidylarginine deiminase (PAD) that citrullinates host proteins, generating anti-citrullinated protein antibody (ACPA/anti-CCP) targets; (2) *Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans* (Aa) drives global hypercitrullination via leukotoxin A (LtxA) targeting neutrophils; (3) the HLA-DRB1 shared epitope is associated with both alveolar bone loss in periodontitis and joint destruction in RA; and (4) collagenase/matrix metalloproteinases are found in both gingival crevicular fluid and synovial fluid.

Clinically, this evidence supports bidirectional screening: periodontitis patients warrant RA surveillance, and RA patients warrant active periodontal management. Treatment of periodontitis in RA patients has separately been shown to reduce RA disease activity.

## Key Contributions

- First meta-analysis restricted to case-control methodology for the periodontitis → RA direction, reducing structural heterogeneity vs mixed-design pools.
- Largest pooled population for this question: >1 million subjects across 13 studies.
- Comprehensive subgroup analyses by RA disease type (incident/mixed/prevalent), disease duration (>5 y, <5 y, mixed), and region (Asia, Europe, North America).
- Points to the shared citrullination/PAD/HLA-DRB1 mechanism as an epidemiologically supported biological bridge.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review + meta-analysis; PRISMA-compliant.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Embase, Web of Science, Wanfang; searched September 2019; no language or time restrictions.
- **Study types included**: Case-control studies only (observational); 13 studies from 4,445 identified records (1,904 duplicates removed).
- **Periodontitis definition**: clinical criteria (PPD/CAL thresholds, varying across studies), questionnaire, or periodontist diagnosis; RA defined by internationally accepted criteria.
- **Effect measure**: pooled OR with 95% CI; Mantel-Haenszel method; random-effect model used (I²>50%).
- **Heterogeneity**: I² test; values >75% classified as high.
- **Quality appraisal**: Downs and Black 27-item tool (study quality, external validity, bias, confounding/selection bias, power).
- **Sensitivity analysis**: sequential leave-one-out.
- **Publication bias**: Egger test (P=0.052, non-significant).

## Results

### Overall RA Risk
- Pooled OR **1.69** (95% CI 1.31–2.17; P<0.0001; I²=89%)
- 69% greater RA risk in periodontitis vs controls.

### Subgroup Analysis

| Subgroup | N studies | OR (95% CI) | P | I² |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mixed RA | 11 | 1.61 (1.26–2.06) | <0.001 | 79% |
| Incident RA | 2 | 1.70 (0.75–3.85) | <0.001 | 96% |
| Prevalent RA | 1 | 0.93 (0.58–1.47) | — | 0% |
| Duration >5 y | 2 | 2.88 (0.66–12.62) | 0.018 | 82% |
| Duration <5 y | 3 | 2.59 (0.83–8.11) | <0.001 | 91% |
| Duration mixed | 8 | 1.53 (1.05–2.22) | <0.001 | 89% |
| Asia | 6 | 1.55 (1.11–2.16) | <0.001 | 94% |
| North America | 3 | 1.19 (0.98–1.44) | 0.264 | 25% |
| Europe | 4 | 5.69 (2.93–11.06) | 0.904 | 0% |

Key observations:
- The incident RA and disease-duration subgroups have sparse data (2–3 studies each) and very wide CIs crossing 1, despite reported significant P-values — interpret with caution.
- North America: only 3 studies including large population-based cohorts (Chou et al, n=628,628; Demmer et al, n=5,190) with OR close to 1 and low heterogeneity (I²=25%), possibly reflecting population-level dilution.
- Europe: only 4 small studies (total n<600) with OR 5.69 and I²=0%; driven by the two high-OR studies (Laugisch et al, n=96; Nesse et al, n=537), likely selection/clinic bias in high-RA prevalence referral populations.

### Quality Appraisal
- All 13 studies: clearly stated hypothesis (100%), representative participants (92%), reliable outcome measures (100%), appropriate statistical tests (100%).
- Key weaknesses: no adjustment for differential follow-up duration (0%); limited assessor blinding (8%); variable confounder adjustment for smoking, alcohol, antibiotics (62%).
