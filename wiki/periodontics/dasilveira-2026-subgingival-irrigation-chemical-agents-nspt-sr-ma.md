## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 16 RCTs (712 patients): adjunctive subgingival irrigation with chemical agents (PVP-I, CHX, essential oils, ozonated water, boric acid) during NSPT gave NO additional benefit over water/saline in PPD reduction (MD 0.01 mm), CAL gain (MD 0.09 mm), or BOP; evidence low to very low.

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis (PRISMA, PROSPERO 1011516) tested a single, specific clinical habit: delivering a **chemical agent (CA) as a subgingival irrigant** during non-surgical periodontal therapy (NSPT), versus NSPT with a water/saline control. Searching MEDLINE/PubMed, Web of Science, and LILACS to March 2025 and applying strict eligibility (≥3-month follow-up; excluding gel vehicles, full-mouth disinfection, added mouthwash, extracts, and systemic antibiotics/corticosteroids), 16 RCTs (712 patients, 13 parallel + 3 split-mouth) were pooled. The agents were povidone-iodine (PVP-I), chlorhexidine (CHX), essential oils (EOs), ozonated water (OW), and boric acid (BA).

The bottom line: **adjunctive CA subgingival irrigation provides no additional clinical benefit over NSPT alone** for PPD reduction, CAL gain, or BOP, with evidence rated low to very low (GRADE). This updates and partially overturns Van der Sluijs 2016 (which had reported a slight PVP-I CAL gain not confirmed here). The authors invoke antimicrobial-stewardship: in the absence of demonstrated benefit, and given CHX tolerance/resistance concerns, routine adjunctive subgingival irrigation is not supported.

## Key Contributions

- Isolates the **delivery channel** (subgingival irrigation, syringe or ultrasonic) from other adjunct routes (gels, slow-release chips, mouthwash, full-mouth disinfection).
- First update since 2016; adds 8 RCTs with stricter eligibility, reversing the earlier "PVP-I helps CAL" signal.
- Subgroup-resolves by agent, irrigation method, follow-up, and furcation involvement — none rescues the negative result.
- Reframes a common clinical practice under antimicrobial-stewardship principles.

## Methodology

- 16 RCTs from 1244 screened records; 712 patients (336 M, 376 F); age 26-72y; follow-up 3-12 months (7×3mo, 6×6mo, 3×12mo).
- Delivery: 13 ultrasonic-scaler irrigation, 3 syringe. 15 studies excluded smokers; 1 (Cosyn 2013) included smokers.
- Agents: PVP-I (10%/1%/0.5%/0.1%/unreported), EOs (×3), CHX 0.2% (×4) / 0.12% (×1), BA 0.75% (×1), OW (×1).
- Random-effects mean-difference meta-analyses for PPD, CAL, BOP; heterogeneity by Cochran Q / I²; RoB 2.0; GRADE.

## Results

- **Overall pooled (non-significant, low quality):**
  - PPD change: MD **0.01 mm** (95% CI -0.11 to 0.14; P=0.82; I²=73%)
  - CAL gain: MD **0.09 mm** (95% CI 0.00 to 0.18; P=0.05; I²=0%)
  - BOP reduction: MD **-1.48** (95% CI -5.84 to 12.88; P=0.51; I²=0%)
- By agent (PVP-I, CHX, EO, BA): no additional benefit in any parameter.
- **Ozonated water** (single study): slight significant PPD reduction (~0.40 mm), but the source RCT itself found no significant effect — clinically marginal; interpret with caution.
- Follow-up: no PPD/BOP difference at any time. CAL gain at **12 months** (non-furcation studies): small but significant MD **0.25 mm** (95% CI 0.09-0.40; P=0.002) — only 2 studies, one with unequal groups; caution.
- Irrigation method (syringe vs ultrasonic): no difference.
- Furcation studies (3): all non-significant (PPD MD 0.02, CAL MD -0.08, BOP MD 0.56).
- RoB: 14 "some concerns" (no prospective registration), 2 high RoB (Rosling 2001, Saglam 2013). No substantial publication bias.
- Adverse effects reported in only 2 studies; 1 study reported a PRO (VAS, no difference); no validated PROMs.
