## Summary

Subcutaneous facial emphysema (SFE) is a rare but potentially serious complication of dental procedures involving pressurised air — historically air-driven handpieces, and more recently air-polishing systems. Reports specifically involving subgingival air-polishing nozzles used within periodontal pockets are limited. This case describes SFE arising after glycine-based air-powder abrasion delivered into a 5 mm deep periodontal pocket for biofilm eradication. The patient developed periorbital and right-cheek swelling, was hospitalised for surveillance, and received antibiotics to control microbial spread; they were discharged stable after 3 days with no further complications.

The authors' core message: SFE may occur during subgingival air polishing even in a supportive periodontal therapy setting and at sites without bleeding on probing (BOP) or suppuration. Air-polishing safety should not be generalised across devices and protocols. Careful case selection, strict adherence to device-specific instructions, and early recognition of swelling/crepitus are essential for safe practice. ## Key Contributions

- Documents subcutaneous facial emphysema from a subgingival air-polishing nozzle used within a periodontal pocket — a sparsely reported scenario.
- Shows the complication can occur during routine subgingival biofilm removal (glycine air-powder, 5 mm pocket), not only with classic air-driven handpieces.
- Reinforces that the complication can arise in a supportive-periodontal-therapy context and at sites lacking BOP or suppuration — i.e., not limited to overtly inflamed/infected sites.

## Methodology

Single-patient case report. Glycine-based air-powder abrasion was applied subgingivally into a 5 mm deep periodontal pocket. Outcome was documented clinically (facial swelling), with hospital surveillance and antibiotic management, and recovery tracked to discharge.

## Results

- Periorbital and right-cheek swelling after the subgingival air-polishing procedure.
- Hospitalisation for surveillance; antibiotics given to control microbial spread.
- Discharged stable after 3 days with no further complications (full recovery).
