## One-line Summary

Partially-blinded randomized crossover trial (n=11 healthy adults) comparing 3% erythrosine vs 3% D&C Red No. 33 vs a mixed two-tone agent for plaque disclosure: erythrosine gave the highest plaque visibility but percentage plaque-index reduction was equivalent across all three, while D&C Red No. 33 had the least residual staining, best taste, and easiest removal — supporting it as a patient-friendly, regulatory-safe alternative.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1186/s12903-026-08786-6](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12903-026-08786-6)), this partially-blinded randomized crossover trial in 11 healthy adults (Chulalongkorn University) compared three plaque disclosing formulations — 3% erythrosine (FD&C Red No. 3), 3% D&C Red No. 33, and a mixed D&C Red No. 33 + green two-tone agent — using the Turesky-modified Quigley–Hein Plaque Index (TQHPI) as the primary outcome. The work is motivated by the U.S. FDA's ban of erythrosine in food and orally ingested drugs effective 15 January 2025 (carcinogenicity in male rats), creating a need for a regulatory-safe substitute. Erythrosine produced the highest plaque visibility (VAS) and most intense baseline staining, but after brushing/flossing the **percentage** plaque-index reduction was statistically equivalent across all three agents — choice of dye did not compromise the plaque-removal endpoint. D&C Red No. 33 produced the least residual staining (lips and oral mucosa), best taste acceptability, and easiest stain removal, and is already FDA-approved in oral-care products. The mixed two-tone formulation failed to differentiate old vs new plaque and added no clinical benefit. Clinically: D&C Red No. 33 is an acceptable, patient-friendly, regulatory-safe disclosing agent that visualizes plaque well enough to guide hygiene instruction without erythrosine's staining, taste, and safety drawbacks.

## Key Contributions

- First head-to-head RCT positioning D&C Red No. 33 as a substitute for the newly FDA-banned erythrosine in plaque disclosure.
- Demonstrates equivalent percentage plaque-index reduction across all three agents despite very different staining intensity.
- Quantifies D&C Red No. 33's acceptability advantages (least residual staining, best taste, easiest removal).
- Shows a D&C Red No. 33 + green two-tone formulation gives no maturity-differentiation benefit in well-maintained mouths.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Randomized crossover, partially blinded; each participant received all three agents in random order, one per visit, 1-week washout.
- **Participants**: n=11 healthy adults (7F/4M, mean age 22.5 ± 1.3 y, 20–25); G*Power sizing (α 5%, power 90%, primary outcome TQHPI).
- **Arms**: A = 3% erythrosine; B = 3% D&C Red No. 33; C = mixed 3% D&C Red No. 33 + green. 3% chosen (6% D&C Red No. 33 less soluble; no 3% vs 6% erythrosine pilot difference).
- **Protocol**: standardized modified Bass brushing + nightly flossing, 7-day familiarization; on test days brush AM only, chew tablet ~1 min, expectorate without rinsing.
- **Primary outcome**: TQHPI on labial anterior surfaces, pre/post brushing, calibrated examiner.
- **Secondary (self-rated)**: plaque visibility (VAS 0–10), residual staining (3-point, immediate + 1 h), ease of removal (3-point), taste (3-point).
- **Statistics**: repeated-measures ANOVA, paired t-tests, Friedman + Wilcoxon signed-rank; α = 0.05.

## Results

- **Plaque visibility (VAS)**: erythrosine > D&C Red No. 33 > mixed; erythrosine significantly higher than both.
- **Baseline TQHPI**: highest with erythrosine, lowest with mixed; erythrosine significantly differed from both.
- **After brushing/flossing**: all three showed significant plaque-index reduction; **percentage reduction had NO significant difference** among agents.
- **Residual staining**: erythrosine highest overall — significantly greater on lips (1 h) and oral mucosa (both times) vs D&C Red No. 33; D&C Red No. 33 least staining; no difference on the wash basin.
- **Ease of removal**: erythrosine most difficult to brush off (significantly worse than D&C Red No. 33); mixed intermediate.
- **Taste**: D&C Red No. 33 best, mixed intermediate, erythrosine worst (all significantly different).
- **Two-tone**: mixed D&C Red No. 33/green failed to differentiate plaque maturity (purplish-brown stain); little mature plaque present in maintained mouths.
- **Regulatory context**: FDA banned erythrosine (FD&C Red No. 3) in food/ingested drugs 15 Jan 2025; D&C Red No. 33 already FDA-approved in oral-care products.
