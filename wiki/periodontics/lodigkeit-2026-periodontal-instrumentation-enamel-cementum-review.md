## One-line Summary

Narrative review of 16 in vitro / in vitro-in vivo studies (last 15 yr): manual instrumentation causes the greatest surface irregularity and cementum removal, ultrasonic scaling gives more uniform surfaces with intermediate loss, and air polishing is the least abrasive with the highest cementum preservation (94% coronal vs 65% for hand) — though clinical relevance is uncertain (lab-based evidence).

## Summary

This narrative review synthesizes preclinical laboratory evidence (16 in vitro and in vitro/in vivo hybrid studies on human teeth, published within the last 15 years) on how periodontal instrumentation alters enamel and cementum, focusing on surface roughness and hard-tissue loss. The central clinical message: all professional debridement methods alter dental hard tissue to some degree, but they rank consistently in abrasiveness. **Hand instrumentation** causes the greatest surface irregularity and cementum removal; **ultrasonic scaling** produces more uniform surfaces with intermediate substance loss; **air polishing** is the least abrasive and best preserves cementum — but it cannot remove calculus, so it belongs to supportive/maintenance therapy rather than standalone root planing. The review also reframes root planing away from deliberate cementum removal toward preservation of biologically active cementum (which supports connective-tissue reattachment and regeneration). A key methodological caution: a lower roughness value is not automatically "better" — ultrasonic's low Rz on cementum may reflect flattening of natural peaks or actual tissue removal rather than preserved integrity. Because evidence is predominantly in vitro and heterogeneous, clinical translation remains uncertain.

## Key Contributions

- Relative abrasiveness ranking for **cementum loss**: hand instrumentation > ultrasonic > air polishing.
- Quantitative anchors — Arefnia: cementum loss air polishing ≤20 µm < ultrasonic ≥20 µm < hand ≥40 µm; enamel loss <3.0 µm (ultrasonic/hand), none for air polishing.
- In vivo cementum preservation (Bozbay 2018): air polishing 94% > ultrasonic 84% > ultrasonic+air polishing 80% > hand 65% (coronal).
- Operator-dependence emphasized: force, angulation, duration, tip wear, and power setting (high power ≈ hand instrumentation) all modulate damage.
- Hand and ultrasonic have equal calculus-removal efficacy; ultrasonic is faster with less operator fatigue, hand offers better tactile feedback.
- Cautions that "smooth surface" is inconsistently defined and that lower roughness can result from cementum removal, not preservation.

## Methodology

- Focused narrative review; PubMed search with Boolean combinations across instrumentation method × substrate (enamel/cementum) × surface-alteration keywords; English-only; 15-year window; final search January 2026.
- Included preclinical lab studies (in vitro on extracted human teeth + in vitro/in vivo hybrid). Excluded animal teeth, artificial models, orthodontic/restorative/implant procedures.
- Outcomes (roughness, morphology, hard-tissue loss) assessed via SEM, AFM, profilometry; grouped narratively by substrate, instrument, and outcome.
- No meta-analysis (heterogeneity); no formal risk-of-bias assessment.

## Results

- **Enamel**: all methods detectably alter the surface, but high enamel resistance makes changes largely clinically insignificant; stainless-steel ultrasonic tips least damaging, titanium tips and titanium hand curettes most damaging; ultrasonic morphology more destructive (enamel-prism destruction) than manual. Kruse 2024: no measurable enamel loss with curettage or air polishing.
- **Cementum**: hand instrumentation → grooves/scratches and greatest removal (can fully expose dentin); ultrasonic → more uniform surface, more preserved cementum, but not atraumatic (microcracks). Magnetostrictive least dentin exposure; piezoelectric slightly more cracks. Rotary instruments most damaging. Worn tips increase roughness and reduce calculus-removal efficiency.
- **Air polishing**: least abrasive on both substrates; cementum Rz (6.64 µm) close to untreated (7.48 µm); minimal enamel effect; but cannot remove calculus — combine with ultrasonic for both preservation and calculus removal.
- Clinical relevance uncertain — evidence is predominantly in vitro and heterogeneous in parameters and measurement.
