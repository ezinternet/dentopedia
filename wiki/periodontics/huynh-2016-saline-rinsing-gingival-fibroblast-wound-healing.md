## One-line Summary

In vitro study (PLOS ONE 2016, n = primary hGFs) demonstrating that 0.9–1.8% NaCl rinsing significantly promotes human gingival fibroblast migration and upregulates COL1, fibronectin, and FAK, with 1.8% optimal and Cl⁻ ion identified as the active mediator; no effect on oral keratinocytes.

## 한줄요약

In vitro 연구 (PLOS ONE 2016): 0.9–1.8% NaCl 세척이 치은 섬유아세포(hGF) 이동을 유의하게 촉진하고 COL1·피브로넥틴·FAK를 상향 조절하며, 1.8%가 최적; Cl⁻ 이온이 핵심 매개체이고 구강 각화세포에는 효과 없음.

## Summary

This PLOS ONE study from Chulalongkorn University (Bangkok) provided the first controlled mechanistic evidence that short-term saline rinsing stimulates gingival wound healing at the cellular level. Primary human gingival fibroblasts (hGFs) were exposed 3 times daily to 2-minute rinses with 0–7.2% NaCl. Using scratch assays, transwell migration assays, MTT viability tests, RT-PCR, and immunofluorescence, the study demonstrated a concentration-dependent, migration-specific effect: physiological to mildly hypertonic concentrations (0.9–1.8%) enhanced wound closure and fibroblast migration, while proliferation was unaffected. The active moiety was identified as the chloride ion (Cl⁻), not sodium — KCl replicated the NaCl effect, whereas phosphate-based equimolar solutions did not. Mechanistically, 1.8% NaCl upregulated type-I collagen (COL1 >3-fold), fibronectin, and FAK mRNA, and reorganized FAK and F-actin toward focal adhesion clusters and stress fibers consistent with enhanced lamellipodia formation. Human oral keratinocytes (hNOKs) were completely unresponsive, indicating fibroblast-specific chloride sensitivity. The findings provide cellular-level scientific support for the centuries-old practice of saline mouth-rinsing and define a therapeutic window: 0.9–1.8% NaCl is beneficial; ≥7.2% is inhibitory.

## Key Contributions

- **Optimal concentration window defined**: 0.9–1.8% NaCl promotes hGF migration; 7.2% inhibits it — a U-shaped (bell-shaped) dose–response.
- **Mechanism clarified**: Migration promotion is via Cl⁻ ion activating focal adhesion dynamics (FAK/F-actin), not sodium or osmolarity alone.
- **ECM production stimulated**: >3-fold COL1 mRNA, significant fibronectin and FAK mRNA upregulation at 1.8% NaCl.
- **Cell-type specificity**: Oral keratinocytes (hNOKs) are unresponsive — consistent with gingival fibroblasts' known phenotypic specialization for rapid, low-scar healing.
- **Proliferation-independent**: The entire wound-closure benefit is migration-driven, not from increased cell number.
- **No MMP/TGF-β activation**: MMP1, MMP2, and TGF-β mRNA were unchanged — saline does not trigger excessive proteolytic remodeling.

## Methodology

- **Cell type**: Primary hGFs (3rd-molar extraction, healthy volunteers 18–25 yr, passages 3–6); hNOKs as control
- **Rinsing protocol**: 2 min rinse 3×/day in NaCl-supplemented medium; 48 h (scratch/MTT), 24 h (transwell)
- **Concentrations**: 0, 0.9, 1.8, 3.6, 7.2% NaCl
- **Ion controls**: 2.9% KCl, 10% NaH₂PO₄, 9.7% KH₂PO₄ (equimolar to 0.121 M Na⁺ / 0.187 M Cl⁻)
- **Assays**: Scratch assay (ImageJ), transwell migration (8-μm membrane, crystal violet), MTT, RT-PCR (COL1, Fn, FAK, GAPDH), immunofluorescence (FAK + F-actin + DAPI)
- **Statistics**: One-way ANOVA (Dunnett T3 / Tukey HSD), independent t-test; α = 0.05; SPSS v.21

## Results

| NaCl % | Scratch closure | Transwell migration | Proliferation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (control) | baseline | baseline | baseline |
| 0.9% | ↑ (p<0.05) | ↑ (p<0.05) | NS |
| 1.8% | ↑↑ (strongest, p<0.01) | ↑↑ (p<0.01) | NS |
| 3.6% | not significant in scratch | ↑ (p<0.05) | NS |
| 7.2% | ↓↓ (inhibited, p<0.001) | NS | NS |

**ECM/cytoskeletal genes at 1.8% NaCl vs. 0%**: COL1 >3× ↑, Fn slight ↑ (p<0.05), FAK marked ↑; TGF-β / MMP1 / MMP2 unchanged.

**Ion specificity**: KCl = NaCl effect (migration ↑); NaH₂PO₄ and KH₂PO₄ = no effect → **Cl⁻ is the active ion**.

**hNOKs**: No migration or viability change at any NaCl concentration tested.

**FAK/F-actin**: 1.8% NaCl → FAK redistribution to peri-nuclear focal clusters + increased F-actin stress fibers → mechanistic link to lamellipodia-driven migration.
