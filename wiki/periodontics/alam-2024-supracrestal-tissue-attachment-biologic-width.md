## Summary

Supracrestal tissue attachment (SCTA) is the 2017-World-Workshop terminology for the older "biologic width" — the physiologic unit of junctional epithelium plus connective tissue attachment surrounding each tooth, with an ideal dimension of roughly 1 mm connective tissue attachment + 1 mm junctional epithelium + 1 mm sulcus. Its preservation is critical to periodontal health when placing restoration margins; violation (typically from deeply placed subgingival margins for esthetics) produces gingival inflammation, microbiota shift, increased plaque, recession, deepened pockets and crestal bone loss. The paper presents a 47-year-old female with discomfort, inflammation and BOP at teeth 35/36 after recent prosthetic crowns; bone sounding showed a margin-to-crest distance <2 mm and the radiograph showed crestal bone loss — diagnostic of SCTA violation. Surgical correction (full-thickness flap 34–36, osteotomy/osteoplasty to re-create positive architecture and re-establish the attachment) resolved the problem. The literature-review tables organize correction strategies — gingivectomy, apically positioned flap with/without osteotomy, surgical and orthodontic extrusion, intra-alveolar transplantation — against keratinized-tissue amount and the bone-level relationship to the CEJ.

## Key Contributions

- Modern SCTA terminology and ideal dimension (1+1+1 mm); violation threshold margin-to-crest <2 mm on bone sounding.
- Worked clinical pathway: identify → evaluate → correct (surgical crown lengthening for this subgingival-crown case).
- Decision tables for surgical vs conservative/ultraconservative SCTA correction.

## Methodology

- Single case report + narrative literature review (lowest experimental evidence tier; no comparison group, no long-term follow-up).
- Diagnosis by bone sounding + periapical radiograph; treatment by envelope flap + osteotomy/osteoplasty, CHX 0.2%, uneventful healing.
