In vitro methods study — an organotypic reconstructed human gingiva model colonized with saliva-derived commensal vs pathogenic multispecies biofilms produces distinct host inflammatory responses, providing a physiologically relevant antimicrobial screening platform.

## Summary
This biological methods paper develops an organotypic human gingiva model — reconstructed epithelium over a fibroblast-populated lamina propria — and colonizes it with saliva-derived multispecies biofilms representing commensal (health-associated) versus pathogenic (disease-associated) communities. The two biofilm types elicit distinct host inflammatory responses, demonstrating that a 3D tissue-equivalent with realistic multispecies biofilms captures host-microbiome interactions better than single-species or monolayer systems. Its value is as a screening platform for antimicrobials and host-modulating agents relevant to gingivitis/periodontitis pathogenesis — a mechanistic substrate beneath clinical biofilm-management evidence.

## Key Contributions
- Organotypic gingiva host-microbiome model with saliva-derived multispecies biofilms.
- Distinct host responses to commensal vs pathogenic biofilms.
- Reusable antimicrobial/drug-discovery screening tool.

## Methodology
In vitro reconstructed human gingiva on collagen hydrogel; saliva-derived commensal and pathogenic biofilms co-cultured; host inflammatory readouts compared.

## Results
- Pathogenic biofilms → stronger/distinct inflammatory response vs commensal.
- More physiologic than single-species models.
