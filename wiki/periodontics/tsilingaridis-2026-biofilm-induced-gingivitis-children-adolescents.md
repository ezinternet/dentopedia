## One-line Summary

EFP/EAPD-commissioned systematic review (269 studies, 47 countries; 22 large low-bias studies pooled) — dental biofilm-induced gingivitis affects ~52% of children/adolescents (95% CI 42–62%, I²>99%; 34% CPI to 80% bleeding-index), rises with age/puberty, is socioeconomically patterned, and is best controlled by supervised fluoride toothbrushing plus school/caregiver education.

## Summary

This 2026 *Journal of Clinical Periodontology* systematic review was commissioned by the European Federation of Periodontology (EFP) and the European Academy of Paediatric Dentistry (EAPD) for their March 2025 focused workshop on gingival and periodontal diseases in children and adolescents. It addresses, for the systemically-healthy under-18 population, the occurrence, determinants, diagnosis, management and oral health–related quality-of-life (OHRQoL) impact of gingival diseases — applying the 2017 World Workshop classification framework.

After screening 33,180 records down to **269 included studies**, the review found that **every eligible study addressed only dental biofilm–induced gingivitis** — no studies met inclusion criteria for any non-biofilm gingival condition, and none addressed gingivitis *diagnosis*. The headline finding is that biofilm-induced gingivitis is **common (pooled ~52%)** in this age group, rises with age and pubertal change, follows a socioeconomic gradient, and is best managed by **supervised toothbrushing with fluoridated toothpaste plus school-/caregiver-based oral health education**. Flossing added no benefit over brushing alone; chlorhexidine (CHX) is an adjunct for selected cases only.

A noted gap: **none of the 269 studies used the 2017 World Workshop gingivitis case definition**, so the pediatric baseline for that current definition remains unmeasured.

## Key Contributions

- First EFP/EAPD-commissioned synthesis of biofilm-induced gingivitis across the whole under-18 population, framed by the 2017 World Workshop classification.
- Pooled occurrence estimate with explicit decomposition of how the diagnostic method drives the prevalence figure.
- Maps determinants (socioeconomic, behavioural, pubertal/biological, malocclusion) and ranks management strategies by evidence.
- Documents three explicit evidence voids: gingivitis diagnosis, non-biofilm gingival diseases in healthy children, and tobacco exposure in under-18s.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review (PRISMA + ENTREQ; OSF-registered) with random-effects proportion meta-analysis for occurrence.
- **Databases**: PubMed Central, Scopus, EMBASE, LILACS, to January 2025.
- **Population**: Children/adolescents < 18 years, without known systemic disorder.
- **Screening funnel**: 33,180 records → 20,228 de-duplicated → 2264 (title) → 433 (abstract/full text) → **269 included** (99 occurrence, 63 determinants, 89 management, 18 OHRQoL, 0 diagnosis).
- **Occurrence pooling**: excluded studies with < 1000 participants (n = 64) and high risk of bias (n = 38) → **22 studies pooled** (Stata 19 `meta`/`esize proportion`).
- **Risk of bias**: RoB 2.0 (RCTs), ROBINS-I (non-randomised), JBI checklists (observational); GRADE for overall strength; post hoc exclusion of high-RoB studies.

## Results

**Occurrence**
- Pooled gingivitis occurrence **52% (95% CI 42–62%)**, heterogeneity **I² > 99%**.
- By diagnostic method: **34% (CPI-based) to 80% (bleeding index)** — method, not biology, dominates the spread.
- Largest occurrence study: **n = 118,601** (12–15-yr Chinese schoolchildren, WHO criteria) → **47% prevalence** (Wang et al.).
- Studies spanned **47 countries**, mostly school-based sampling.
- **None** of the included studies used the 2017 World Workshop gingivitis criteria.

**Determinants** (63 studies, 61 cross-sectional → associations only)
- **Age**: occurrence rises with age through childhood/early adolescence (≥ 9 concordant studies) — attributed to pubertal sulcular microflora shifts and elevated sex steroid hormones amplifying the host inflammatory response to plaque.
- **Gender**: boys > girls in most studies (3 found no difference, 1 found girls higher).
- **Socioeconomic**: lower parental education, public-school attendance, immigrant/minority status positively associated; mixed for income/occupation.
- **Behaviour**: ≥ twice-daily and supervised brushing, fluoride toothpaste, frequent flossing (self-reported), and regular dental visits all associated with better gingival health.
- **Microbiota/malocclusion**: pubertal microbiota changes and malocclusions (crowding, overjet, open bite, crossbite) positively associated.

**Management** (89 studies; 41 excluded for high RoB)
- **Supervised toothbrushing + fluoridated toothpaste** = cornerstone; school-/caregiver-based oral health education reduces inflammation in already-affected children.
- **Flossing**: a 4-week school RCT (Rich et al.) found supervised flossing added to brushing gave **no improvement** in gingivitis or plaque vs brushing alone.
- **Electric vs manual toothbrush**: inconclusive (split results, methodological limits).
- **Mouthwashes**: 0.1–0.2% CHX and herbal formulations reduce GI; CHX generally outperformed honey-based at 3 months; CHX is adjunctive only.
- **Professional cleaning**: effective (every 2 weeks/monthly) but logistically unrealistic relative to gingivitis prevalence.

**OHRQoL** (18 studies, 16 cross-sectional; C-OIDP most common instrument)
- Gingival inflammation mostly associated with **worse OHRQoL**, though some non-significant.
- Largest study **n = 89,582** (Wu et al., China, 12–15 yr) — teeth with gingival bleeding significantly associated with worse C-OIDP scores.
- Associations with relaxing/smiling/social contact were concentrated in younger (12-yr) children, not 15-yr-olds.
