## Summary

The biologic width (BW), per Cohen, is the supracrestal tissue between the base of the gingival crevice and the alveolar crest — junctional epithelium plus connective tissue attachment — and its preservation is central to periodontal health when placing restoration margins. The widely cited textbook dimensions (BW 2.04 mm, sulcus depth 0.69 mm) come from Gargiulo et al.'s histologic cadaver work. This clinical study measured BW directly in living patients: 42 healthy adults, 504 sites on 126 teeth, by bone sounding under anesthesia (teeth already anesthetized for adjacent procedures). Clinical BW was computed as (gingival-margin-to-bone-crest) minus sulcus depth at four sites per tooth. The measured clinical BW (1.13 mm) was significantly *smaller* than Gargiulo's histologic value, and clinical sulcus depth (1.96 mm) significantly *larger* than the histologic 0.69 mm. The authors argue against using a single "magic number" for BW as a treatment objective and recommend establishing a patient/site-specific margin-to-crest distance when restoring subgingivally fractured or carious teeth, leading to more stable periodontal tissues — relevant when planning crown lengthening.

## Key Contributions

- Direct clinical (bone-sounding) measurement of biologic width vs the classic histologic standard.
- Clinical BW 1.13 mm (< 2.04 mm) and clinical SD 1.96 mm (> 0.69 mm), both significant (p<0.05).
- Recommends site-specific margin-to-crest planning over a fixed BW value.

## Methodology

- Cross-sectional clinical measurement; 42 healthy adults (mean 27 y), full-mouth BOP and plaque <20%.
- Two periodontists; 4 sites/tooth; clinical BW = (GM→crest) − SD; SPSS 20.0 (Friedman ANOVA, t-tests).
- Limitations: single-center pilot, Lebanese population, bone-sounding error, subtraction propagates two measurement errors, no longitudinal outcome.
