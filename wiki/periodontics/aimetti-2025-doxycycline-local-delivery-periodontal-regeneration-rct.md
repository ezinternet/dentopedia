## One-line Summary

Triple-blind RCT (n=60, stage III/IV periodontitis) found that locally delivered doxycycline 2 weeks before MIST with EMD + xenograft (BOOST approach) significantly improved CAL gain (4.1 vs. 3.2 mm, p=0.019), composite success (83.3% vs. 46.7%, NNT=2.73), and radiographic bone fill (3.5 vs. 2.7 mm) at 1 year.

## Summary

This triple-blind parallel-arm RCT from the University of Turin investigated the BOOST (BiO-Optimizing Site Targeted) concept: the subgingival application of locally delivered doxycycline hyclate gel (Ligosan) 2 weeks before minimally invasive periodontal regeneration surgery as a strategy to optimize the inflammatory microenvironment at the surgical site. The underlying rationale is that persistent bleeding on probing (BoP+) at sites intended for regenerative surgery — a recognized risk factor for post-surgical complications and suboptimal outcomes — reflects a local immuno-inflammatory burden that standard step 1-2 therapy alone fails to fully resolve. Doxycycline's dual antimicrobial and host-modulatory properties (inhibition of matrix metalloproteinases via zinc-ion binding, reduction of IL-1β and IL-6 in gingival crevicular fluid, stimulation of osteoblast differentiation) make it a pharmacologically rational site-preparation agent.

Sixty non-smoking stage III/IV periodontitis patients with one residual site meeting strict criteria (PPD ≥ 6 mm, BoP+, intrabony defect ≥ 3 mm, no furcation involvement, FMPS/FMBS < 15%) were randomized 1:1 to receive either subgingival debridement plus BOOST (doxycycline gel) or debridement alone (simulated procedure). Two weeks later, all sites underwent MIST with a standardized regenerative protocol: EDTA root conditioning, enamel matrix derivatives (Emdogain), and bone xenograft (Bio-Oss). The triple-blind design with concealed allocation and blinded clinical/radiographic examiners and blinded patients represents the highest standard for a periodontal surgical RCT.

The BOOST approach produced a cascade of benefits: at the time of surgery (T1), BoP had fallen from 100% to only 30% in the BOOST group versus 66.7% in controls (p<0.001), indicating meaningful inflammation reduction in the 2-week window. This translated to superior early wound healing (EHI 1.5 vs. 2.2, p=0.041), reflecting better interproximal soft tissue quality at 2 weeks post-operatively. By 12 months, the BOOST group showed significantly greater CAL gain (4.1 ± 1.9 vs. 3.2 ± 2.0 mm, p=0.019), PPD reduction (4.4 ± 1.8 vs. 3.6 ± 1.9 mm, p=0.040), pocket closure (83.3% vs. 60.0%, p=0.045; NNT=4.29), composite outcome success (83.3% vs. 46.7%, p=0.006; NNT=2.73), and radiographic bone fill (3.5 ± 1.2 vs. 2.7 ± 1.3 mm, p=0.027). The defect angle change (9.7° vs. 3.6°, p<0.001) further confirms more complete bone defect resolution.

Multivariate regression identified four independent predictors of CAL gain: treatment allocation (BOOST: β=1.12), defect morphology (contenitive/more walls: β=0.98), early wound healing index (EHI 1-2 vs. 3-5: β=0.93), and baseline PPD (β=0.71, all p<0.01). The EHI mediation suggests the benefit of BOOST is at least partly mediated through improved early wound healing — a plausible biological mechanism linking pre-surgical inflammation control to long-term regenerative outcomes. With NNT=2.73 for the composite outcome, the clinical utility is exceptional: one must treat only 2-3 patients with BOOST to gain one additional treatment success compared to standard care.

## Key Contributions

1. Established the BOOST concept as an evidence-based pre-surgical strategy: locally delivered doxycycline 2 weeks before MIST significantly improves periodontal regeneration outcomes beyond the standard of care.
2. Demonstrated a mechanistic chain: BOOST → BoP reduction → better EHI → better 12-month CAL/PPD/bone fill outcomes, with EHI identified as an independent predictor of CAL gain in multivariate analysis.
3. Achieved a clinically impactful NNT of 2.73 for composite outcome success — one of the more favorable NNTs in periodontal regeneration literature.
4. Repositioned local antimicrobials from step-2 adjunct role (modest and diminishing long-term benefit) to a targeted pre-surgical inflammation control tool with anti-collagenolytic host-modulatory rationale.
5. Provided an all-or-none 100% completion rate (60/60) over 1 year, strengthening the internal validity of the trial.
6. Utilized stringent composite outcomes (COM, pocket closure) rather than mean values alone, making clinical decision-making easier and comparing favorably to emerging periodontal outcome standards.

## Methodology

- **Design:** Triple-blind, parallel-arm RCT (1:1 allocation, permuted blocks)
- **Population:** n=60, stage III/IV periodontitis (EFP 2018 classification), non-smokers, FMPS/FMBS < 15%, PPD ≥ 6 mm + BoP+ + intrabony component ≥ 3 mm (one study tooth per patient)
- **Exclusion:** Smokers, diabetes/immune deficiency, furcation involvement, prior surgery at site, tetracycline allergy, recent antimicrobials
- **Test intervention:** Subgingival debridement + single subgingival DOX gel administration (Ligosan, Kulzer, Germany) 2 weeks before surgery
- **Control intervention:** Subgingival debridement alone (DOX procedure simulated; concealed allocation)
- **Surgery (both):** MIST + EDTA + EMD (Emdogain) + xenograft (Bio-Oss) + Gore-tex suture; performed by postgraduate residents under experienced supervision
- **Follow-up:** T0 (baseline, 14 days pre-surgery), T1 (day of surgery), T2 (2 weeks post-op, EHI), T3 (12 months, all primary/secondary outcomes)
- **Primary outcome:** CAL gain at 12 months
- **Calibration:** ICC 0.92 (CAL), 0.95 (PPD), 0.86 (radiographic)
- **Statistical analysis:** Shapiro-Wilk, Mann-Whitney with Bonferroni, Friedman/Dunn, chi-squared/Fisher, multivariate linear regression (SPSS v28)
