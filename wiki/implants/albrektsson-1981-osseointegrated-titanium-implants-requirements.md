## One-line Summary

Landmark 1981 clinical/ultrastructural paper (n=38 stable titanium screws retrieved from 18 patients; ~3000-implant, 400-patient base) that defined osseointegration as direct bone-to-implant contact and codified the **six prerequisites** for achieving it — implant material, design, finish, bone status, surgical technique, and loading conditions.

## Summary

This is the paper that turned **골유착 (Osseointegration)** from a contested claim into a defined, reproducible principle. Working from ~3000 titanium implants placed at Gothenburg since 1965 and 400 patients followed up to 15 years, Albrektsson and Brånemark retrieved 38 stable, radiographically integrated screws (removed for incidental, non-failure reasons) from 18 patients and analyzed the bone-implant interface by X-ray, SEM, TEM, and histology.

They defined osseointegration as a **direct contact between living bone and implant** with no interposed fibrous layer — and provided the **first TEM of an intact human bone-implant interface** (a jaw fixture sectioned at 30 months without separating bone from metal), showing direct contact down to the electron-microscopic level and suggesting possible direct chemical bonding between bone and the titanium oxide layer. Titanium's instantaneously-formed stable oxide (~100 Å) means no bare metal touches tissue, so the authors argued titanium "may be regarded as a ceramic, not as a metal."

The lasting legacy is the **six prerequisites for osseointegration**, the conceptual scaffold under essentially every modern implant-stability, surface, and loading topic.

## Key Contributions

- **Definition of osseointegration**: direct living-bone-to-implant contact, no interposed connective tissue — the only one of four interface types (Figure 1) whose long-term fate is predictable and that tolerates heavy load.
- **First TEM of an intact human bone-implant interface**, extending the "direct contact" finding from light- to electron-microscopic level.
- **The six prerequisites** (see Results) — the paper's enduring framework.
- **Titanium-as-ceramic rationale**: the stable ~100 Å oxide layer (TiO/TiO₂/Ti₂O₃/Ti₃O₄) explains corrosion resistance and bonding capacity; titanium scored highest (least corrosive) on Anodic Back e.m.f. (>5× vitallium, >7× stainless steel).
- **Biological seal**: soft tissue adheres to the transmucosal/transcutaneous implant, blocking microorganism infiltration; no adverse effects through gingiva/skin over loading times up to 90 months.

## Methodology

- **Clinical base**: ~3000 threaded cylindrical titanium implants since 1965 (2895 in jaws; 124 in tibial/temporal/iliac bone). Requirements drawn from 400 patients followed up to 15 years.
- **Retrieved sample (Table I)**: 38 stable, integrated, surgeon-judged-stable screws from 18 patients — Group I (10 jaw fixtures, 30–90 months), Group II (22 tibial + 2 iliac screws, ~5–6 months), Group III (temporal-bone screws, 12–18 months). All removed for reasons unrelated to integration failure.
- **Surgery (Figure 3)**: graded drills → counter-sink → tap under generous saline irrigation; cover screw; defined unloaded healing of several months; then abutment.
- **Analysis**: X-ray, SEM, TEM, histology of the interface zone; EDAX to confirm titanium deposits. Implants trephined out under saline with an intact, inseparable bone cover.

## Results

**The six prerequisites for osseointegration**:

| # | Factor | Core requirement |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Implant material** | Tissue-tolerant, load-bearing, corrosion-resistant; **non-alloyed (unalloyed) titanium** preferred — the only metal shown to keep direct bone contact in man >10 years. |
| 2 | **Implant design** | Exact bone-implant fit; **cylindrical, threaded** geometry for maximal contact and minimal early micromovement; threads ↑ interaction surface area. |
| 3 | **Implant finish** | Surface effect hard to quantify; very smooth surfaces avoided, "somewhat rougher" finishes preferred (empirical only). Anodization/collagenization/bioglass flagged as future routes. |
| 4 | **Status of the bone** | Healthy bone essential; cortical vs. cancellous bed matters less (spongy bone "corticalizes" around metal); severe resorption may need a (preformed) graft. |
| 5 | **Surgical technique** | "Minimal tissue violence" — constant cooling, adequate drill geometry/speed, careful tapping — to limit thermal/surgical trauma. |
| 6 | **Implant loading conditions** | Osseointegration occurs **only in perfectly stable situations**. Necrotic border zone must remodel first; **unloaded healing 3–4 months in man** (≈6 weeks in rabbits). Immediate loading and rigid coupling to teeth (micromovement) cause the fibrous sheath that prevents integration. |

**Outcome benchmarks cited** (Adell et al. 1981, 400 patients / 2775 implants):
- 5-year survival of functioning jaw bridges ≈ **100% lower jaw, 95% upper jaw**.
- Individual-fixture survival **91% lower, 82% upper**.
- Loss of anchorage over 5 years **< 10%** across the complete material.
- No titanium wear products despite 6–90 months in situ; no microorganism infiltration; biological seal confirmed.

**Conclusions**: osseointegration can be permanently achieved in man; mechanism of bone-titanium bonding still incompletely understood; direct contact now shown at the electron-microscopic level; to guarantee it, use threaded unalloyed titanium of defined finish/geometry, delicate surgery, and ≥3–4 months unloaded healing.
