## One-line Summary
Systematic review with qualitative synthesis (34 studies: 9 bacterial/biofilm + 13 soft-tissue + 13 clinical) reframing UV photofunctionalization as a **surface-agnostic, topography-preserving "positive-sum" reactivation technology** — most consistently reducing early bacterial attachment/biofilm and enhancing fibroblast/epithelial sealing on titanium (emerging on zirconia), with the strongest clinical signal being accelerated implant stability development.

## Summary
**Abstract-only — full text not retrieved.** This is the newest (2026) and broadest UV photofunctionalization (UV-PF) systematic review, from the Ogawa group (same lineage as our Ogawa 3D-theory and Park tri-interface pages). It evaluates whether UV-PF of titanium and zirconia confers clinically relevant advantages across three peri-implant-health domains — (1) bacterial attachment/biofilm, (2) soft-tissue sealing, and (3) human clinical outcomes — and synthesizes 34 articles qualitatively (no meta-analysis).

Its central reframing: UV-PF is a **surface-agnostic physicochemical reactivation technology**. By removing storage-acquired hydrocarbons ("biological aging") and restoring a high-energy superhydrophilic state, it improves interfacial biology *without altering surface topography*. This sidesteps the usual surface-design trade-off (micro-roughness accelerates bone but retains plaque; smooth transmucosal surfaces deter bacteria but with uncertain soft-tissue integration), making UV-PF an **interface-first, positive-sum** strategy. Clinically it implies clinicians could pick **zone-specific topographies** — smooth transmucosal + rough endosteal — and let UV-PF maximize biology in each zone.

## Key Contributions
- Establishes the **surface-agnostic / topography-preserving** concept for UV-PF as the organizing thesis.
- First UV-PF SR to jointly span bacterial/biofilm + soft-tissue + clinical domains across **both titanium and zirconia**.
- Raises the evidence tier from narrative review (Park 2025) to **systematic review** while extending material scope to zirconia.
- Translates the concept into a **zone-specific topography** clinical strategy.

## Methodology
- **Systematic review with qualitative synthesis** (`confidence: sr`); no meta-analysis (heterogeneity precluded pooling).
- **34 articles** selected after search/screening/full-text evaluation: **9 bacterial/biofilm**, **13 soft-tissue** (1 overlapping with bacterial), **13 clinical**.
- Synthesis explicitly tracked **protocol heterogeneity**: UV wavelength band, exposure duration, device configuration, material/surface type.
- Materials: titanium and zirconia.

## Results
*(abstract-level; no pooled effect estimates.)*

| Domain | n | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Bacterial / biofilm | 9 | **Most consistent** finding: reduced early bacterial attachment and/or early biofilm across several Ti topographies → early anti-adhesive, biofilm-suppressive phenotype. |
| Soft-tissue sealing | 13 (1 shared) | Generally enhanced fibroblast/epithelial attachment, spreading, sealing-relevant behaviors on Ti **and** zirconia; optimal underlying topography **unresolved**. |
| Human clinical | 13 | Most consistent signal = **accelerated/enhanced implant stability development**; selected studies suggested favorable peri-implant soft-tissue and/or crestal bone trends, but outcomes **variable**. |

- Concept evidence **strongest for titanium**; zirconia support **emerging** (mainly soft-tissue/interface models).
- Clinical evidence most strongly supports **accelerated osseointegration/stability** rather than the harder peri-implant-health endpoints; limited by protocol/surface/endpoint/follow-up heterogeneity.
- Future need: longitudinal studies with **standardized peri-implant health, microbiologic, and mucosal inflammatory endpoints**.
