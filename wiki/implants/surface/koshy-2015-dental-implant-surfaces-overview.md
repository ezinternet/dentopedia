## Summary

Integration of dental implants depends heavily on surface characteristics that dynamically interact with hard and soft tissues. This review organizes titanium implant surface modification into three major classes:

1. **Additive methods** — add material to increase surface area and create macrotopography: titanium plasma spray (TPS, Ra ~7 µm), hydroxyapatite (HA) coating, calcium phosphate coating, magnetron sputter coating.
2. **Subtractive methods** — remove material to alter microtopography: sandblasting/grit blasting (Al₂O₃, TiO₂, ZrO₂), acid etching (H₂SO₄/HCl), combined SLA (sandblasted large-grit acid-etched, the most widely studied, Sa ~1.5–2 µm), and anodization (electrochemical TiO₂ micropores 0.5–10 µm incorporating Ca/P).
3. **Material modification** — no net material addition/removal: electron beam, laser treatment, ion implantation.

A multilevel roughness framework guides expectations:
- **Macrotopography (>10 µm)**: mechanical interlocking; threaded implants (V-thread, buttress, reverse buttress) are the dominant design.
- **Microtopography (1–10 µm)**: secondary osseointegration via cell adhesion, BIC enhancement. Optimal Sa 1–2 µm balances BIC improvement against peri-implantitis risk.
- **Nanotopography (<1 µm)**: governs protein adsorption, fibronectin conformation, and early osteoblast signaling; achieved by discrete crystalline deposition (DCD), alkali treatment, sol-gel, or nanoscale laser ablation.

Bioactive coatings (BMP-2, RGD peptides, fibronectin) represent the next frontier, using the implant surface as a vehicle for local growth-factor delivery.

Clinical rule: transmucosal collar zone benefits from a smooth surface to minimize plaque retention; short implants in poor-quality bone benefit from maximally rough subosseous surfaces.

## Key Contributions

- Unified three-class taxonomy (additive / subtractive / material-modification) for implant surface methods.
- Identifies Sa 1–2 µm as the clinically optimal roughness range.
- Flags HA delamination as a long-term concern despite excellent early bone apposition.
- Highlights nanotopography as the emerging tier beyond micro-roughness.
- Practical clinical guidance: rough ↔ poor bone quality; smooth ↔ transmucosal zone.

## Methodology

Narrative literature review; no systematic search/PRISMA protocol. Synthesizes bench, animal, and clinical evidence from foundational authors (Wennerberg, Albrektsson, Brånemark, Cooper, Linder). Roughness classification follows Wennerberg's Sa/Ra framework.

## Results

| Method | Roughness | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| Machined/smooth | Ra <0.5 µm | Lower BIC; adequate for transmucosal collar |
| TPS | Ra ~7 µm | High early BIC but excessive roughness → peri-implantitis risk |
| SLA | Sa ~1.5–2 µm | Gold-standard subtractive; highest BIC/removal torque data |
| HA coating | Variable | Early bone apposition ↑; delamination risk long-term |
| Anodization | 0.5–10 µm pores | Incorporates Ca/P; TiUnite (Nobel), Friatec examples |
| Nanosurface | <1 µm | Improved protein adsorption and fibronectin conformation → better early cell adhesion |
- Rough surfaces: higher removal torque and BIC (Wennerberg 1997, Wilke 1990, Johansson 1990)
- Moderate roughness (Sa 1–2 µm) balances BIC gain vs. peri-implantitis risk
