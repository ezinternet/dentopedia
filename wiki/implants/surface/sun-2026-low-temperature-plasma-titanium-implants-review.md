## 한줄요약
Narrative review (Biomaterials Advances 2026): low-temperature plasma (LTP) alters titanium surface chemistry without affecting bulk properties — increases wettability, introduces reactive functional groups, reduces initial bacterial adhesion, and enhances osteoblast adhesion/proliferation/differentiation; plasma-aging and clinical-translation barriers remain unresolved.

## Summary
Review of low-temperature plasma (LTP) as a non-thermal surface activation strategy for titanium dental implants. LTP changes only the outermost surface chemistry — introducing reactive functional groups and increasing wettability — while leaving bulk mechanical properties intact. In vitro and animal evidence supports improved osteoblast response and reduced bacterial colonization; clinical translation is constrained by plasma aging (signal decay with storage) and lack of standardized parameters.

## Key Contributions
- Consolidates mechanism: surface chemistry modulation → protein adsorption → cellular/bacterial response
- Integrates antibacterial and osteogenic outcomes as dual functionalities
- Critically discusses plasma aging and barriers to clinical translation

## Methodology
- Narrative review of in vitro, in vivo (animal), and conceptual literature
- No SR methodology — selective synthesis

## Results (qualitative)
- Surface chemistry: introduction of reactive groups, increased wettability
- Protein adsorption at implant interface modulated
- In vitro: enhanced osteoblast adhesion, proliferation, osteogenic differentiation
- In vivo: peri-implant immune-inflammatory modulation, biofilm inhibition
- Dual functionality (antibacterial + osteogenic) feasible via plasma engineering

## Limitations
- Narrative — selection bias and no quantitative pooling
- Parameter heterogeneity (plasma source, exposure time, gas mix)
- Few human RCTs
- Plasma aging mitigation strategies not standardized
