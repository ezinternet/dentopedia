## Summary
Abraham (2014) is a historical-perspective narrative review tracing dental implants across roughly 4,500 years. It begins with ancient civilizations using gold ligature wire (Egyptians, ~2500 BC), gold bands and ox-bone replacements (Etruscans, ~500 BC), shell implants in Mayan mandibles (~600 AD, with bone formation visible on 1970s radiographs), and stone implants in early Honduran culture (~800 AD). It then moves to early modern figures — John Hunter's 1700s tooth-in-rooster-comb experiment, Maggiolo's 1809 gold tube, Greenfield's 1913 iridio-platinum lattice, the Strock brothers' 1930s Vitallium screws, and Formiggini's 1940s spiral design — and finally to Brånemark's titanium osseointegration paradigm.

The second half of the review enumerates modern surface treatments and coatings: machined, sand-blasted, acid-etched, sand-blasted + acid-etched (SLA), hydroxyapatite (HA), fluoride, and statin-loaded surfaces. It frames the contemporary research goal as reducing healing time to osseointegration. As of 2014, the author flags laminin as the current frontier.

This is a teaching/orientation paper, not an evidence-based review. It is useful as a chronological anchor in lectures or trainee notes; it should not be cited to support any specific clinical decision about surface choice, and it predates much of the modern nanoscale-topography and UV photofunctionalization literature.

## Key Contributions
- Compact, lecture-ready chronology of dental implant history from Egyptians through Brånemark.
- Names the key figures and their contributions: Hunter, Maggiolo, Greenfield, Strock (Alvin & Moses), Formiggini ("Father of Modern Implantology"), Perron Andres, Linkow, Brånemark.
- Identifies the Strock brothers as first successful endosteal implant placement, using Vitallium (chromium-cobalt alloy).
- Catalogs modern surface modifications: HA, composites, carbon, glass, ceramic, titanium oxide; treatments: sand-blasting, oxidation, fluoridation, acid-etching, statin coatings.
- Useful as the historical-context slide for any lecture on implant surface science.

## Methodology
Narrative review with no systematic search, no inclusion criteria, no quality appraisal. The structure is biographical and chronological, organized around named clinicians and inventors rather than around thematic evidence categories.

Single author, Department of Periodontics at Baylor College of Dentistry. No conflicts of interest declared. Published in The Open Dentistry Journal (open-access journal).

## Results
Not applicable — this is a narrative review with no quantitative outcomes. Useful factual claims:
- Vitallium = chromium-cobalt alloy.
- Formiggini's spiral implant (1940s) constructed from stainless steel wire wound on itself.
- 2014-era surface "frontier" was laminin coating.
- HA, fluoride, and statin coatings are mentioned but not evaluated for comparative efficacy.

## Critical Reading / Living-document Notes
- **Do not** cite this paper as evidence for the superiority of any surface treatment. It does not present comparative data.
- The historical timeline contains some folkloric claims (Hunter's rooster-comb tooth) that should be presented as such in didactic settings.
- The statin and laminin coating claims are now outdated; modern surface research is dominated by SLA-derivatives (SLActive), calcium-modified (CA), UV-photofunctionalized, and nanoscale topography surfaces — none of which are covered here in depth.
- For an evidence-based picture of how surface affects osseointegration, pair with Coelho & Jimbo 2014, Wennerberg & Albrektsson reviews, and contemporary RCTs comparing SLActive vs SLA.
- For trainee orientation in our clinic: this paper is fine for the "where did implant dentistry come from" lecture slide but should not be used for "which surface should I select" decisions.
