## One-line Summary

FEA + in vitro strain gauge (customized angled abutments 20–33° based on CBCT incisor inclination vs standard 15°): customized higher-angle abutments → higher bone stress under axial loading; standard 15° → higher stress under oblique loading; load direction determines the winner.

## Summary

This study addresses a very specific clinical scenario: when placing an immediate implant in the anterior maxilla, the tooth was inclined at a patient-specific angle — what abutment angle should correct the crown direction?

The authors used CBCT measurements of actual incisor inclination across age groups (20°–33°) to design customized abutments, then compared them to the standard 15° commercial abutment via FEA and bench experiments.

**The paradox**: Matching the crown to the natural tooth axis using a higher-angle abutment **increases** bone stress under axial loading, but **decreases** it under oblique loading. Since real mastication involves mostly oblique forces, customized abutments may actually be more favorable for long-term bone health — but axial loading (clenching, parafunctional) becomes riskier.

## Key Contributions

- First study designing abutments from patient-specific CBCT incisor inclination (not from standardized idealized models)
- Demonstrated load-direction dependency of abutment angle effect
- Provided in vitro strain gauge validation (not just FEA)
- Age-group analysis: inclination varies across patient ages (20°–33°)

## Methodology

- CBCT measurement of 4 age-group average incisor inclinations: 21.9°, 24.15°, 20.22°, 33°
- 3D maxillary bone models from individual patient CBCTs
- FEA: 100 N axial + 50 N oblique loading on each model
- In vitro: strain gauge experiment — 21.9° customized vs 15° standard; both axial and oblique
- Control: standard commercial 15° angled abutment

## Results

**Under axial loading**:
- Customized higher-angle abutments (20°–33°) → ELEVATED stress on implant and surrounding bone vs 15° control
- Mechanism: more angulation = larger bending moment at implant neck under vertical force

**Under oblique loading**:
- Standard 15° abutments → HIGHER stress than customized abutments
- Customized abutments are more aligned with the oblique force vector → better load transfer

**In vitro (21.9° vs 15°)**:
- No statistically significant difference in bone strain under axial loading
- Standard 15° → significantly greater bone strain under oblique loading

## Clinical Implication — Adjacent Tilted Tooth

This paper directly addresses the user's clinical question (what to do when the adjacent tooth is tilted):

1. **Place the implant perpendicular to the occlusal plane** (axial loading principle — not parallel to the tilted tooth)
2. **Correct crown direction with an angled abutment** matched to the tooth inclination angle
3. **Under real oblique masticatory forces**, the custom-angle abutment actually reduces bone stress vs. a lower-angle abutment that misdirects the crown
4. **But**: if patient has parafunction (bruxism, heavy clenching), the higher-angle abutment creates more stress under those axial forces — manage occlusion carefully
5. **Abutment type**: prefer multiunit/screw-retained over cemented when using angled abutments (Erdoğdu 2024)
