## Summary
This editorial commentary in the Journal of the Korean Association of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgeons argues that bruxism — clenching, grinding, or mandibular thrusting during sleep or wakefulness — is associated with **late** implant complications (after osseointegration is achieved), specifically fixture fracture, screw loosening, screw fracture, prosthesis fracture, severe marginal bone loss, and decementation. The author proposes pre-implant screening (history of repetitive prosthesis failure, clinical signs such as masticatory muscle hypertrophy, linea alba, severe attrition, and instrumental assessment via EMG or polysomnography) and recommends adjunctive treatment with occlusal splint and/or botulinum toxin injection into masticatory muscles before implant installation.

## Key Contributions
- Clarifies the **early-vs-late failure framework**: bruxism does not impair osseointegration (early failure); it drives late failure via occlusal overload.
- Provides a **practical screening hierarchy** (non-instrumental → instrumental) for OMFS practice in Korea.
- Endorses **botulinum toxin** as an adjunctive treatment alongside occlusal splint — relevant in Korean dental practice where BoNT injection into the masseter is widely available.
- Frames bruxism management as part of **pre-implant treatment planning**, not as an afterthought after complications occur.

## Results
**Late complications associated with bruxism** (from review of cited literature):
- Implant fracture (fixture)
- Screw loosening
- Screw fracture
- Prosthesis fracture (ceramic, porcelain)
- Severe marginal bone loss (MBL)
- Decementation

**Diagnostic approach**:
| Tier | Method | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-instrumental | Clinical inspection (masseter hypertrophy, linea alba, lip/tongue indentation, attrition pattern, prosthesis complications); questionnaires | Cheap, fast, in-chair | Self-report unreliable; past vs current bruxism hard to distinguish |
| Instrumental | EMG (awake bruxism), PSG (sleep bruxism — gold standard) | Most accurate | Time-consuming, costly |

**Treatment options**:
- **Occlusal splint** — first-line, non-invasive; distributes occlusal force; favorable prognosis for implants/prostheses
- **Botulinum toxin** injection into masseter/temporalis — reduces muscle strength and parafunctional force

**Author's pragmatic recommendation**: even though the bruxism–implant failure correlation is "still controversial," the available evidence is sufficient to justify pre-implant screening; if bruxism is identified, the patient must be informed of increased risk and offered splint and/or BoNT before surgery.

## Clinical Implications
1. **Pre-implant bruxism screening should be a standard step** alongside medical history and CBCT planning — at minimum, ask about history of repetitive prosthesis fracture and inspect for masseter hypertrophy, linea alba, and attrition.
2. **Informed consent** should explicitly cover increased risk of late failures (fracture, screw issues, MBL) in suspected bruxers.
3. **Splint therapy first, BoNT as escalation**: occlusal splint is non-invasive and reversible. BoNT (typically masseter, often 25–50 U per side for adults — though dose not specified in this paper) is escalation when splint compliance fails or severity is high.
4. **Implant design choices in bruxers**: although not specified by this commentary, the clinical literature recommends: wider/longer fixtures, monolithic zirconia or metal-ceramic crowns rather than veneered ceramic, screw retention over cement retention for retrievability, splinting multiple implants, careful occlusal scheme (group function or canine guidance with shallow guidance) — consider in conjunction with the dedicated TMD/bruxism literature.
5. **Continuing maintenance**: bruxers need closer follow-up. Occlusal adjustments and night-time splint compliance should be re-checked at every recall.
6. **Limitation of this paper**: it is an editorial — no dose, no decision threshold, no follow-up interval is specified. Use it as a framing document, then layer in the systematic reviews (Manfredini 2014, Minakuchi 2022) and BoNT efficacy SRs for quantitative guidance.
