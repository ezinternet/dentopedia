## Summary

This narrative review (Khaohoen et al., J Clin Med 2023; 119 included articles) examines how implant **material**, implant **design**, and **surgical technique** interact with **bone density** from biomechanical and biological standpoints. Bone quality is framed by Misch's **D1–D4** classification (dense cortical → fine trabecular) and Hounsfield-unit ranges, collapsed to high (D1–D2) vs low (D3–D4). Compromised bone density drives early and late failure (~7.7% of cases) through loss of primary stability. The central message: in low-density bone, no single factor suffices — material choice, optimized geometry, and an appropriate surgical technique must be combined to secure primary stability and osseointegration.

## Key Contributions

- Consolidated **low-density-bone optimization guideline** for titanium implants: conical macrogeometry, wider diameter, longer length, reverse-buttress self-tapping threads, small pitch, deep thread depth.
- Head-to-head property comparison of **titanium (gold standard), zirconia (esthetic/metal-free alternative), and PEEK (low-modulus, anti-stress-shielding alternative)**.
- Explicit linkage of **surgical technique** (osseodensification, under-drilling, magnetodynamic) to primary-stability gains in soft (D3–D4) bone.
- Survival data stratified by bone density and surface treatment.

## Methodology

PICO-framed literature synthesis. **P** edentulous area treated with implant surgery; **I** implant materials (titanium, zirconia, other); **C** bone densities D1/D2–D3/D4 (high vs low); **O** stress concentration, BIC, push-in/removal torque, fracture resistance, LTD, MBL, success/survival; **S** RCT/non-RCT, case reports, animal, in vitro, FEA, reviews. Search of PubMed/Medline, Scopus, Embase + gray literature up to 2023 (10-year limit, English): 529 found → **119 included**; **7 studies** carried Misch bone-density clinical data. Reference management EndNote v9; data tabulated in Excel.

## Results

- **Biomechanics of bone density**: D4 bone shows the highest stress, concentrated at the cervical/crestal socket; lower density + thinner cortex increase bone strain and reduce stability. Elastic-modulus mismatch causes **stress shielding** and peri-implant bone loss. Frost's mechanostat: ~1500–3000 microstrain = bone gain window; >25,000 = microdamage.
- **Stability metrics**: insertion torque 30–50 Ncm acceptable; **ISQ below ~70–75** flags higher failure risk; ~60% BIC = good integration (Albrektsson).
- **Material properties (Table 5)**: cpTi(IV) E≈104 GPa; Ti-6Al-4V ≈110 GPa; Ti-Zr (Roxolid) ≈96 GPa / ~953 MPa; 3Y-zirconia E≈210 GPa, 900–1200 MPa flexural; ATZ E≈220 GPa, 1800–2400 MPa; PEEK E≈3–4 GPa (bone-like), CFR-PEEK ≈18 GPa, GFR-PEEK ≈12 GPa. Titanium remains gold standard; PEEK lowers stress shielding but has inferior osseointegration (lower BIC, less osteoblast differentiation); ATZ > Y-TZP in fracture strength (one-piece). Zirconia subject to low-temperature degradation (aging).
- **Geometry for low-density bone (Table 6)**: conical shape, diameter ≥4 mm, length 9–11 mm, reverse-buttress/self-tapping threads, pitch lower than standard, thread depth higher than standard. Immediate placement: conical, ≥4 mm, ≥11 mm, self-tapping. Hybrid (apical conical + crestal cylindrical) gives highest primary stability. Zirconia diameter should exceed 3.25 mm.
- **Surgical technique (D3–D4)**: undersized drilling, **osseodensification**, osteotome, piezoelectric (faster healing, no primary-stability gain), magnetodynamic (higher IT/ISQ/RT vs conventional). OD/under-preparation/expander improved primary stability vs conventional drilling (which gave lower ISQ).
- **Clinical survival**: zirconia 1-piece ~95% / 2-piece ~94% (2-piece not recommended); narrow-diameter Ti-Zr >95%, MBL <1 mm. By density: D-I 97.6%, D-II 96.2%, D-III 96.5%, **D-IV 88.8%**; rough-surface implants survive better than machined in low-density bone (97.1%). Across 7 included studies (2012–2022), low bone quality consistently showed higher MBL (Held 2013 D-IV ~1.46 mm vs ~0.2–0.7 mm denser).
