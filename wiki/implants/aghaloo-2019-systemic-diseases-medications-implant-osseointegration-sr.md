## One-line Summary

ITI consensus systematic review (abstract-only) finding that most systemic diseases — well-controlled diabetes, osteoporosis, HIV, CVD, neurologic disorders, hypothyroidism, and rheumatoid arthritis — show no direct evidence of reduced implant osseointegration, while SSRIs/PPIs show preliminary negative effects and antiresorptives warrant ONJ caution.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.11607/jomi.19suppl.g3](https://doi.org/10.11607/jomi.19suppl.g3)), this ITI consensus systematic review (abstract-only ingest — full text not retrieved) evaluated whether common systemic disorders and concomitant medications impair dental implant osseointegration. Across diabetes, osteoporosis, HIV, cardiovascular disease, neurologic disorders, hypothyroidism, and rheumatoid arthritis, the literature did not demonstrate a directly reduced osseointegration rate; the more important caveats are increased long-term peri-implant complications (notably in diabetes) and osteonecrosis of the jaws (ONJ) risk with antiresorptives. A newer signal is that medications — specifically SSRIs and PPIs — may negatively affect osseointegration, though this is preliminary.

Clinically: most systemic diseases, when controlled, are not absolute contraindications to implants and do not by themselves lower integration rates — but they raise long-term complication risk and demand attention to disease control, comorbidities, and the patient's full medication list (screen for antiresorptives, SSRIs, and PPIs) rather than the diagnosis label alone.

## Key Contributions

- Separates the question of **osseointegration rate** (largely unaffected by controlled systemic disease) from **long-term peri-implant complications** (which can rise).
- Identifies **medications** (SSRIs, PPIs) as an emerging risk signal distinct from the underlying disease.
- Frames antiresorptive-associated **ONJ** — not failure to integrate — as the dominant caution in the osteoporosis/bone-malignancy setting.

## Methodology

Systematic review under the ITI consensus framework, assessing systemic disorders (diabetes, osteoporosis), additional conditions (neurocognitive disease, cardiovascular disease, HIV, hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis), and medications (SSRIs, PPIs, antihypertensives) for their effect on implant osseointegration. Abstract-only — detailed search strategy, study counts, and pooled estimates are not available in the retrieved text.

## Results

1. **Diabetes** — Literature does NOT show that diabetes negatively affects osseointegration; most studies enrolled well-controlled diabetics with prophylactic antibiotics. Increased long-term bone and soft-tissue complications were observed.
2. **Osteoporosis** — Recent studies/reviews fail to demonstrate a lower osseointegration rate; caution for osteonecrosis of the jaws (ONJ), especially with bone malignancies and antiresorptive medications.
3. **HIV, cardiovascular disease, neurologic disorders, hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis** — No direct evidence of a decreased osseointegration rate.
4. **Medications** — Preliminary evidence that SSRIs or PPIs may negatively affect osseointegration (recent, requires validation).
5. **Conclusion** — Disease control, concomitant medications, and comorbidities complicate osseointegration and must guide treatment approaches and clinical guidelines.
