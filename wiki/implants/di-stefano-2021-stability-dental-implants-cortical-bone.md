## Summary
Systematic review of 13 clinical studies examining the relationship between cortical bone thickness and implant primary/secondary stability. The majority of studies find a **positive correlation**: thicker cortical bone → higher ISQ and insertion torque. Bicortical anchorage produces the highest stability values. The MBL–cortical thickness relationship remains inconclusive.

## Key Contributions
- **Cortical thickness → primary stability**: positive correlation confirmed in most studies (Miyamoto r=0.84; Salimov r=0.935 for IT, r=0.888 for ISQ)
- **Bicortical anchorage**: significantly higher IT and ISQ than monocortical (p < 0.05)
- **Minimum threshold**: ≥1.0 mm cortical thickness recommended for mini-implants
- **ISQ not purely cortical**: Waechter 2017 found ISQ also depends on cancellous bone availability
- **MBL**: no consistent correlation with cortical thickness (Dias 2016: r = −0.029, p = 0.832)

## Key Data Points

| Study | n | Correlation | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Miyamoto 2005 | 225 implants | ISQ ↔ cortical thickness r=0.84 | <0.0001 |
| Salimov 2014 | 65 implants | IT ↔ cortical density r=0.935 | <0.001 |
| Mantovani 2018 | 97 implants | Bicortical > monocortical ISQ/IT | <0.05 |
| Dias 2016 | 57 implants | ISQ ↔ cortical thickness r=0.145 | 0.292 (NS) |

## Clinical Takeaways
- **CBCT pre-op**: assess cortical thickness to anticipate primary stability
- **Bicortical purchase**: when possible, engage both cortical plates for maximum stability
- **Mandible > maxilla**: higher cortical-to-trabecular ratio = generally better primary stability
- **ISQ interpretation**: influenced by both cortical and cancellous bone — cortical thickness alone does not fully predict ISQ
- **MBL**: cortical thickness is not a reliable predictor of long-term bone loss
