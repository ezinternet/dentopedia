## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 14 case reports/series (37 systemic-sclerosis patients, 153 implants): implant survival was 100% in case reports and 89.2% in the single case series, with SSc status showing no discernible impact on survival — provided disease-specific risk assessment and microstomia management are addressed.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1002/iid3.812](https://doi.org/10.1002/iid3.812)), this first comprehensive systematic review compiled the available evidence on dental implant survival in patients with systemic sclerosis (SSc / scleroderma), pooling 14 studies (1 retrospective case series and 13 case reports) covering 37 patients and 153 implants across 11 countries. Reported implant survival was 100% in every case report and 89.2% (95% CI 79.1–95.6) in the single case series, and the authors conclude there is scant evidence that SSc has a negative impact on implant survival. The evidence base, however, is exclusively low-level (no control groups, no RCTs, small heterogeneous samples), so a firm recommendation cannot be drawn. The dominant SSc-specific obstacle is progressive microstomia (restricted mouth opening from perioral fibrosis), compounded by xerostomia, disturbed oral vascularity/healing, tooth mobility, and altered bone turnover.

Clinically: implants are a viable rehabilitation option for SSc patients and appear to survive well, but only after comprehensive interdisciplinary (dentist + rheumatologist/dermatologist) risk assessment; favor fixed implant-supported restorations over removable dentures, and plan for microstomia (short implants/angled abutments, sectional impressions, commissurotomy or foldable/collapsible prostheses) plus lifelong twice-yearly peri-implant monitoring.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic review isolating the SSc subpopulation for the dental-implant survival question.
- Aggregates a quantitative survival picture (100% case reports; 89.2% case series) from previously scattered single cases.
- Translates SSc oral pathophysiology into actionable prosthetic/surgical decision logic (fixed > removable; access workarounds for microstomia).
- Frames the SSc–peri-implant inflammation link (shared vascular dysfunction; hypoxia-driven RANK/RANKL bone dysregulation) as a rationale for stricter maintenance.

## Methodology

- PRISMA-adherent systematic review; protocol registered on Open Science Framework.
- PubMed, Scopus, Google Scholar searched January 2023 by two independent reviewers; dental-implant × systemic-sclerosis MeSH/keyword strings.
- Inclusion: case reports/series from the last 30 years on SSc and implant survival/outcomes. Exclusion: non-English, reviews, posters, abstracts, insufficient data.
- Screening: 593 → 579 (after de-dup) → 14 included; inter-rater agreement κ = 0.86.
- Dual-reviewer quality assessment and data extraction with third-author adjudication.

## Results

- **Included studies**: 14 — 1 retrospective case series (Jackowski 2021) + 13 case reports.
- **Patients**: 37 total, all scleroderma; age range 28–77 y, mean 58.16 ± 12.88 y; 94.6% female (case reports all female; case series 22 F / 2 M).
- **Implants**: 153 total across multiple systems (Straumann, Nobel Biocare, ITI, MIS, Odontit, etc.).
- **Follow-up**: 1–10 years (several case reports unreported).
- **Survival**: 100% in all case reports; 89.2% (95% CI 79.1–95.6) in the case series.
- **Effect of SSc**: no significant/discernible negative impact on implant survival.
- **Case series (Jackowski 2021, n=24, 72 implants)**: peri-implant pocket depth 2.4–2.8 mm; mean interincisal distance 29.54 mm; plaque index correlated with sulcus bleeding index and peri-implant pocket depth.
- **Prosthetics**: fixed restorations reported superior to removable; fixed designs indicated while interincisal distance remains above ~3 cm.
