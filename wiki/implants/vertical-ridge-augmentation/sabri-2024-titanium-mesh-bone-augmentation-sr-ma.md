## One-line Summary

SR+MA (22 trials, n≥196): Ti mesh yields 3.36 mm vertical / 3.26 mm horizontal bone gain; pooled complication rate 10.8% (exposure most common); non-inferior to collagen membrane for horizontal gain.

## Summary

Sabri et al. (2024) performed a systematic review and meta-analysis of 22 clinical trials evaluating titanium mesh (Ti mesh) as a scaffold for alveolar bone augmentation. This is the most comprehensive pooled dataset to date covering both vertical and horizontal outcomes.

**Bone gain (meta-analysis):**
- Vertical: **3.36 mm** (95% CI 2.44–4.64 mm; range 1.4–5.7 mm; n=196 subjects)
- Horizontal: **3.26 mm** (95% CI 2.93–3.63 mm; range 2.6–3.7 mm; n=81 subjects)

**Complications (pooled across clinical trials):**
- Overall rate: **10.8%**
- Most common: mesh exposure (regardless of mesh type)
- Second most common: graft failure

**Ti mesh vs collagen membrane (horizontal gain):**
Controlling for staged vs simultaneous implant placement, no significant difference in horizontal bone gain was found. This means Ti mesh offers no horizontal gain advantage over collagen membranes, but matches their performance with additional space-maintenance capability for larger defects.

**Clinical implication:** Ti mesh is a feasible scaffold for defects requiring ~4 mm 3D reconstruction. The acceptable complication rate and predictable gain support its use in appropriate cases.

## Key Contributions

- Largest pooled evidence base for Ti mesh bone augmentation (22 studies)
- Separate pooling of vertical vs horizontal outcomes (previously conflated in literature)
- Demonstrates Ti mesh ≈ collagen membrane for horizontal gain (neither superior)
- Establishes pooled 10.8% complication rate as reference benchmark
- Identifies scarcity of patient-reported outcomes (PROMs) as evidence gap

## Methodology

- Comprehensive electronic + manual database search
- Inclusion: RCTs and non-randomized prospective controlled trials; Ti mesh in ≥1 arm
- Primary outcomes: vertical/horizontal bone gain, complication rate
- Meta-analysis: random-effects model
- Heterogeneity acknowledged; no specific risk-of-bias tool named
