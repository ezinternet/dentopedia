## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 16 RCTs/CCTs (n=446) confirms CTG yields +0.64 mm peri-implant soft-tissue thickness gain over no graft and +0.51 mm over substitutes, with superior esthetics but equal MBL outcomes.

## Summary

Valles et al. (2022) conducted a PRISMA-compliant SR+MA — part of the DGI/SEPA/Osteology consensus supplement — specifically targeting **peri-implant soft tissue thickening (STT)** as the primary outcome, distinct from keratinized mucosa width augmentation. Two separate PICOS questions were addressed:

- **PICOS 1** (CTG vs no graft): 8 trials, 254 patients — CTG produced a statistically significant STT gain of **+0.64 mm** (95% CI [0.16; 1.13]; p=0.01) and significantly less soft-tissue recession (WMD +0.50 mm; p<0.001).
- **PICOS 2** (CTG vs substitutes): 8 trials, 192 patients — CTG produced **+0.51 mm** greater STT than substitutes (95% CI [0.28; 0.75]; p<0.001), better Pink Esthetic Scores (WMD +1.02; p=0.01), and less recession (WMD +0.50 mm; p=0.014). Substitutes tested: collagen matrix (XCM/Mucograft), acellular dermal matrix (ADM), and platelet-rich fibrin (PRF).

In both comparisons, no statistically significant differences were found for marginal bone level (MBL), probing pocket depth, plaque, or bleeding indices. The sole significant disadvantage of CTG over substitutes was greater analgesic consumption (WMD +1.68 tablets; p<0.001).

Long-term follow-up (≥1 year) showed a larger STT advantage for CTG over substitutes than short-term (<1 year), suggesting partial resorption of substitutes over time. A reference STT threshold of ≥2 mm is cited to minimize implant/prosthetic component transparency through peri-implant mucosa.

## Key Contributions

1. First SR+MA to isolate **STT gain** (not KM width) as the primary outcome — fills a gap in the evidence hierarchy for tissue-thickening decisions.
2. Quantifies CTG advantage over substitutes with 95% prediction intervals: +0.51 mm WMD (PI [−0.09; 1.12]) — wide PI signals individual study variability despite the significant pooled effect.
3. Confirms esthetic (PES) and soft-tissue margin stability benefits of CTG over substitutes, establishing CTG as the gold standard for STT augmentation.
4. Long-term analysis demonstrates that substitute STT gains diminish relative to CTG at ≥1 year — important for treatment planning when donor-site morbidity is a concern.
5. No MBL benefit identified for CTG over either no graft or substitutes — STT augmentation does not independently protect marginal bone.

## Methodology

| Feature | PICOS 1 | PICOS 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Comparison | CTG vs no graft | CTG vs substitutes |
| Studies included | 8 trials (9 articles) | 8 trials |
| Patients | 254 | 192 |
| Follow-up range | 3–60 months | Not specified separately |
| Implant protocols | Immediate + delayed | Immediate + delayed |
| STT measurement | STL superimposition, transmucosal probing, ultrasound | Same |
| Effect measure | WMD (random-effects, REML) | WMD / SMD |
| Heterogeneity | I²=78.6% (high) | Low–moderate |

Subgroup analyses were pre-specified for: (a) implant placement timing (immediate vs delayed), (b) follow-up duration (<1 year vs ≥1 year), (c) timing of soft tissue augmentation (simultaneous vs staged). Only follow-up duration produced a significant subgroup difference (PICOS 2: greater CTG benefit at ≥1 year).

Risk of bias was assessed per study; inter-reviewer reliability was ≥95% agreement and kappa ≥0.893 at both screening stages.

| Recession (less) | CTG vs no graft | +0.50 mm | [0.19; 0.80] | <0.001 |

| Volumetric change | CTG vs substitutes | SMD −0.61 | [−0.14; 1.36] | 0.11 (NS) |

Key individual study observations:
- Hosseini 2020 (60-month CCT): STT at 1 mm depth — CTG −0.04 mm vs no graft −0.68 mm from baseline; CTG group maintained stability while control deteriorated
- Wiesner 2010: most pronounced STT gain — CTG +1.2 mm (SD 0.63) vs no graft −0.15 mm (SD 0.34)
- Cairo 2017, Thoma 2020: collagen matrix achieved meaningful but lesser STT gains vs CTG (approximately 0.5–0.7 mm less)
