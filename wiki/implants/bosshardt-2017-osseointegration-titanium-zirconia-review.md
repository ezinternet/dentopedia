## One-line Summary

Narrative review (Periodontology 2000, 2017) synthesizing preclinical osseointegration biology for cpTi, TiZr, Ti6Al4V, and zirconia implants — TiZr matches cpTi in %BIC, Ti6Al4V underperforms with more multinucleated giant cells, and SLActive accelerates early bone apposition over SLA.

## Summary

Bosshardt, Chappuis, and Buser review the biology of osseointegration for contemporary implant biomaterials published in Periodontology 2000 (2017). The review covers the complete temporal sequence of wound healing and osseointegration, the influence of surface modifications on 골-임플란트 접촉률 (Bone-to-Implant Contact, BIC), comparative performance of titanium alloys vs. commercially pure titanium (cpTi), and ceramic (zirconia) implant behavior. A central theme is that surface characteristics — both micro-topography and surface chemistry — are the dominant determinants of osseointegration speed, while the long-term maintenance of osseointegration remains a clinical open question.

## Key Contributions

### Temporal Sequence of Osseointegration

Osseointegration follows intramembranous osteogenesis:
1. **Hemostasis + coagulum** (hours)
2. **Granulation tissue** (days)
3. **Woven bone formation** (starts week 1, both animals and humans)
4. **Parallel-fibered → lamellar bone** maturation
5. **Bone remodeling** (lifelong; also occurs at the bone-implant interface)

Gene expression at 4 days–2 weeks switches from immuno-inflammatory (cell proliferation) to regenerative (angiogenesis, osteogenesis, neurogenesis), marking the pro-osteogenic phase.

### Primary vs. Secondary Stability

- **1차 안정성 (Primary Stability)**: Mechanical; determined by implant macro-design, bone density, press-fit. Decreases post-placement.
- **2차 안정성 (Secondary Stability)**: Biological; from new bone apposition. Increases after week 1.
- **안정성 강하 (Stability Dip)**: Transient drop in total stability at ~3–4 weeks. Minimized by balanced cortical/trabecular bone ratio and faster-osseointegrating surfaces (SLActive).

### Surface Modification Hierarchy (cpTi)

From lowest to highest %BIC at early time points:
- Machined/smooth (Ra 0.5–1.0 μm) → baseline
- TPS (titanium plasma-sprayed) → higher %BIC than smooth
- SLA (sandblasted large-grit acid-etched) → significantly higher %BIC than TPS
- **SLActive** (chemically activated SLA, hydrophilic) → significantly higher early %BIC than SLA at 2 and 4 weeks (miniature pig + human studies)
- Hydroxyapatite-coated: initially high %BIC but coating resorption observed consistently

### Titanium Alloys

- **TiZr (Roxolid)**: No statistically significant difference in %BIC vs. cpTi grade 4 (with equivalent surface treatment). Fast osseointegration confirmed in miniature pig and canine models.
- **Ti6Al4V**: Comparable %BIC to cpTi in earlier studies, but in a direct comparison (miniature pig, wound chamber model), Ti6Al4V peaked at **significantly lower %BIC** and then declined. More importantly, Ti6Al4V surfaces were covered by **significantly more 다핵거대세포 (Multinucleated Giant Cells, MNGCs)** than TiZr or cpTi.

### Zirconia (Y-TZP and Alumina-Toughened ZrO₂)

- Bone apposition confirmed on sandblasted, acid-etched, sintered, and coated zirconia surfaces
- **Acid-etching after sandblasting** → higher %BIC than sandblasting alone
- **Alkaline-etching** → lower %BIC than sandblasting alone (detrimental)
- **Alumina-toughened zirconia**: Lower %BIC than cpTi at 4 and 8 weeks (miniature pig)
- MNGCs present on all ceramic surfaces in higher numbers than on cpTi

### Multinucleated Giant Cells (MNGCs)

MNGCs are fused macrophages and are an integral part of normal osseointegration. Key points:
- Present on ALL biomaterials during healing
- Present in **significantly higher numbers** on Ti6Al4V and zirconia than on cpTi/TiZr
- **Cannot predict future implant loss** — the clinical significance of elevated MNGCs is unclear
- MNGC response may be modulated by macrophage polarization state (M1 vs. M2)

### Marginal Bone Loss: Open Questions

Unexplained marginal bone loss (not classical peri-implantitis) remains a critical gap:
- **Titanium particle/ion release**: Macrophages can corrode Ti surfaces and take up metal ions. Inflammatory response modulated by macrophage polarization.
- **Polypharmacy**: SSRIs, antiresorptives (bisphosphonates), antiangiogenics, immunosuppressants all interfere with bone turnover and implant longevity
- **Osteoimmunology**: Intimate link between immune system and bone biology — disrupted by medication
- Mandibular alveolar bone turns over ~10× faster than long-bone diaphysis → implant surfaces in mandible are more vulnerable to remodeling imbalance

## Methodology

- Narrative review design; predominantly animal (miniature pig, canine, rat) data
- **Gold standard metric**: histomorphometry (%BIC — % implant surface covered by bone, further classified as new vs. old bone)
- **Wound chamber model** (Berglundh design): standardized U-shaped trough isolates early healing analysis from parent bone contact variability
- Push-out/pull-out tests and removal torque for biomechanical interlocking assessment
- Resonance frequency analysis (RFA / ISQ) for clinical stability monitoring
- Human biopsy data (SLA vs. SLActive study by Lang, Bosshardt et al.) included
- Soft tissue integration explicitly out of scope

## Results

| Comparison | Finding |
|---|---|
| SLActive vs. SLA (early) | SLActive: significantly higher %BIC at 2 and 4 weeks (animal + human) |
| TiZr vs. cpTi | No significant %BIC difference; equivalent osseointegration |
| Ti6Al4V vs. cpTi/TiZr | Lower peak %BIC; significantly more MNGCs |
| Zirconia acid-etch vs. sandblast-only | Acid-etch: higher %BIC |
| Zirconia alkaline-etch | Lower %BIC than sandblast-only |
| Alumina-ZrO₂ vs. cpTi | Lower %BIC at 4 and 8 weeks |
| MNGCs on Ti6Al4V vs. cpTi | Significantly more on Ti6Al4V |
