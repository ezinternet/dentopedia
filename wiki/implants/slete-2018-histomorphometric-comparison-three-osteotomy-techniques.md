## One-line Summary
Ex vivo porcine-tibia histomorphometric pilot (n=6/group × 3 osteotomy techniques): immediate Bone-to-Implant Contact (BIC) was highest with Osseodensification (OD) 60.3% > Summers osteotome 40.7% > standard drilling 16.3%, while bone-volume % in the 2-mm peri-implant zone was similar (SD ~54%, SO ~49%) — indicating the BIC difference reflects contact geometry/trabecular compaction, not absolute bone volume. Descriptive-only pilot (no p-values), no healing/remodeling assessed.

## Summary
Slete et al. ran a bench histomorphometric pilot comparing three osteotomy techniques in fresh porcine tibia: standard subtractive drilling (SD), Summers osteotome compaction (SO), and osseodensification (OD) with Densah burs. Eighteen 4.7 × 13 mm tapered screw-vent implants were placed (six per group) and analyzed for immediate BIC, bone volume percentage in a 2-mm peri-implant zone, and trabecular integrity.

OD achieved 60.3% BIC versus 40.7% for SO and 16.3% for SD. OD additionally produced a uniform compacted-bone "burnished" zone roughly 0.7 mm thick laterally and apically, with intact whole trabeculae filling intrathread spaces — a contact geometry not replicated by SO (which showed trabecular fracture and microdamage) or SD (which left visible gap). Notably, BV% in the 2-mm zone was similar between SD (~54%) and SO (~49%); the BIC gap therefore reflects contact geometry and trabecular compaction rather than absolute bone volume. The study is a small pilot — no inferential statistics are extractable — but provides the bench foundation for the clinical OD primary-stability claim later tested in vivo and in patients.

## Key Contributions
- Bench-level mechanistic comparison of three osteotomy paradigms in the same low-density bone substrate.
- Quantifies immediate BIC: OD 60.3% > SO 40.7% > SD 16.3%.
- Identifies apical bony condensation as unique to OD.
- Shows trabecular fracture/microdamage as a hallmark of osteotome (SO) preparation, absent under OD.
- Distinguishes BIC (contact geometry) from BV% (raw bone volume) — SD and SO are close on BV% but very different on BIC.

## Methodology
Fresh porcine tibial plateau substrate. Three osteotomy groups (n = 6 each): SD (1.7 mm pilot then manufacturer sequence to 4.7 mm), SO (1.7 mm pilot then Summers osteotomes sizes I, II, III), OD (Densah burs in CCW per manufacturer protocol). All 18 sites received 4.7 × 13 mm tapered screw-vent implants. Tibias fixed in 10% formalin, processed for histologic sectioning and staining; histomorphometric analysis of immediate BIC and BV% within a 2-mm peri-implant zone. No formal inferential statistics reported in extractable text; values presented descriptively.

## Results
- Immediate BIC: OD 60.3%, SO 40.7%, SD 16.3% (descriptive).
- BV% in 2-mm peri-implant zone: SD ~54%, SO ~49%; OD greatest (exact OD value not extractable).
- OD-compacted zone: ~0.7 mm uniform thickness, laterally and apically; whole intact trabeculae visible in intrathread spaces at 20×–100× magnification.
- SO group: histologic evidence of trabecular fracture and microdamage.
- SD group: visible gap between drill-cut bone and implant thread, lowest BIC.

## Limitations
- Ex vivo porcine tibia, not in vivo healing — no information on remodeling, secondary stability, or osseointegration outcome.
- Pilot n = 6 per group, descriptive statistics only — no p-values, no CIs, no formal hypothesis test.
- BIC at placement is a mechanical-contact measurement, not the histologic BIC after osseointegration that drives clinical survival.
- Confidence label "in-vivo" inherited from the JSON tag, but the methodology is actually ex vivo (fresh porcine tibia, not living animal); the wiki tag is kept for index consistency but this nuance matters for interpretation.
- Authors located near Densah/Versah headquarters (Jackson, MI) — declare-and-interpret-with-caution.
- BV% similarity between SD and SO (54% vs 49%) suggests the dramatic BIC difference is largely a contact-geometry artifact of how each technique presents bone to the implant thread, not a true bone-volume effect.
