## One-line Summary

Systematic review (PRISMA) of 7 human studies (2008–2021; 1462 participants, 4450 implants); implant loss 3.9–11.4% (avg 8.37%), highest (11.1%) in severe vitamin D deficiency compounded by smoking/periodontitis — but overall no significant association between vitamin D level and osseointegration; supports pre-surgical vitamin D screening.

## 한줄요약

사람 대상 7편(2008–2021; 참가자 1462명·임플란트 4450개)을 묶은 PRISMA 체계적 고찰(systematic review, SR). 임플란트 소실률(implant loss rate)은 3.9–11.4%(평균 8.37%)로, 중증 비타민 D 결핍(severe vitamin D deficiency)에 흡연·치주염이 겹치면 최고 11.1%. 다만 비타민 D 수치와 골유착(osseointegration) 사이에 통계적으로 유의한 연관성은 없었고, 술전 비타민 D 선별검사를 권고. 비타민 D 수용체 다형성(Vitamin D Receptor polymorphism, VDR) 결과는 연구마다 엇갈림(TaqI 무관, rs3782905 allele G는 불량 골유착과 연관).

## Summary

This human-only systematic review (Buzatu 2024, *Nutrients*) asks whether serum vitamin D status predicts dental implant osseointegration. Seven studies (2008–2021; Brazil, USA, UK, Germany, Italy, Poland) covering 1462 participants and 4450 implants were synthesized under PRISMA. The headline tension: descriptively, the worst outcomes cluster in vitamin D deficiency (loss up to 11.1–11.4%), yet the review's formal conclusion is that there is **no statistically significant association** between vitamin D level and osseointegration in humans — a contrast with the more consistently positive animal literature. The practical takeaway the authors endorse is screening, not promised benefit: evaluate vitamin D pre-surgically and correct deficiency, especially in patients who also smoke or have periodontal disease.

## Key Contributions

- A **human-only** evidence map of vitamin D and implant osseointegration: 7 studies, 1462 participants, 4450 implants.
- Quantifies a **human implant-loss range of 3.9–11.4%** (overall average 8.37%).
- Surfaces the **Mangano dose-response signal**: 3.9% overall loss → 11.1% in severe deficiency; ~4× higher early loss when low vitamin D coincides with smoking and periodontal disease.
- Documents a **mixed genetic picture**: VDR TaqI polymorphism not associated with loss (Alvim-Pereira), but rs3782905 allele G significantly associated with poor osseointegration (Pereira).
- Frames the bottom line **conservatively** — deficiency is a plausible risk factor and worth screening, but causal/direct influence remains inconclusive in humans.

## Methodology

- **Databases**: PubMed, Cochrane Library, Web of Science; scope to December 2023; PRISMA; registered (PROSPERO + OSF osf.io/dx5sm).
- **Screening**: 677 records → 195 screened (106 duplicates removed) → 7 included; two reviewers, third resolving disagreements.
- **Quality**: Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (cohorts) + Cochrane risk-of-bias tool (RCT); rated low/medium/high.
- **Included designs**: 2 case–control, 3 case reports, 1 case series, 1 retrospective cohort, 1 RCT (Kwiatek — the only high-quality study).
- **No meta-analysis** — heterogeneity precluded pooling (hence `confidence: sr`, not `sr+ma`).

## Results

| Study | Country | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| Alvim-Pereira (2008) | Brazil | 9.8% loss; **no** association VDR TaqI ↔ loss |
| Pereira | Brazil | 11.4% loss; rs3782905 allele G ↔ poor osseointegration |
| Flanagan | USA | Successful implant under vitamin D3 supplementation (low serum vit D not prohibitive if calcium managed) |
| Bryce | UK | Successful implant despite severe deficiency (age 29) |
| Fretwurst | Germany | 2/9 implants failed under deficiency; replaced successfully after supplementation (vit D → 46 μg/L) |
| Mangano (n=885) | Italy | **3.9% overall → 11.1% in severe deficiency**; ~4× early loss with low vit D + smoking + periodontitis |
| Kwiatek (2021, RCT) | Poland | Significantly higher bone density after 12-wk supplementation (23.9 → 33.1 ng/mL) |

- Totals: 1462 participants, 4450 implants; **average loss 8.37%**.
- Anatomical context cited: anterior maxilla osseointegration ~90% vs posterior maxilla ~70%.
- Smoking alone cited to raise implant failure by >140% (context for the Mangano compounding effect).
- **Limitations**: high heterogeneity, mostly observational/case-level evidence (single RCT), no standardized supplementation protocol, possible publication/language bias → causality cannot be inferred. Authors call for prospective intervention trials.
