## One-line Summary

Umbrella review (PRISMA, JBI appraisal) of 5 systematic reviews — 351 records → 5 SRs included → 8 relevant primary studies; vitamin D appears to have a positive effect on osseointegration and on reducing dental implant failure, but the evidence is methodologically limited (no meta-analysis, selective reporting, inconsistent reference levels and doses).

## Summary

This is the first **umbrella review** dedicated to vitamin D and dental-implant osseointegration, sitting one tier above the existing systematic reviews and integrating them. A five-database PRISMA search returned 351 records, narrowing to **5 systematic reviews** (published 2021–2022). Those 5 SRs collectively cited 51 primary studies (22 duplicated → 29 unique), but only **8 primary studies (2014–2021)** actually met the umbrella's eligibility criteria — exposing that none of the underlying SRs captured the field cleanly.

The clinical bottom line: **vitamin D appears to positively influence osseointegration, marginal bone loss, implant survival, and early dental implant failure (EDIF) — but the signal is inconsistent and frequently not statistically significant.** Methodological quality of the SRs (JBI 11-item) ranged from low (score 4) to high (score 9); none performed a meta-analysis, and the authors document confirmation/selective-reporting bias in two of them. The practical recommendation is to consider checking serum vitamin D before implant placement and to supplement patients with severely compromised levels, while treating vitamin D as a *contributing* factor, not the sole determinant of implant success.

## Key Contributions

- **Top-of-pyramid synthesis** for the vitamin D–osseointegration subdomain (review of reviews).
- Maps the evidence funnel and shows the underlying SRs are heterogeneous and incomplete (largest SR = Werny 2022 with 18 primary studies; most recent SR included only 4).
- Re-analyzes the 29 underlying primary studies individually because the included SRs were judged deficient — a step beyond standard umbrella methodology.
- Flags field-wide flaws: no meta-analysis, inconsistent vitamin D reference thresholds, unreported supplementation doses, and selective outcome reporting.
- Gives a concrete clinical hook: high deficiency prevalence justifies pre-implant serum vitamin D screening.

## Methodology

- Umbrella review, PRISMA-guided, protocol registered in the Open Science Framework; conducted Jan–Apr 2023.
- Five databases (PubMed, Web of Science, CINAHL Plus, Cochrane Library, Academic Search Complete); string ["vitamin D" AND ("dental implant" OR "dental implants")]; no year limit; PT/EN/ES.
- Two independent reviewers for selection, JBI quality appraisal (11 items), and data extraction; consensus/third reviewer for disagreements.
- Excluded SR/MA of diseased-only subjects, abstracts/commentaries, irrelevant outcomes, duplicated/incomplete data.

## Results

- **Bottom line**: positive but inconsistent association between vitamin D and implant success.
- **Mangano (2018)** (largest primary study, n=885, 168-mo follow-up): higher EDIF tendency with deficiency, **not significant (p = 0.105)**.
- **Tabrizi (2022)**: MBL higher with deficiency (1.38 ± 0.33 mm at 8.13 ng/mL) vs. sufficiency (0.78 ± 0.12 mm at 35.37 ng/mL); low serum vitamin D accounted for 50.6% of mean MBL (R = 0.506, p = 0.001); each +1 ng/mL → MBL −0.02 mm (p < 0.001); 0.60 mm 1-year difference may not be clinically significant.
- **Hakim (2021)**: 6 of 12 studies found a significant deficiency–DIF association.
- **Supplementation RCTs**: Garg (2020) cholecalciferol 60,000 IU/month ×3 mo (≈2000 IU/day); Kwiatek (2021) 8000 IU/day ×12 wk — both report improved osseointegration despite very different dosing.
- **JBI scores 4–9** (low to high); none performed meta-analysis; selective/confirmation bias documented in Werny (2022) and Hakim (2021).
- Aside: digitally guided surgery showed threefold lower DIF vs. freehand (2.25% vs. 6.42%, Bazal-Bonelli 2022).
