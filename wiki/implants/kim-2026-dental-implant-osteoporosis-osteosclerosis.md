## One-line Summary

Korean master's narrative review of 30 human studies (2008–2025) on dental implants in osteoporosis and five osteosclerotic jaw lesions: osteoporosis short-term survival is healthy-comparable (97.9–100%) but falls to 82.6–94.1% at 5–10 years with greater marginal bone loss; osteosclerotic-lesion outcomes are lesion-type dependent (florid COD 66.7% vs localized 100%; intralesional placement worst).

## Summary

This narrative review consolidates implant decision-making at the two extremes of bone metabolism in an aging population: osteoporosis (low density / impaired quality) and osteosclerosis (excessive density / pathologic bone). Thirty human studies (2008–2025) were gathered via Google Scholar, PubMed, the Chonnam National University library databases, and Perplexity AI search. For osteoporosis, short-term implant survival is essentially equivalent to healthy controls, but long-term (5–10 year) survival and marginal bone level deteriorate, making long-term radiographic follow-up the central clinical message; oral bisphosphonates are not an absolute contraindication, and a drug holiday is reserved for high-risk profiles (IV antiresorptives, steroid co-therapy) with endocrinology co-management. For five osteosclerotic lesions, outcomes diverge by lesion biology: condensing osteitis and idiopathic osteosclerosis do well once inflammation is controlled and the lesion is stable, whereas florid cemento-osseous dysplasia (COD) and intralesional placement carry high failure risk; cementoblastoma requires complete tumor resection before placement, and hypercementosis fails when extraction leaves residual tissue. As a single-author, non-systematic master's thesis with no quality appraisal or quantitative synthesis, the evidence weight is low and the reported survival figures are vulnerable to selection bias — this page is a structured map of considerations, not a pooled estimate.

## Key Contributions

- Frames osteoporosis (low density) and osteosclerosis (high density) within one review, contrasting implant strategy across opposite ends of bone quality.
- Tabulates implant outcomes for five osteosclerotic lesions — COD, condensing osteitis, idiopathic osteosclerosis, cementoblastoma, hypercementosis — a topic previously absent from this wiki.
- Separates osteoporosis short-term vs long-term (5–10 yr) survival and distills a bisphosphonate drug-holiday co-management pathway.
- Aggregates surgical-technique cautions (e.g., two-stage placement to avoid overheating in sclerotic bone; surface-treated implants / avoidance of immediate loading in osteoporotic bone).

## Methodology

- Design: narrative review (not systematic). Search 2008-01 to 2025-08, human studies only, n = 30 included.
- No predefined search protocol, risk-of-bias appraisal, or meta-analytic pooling; individual study/case-report values quoted directly and summarized in tables (Tables 1–7).
- Sources include AI-assisted retrieval (Perplexity) alongside conventional databases.

## Results

- Osteoporosis short-term (3 mo–1 yr): 97.9–100% survival, comparable to healthy controls (e.g., Merheb 2016: 160 implants, 100% at 3 mo).
- Osteoporosis long-term (5–10 yr): 82.6–94.1% (Holahan 2011 mean 82.6%); marginal bone loss significantly greater in some series (Pandey: osteoporotic mesial 3.4 / distal 3.5 mm vs non 1.2 / 1.4 mm, significant) but not others (Wagner 2017: 1.1±1.2 / 1.2±1.3 vs 0.6±1.3 / 0.5±1.3 mm, NS) — inter-study disagreement.
- Bisphosphonates: low MRONJ incidence with oral agents → not an absolute contraindication; some long-term data show reduced peri-implant bone loss. Drug holiday (3 mo pre- to 3–6 mo post-surgery) considered only for high-dose IV / steroid co-therapy, balanced against fracture risk via endocrinology co-management.
- Osteosclerotic lesions: localized COD 100% vs florid COD 66.7% survival; intralesional placement → higher failure. Condensing osteitis / idiopathic osteosclerosis → good prognosis when stable. Cementoblastoma → success after complete resection (immediate or delayed). Hypercementosis → failure risk when residual tissue left after extraction.
