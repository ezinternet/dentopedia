## One-line Summary

PRISMA systematic critical review (55 studies, 9 autoimmune diseases) finding dental implant survival comparable to the general population, though secondary Sjögren's and erosive oral lichen planus raise peri-mucositis and marginal bone loss risk.

## 한줄요약

PRISMA 체계적 비판적 고찰 (55편, 자가면역질환 9종): 치과 임플란트 생존율은 일반 인구와 동등하나, 이차 쇼그렌증후군과 미란성 구강편평태선에서 임플란트주위점막염(peri-mucositis)과 변연골소실(MBL) 위험 증가.

## Summary

This PRISMA systematic critical review (University of Geneva, 2022) evaluated complications and survival rates of dental implants in patients with autoimmune diseases. Fifty-five studies reporting on 9 distinct autoimmune diseases were analyzed from Google Scholar and PubMed (search stop: September 2021). Despite marked heterogeneity and methodological limitations, implant survival rates across the autoimmune disease spectrum were comparable to those reported in the general population. No specific contraindications were identified for any of the 9 conditions reviewed. The key practical exceptions requiring proactive management are: (1) prophylactic low-dose corticosteroids in erosive/active oral lichen planus patients, and (2) strict glycemic control in Type 1 diabetes patients. Meticulous oral hygiene maintenance and long-term follow-up emerged as the universal determinants of uneventful implant treatment across all conditions.

Clinically: implants are safe and viable for edentulous autoimmune-disease patients under controlled disease and strict recall protocols. Secondary Sjögren's syndrome and erosive OLP patients carry the highest peri-implant soft-tissue risk and require the most vigilant monitoring.

## Key Contributions

- Broadest single synthesis across the autoimmune-disease spectrum — 9 diseases in one review versus prior disease-specific reviews (e.g., Sjögren-only, OLP-only, sclerosis-only).
- Demonstrates implant survival comparable to the general population despite the autoimmune burden.
- Identifies secondary Sjögren's syndrome and erosive OLP as the specific higher-risk subgroups for peri-implant mucositis and marginal bone loss.
- Quantifies the corticosteroid-control effect in OLP: 100% survival with LDSC protocol vs. 24% without (Aboushelib & Elsafi).
- Links poor glycemic control (HbA1c ≥8.0%) to multiple implant failures in the only T1DM patient with inadequate control.
- Formally argues against blanket contraindication of implants in autoimmune-disease patients; reframes the clinical question from "is this contraindicated?" to "what modifications of protocol and follow-up are required?"

## Methodology

- Design: PRISMA-guided systematic critical review; narrative synthesis — no meta-analysis possible due to heterogeneity.
- Databases: Google Scholar + PubMed; search stop date September 2021.
- Inclusion: full-text, human, English-language studies of autoimmune-disease patients treated with dental implants.
- Primary outcomes: implant survival rate, peri-implant complications (PIM, peri-implantitis, MBL).
- Study types: predominantly retrospective cohorts, case series, and case reports; few prospective controlled studies (especially for rarer diseases).

### Sjögren's Syndrome (SS)

Survival comparable to the general population in most studies. Korfage et al. (n=50, 140 implants): 97% at 46 months. Albrecht et al. (n=32, 104 implants): 95.2%. Siddiqui et al.: 87% overall but all failures were in the preloading phase; loaded implants achieved 100% at 40 months. Chrcanovic et al. (n=19, 107 implants): 97.2% at 125-month mean follow-up with increased MBL.

Two key vulnerabilities in SS patients: (1) peri-implant soft-tissue susceptibility — PIM prevalence up to 94% (Korfage) due to hyposalivation and impaired oral self-clearance; (2) secondary SS + RA co-occurrence → significantly increased MBL and bleeding index from chronic corticosteroid use and reduced hand dexterity impairing oral hygiene.

Conclusion: no contraindication; peri-implant soft-tissue monitoring and enhanced hygiene support are mandatory.

### Oral Lichen Planus (OLP)

Hernandez et al. (n=18 OLP, 56 implants vs controls): 100% OLP vs. 96.8% control. López-Jornet et al. (n=16): 96.4% OLP vs. 92% control. The critical modifier is disease control status:

- Aboushelib & Elsafi: 24% survival (uncontrolled active OLP) vs. 100% survival (oral corticosteroids + low-energy soft-tissue laser protocol).
- Khamis et al. (4-year follow-up, LDSC vs. no LDSC vs. controls): LDSC group MBL = controls; no-LDSC group showed significant MBL increase and 13.7% OLP recurrence.
- Anitua et al. (short implants ≤8.5 mm + prophylactic corticosteroids, n=23, 66 implants): 98% success at 68-month follow-up.

Desquamative gingivitis (DG) in erosive OLP is associated with higher PIM and PID rates. Implant placement does not negatively affect OLP disease course (Czerninski: 100% success, no change in OLP signs/symptoms). Rare but important: one case of oral squamous cell carcinoma arising adjacent to a peri-implantitis site in an OLP patient (Noguchi et al.).

Conclusion: not contraindicated in controlled OLP; LDSC protocol mandatory for active/erosive forms.

### Type 1 Diabetes (T1DM)

Most studies mixed T1DM and T2DM; limited T1DM-exclusive data. Sannino et al. (RCT-design, n=53 T1DM vs 53 controls, 205 implants, 24 months): 95.2% vs. 97.0% — no statistically significant difference. De Araújo Nobre et al.: T1DM worse than T2DM (80% vs. 90.5%); T1DM MBL exceeded 0.2 mm/year (failure criterion years 1–5). Fiorellini et al. (n=40 well-controlled, 78 months): 85.7% — most failures within year 1 of loading. Alberti et al. (n=19 diabetics, 10-year follow-up): 96.51% for T1DM; the only patient with HbA1c ≥8.0% suffered multiple implant failures.

Key message: glycemic control is the primary determinant of implant success in T1DM. Failures cluster in early healing/loading phases. Peri-operative monitoring for hypoglycemia is essential.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

All 6 studies showed 100% implant survival. El-Sherbini (prospective, n=14 RA vs controls): no survival difference. Case reports (3 studies): all 100% survival. Primary concern is RA+SS co-occurrence (covered under SS section), not isolated RA.

### Miscellaneous Conditions

- **SSc** (4 studies, mostly case reports): all 100% survival; microstomia is a logistical challenge but not a contraindication.
- **CD** (3 studies): Alsaadi et al. reported 66.7% survival (notably lower); other CD studies: 100%. Evidence too sparse for conclusions.
- **SLE** (3 studies): predominantly 100% survival; one patient with SLE + CVID: 93% at 18 months.
- **MMP** (2 case reports): 100% survival at 8 and 180 months.
- **PV** (1 case report): 100% survival (2 implants, 32 months).

### Clinical Conclusions

No evidence-based specific contraindications for dental implants in any of the 9 autoimmune diseases reviewed. Required modifications:
1. **OLP**: prophylactic LDSC protocol + disease control before and during implant phase.
2. **T1DM**: strict glycemic control (HbA1c target), peri-operative glucose monitoring, infection surveillance.
3. **Universal**: scrupulous oral hygiene + long-term peri-implant monitoring for all autoimmune patients (especially secondary SS and erosive OLP).
