## Summary

This landmark narrative review, published in a special Periodontology 2000 volume celebrating the 50th anniversary of osseointegration, provides a comprehensive account of the evolution of implant dentistry from the 1960s through 2016.

**Historical foundations**: The field was transformed by two independent pioneers — Professor P. I. Brånemark (Gothenburg) who established osseointegration in edentulous patients using machined titanium screw implants (Brånemark System), and Professor André Schroeder (Bern) who simultaneously developed the ITI implant with a titanium plasma-sprayed (TPS) surface and coined "functional ankylosis." These two schools replaced the unreliable blade and subperiosteal implants and established the scientific basis for modern implant therapy.

**Surface evolution**: The shift from machined (turned) surfaces (Sa ≈ 0.5 µm) to moderately rough surfaces (Sa 1–2 µm) — exemplified by SLA (Straumann) and TiUnite (Nobel Biocare) — represented the most consequential material science advance. Moderately rough surfaces accelerate bone apposition, increase bone-implant contact (BIC), and enabled faster loading protocols.

**Bone augmentation**: GBR using barrier membranes (resorbable collagen preferred; DBBM as filler) and sinus floor elevation (lateral window approach for RBH ≥4 mm) dramatically widened implant indications to anatomically deficient sites, decoupling implant therapy from bone volume constraints present at the time of tooth loss.

**Extraction socket protocols**: The ITI consensus on implant placement timing (Types 1–4) rationalized the choice between immediate, early, and late placement based on socket healing biology, risk profile, and esthetic zone demands.

**Digital implant dentistry**: CBCT enables 3D preoperative assessment (nerve proximity, bone volume, density) with lower radiation than medical CT. Computer-guided surgery and CAD/CAM prosthetics are identified as major current trends though RCT-level evidence on long-term outcomes was still emerging as of 2016.

**Open questions**:
1. *Peri-implantitis*: Prevalence estimates vary widely due to non-standardized case definitions; biofilm-driven etiology is mainstream but an alternative foreign-body reaction hypothesis (Albrektsson) challenges the inflammatory model.
2. *Zirconia implants*: First-generation ceramic implants failed; second-generation Y-TZP implants show early promise but lack long-term RCT data.
3. *Aging patients*: Increasing mean age of implant recipients requires protocol adaptations (medical comorbidities, polypharmacy, reduced bone quality).

## Key Contributions

- Definitive historiography of dual founding-school origins (Brånemark vs. Schroeder) with equal credit
- Establishes Sa 1–2 µm as the moderately rough surface target still cited in guidelines today
- First major review to formally frame peri-implantitis as an open controversy alongside the foreign-body reaction debate
- Comprehensive synthesis of GBR + sinus augmentation evidence underpinning current ITI consensus protocols
- Identifies digital implant dentistry as the field's near-term transformative trend

## Methodology

- **Type**: Commissioned narrative review for Periodontology 2000 special volume (Vol. 73, 2017)
- **No PRISMA**: Narrative design; expert-selected evidence base
- **Scope**: 1960s–2016; >100 references spanning preclinical histology, RCTs, SRs, and cohort studies
- **Authors' affiliation**: University of Bern (Buser), University of Gothenburg (Sennerby), Ghent University (De Bruyn)

## Results

| Domain | Key Evidence/Benchmark |
|---|---|
| OI definition | Direct bone-to-implant anchorage without fibrous tissue interposition |
| Optimal surface roughness | Sa 1–2 µm (moderately rough); machined Sa ~0.5 µm inferior in early BIC |
| SLA surface | >10 years high-quality evidence; enables early loading at 3–6 weeks in good bone |
| GBR horizontal defects | Simultaneous GBR at implant placement; resorbable membrane + DBBM standard |
| GBR vertical defects | Staged approach (regenerate first, implant second) for large deficits |
| Sinus floor elevation | Lateral window: RBH ≥4 mm reliable; transcrestal: RBH ≥6 mm |
| Immediate placement (Type 1) | Predictable with ≥1 mm buccal bone; ≤2 mm gap → DBBM fill; experienced surgeon required |
| CBCT | Superior 3D anatomy vs. panoramic; lower dose than medical CT; standard for complex cases |
| Peri-implantitis prevalence | ~10–20% patient-level at ≥5 years; wide CI due to case definition heterogeneity |
| Zirconia implants | Y-TZP promising preclinically; no long-term RCT data as of 2017 |
