## One-line Summary

In-vitro study (33 Dentium SuperLine II implants, 3 groups of n=11): both sodium bicarbonate (40 µm) and erythritol (14 µm) air-abrasion powders removed biofilm far better than untreated control (mean OD 0.130 / 0.129 vs 0.728; p<0.05) with no significant difference between the two powders (p>0.05) — favoring smaller-particle erythritol for lower implant-surface damage.

## Summary

Peri-implantitis maintenance hinges on removing biofilm from the implant surface while minimizing surface damage. Air-polishing (air abrasion) therapy is a standard mechanical-debridement option, but the optimal powder is unsettled. This in-vitro study compared the two most common air-polishing powders — **sodium bicarbonate (SB, 40 µm)** and **erythritol (ERY, 14 µm)** — on bone-level implants to test the intuitive hypothesis that the **larger particle removes biofilm more efficiently**.

It did not. Both powders cleaned biofilm equally well and far better than no treatment, with no significant SB-vs-ERY difference. Because prior literature shows SB is the most surface-damaging powder (crater-like defects that can promote plaque accumulation and affect osseointegration) while ERY/glycine barely alter the surface, the equal cleaning shifts the clinical recommendation toward the **smaller-particle erythritol**. The study did not measure surface roughness directly (stereomicroscope only), and all conclusions are bounded by the idealized in-vitro, single-donor model.

## Key Contributions

- Direct head-to-head in-vitro comparison of SB (40 µm) vs ERY (14 µm) air-polishing powders for implant-surface biofilm removal.
- Refuted the larger-particle-cleans-better hypothesis: SB and ERY equivalent in biofilm removal.
- Combined the equal-cleaning finding with prior surface-damage evidence to argue for ERY (smaller particle, less surface damage) as the preferred clinical powder.

## Methodology

- **Design**: in-vitro, 33 bone-level implants (Dentium SuperLine II, 4.0 × 8.0 mm), 3 groups of n=11 — control (no treatment), SB, ERY.
- **Biofilm**: 50% stimulated saliva coating (24 h, 37 °C) + subgingival plaque inoculum (single donor) adjusted to 0.5 McFarland, 48 h anaerobic incubation, PBS washes.
- **Treatment**: AIRFLOW Prophylaxis Master handpiece (EMS); 15 s, power level 5, ~2 mm distance, manual rotation; identical settings for both powders. Powders: AIRFLOW Powder Classic Comfort (SB) and AIRFLOW Powder Plus (ERY).
- **Readout**: crystal-violet 0.5% staining → dissolved in 20% acetone/ethanol → optical density (OD) on microplate reader (Synergy H1, BioTek); OD corrected by negative control (0.0635). Surface imaging by stereomicroscope (all groups) and SEM (JSM-6610LV).
- **Statistics**: one-way ANOVA + post-hoc Dunnett's T3; Shapiro–Wilk normality; SPSS v28; p<0.05.

## Results

| Group | Powder (particle size) | Mean OD ± SD |
|---|---|---|
| Control | none | 0.728 ± 0.188 |
| SB | sodium bicarbonate (40 µm) | 0.130 ± 0.068 |
| ERY | erythritol (14 µm) | 0.129 ± 0.049 |

- Both SB and ERY significantly better than control (p<0.05).
- No significant difference between SB and ERY (p>0.05) — larger-particle hypothesis not supported.
- Stereomicroscope: control fully biofilm-covered; both treated groups showed only sparse residual spots, including hard-to-reach thread/valley surfaces; similar topography between groups 2 and 3.
- SEM: no marked surface-topography differences between SB and ERY groups (surface roughness not quantified).
- Literature-based caveat: SB is the most surface-damaging powder (crater-like defects); ERY/GLY barely alter the surface → smaller particle preferred clinically despite equal cleaning.
- **Limitations**: no direct surface-roughness measurement; idealized in-vitro setting (accessibility/microflora differ clinically); single-donor biofilm; crystal-violet OD does not distinguish live/dead cells (no antimicrobial readout).
