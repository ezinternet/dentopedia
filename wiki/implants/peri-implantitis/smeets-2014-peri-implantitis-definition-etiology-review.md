## Summary

Smeets et al. (2014) provide a comprehensive narrative overview of peri-implant disease published in Head & Face Medicine. The review distinguishes mucositis (reversible soft-tissue inflammation, up to 80% prevalence) from peri-implantitis (progressive irreversible bone destruction, 28–56% prevalence). The histological basis for susceptibility is explained by peri-implant tissue's reduced vascularity and parallel collagen fiber orientation compared to the natural periodontium. The review identifies the microbial etiology (polymicrobial anaerobic infection; Staphylococcus aureus with high predictive value), major risk factors led by smoking (+4.7× risk when combined with periodontitis history; +0.16 mm/year bone loss), and a four-step CIST treatment escalation from mechanical debridement through regenerative surgery. The authors note that while conservative methods suffice for mucositis and mild peri-implantitis, advanced disease requires surgical intervention — resective for supra-alveolar (class II) defects, regenerative/GBR for intraosseous (class I) defects.

## Key Contributions

- **Dual-entity framework**: Mucositis (reversible, soft tissue) vs peri-implantitis (irreversible, bone-destructive) — clinical and histological differentiation
- **Prevalence estimates**: Mucositis ≤80%; peri-implantitis 28–56%; 20% of patients / 10% of implants (Mombelli re-analysis)
- **Risk factor hierarchy**: Smoking > periodontitis history > poor oral hygiene > systemic disease > iatrogenic ("cementitis", poor soft tissue quality)
- **Bone defect classification**: Schwarz class I (intraosseous) / class II (supra-alveolar); Spiekermann I–IV morphologies
- **CIST protocol**: Stepwise escalation — A (mechanical) → B (antiseptics) → C (antibiotics) → D (surgery)
- **Biomarker gap**: Salivary markers (osteocalcin, TRAP, DKK-1, OPG, CatK) cannot reliably differentiate peri-implantitis from other periodontal inflammation

## Methodology

Narrative review. Literature search strategy not formally reported. Draws on European Workshop on Periodontology consensus reports (6th EWP), clinical studies, meta-analyses, and histological/molecular research. No PRISMA flow.

## Results

**Epidemiology**
- Mucositis: up to 80% incidence
- Peri-implantitis: 28–56% (Lindhe & Meyle 6th EWP); 20% patients / 10% implants (Mombelli)
- Periodontitis history: 6× increased incidence (Zitzmann)
- After 10 years: 10–50% of implants show signs of peri-implantitis

**Microbiology**
- Key pathogens: Prevotella intermedia, P. nigrescens, Streptococcus constellatus, Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans, Porphyromonas gingivalis, Treponema denticola, Tannerella forsythia
- Staphylococcus aureus: 80% positive predictive value, 90% negative predictive value for peri-implantitis
- 71.7% antimicrobial resistance rate in 120-patient cohort (Rams)

**Risk factors**
- Smoking: +0.16 mm/year bone loss; smoking + periodontitis history = 4.7× risk
- Periodontitis history: strongest non-smoking risk factor
- Maxillary implants at significantly higher risk than mandibular

**Treatment outcomes**
- Conservative (debridement ± antiseptics ± antibiotics): effective for mucositis and moderate peri-implantitis
- Er:YAG laser and PDT: viable adjuncts
- Surgery: more effective than conservative in advanced disease
  - Resective (osseous recontouring): class II supra-alveolar defects
  - Regenerative (GBR with bone substitutes/membranes): class I intraosseous defects

**Histological/molecular**
- MMP-8 elevated up to 971% in peri-implant vs healthy tissue
- Increased VEGF, nitric oxide 1/3, lymphocytes, leukocytes, Ki-67 in lesions
