## One-line Summary

Multicenter single-blind RCT (n=46) showing adjunctive subgingival 2% minocycline ointment reduces probing depth and BOP at 12 weeks versus debridement alone in peri-implantitis, with transient microbiologic benefits at 8 weeks but insufficient follow-up for radiographic conclusions.

## Summary

This multicenter Korean RCT (Lee et al., 2026; two university dental hospitals) evaluated whether subgingival placement of 2% minocycline hydrochloride ointment as an adjunct to mechanical debridement improved outcomes in nonsurgical peri-implantitis treatment over 12 weeks.

48 participants were randomized; 46 analyzed per-protocol. The test group (n=22) received subgingival debridement plus minocycline ointment at baseline and weeks 1–3; the control group (n=24) received debridement plus placebo ointment on the same schedule.

At 12 weeks, the test group showed statistically superior probing pocket depth (PD) reduction and lower BOP frequency. Microbiologic benefits (lower peri-implant pathogen counts) were significant at 8 weeks (p < 0.05) but less consistent by 12 weeks. Early post-treatment swelling was lower in the test group at 3 weeks. Radiographic marginal bone level (MBL) results could not be reliably interpreted given the short follow-up. No significant difference in OHRQoL was found between groups.

The authors conclude that locally delivered 2% minocycline may offer short-term clinical and exploratory microbiologic advantages over debridement alone, while avoiding systemic antibiotic exposure.

## Key Contributions

- Multicenter RCT design (two Korean university centers) strengthens external validity for local antibiotic delivery in peri-implantitis
- Demonstrates statistically significant improvement in PD and BOP at 12 weeks with adjunctive minocycline ointment
- Microbiologic analysis adds mechanism: pathogen suppression is strongest at 8 weeks, less sustained at 12
- Patient-reported outcomes (OHRQoL, swelling) included — early post-treatment comfort advantage in test group
- Supports local drug delivery (LDD) as a systemic-antibiotic-sparing strategy

## Methodology

- **Design**: Single-blind (examiner-blind), randomized, placebo-controlled, multicenter RCT
- **n**: 48 randomized → 46 per-protocol (2 test-group withdrawals post-randomization)
- **Test** (n=22): subgingival mechanical debridement + 2% minocycline HCl ointment (subgingival placement)
- **Control** (n=24): subgingival mechanical debridement + placebo ointment
- **Treatment schedule**: Baseline + weeks 1, 2, 3 (4 applications total)
- **Follow-up**: 12 weeks
- **Primary outcomes**: PD, BOP
- **Secondary outcomes**: MBL (radiographic), microbiologic (peri-implant pathogens), patient discomfort, OHRQoL

## Results

| Outcome | Timepoint | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Probing Depth (PD) | 12 weeks | Test < Control (baseline-adjusted, significant) |
| Bleeding on Probing (BOP) | 12 weeks | Test < Control (less frequent) |
| Peri-implant pathogens | 8 weeks | Test < Control (p < 0.05) |
| Peri-implant pathogens | 12 weeks | Between-group differences less consistent |
| MBL (radiographic) | 12 weeks | Insufficient follow-up; cautious interpretation required |
| Early swelling | 3 weeks | Test < Control (p < 0.05) |
| OHRQoL | 12 weeks | No significant between-group difference |

**Bottom line**: Short-term clinical benefit (PD, BOP) with adjunctive minocycline; microbiologic benefit transient; no long-term bone stability data available from this study.
