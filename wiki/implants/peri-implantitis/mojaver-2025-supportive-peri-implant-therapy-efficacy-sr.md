## One-line Summary

Systematic review (25 studies: 9 RCTs, 13 cohorts, 3 case-control; narrative synthesis) finding that individualized, risk-based supportive peri-implant therapy (SPiT) recall intervals consistently improve probing depth and bleeding on probing versus fixed-interval or no maintenance — heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis.

## Summary

Peri-implant mucositis and peri-implantitis are increasingly prevalent, necessitating evidence-based maintenance strategies. This systematic review (JADA 2026, vol. 157[2], pp. 141–152) evaluated whether **professionally delivered supportive peri-implant therapy (SPiT)** prevents or slows disease progression, with primary outcomes of radiographic bone loss, probing depth (PD), bleeding on probing (BoP), and patient-reported satisfaction.

The search covered MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane Library, and Web of Science through April 30, 2025. From 25 included studies (9 RCTs, 13 cohorts, 3 case-control; n = 60–250 per study; follow-up 3 months to 7 years), a consistent signal emerged: **individualized, risk-based recall intervals outperform fixed-interval maintenance or no maintenance** across all key outcomes. Due to high clinical and methodological heterogeneity — variable SPiT interventions, patient populations, adjunctive therapies, follow-up durations — a formal meta-analysis was not performed; results are synthesized narratively.

The authors position this as a more comprehensive update than prior reviews (Lin et al., Ramanauskaite & Tervonen, Roccuzzo et al.) by explicitly stratifying SPiT by individualized risk-based intervals and by critically examining subgroup outcomes related to systemic conditions (smoking, diabetes) and adjunctive therapies.

## Key Contributions

1. **Risk-based recall is consistently superior**: individualized recall intervals (typically 3–4 months for high-risk patients) outperform fixed annual schedules across cohort studies and RCTs.
2. **Quantitative benchmarks** for PD reduction (1.0–1.5 mm advantage) and BoP reduction (10–25 percentage points) vs no maintenance or fixed-interval care.
3. **Bone loss attenuation**: risk-based SPiT limited marginal bone loss to ≤1 mm over ≥24 months vs ~1.5 mm in controls (7 cohort studies).
4. **Adjunctive therapy signal**: aPDT is the only adjunct with repeated independent evidence of meaningful additional PD reduction (≈0.6–0.8 mm); laser therapy and growth-factor gels show no consistent benefit beyond mechanical debridement alone.
5. **Systemic comorbidity evidence gap**: only 20% of studies quantified smokers, only 8% documented diabetes — the review underscores this as the field's most critical reporting gap.
6. **Disease incidence**: long-term cohorts (≥36 months) report 40–70% lower peri-implantitis incidence or implant loss in SPiT-adherent vs non-adherent patients.

## Methodology

- **Search**: MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane Library, Web of Science; through 2025-04-30. PRISMA 2020 compliant.
- **Included study types**: RCTs (9), prospective or retrospective cohort (13), case-control (3).
- **Participant range**: 60–250 per study.
- **Follow-up range**: 3 months to 7 years.
- **SPiT modalities**: 19 studies mechanical-only; 6 added adjuncts at recall (air-polishing, aPDT, chlorhexidine rinse, tetracycline fibers, antimicrobial ointment, laser).
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane ROB 2.0 (RCTs); Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (observational, 0–9).
- **Synthesis**: Narrative — meta-analysis was deemed inappropriate due to substantial heterogeneity.
- **Control definitions**: "no treatment" = no scheduled professional maintenance; "standard care" = fixed 6- or 12-month prophylaxis without risk stratification.

## Results

### Probing depth (PD)
- SPiT groups showed mean PD reductions of 1.0–1.5 mm more than control groups.
- Largest single gain: −1.5 mm (Heitz-Mayfield & Salvi 2016, 5-year RCT: 3.5 mm SPiT vs 5.0 mm control).
- Risk-based protocols (Monje 2015, Frisch 2019, Ravidà 2020) consistently outperformed standard care: 3.0–3.2 mm (SPiT) vs 4.5–4.8 mm (standard).

### Bleeding on probing (BoP)
- 11 trials reported BoP; SPiT groups showed relative decreases of 10–25 percentage points vs control.
- Example: Frisch 2019 — 12% (SPiT) vs 32% (no therapy); Monje 2015 — 10% vs 30%.

### Marginal bone loss
- 7 cohort studies quantified bone loss.
- Risk-based SPiT limited loss to ≤1 mm over ≥24 months (e.g., Costa 2019: ~0.8 mm SPiT vs ~1.4 mm no therapy; Frisch 2019: 1.02 mm vs 1.53 mm).
- Roccuzzo 2018: low-risk patients ~0.7 mm; high-risk ~1.0–1.1 mm; no therapy group substantially higher.

### Disease progression / implant loss
- Long-term cohorts (≥36 months): 40–70% lower peri-implantitis incidence or implant loss in adherent SPiT patients vs non-adherent.
- Stepwise increases in inflammation and disease with poorer compliance (Bertoldi 2019, Frisch 2019).
- Case-control studies: consistently higher odds of peri-implantitis in erratic/no-maintenance patients.

### Adjunctive therapies
- aPDT (Lin 2019): additional PD reduction ~0.8 mm, BoP reduction; confirmed across >1 independent study — the only adjunct with consistent additive benefit.
- Growth-factor gels (Khouly 2020): modest additional gains (≤0.8 mm PD) in one RCT.
- Laser therapy (Almarzooq 2023): failed to outperform mechanical debridement alone.
- Chlorhexidine rinse, tetracycline fibers, air-polishing: benefits confined to single small trials.

### Systemic factors
- Only 5 studies (20%) quantified smokers; only 2 (8%) documented diabetes.
- Smokers on quarterly risk-adjusted SPiT: fewer peri-implantitis recurrences vs smokers with no maintenance.
- One cohort with well-controlled type 2 diabetes: ~30% less marginal bone loss with risk-based SPiT vs fixed schedules.
- No study assessed cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, or immunosuppression.

### Patient satisfaction
- Reported on scales 1–10 or 1–5; SPiT groups scored 7.8–9.5 across studies, consistently higher than control groups.

### Risk of bias
- 7 of 9 RCTs: low risk across all domains; 2 RCTs: some concerns (incomplete outcome-assessor blinding).
- NOS scores: 7–9 of 9 for cohort/case-control studies — overall low risk of bias.

### Clinical recommendations from the authors
- Abandon one-size-fits-all annual recall in favor of genuinely individualized maintenance (often every 3–4 months for high-risk patients).
- When conventional debridement fails to quell inflammation, consider aPDT as the best-supported adjunct.
- More frequent recalls and reinforced home-care instruction are prudent for smokers and patients with diabetes until stronger evidence is available.
