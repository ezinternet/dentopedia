Chinese multi-center expert consensus (IJOS 2025, 29 authors) establishing a site- and residual-KM-width-driven decision tree for selecting ARF, FGG, SFGG, or soft tissue substitutes for peri-implant keratinized mucosa augmentation at second-stage surgery.

## Summary

This expert consensus from 20 leading Chinese dental institutions addresses a practical clinical gap: how to systematically choose among surgical techniques for peri-implant keratinized mucosa (PIKM, 각화점막) augmentation specifically at the second-stage (implant uncovery) surgery.

The consensus establishes that ≤2 mm of PIKM is associated with peri-implantitis, biofilm accumulation, mucosal recession, marginal bone loss (MBL), and patient discomfort. The minimum target is ≥4 mm total KM (≥2 mm buccal + ≥2 mm lingual/palatal). Second-stage surgery is presented as the optimal augmentation window because osseointegration is confirmed, soft tissue is stable, and augmentation integrates into a mandatory surgical visit — reducing future intervention burden.

Four main technique categories are reviewed and compared:
- **ARF** (Apically Repositioned Flap): repositions existing KM apically; no donor site; posterior maxilla first choice; limited by pre-existing KM stock
- **FGG** (Free Gingival Graft): gold standard for width and thickness; ~4.1 mm gain; 12–16% contraction; donor site morbidity limits patient acceptance
- **SFGG** (Strip FGG): narrow graft strip + soft tissue substitute; reduces donor harvest; esthetic zone compromise solution
- **Soft Tissue Substitutes** (ADM, XCM): no donor site; shorter surgery; higher contraction (34–72%); lower KM gain vs FGG; better patient comfort

## Key Contributions

1. **Explicit decision tree for second-stage surgery**: integrates implant site (maxillary anterior/posterior, mandibular), residual KM width, mucosa biotype, and patient preference into a single actionable flowchart — the first such consensus document dedicated to this timing window.
2. **Mandibular threshold protocol**: residual KM <2 mm → FGG must be performed *before* second-stage; 2–3 mm → SFGG or FGG; 3–4 mm → ARF, SFGG, or FGG eligible.
3. **Esthetic zone guidance**: SFGG + soft tissue substitute preferred over FGG in the maxillary anterior zone to avoid color "patchiness."
4. **Biotype integration**: Thin mucosa biotype → FGG or MARF required (connective tissue component increases thickness); ARF or XCM alone insufficient.
5. **Technique comparison table**: single-table synthesis of advantages/disadvantages enabling rapid chairside reference.

## Methodology

- **Type**: Expert consensus (narrative review), not SR/MA
- **Authorship**: 29 specialists from 20 Chinese academic centers (West China, Tongji, Peking, SJTU, Sun Yat-sen, Zhejiang, etc.)
- **Evidence base**: Review of published RCTs, prospective/retrospective cohorts, and prior international consensus statements (Osteology Foundation 2018, EAO 2021, Sanz 2022); expert opinion where trial evidence is sparse
- **Scope**: PIKM augmentation at second-stage surgery only; does not cover pre-implant or post-restoration timing

## Results

**Technique performance at a glance**:

| Technique | Mean KM gain | Contraction rate | Donor site | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ARF | ~1.15 mm | 40–70% | None | Posterior maxilla optimal; mandible needs ≥3–4 mm residual KM |
| FGG | ~4.1 mm | 12–16% | Palate | Gold standard; thin biotype / <2 mm KM; donor morbidity |
| ADM | ~2.59 mm | 56–72% | None (allograft) | Delayed healing +5–20 days vs FGG |
| XCM | ~1.8 mm | 34–51% | None (xenograft) | Best patient comfort; lower KM gain; esthetic integration good |

**Decision tree summary (second-stage)**:
- Maxillary anterior (esthetic zone): SFGG + substitute first; FGG if aesthetics not priority
- Maxillary posterior: ARF (palatal KM typically ample)
- Mandible, residual KM ≥3–4 mm: ARF, SFGG, or FGG
- Mandible, residual KM 2–3 mm: SFGG or FGG (individualize by preference/aesthetics/technique)
- Mandible, residual KM <2 mm: FGG before second-stage surgery

**Mucosa thickness**: Thin biotype mandates FGG or MARF to add connective tissue bulk; ARF alone or XCM alone does not reliably address thickness deficiency.
