## One-line Summary

EBD critical summary (Mourão & Dos Santos Pereira 2025) appraising a 2-arm RCT of tapered vs cylindrical implants over 6 weeks of early healing: tapered gave higher insertion torque (MIT) but cylindrical gave higher ISQ/IST at 6 weeks; surgical technique mattered more than macrogeometry, and unstandardized osteotomy confounds the design comparison.

## Summary

According to PubMed ([DOI 10.1038/s41432-025-01202-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41432-025-01202-y)), this is an Evidence-Based Dentistry **critical summary / structured commentary** — not a primary trial — by Mourão & Dos Santos Pereira (2025), appraising a separately published parallel-group RCT (Charoenniwassakul et al., *J Clin Periodontol* 2025) that compared tapered vs cylindrical implant stability across six weeks of early healing using Maximum Insertion Torque (MIT), Implant Stability Quotient (ISQ, via resonance frequency analysis), and Implant Stability Test (IST, via percussion). This page is ingested **abstract-only** (full commentary text not retrieved). The appraised RCT found tapered implants achieved significantly higher MIT, while cylindrical implants showed higher ISQ and IST at six weeks; both designs displayed the physiological transient stability dip and both reached stability adequate for early loading in good-quality healed bone. Critically, the commentary flags that osteotomy preparation was not standardized between groups, so differences cannot be attributed to macrogeometry alone.

Clinically: in good-quality healed bone, both tapered and cylindrical implants reach early-loading-adequate stability, and surgical technique (notably under-preparation/drilling protocol) drives primary stability more than implant shape — do not pick macrogeometry expecting a stability advantage on its own. High insertion torque (tapered) does not guarantee higher 6-week RFA/percussion stability. These conclusions do not extend to immediate loading, regenerated bone, or low-density bone sites.

## Key Contributions

- Independent critical appraisal of a head-to-head **tapered vs cylindrical** macrogeometry RCT on early-healing stability.
- Documents a dissociation between **mechanical** (insertion torque) and **secondary/biological** (ISQ, IST) stability across designs.
- Identifies a key confounder: **non-standardized osteotomy preparation** between arms.
- Reframes macrogeometry as secondary to **surgical technique** for primary stability in healed bone.

## Methodology

Appraised RCT (as summarized in the commentary):
- Randomized, two parallel groups: tapered vs cylindrical implants; six-week follow-up.
- Outcomes: MIT (insertion torque), ISQ (resonance frequency analysis), IST (percussion analysis).
- Population: healthy adults, posterior edentulous areas healed ≥2 months, no grafting; smokers and patients with uncontrolled systemic disease, bone-metabolism conditions, or maxillomandibular pathology excluded.
- Statistics: repeated-measures ANOVA; multiple imputation for missing follow-up data; correlations among under-preparation, torque, and secondary stability; mechanical vs biological stability interpreted separately.

## Results

- Tapered implants: **significantly higher MIT** (maximum insertion torque).
- Cylindrical implants: **higher ISQ and IST** at six weeks.
- Both designs: **transient stability reduction** (early-healing stability dip).
- Osteotomy preparation **not standardized** between groups → differences not attributable to design alone.
- Both designs reached **stability adequate for early loading** in good-quality healed bone.
- **Surgical technique > implant macrogeometry** for primary stability.
- Conclusions **not applicable** to immediate loading, regenerated bone, or low-density bone.
