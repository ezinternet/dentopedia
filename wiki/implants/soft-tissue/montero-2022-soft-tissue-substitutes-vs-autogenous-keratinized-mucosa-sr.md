## One-line Summary

Systematic review (11 studies, 282 subjects, PROSPERO-registered) finding FGG augments peri-implant keratinized mucosa significantly more than soft tissue substitutes overall (WMD −0.9 mm, p = .001), but xenogeneic collagen matrices (Mucograft®) are not significantly inferior to autogenous grafts when analyzed alone (WMD −0.8 mm, p = .062), and reduce surgical time by ~18.5 min with better esthetics and lower postoperative pain.

## Summary

This PROSPERO-registered systematic review (CRD42020190501), commissioned for the 2021 SEPA/DGI/Osteology Foundation consensus workshop on peri-implant soft tissues, evaluated 11 investigations (5 RCTs, 1 CCT, 5 case series; n = 282 subjects; ≥440 implants) comparing soft tissue substitutes with autogenous grafts for peri-implant keratinized mucosa (KM) augmentation.

Two focused PICOS questions were addressed: PICOS #1 compared substitute vs autogenous graft efficacy in controlled trials; PICOS #2 assessed the effectiveness of substitutes alone across all study designs. The primary outcome was change in KM width (mm). Secondary outcomes included shrinkage, surgical time, postoperative pain, esthetics, and peri-implant clinical parameters.

**Core finding**: Autogenous grafts — particularly free gingival grafts (FGG) — are significantly more effective than soft tissue substitutes overall. However, xenogeneic collagen matrices (predominantly Mucograft®) showed non-significant difference vs autogenous grafts when considered alone, and achieved clinically meaningful KM gains exceeding 2 mm, with substantially lower morbidity.

## Key Contributions

1. **Quantified the KM gap**: Substitutes overall yield 0.9 mm less KM gain than autogenous grafts (WMD −0.9 mm; 95% CI [−1.4; −0.3]; p = .001), driven primarily by FGG superiority (WMD −1.0 mm vs FGG; p < .001).

2. **Xenograft non-inferiority signal**: When only xenografts are compared to autogenous grafts (5 studies), the difference is not statistically significant (WMD −0.8 mm; 95% CI [−1.6; 0.0]; p = .062), supporting clinical use as an alternative.

3. **Collagen matrix vs CTG equivalence**: The sole RCT comparing Mucograft® to CTG (Lorenzo 2012) found no KM difference (WMD 0.0 mm; p = .948) — the most clinically actionable finding for surgeons avoiding palatal harvest.

4. **Substitute effectiveness alone**: Pooled across all study designs, xenografts achieve +3.5 mm KM gain (WME; 95% CI [2.5; 4.6]) — well above the ≥2 mm clinical threshold. Allografts (ADM) achieve only +1.6 mm.

5. **Morbidity advantages**: Substitutes reduce surgical time by ~18.5 min, lower postoperative pain and analgesic use, and provide superior esthetic outcomes vs FGG (better blending/texture), comparable to CTG.

6. **No shrinkage disadvantage**: No significant difference in overall dimensional change between substitutes and autogenous grafts (WMD −3.7%; p = .256), though allografts individually shrink ~52%.

## Methodology

- **Study types included**: RCTs and CCTs for PICOS #1; additionally cohort studies and case series for PICOS #2 (minimum follow-up 6 months, ≥10 patients / ≥5 per group)
- **Substitutes evaluated**: Porcine xenogeneic collagen matrices (Mucograft® n = 7, Mucoderm® n = 1); acellular dermal matrix allografts (AlloDerm®, SureDerm® n = 2)
- **Controls**: FGG (5 studies) or CTG (1 study)
- **Surgical technique**: Apically positioned flap (APF) + graft in all but one (bilaminar, Papi 2018)
- **Timing**: Post-implant placement (8 studies), simultaneous (1), pre-implant (1)
- **Risk of bias**: RoB 2.0 (RCTs); ROBINS-I (CCT); NOS (case series) — all RCTs had some concerns or high risk of bias
- **Meta-analysis**: WMD/WME with DerSimonian–Laird random-effects; high heterogeneity (I² > 90%) in most pooled estimates; no significant publication bias for head-to-head comparisons

**Key individual studies** (PICOS #1):
- Lorenzo 2012 (Spain): Mucograft® vs CTG; 22 pts; 6 mo → KM: no difference
- Basegmez 2013 (Turkey): AlloDerm® vs FGG; 36 pts; 6 mo → FGG superior
- Schmitt 2016 (Germany): Mucograft® vs FGG; 176 implants; up to 5 yr → FGG superior short-term; both maintained at 5 yr
- Vellis 2019 (USA): Mucograft® vs FGG; 30 pts split-mouth; 6 mo → FGG superior
- Tarasenko 2020 (Russia): Mucograft® vs FGG; 40 pts; 6 mo → FGG superior
- Solonko 2022 (Spain; unpublished at time): Mucograft® vs FGG in peri-implantitis patients; 46 pts; 6 mo

## Results

### KM Augmentation — Head-to-Head (PICOS #1)

| Comparison | n | WMD (mm) | 95% CI | p | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| All substitutes vs all autogenous | 6 | −0.9 | [−1.4; −0.3] | .001 | Autogenous superior |
| Substitutes vs FGG | 5 | −1.0 | [−1.6; −0.5] | <.001 | FGG superior |
| Xenografts vs autogenous | 5 | −0.8 | [−1.6; 0.0] | .062 | Not significant |
| Xenograft vs CTG (1 RCT) | 1 | 0.0 | [−0.9; +0.9] | .948 | No difference |

### KM Augmentation — Effectiveness of Substitutes (PICOS #2)

| Subgroup | n | WME (mm) | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|
| All substitutes | 9 | +3.0 | [2.3; 3.8] |
| Xenografts only | 7 | +3.5 | [2.5; 4.6] |
| Allografts only | 2 | +1.6 | [1.4; 1.7] |
| Post-implant (5 studies) | 5 | +2.5 | [1.9; 3.1] |

### Shrinkage

No significant overall difference vs autogenous (WMD −3.7%; p = .256). Allograft (AlloDerm®) showed ~52% shrinkage individually. Xenograft shrinkage: −16.5% (1–6 months from baseline).

### Secondary Outcomes

- **Surgical time**: ~18.5 min reduction with substitutes
- **Pain/analgesics**: Significantly lower with substitutes
- **Esthetics**: Substitutes > FGG; substitutes ≈ CTG (blending, contour, texture)
- **Peri-implant parameters**: Both groups improved PI, BOP, PD; no between-group differences
- **Marginal bone levels**: No significant difference
- **Complications/graft loss**: No significant difference

### Clinical Bottom Line

- FGG remains the gold standard for maximum KM gain
- Xenogeneic collagen matrix (Mucograft®) is a reasonable alternative, especially when: (a) palatal harvest is not feasible or declined; (b) large-area grafting exceeds autograft availability; (c) patient preference for less morbidity; (d) esthetic blending is a priority
- Allografts (ADM) show significantly less KM gain and high shrinkage — less supported by current evidence
- CM can achieve >2 mm KM, meeting the threshold associated with peri-implant health maintenance in most studies
