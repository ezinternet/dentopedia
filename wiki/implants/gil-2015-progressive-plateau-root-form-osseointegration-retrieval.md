## One-line Summary

Retrospective human retrieval study (n=93 Bicon plateau root form implants, 120 days–~18 years in function) demonstrating that both BIC and BAFO increase progressively over functional loading time, independent of implant surface type, diameter, length, jaw, and region.

## Summary

Gil et al. (2015) present a retrospective histomorphometric analysis of 93 plateau root form implants (Bicon LLC, Boston, MA) retrieved from human patients over approximately 15 years for prosthetic retreatment reasons — not due to failure. Implants had been under functional loading for 120 days to approximately 18 years, representing the widest temporal range in the plateau root form retrieval literature at publication.

The central hypothesis — that bone-to-implant contact (BIC) and bone area fraction occupancy (BAFO) increase over functional loading time — was confirmed. A multivariable statistical model tested BIC and BAFO against six independent variables: implant surface type, diameter, length, jaw (maxilla/mandible), region (anterior/posterior), and time in function. Only loading time emerged as a significant predictor; design and location variables did not modify the relationship.

This finding supports a model of **progressive osseointegration**: the implant-bone interface is not static after initial healing but continues to densify and expand under long-term physiologic loading. The result complements the parallel mechanical evidence from Baldassarri et al. (2012), who found increasing bone elastic modulus and hardness in the same Bicon retrieval cohort over a 0.3–24-year range.

## Key Contributions

- **Largest single-manufacturer human retrieval database** for plateau root form implants at the time of publication (n=93, Bicon LLC).
- **Confirmed progressive osseointegration**: BIC and BAFO both increase with years in function, not just during initial healing.
- **Isolated the time effect**: Multivariable analysis showed surface type, diameter, length, jaw, and region did not confound the loading-time relationship.
- **Clinical implication**: Long-term osseointegrated implants become more bone-integrated over time — retrieval for prosthetic retreatment does not sacrifice osseointegration quality.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Retrospective human retrieval/histomorphometric study.
- **Implants**: Bicon plateau root form implants; multiple surface types and dimensions.
- **Sample**: n=93 retrieved implants; retrieval indication was prosthetic retreatment (clinically osseointegrated at retrieval).
- **Loading range**: 120 days to approximately 18 years of functional loading.
- **Histology**: Undecalcified sections prepared by the Donath & Breuner (1982) Sage-Schliff sawing-and-grinding technique.
- **Outcome measures**: BIC (% implant surface in direct bone contact) and BAFO (% peri-implant area occupied by mineralized bone).
- **Analysis**: Multivariable statistical model with six independent variables (surface, diameter, length, jaw, region, loading time).

- Loading range: 120 days to ~18 years (one of the widest human retrieval windows in implantology).
- BIC and BAFO trends are consistent with prior smaller Bicon retrieval studies (Coelho et al. 2009, 2010) and with animal-level evidence from plateau root form implants.
- Findings support that the peri-implant bone interface undergoes continuous remodeling toward greater bone apposition under sustained functional loading.
