## One-line Summary

Systematic review of 41 dental implant trials: industry-sponsored studies reported ~5× lower annual failure rates (OR 0.21, 95% CI 0.12–0.38) than non-industry-sponsored trials after controlling for publication age, and 63% of trials never disclosed their funding source.

## Summary

Popelut et al. (2010) conducted the first systematic examination of sponsorship bias in dental implant clinical research. They searched MEDLINE and the Cochrane database for systematic reviews on implant survival (1993–2008), extracted 41 analyzable trials from 5 SRs, and coded funding source (industry, industry-associated, non-industry, unknown) with near-perfect interrater reliability (kappa = 0.90).

The mean annual failure rate across all trials was 1.09%. Non-industry trials had a mean rate of 2.73% — approximately 3× the overall average. Quasi-Poisson multivariate regression controlling for publication age showed that industry-associated trials (OR = 0.21) and trials with unknown funding (OR = 0.33) had significantly lower annual failure rates than non-industry trials.

Critically, 63% of trials never reported their funding source, 66% had a high risk of methodological bias, and only 2/41 disclosed a conflict-of-interest statement. One implant brand dominated 59% of the trials, and its country of origin was the most common among corresponding authors.

## Key Contributions

1. **First sponsorship-bias study in implant dentistry**: Applied the pharmaceutical-industry sponsorship-bias methodology to dental implant research.
2. **OR 0.21 for industry-associated trials**: After adjusting for publication age, industry-sponsored studies were 5× less likely to report failure than non-industry studies.
3. **Unknown funding = lower failure too** (OR 0.33): Trials that withheld funding information also reported lower failure rates, suggesting deliberate non-disclosure may be a bias signal.
4. **Massive transparency gap**: 63% non-disclosure of funding, 73% no statistical advisor, 83% no periodontal status reporting.
5. **Implications for tooth preservation**: If implant success rates are inflated by sponsorship bias, teeth may be extracted unnecessarily based on misleadingly favorable implant outcome data.

## Methodology

- **Data source**: 5 SRs (Pjetursson 2004, 2007; Berglundh 2002; Jung 2008; Aglietta 2009) → 38 primary articles → 41 trials
- **Prosthetic designs included**: implant-supported single crown (IS-SC), implant-supported fixed partial denture (IS-FPD), implant-tooth supported FPD (ITS-FPD)
- **Exclusions**: edentulous patients, immediate/early loading, sinus-lift
- **Failure rate metric**: implant losses / total implant-years (Poisson-appropriate count outcome)
- **Quality criteria**: inclusion/exclusion definition, blinding, dropout reporting; ≥2/3 = low risk of bias
- **Statistics**: quasi-Poisson regression (overdispersion handling); backward selection via deviance/F-ratio

## Results

| Variable | Finding |
|---|---|
| Total analyzable trials | 41 |
| Mean AFR (all trials) | 1.09% (95% CI 0.84–1.42%) |
| Mean AFR, non-industry only | 2.73% (95% CI 1.14–6.55%) |
| Funding not reported | 63% (26/41) |
| Risk of bias | 66% (27/41) |
| COI statement disclosed | 2/41 (5%) |
| Industry-associated OR | 0.21 (95% CI 0.12–0.38) |
| Unknown funding OR | 0.33 (95% CI 0.21–0.51) |
| Publication age effect | AFR × 1.12 per additional year |

Univariate analysis: prosthetic design (p = 0.023) and funding source (p = 0.005) were significant. Multivariate final model retained only publication age and funding source.
