## One-line Summary

Sheep in-vivo study (n=12, 144 Bicon plateau-root-form implants, 3.5 mm vs. 6.0 mm) demonstrates that irrigation effect on BIC is diameter-specific and speed-dependent: narrow implants benefit from irrigation at 50 RPM, wide implants at 500–1,000 RPM at 6 weeks; BAFO is governed by healing duration only.

## Summary

The Witek/Coelho group (NYU-based) systematically evaluated how three surgical instrumentation variables — drilling speed (50, 500, 1,000 RPM), irrigation presence/absence, and implant diameter (3.5 mm narrow vs. 6.0 mm wide) — affected osseointegration of plateau-root-form Ti-6Al-4V healing-chamber implants (Bicon LLC) in an ovine in-vivo model. Twelve skeletally mature female sheep received 144 implants (72 narrow + 72 wide), with histomorphometric outcomes (BIC and BAFO) measured at 3 and 6 weeks. No implant failures or adverse tissue responses were observed in any group.

The principal finding is that irrigation's effect on BIC is diameter-dependent and speed-contingent. For narrow 3.5-mm implants at 6 weeks, irrigation significantly improved BIC at 50 RPM (30.6 ± 6.1% irrigated vs. 19.7 ± 6.1% non-irrigated); at 500 and 1,000 RPM the difference was not significant. For wide 6.0-mm implants at 6 weeks, irrigation was beneficial at the higher speeds (500 and 1,000 RPM), suggesting that wider osteotomies generate more frictional heat at moderate-to-high speeds, making cooling more critical. BAFO, reflecting bone infill within healing chambers, was sensitive only to healing time (3 vs. 6 weeks) — not to speed or irrigation — indicating that the plateau-root-form healing-chamber macrogeometry drives bone occupancy independently of thermal intraoperative variables.

## Key Contributions

1. **Diameter-specific irrigation threshold**: Irrigation significance shifts between narrow and wide implants — clinically relevant when choosing protocol for different implant diameters.
2. **BAFO = healing time, not technique**: Healing chambers normalized bone area occupancy across instrumentation protocols, validating the macrogeometry rationale for retaining bone-forming elements.
3. **Safety of both diameter classes**: Narrow (3.5 mm) and wide (6.0 mm) short plateau-root-form implants demonstrated acceptable osseointegration across all surgical protocols studied; no failures.
4. **Multifactorial design**: Unlike single-variable studies, the factorial arrangement allows simultaneous estimation of speed × irrigation interaction effects within the same animal model.

## Methodology

- **Animal model**: 12 skeletally mature female sheep
- **Implants**: 144 plateau-root-form healing-chamber Ti-6Al-4V implants (Bicon LLC); 72 narrow (3.5 mm diameter) + 72 wide (6.0 mm diameter)
- **Independent variables**: Drilling speed (50, 500, 1,000 RPM) × irrigation (present/absent) × diameter (3.5 vs. 6.0 mm)
- **Healing periods**: 3 weeks and 6 weeks
- **Outcome measures**: BIC (bone-to-implant contact, %) and BAFO (bone area fraction occupancy, %)
- **Analysis**: Histomorphometric evaluation; statistical comparison between groups (exact model not recoverable from abstract-only PDF)
