## One-line Summary
In vivo animal study (atrophic porcine mandible, 12 implants, 6 per arm, 4-week healing) comparing osseodensification (OD) vs conventional Summers osteotome for alveolar ridge expansion: OD nearly doubled BIC (62.5% vs 31.4%, P=0.018) but BAFO was unchanged (P=0.198), indicating denser interface intimacy without added bone volume; small sample and single early time point limit clinical extrapolation.

## Summary
Tian et al. compared OD drilling (Densah-style counterclockwise burs) against the conventional Summers osteotome technique for alveolar ridge expansion in horizontally atrophic porcine mandibles. Twelve 4 × 13 mm endosteal implants were placed (six per arm) into ridges that had been deliberately atrophied by prior premolar extractions. Animals were sacrificed at 4 weeks and analyzed histologically.

The headline finding is a near-doubling of BIC% in the OD arm (62.5% vs 31.4%, P = 0.018). Importantly, BAFO% — the areal fraction of bone in the peri-implant region — was not statistically different between groups (P = 0.198). The authors interpret this as OD generating a denser bone–implant interface (compaction autografting + spring-back) without changing total peri-implant bone volume.

This is one of several in vivo studies from the NYU/Coelho group that translated the bench-level OD claims of Huwais & Meyer (2017) into preclinical osseointegration outcomes. The work is interesting as a proof-of-principle in the *ridge-expansion* context rather than only the *low-density* context where OD had been previously studied, but the n=6/arm design and 4-week single-time point sharply limit how confidently the results should be ported to human clinical practice.

## Key Contributions
- First in vivo histologic comparison of OD vs Summers osteotome in horizontally atrophic ridges (a ridge-expansion clinical scenario rather than D3/D4 density enhancement).
- BIC% nearly doubled with OD (62.5% vs 31.4%, P = 0.018) at 4 weeks.
- BAFO% unchanged — suggests OD improves *interface intimacy* without adding total bone volume.
- Supports the mechanistic story that compaction autografting + autogenous bone-spicule retention enhances early osseointegration.
- Funded by BRR Tech — manufacturer ties are common across the OD literature and should be considered when weighting evidence.

## Methodology
Porcine model: 8 weeks after bilateral premolar extractions, ridges had become horizontally atrophic. Twelve 4 × 13 mm implants were placed via either OD drilling (n=6) or conventional Summers osteotome (n=6) — design treated as case-controlled split between sides/animals. After 4 weeks of healing the segments were retrieved, stained with Stevenel's Blue + Van Gieson's Picro Fuschin, and analyzed for BIC% and BAFO%. A pooled-standard-error post-hoc test (α = 0.05) was applied after a significant omnibus test.

## Results
- BIC%: OD ≈ 62.5%, conventional ≈ 31.4%, P = 0.018.
- BAFO%: OD vs conventional not significantly different, P = 0.198.
- No insertion torque, ISQ, removal torque, or survival data reported in the available abstract; biomechanical outcomes were not the primary endpoint.

## Critical Reading / Living-document Notes
- 95% CI bounds for BIC and BAFO are referenced in the abstract but not numerically extracted in the available text — the magnitude of the BIC difference is plausibly imprecise.
- Single 4-week endpoint cannot distinguish faster early osseointegration from a sustained benefit; later remodeling could narrow the gap.
- BAFO null result is consistent with OD's mechanism being *interface densification*, not *volume increase* — clinicians should not expect OD alone to make a sub-3 mm ridge implantable.
- Industry-linked author roster and BRR Tech funding warrant caution; SR/MAs such as Marzorati 2026 and Yu 2022 should be the primary anchor for clinical decision-making.
