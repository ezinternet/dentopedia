## One-line Summary

Narrative review of six implant stability measurement methods (clinical perception, percussion, reverse torque, cutting torque resistance, Periotest, RFA/ISQ), concluding no single critical value reliably predicts implant success and RFA/ISQ is the most useful noninvasive option.

## Summary

Mistry et al. (2014) is a practitioner-oriented narrative review distinguishing **primary stability** (mechanical anchorage at placement, determined by local bone quality/quantity, implant type, and placement technique) from **secondary stability** (the biological increase from bone formation and remodeling at the implant/tissue interface). Reliable stability assessment supports loading decisions — immediate, early (6–8 weeks), or classical delayed (3–6 months) — and improves documentation and patient communication.

Because the histologic "gold standard" for osseointegration is too invasive and ethically constrained, the review surveys six noninvasive/clinical alternatives, grading them from crude to objective: clinical perception and percussion (poor, qualitative), reverse torque (disrepute — may fracture an integrating surface; mobilized implants can re-integrate), cutting torque resistance (bone-density correlate, but surgery-only and no failure cutoff), Periotest (electronic percussion, −8 to +50), and RFA/ISQ (the most useful noninvasive method). The bottom line: too many variables affect each method for any single critical value to define success, failure, or long-term prognosis.

## Key Contributions

- Side-by-side catalog of six implant-stability methods with mechanism + limitations, useful as a conceptual primer.
- Consolidates **Periotest** interpretation bands and **RFA/ISQ** loading thresholds (47 / >49 / >54) drawn chiefly from Nedir et al. 2004.
- Articulates why no universal cutoff is defensible: effective implant length, bone quality/quantity, implant geometry, and measurement direction/position all shift readings.

## Methodology

Non-systematic narrative review (practitioner section, Journal of Dental Implants). No literature search protocol, quality appraisal, or pooled analysis; thresholds are quoted from individual primary sources rather than synthesized.

## Results

- **Periotest bands**: −8 to 0 = good osseointegration, loadable; +1 to +9 = clinical exam needed, usually not loadable; +10 to +50 = insufficient, not loadable. Must be measured mid-buccal, perpendicular to the implant axis; molars are impractical (buccal mucosa); cannot diagnose borderline cases or replace radiography. In-vitro: no significant operator-to-operator difference, high inter-unit repeatability.
- **RFA/ISQ**: classically ranges 40–80; higher = more stable. Stability determinable at ISQ 47; all implants with ISQ >49 osseointegrated when healed 3 months; all with ISQ >54 osseointegrated when immediately loaded. ISQ is affected by effective implant length (exposed threads + abutment height, inversely proportional to resonance frequency), bone quality/quantity, and implant length/diameter/shape. The magnetic RFA device yields higher ISQ than the wired electrical device for nonsubmerged implants.
- **RFA drawbacks**: transducer limited to ~60 measurements (expensive); requires a transducer fixed to the implant, so cemented restorations cannot be monitored.
- **Other methods**: clinical perception/percussion are unreliable and qualitative; reverse torque has fallen into disrepute; cutting torque resistance only correlates with bone density intra-surgically and cannot assess secondary stability.
