Narrative review (MEDLINE 1983–Oct 2013) establishing that secure primary (mechanical) stability is a prerequisite for osseointegration, governed by three factors — bone quality/quantity, implant geometry, and surgical technique — and evaluated clinically by Periotest or RFA (ISQ >65 favorable, <45 poor); micromotion >50–100 µm jeopardizes integration.

## Summary

This narrative literature review (King Saud University / Stony Brook group, including G.E. Romanos) consolidates why **primary stability is a prerequisite for successful osseointegration**. It distinguishes **primary stability** (mechanical engagement of the implant with surrounding bone at placement) from **secondary biological stability** (from bone regeneration and remodelling), noting a positive association between the two: impaired primary stability jeopardizes the osseointegration process.

The review organizes the determinants of primary stability into three predominant factors — **bone density/quality and quantity, implant design/geometry, and surgical technique** — then surveys the two clinical evaluation methods (Periotest, RFA/ISQ) and the role of micromotion. It is a non-systematic synthesis (no pooled estimates), so it functions as a framework and an early citation point for now-standard thresholds (ISQ >65 / <45; immediate-loading torque 32–40 Ncm; micromotion 50–100 µm) rather than as graded evidence.

## Key Contributions

- **Two-stage stability framework**: primary (mechanical) → secondary (biological), with primary stability as the prerequisite for osseointegration.
- **Three-factor model** of primary stability: bone quality/quantity, implant geometry, surgical technique (Fig. 1).
- **Threshold reference point**: cites ISQ >65 as most favorable and <45 as poor primary stability; immediate-loading insertion torque thresholds of 32/35/40 Ncm (and ~15–20 Ncm acceptable in poor-quality bone); micromotion 50–100 µm as the limit above which integration is impaired.
- **PT vs RFA comparison**: Periotest is less sensitive (narrow value range); RFA/ISQ is a reliable stability indicator but is transducer-seating dependent and cannot be measured after final abutment/prosthesis connection.

## Methodology

Narrative literature review. MEDLINE–PubMed searched 1983 to October 2013 using keyword combinations (bone, dental, design, implant, immediate loading, implant length/diameter, maxilla, mandible, narrow/wide implant, osseointegration, primary stability, surgical technique). Reference lists hand-searched. Eligibility: human and experimental studies, control group present, English-language only. No systematic-review apparatus (no PRISMA flow, risk-of-bias grading, or quantitative pooling); content thematically summarized.

## Results

- **Bone quality/quantity**: poor (soft) bone is the main risk factor for failure; mandible > maxilla survival, posterior maxilla worst (thin cortical + thick trabecular). Stability correlates with cortical thickness and bone density (e.g., 158 sites/85 patients showed strong density–stability correlation). CT is the preferred radiographic method for assessing residual bone. Intraoperative bone condensing / osteotomy undersizing raises density and primary stability.
- **Implant design**: tapered implants compress cortical bone and accommodate labial concavities; rough surfaces (sandblasted, acid-etched) increase surface area, osteogenesis, and BIC vs machined surfaces in poor bone; small-diameter (<3.0 mm) implants give sufficient stability in limited bone volume; microthreads, progressive thread design, and platform switching help preserve crestal bone. Optimal bone quality/quantity can compensate for design inadequacy.
- **Surgical technique**: undersized drilling and Summers' osteotome bone-condensing increase peri-implant density and initial stability. Immediate-loading torque thresholds: 32/35/40 Ncm and higher; ~15–20 Ncm can suffice in poor-quality bone with high survival.
- **Evaluation**: Periotest (percussion) — less sensitive. RFA/Osstell → ISQ: **>65 favorable, <45 poor**; limited by transducer seating and inability to measure after final abutment connection; authors note no justification for routine clinical use of PT/RFA without further refinement.
- **Micromotion**: >50–100 µm forms fibrous tissue and induces resorption, risking failed osseointegration; well-controlled micromotion can favor bone formation, supporting immediate functional loading in healed ridges and fresh extraction sockets.

**Bottom line**: primary stability is essential for integration, and it is the product of bone, implant geometry, and surgical technique acting together — any one can partly compensate for another.
