## One-line Summary

Prospective case–control study (n=32 patients, 48 NDIs, 3 months): ISQ of 3.3 mm TiZr SLActive implants rose significantly in both T2DM (HbA1c>6.5%, mean 7.34%) and normoglycemic patients from Day 3 to Month 3, reaching equivalent values (~64) with no between-group difference and no HbA1c–ISQ correlation.

## Summary

Diehl et al. (2022) conducted a prospective case–control study at Witten/Herdecke University to determine whether uncontrolled Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) impairs implant stability of narrow-diameter implants (NDIs) compared to healthy controls.

Thirty-two patients (16 T2DM with HbA1c > 6.5%, mean 7.34%; 16 normoglycemic controls with HbA1c < 5.9%) were matched for age, sex, and implant site. All received 3.3 mm diameter TiZr Roxolid tissue-level implants (SLActive surface, Institut Straumann AG) in the posterior region without bone augmentation. A total of 48 implants were placed (23 test, 25 control). Resonance frequency analysis (Osstell ISQ) was performed at Day 3, Day 7, Week 4, and Month 3.

Both groups showed a significant ISQ increase from Day 3 to Month 3. The T2DM group rose from 55.87 ± 5.99 to 63.84 ± 6.05; the control group from 51.41 ± 9.62 to 63.84 ± 6.18. No significant between-group differences were found at any time point. Pearson correlation confirmed no association between HbA1c and ISQ. All 48 implants successfully osseointegrated.

The control group's largest ISQ jump occurred between Day 3 and Day 7 (p=.024), while the T2DM group showed a steadier rise with significance only between Day 3 and Month 3 (p=.0098), suggesting a slightly different but clinically equivalent integration trajectory. A post-hoc positive correlation between HbA1c and ISQ was found in maxillary T2DM implants at Week 4, but was attributed to the very small subsample (n=7) and considered incidental.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates ISQ equivalence of NDIs in T2DM vs. normoglycemic patients at 3 months, with both groups reaching ~64 ISQ prior to loading
- Documents a steadier ISQ rise trajectory in T2DM (no early spike) vs. rapid Day 3–7 gain in controls, without adverse clinical consequence
- Challenges the Oates et al. (2009) finding of HbA1c–stability correlation — here, no correlation was found, possibly explained by the lower mean HbA1c (7.34% vs. > 8.1%) and use of the hydrophilic SLActive surface
- Supports NDI as a strategy to reduce wound-healing burden in T2DM patients by avoiding bone augmentation procedures
- All 48 NDIs osseointegrated, establishing a 100% success rate at 3 months in this diabetic cohort

## Methodology

- **Design**: Prospective case–control, single center (Witten/Herdecke University, Germany)
- **n**: 32 patients, 48 implants (0 dropouts); mean age 67 years
- **Groups**: T2DM (HbA1c > 6.5%, mean 7.34% ± 0.73) vs. normoglycemic (HbA1c < 5.9%, mean 5.3% ± 0.4)
- **Implant**: 3.3 mm Straumann Roxolid TiZr tissue-level, SLActive surface; lengths 8/10/12 mm; transmucosal protocol; no augmentation
- **ISQ measurement**: Osstell ISQ meter + SmartPeg Type 04; mesial + buccal, mean recorded; single calibrated investigator
- **Timepoints**: Day 3 (V3), Day 7 (V4), Week 4 (V5), Month 3 (V6)
- **Statistics**: Repeated-measures ANOVA/Friedman + Dunnett; Sidak for between-group; Pearson for HbA1c correlation; GraphPad Prism 8

## Results

| Group | Day 3 ISQ | Month 3 ISQ | Significant change? |
|---|---|---|---|
| T2DM (n=23) | 55.87 ± 5.99 | 63.84 ± 6.05 | Yes (p=.0098) |
| Control (n=25) | 51.41 ± 9.62 | 63.84 ± 6.18 | Yes (p=.0216) |

- Between-group difference at any timepoint: Not significant (Sidak's test)
- HbA1c vs. ISQ correlation: Not significant (Pearson) at any visit or jaw position (except incidental maxillary T2DM at Week 4, r=0.80, p=.031, n=7 — likely spurious)
- Jaw effect (maxilla vs. mandible): No significant ISQ difference
- All 48 implants osseointegrated by Month 3
- Final ISQ (~64) is slightly below values reported for larger-diameter Straumann implants, consistent with known diameter–ISQ relationship; within the 55–65 "safe" Straumann range per Sennerby & Meredith (2008)
