## Summary

Erosive tooth wear (ETW) is prevalent and irreversible, and although many anti-erosive dentifrices exist, heterogeneity among systematic reviews has prevented clear clinical guidance on which chemistry to choose. This umbrella review sits at the top of the evidence pyramid — a systematic review of systematic reviews/meta-analyses — and critically appraises the highest-level secondary evidence on dentifrice effectiveness against enamel and dentin loss.

Eight systematic reviews met inclusion. The consistent signal: **fluoridated dentifrices reduce enamel and dentin tissue loss versus non-fluoridated controls**, and among fluoride chemistries, **stannous-containing formulations (stabilized SnF2 and multi-ion fluoride systems) most consistently provide greater protection than conventional NaF/NaMFP** comparators under both erosive and erosive-abrasive protocols. Evidence for calcium-based and biomimetic technologies is heterogeneous and largely short-term experimental. Certainty (ConQual) is **moderate** for stannous-based systems and **low or very low** for emerging technologies.

The central caveat is methodological: the evidence base is dominated by **in vitro / in situ models with short follow-up**, so the experimental protection signal should NOT be over-interpreted as proof of reduced clinical ETW progression. Clinical evidence is more established for dentine hypersensitivity outcomes than for ETW, and ETW-specific patient-centered outcomes remain scarce. The pragmatic takeaway is that stannous-containing dentifrices may be preferred for patients at increased erosive risk, while long-term, independently funded clinical trials with standardized ETW and patient-reported outcomes are still needed.

## Key Contributions

- First-pass top-of-pyramid synthesis (umbrella review of 8 SRs) translating fragmented dentifrice evidence into a chemistry-choice ranking.
- Establishes a hierarchy: **fluoridated > non-fluoridated**, and within fluoride, **stannous (SnF2 / multi-ion) > NaF/NaMFP** under erosive/erosive-abrasive conditions.
- Grades certainty explicitly (ConQual): **moderate** for stannous systems vs **low/very-low** for calcium-based/biomimetic emerging tech.
- Names the validity ceiling: predominance of in vitro/in situ short-term evidence; scarcity of ETW-specific patient-centered outcomes.

## Methodology

- **Design**: umbrella review (systematic review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses) following **PRISMA 2020** and **Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI)** guidance.
- **Databases**: PubMed, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library — searched up to **January 2026**.
- **Inclusion**: reviews evaluating fluoridated and non-fluoridated dentifrices under in vitro, in situ, or clinical conditions.
- **Appraisal**: methodological quality via **AMSTAR 2**; certainty of evidence via **ConQual**.
- **Synthesis**: narrative (no quantitative pooling) due to heterogeneity across included reviews.

## Results

- **Fluoride effect**: fluoridated dentifrices generally reduced enamel and dentin tissue loss vs non-fluoridated controls, predominantly demonstrated in vitro and in situ.
- **Stannous superiority**: stabilized SnF2 and multi-ion fluoride systems most consistently showed greater protection than conventional NaF/NaMFP under erosive and erosive-abrasive protocols.
- **Emerging technologies**: calcium-based and biomimetic formulations — heterogeneous and largely short-term experimental evidence.
- **Certainty**: moderate (stannous-based) vs low/very-low (emerging tech).
- **Clinical translation**: stronger clinical evidence exists for dentine hypersensitivity than for ETW; ETW-specific patient-centered outcomes are scarce.
- **Practice signal**: stannous-containing dentifrices may be preferred for patients at increased erosive risk based on consistent experimental protection.
- **Key caveat**: evidence dominated by in vitro/in situ models and short follow-up — NOT proof of reduced clinical ETW progression; over-interpretation should be avoided.
