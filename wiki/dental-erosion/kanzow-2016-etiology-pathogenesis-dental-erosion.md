## Summary
Kanzow et al. provide a concise authoritative definition and aetiological framework for dental erosion,
establishing it as distinct from caries (no bacteria) and mechanical wear (no acid). They classify
aetiological sources into extrinsic — dietary acids (citric, phosphoric, carbonic, maleic) in beverages,
fruits and sweets, as well as occupational/environmental acid exposure — and intrinsic — gastroesophageal
reflux (GERD), eating disorders, and rumination releasing gastric hydrochloric acid onto tooth surfaces.
Severity is modulated by four parameter clusters: nutritional habits (frequency, acid type, TA,
chelating capacity of citrate), salivary protective factors (flow rate, buffering, pellicle formation),
systemic diseases affecting saliva, and concurrent mechanical forces (abrasion and attrition synergistically
remove softened enamel surface). Clinical diagnosis requires combining physical examination of erosion
pattern (palatal = intrinsic; labial/buccal = extrinsic) with thorough medical, dietary, and occupational
history to enable personalised risk-reduction strategies.

## Key Contributions
- Authoritative single-source definition: dental erosion = acid-related, non-microbial, irreversible loss.
- Extrinsic vs intrinsic acid classification framework foundational to clinical aetiological diagnosis.
- Identifies four severity-modifying parameter clusters: nutrition, saliva, systemic disease, mechanical.
- Chelating effect of citrate ion (Ca²⁺ binding, active at pH > 4) distinguished from direct H+ attack.
- Clinical diagnosis pathway: erosion pattern recognition + anamnesis → targeted risk reduction.

## Results
(Qualitative — key theses)
- Dental erosion = acid-related, non-microbial tooth-structure loss; distinct from caries and
  mechanical wear.
- Extrinsic aetiology: dietary acids (fruits, beverages, sweets), occupational acids, environmental.
- Intrinsic aetiology: GERD, anorexia/bulimia (vomiting), rumination — expose palatal surfaces to HCl.
- Severity determined by: (1) acid frequency/duration/type/TA; (2) salivary flow, buffering, pellicle;
  (3) systemic diseases reducing salivary protection; (4) abrasion/attrition removing softened layer.
- Clinical strategy: pattern + anamnesis → identify + eliminate/reduce risk factors.
