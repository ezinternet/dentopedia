Narrative review (J Dent 2014 Supplement, 143 references) summarising chemical, biological, and host
factors of enamel mineral loss from caries and erosion, with clinical management frameworks,
concluding that evidence-based prevention — dietary advice, oral hygiene, fluoride — is the
foundation of lifelong hard-tissue management.

## Summary
West and Joiner synthesise evidence on enamel mineral loss across its major pathways: caries
(bacterial acid via fermentable carbohydrate metabolism), erosion (intrinsic gastric or extrinsic
dietary acids), abrasion, and attrition. Enamel is described as 85% substituted calcium
hydroxyapatite by volume, dissolving when plaque/oral fluid pH falls below the critical pH (~5.5).
For caries, the review notes a declining prevalence trend in developed countries with management
moving toward minimally invasive risk-based approaches. For erosion, prevalence is increasing
with age, and both intrinsic (GERD, eating disorders) and extrinsic (acidic diet, beverages)
sources are detailed. Clinical management relies on identifying aetiology through thorough history,
then implementing tailored prevention including fluoride and remineralisation. The authors stress
that patients retaining natural dentition longer than ever before face cumulative physicochemical
insults, making lifelong preventive strategies imperative.

## Key Contributions
- Unified framework covering caries, erosion, abrasion, and attrition as interrelated enamel
  mineral loss processes.
- Enamel composition quantified: 85% vol mineral (substituted hydroxyapatite), 12% water, 3% organic.
- Critical pH concept (~5.5) explained in relation to hydroxyapatite saturation.
- Distinguishes intrinsic (GERD, eating disorders) from extrinsic (dietary) erosion aetiology.
- Erosion prevalence increasing and age-dependent; caries prevalence declining in developed nations.
- Evidence-based prevention (diet, hygiene, fluoride, remineralisation) as cornerstone of management.

## Results
(Qualitative narrative — key theses)
- Enamel mineral loss from caries: driven by pH cycling below ~5.5 due to plaque acid production
  from fermentable carbohydrates; subsurface demineralisation precedes surface cavitation.
- Enamel mineral loss from erosion: intrinsic (HCl from GERD) and extrinsic (citric, phosphoric,
  carbonic, malic acids in diet); erosion often co-occurs with abrasion (synergistic).
- Prevalence: caries declining in developed nations; erosion prevalence increasing with age;
  tooth wear affects QoL (satisfaction, aesthetics, sensitivity, psychological impact).
- Management: risk assessment → identify aetiology → targeted prevention → minimally invasive
  restoration; fluoride and remineralisation therapies are key adjuncts.
