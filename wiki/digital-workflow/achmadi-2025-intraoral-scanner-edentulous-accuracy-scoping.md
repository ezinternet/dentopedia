## Summary
Scoping review (PRISMA-ScR + Arksey & O'Malley framework) comparing intraoral scanner (IOS) vs laboratory scanner accuracy for fully and partially edentulous arch impressions. Four databases searched; 312 articles retrieved; 8 studies selected. Published BDJ Open 2025, Universitas Padjadjaran, Indonesia.

IOS is extensively used and shows promise for edentulous cases, but morphological differences (smooth featureless mucosa, reduced anatomical landmarks) limit scanning accuracy compared to dentate arches. Laboratory scanners via indirect digitization of conventional casts generally maintain higher accuracy in edentulous conditions.

## Key Contributions
- Identifies the core accuracy challenge for IOS in edentulous arches: smooth ridge surfaces impede tracing and stitching algorithms
- Cumulative error increases with extended scan path length — especially problematic in edentulous cases lacking anatomical anchors
- Laboratory scanner (indirect method: conventional impression → cast → digitization) generally more accurate for fully edentulous
- Both IOS and lab scanner studied for fully and partially edentulous arches; partially edentulous performs better than fully edentulous for IOS
- Factors influencing edentulous scan accuracy: ridge height/morphology, software stitching algorithm quality, operator technique, device generation

## Methodology
- Design: Scoping review; PRISMA-ScR; Arksey & O'Malley 5-stage approach
- Databases: PubMed, Scopus, SpringerLink, ScienceDirect; publications within last decade
- Inclusion: Studies analyzing IOS and/or lab scanner accuracy in edentulous cases
- Output: Thematic analysis; tabulated results
- Final selection: 8 of 312 articles

## Results
- IOS: extensively utilized; considerable promise; accuracy limited in edentulous by surface morphology
- Laboratory scanner: higher accuracy in edentulous via indirect method
- Key challenge: smooth edentulous ridge → feature-matching failures → stitching errors
- Partially edentulous: better IOS accuracy than fully edentulous (remaining teeth provide landmarks)
