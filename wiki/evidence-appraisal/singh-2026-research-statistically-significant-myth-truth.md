## One-line Summary
Editorial arguing that statistical significance is not the same as clinical significance — a non-significant result can still be clinically meaningful when sample size is small, power is low, or variability is high, and a significant one may be clinically trivial.

## Summary
This editorial reminds clinicians that the p-value is a poor sole arbiter of a study's worth. A statistically significant finding may be clinically trivial, and a clinically important effect (e.g., a modestly better restorative material) may fail to reach significance because of small samples, inadequate power, or high variability. The take-home is to interpret dental research through effect size, confidence intervals, and clinical relevance rather than a binary significance verdict — a concise anchor for critical appraisal habits.

## Key Contributions
- Crisp clinician-facing distinction between statistical and clinical significance.
- Flags the usual culprits behind misleading p-values.

## Results
Statistical significance and clinical significance are not equivalent; clinicians should weigh effect size, CIs, and clinical relevance alongside the p-value.
