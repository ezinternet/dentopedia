## 한줄요약
임상의를 위한 p-value, 95% CI, NNT 3종 세트 튜토리얼 (Indian J Urol 2011): 전립선암 화학예방 시나리오로 세 지표가 같은 데이터의 다른 측면임을 보여주는 EBM 입문.

## Summary
A teaching paper that argues p-value, 95% confidence interval, and number-needed-to-treat (NNT) should be read together, not chosen between. The clinical scenario (60-year-old post-negative prostate biopsy asking about 5α-reductase chemoprevention) is non-dental but the framework transfers verbatim to dental decisions (e.g., "how many implants must I place with osseodensification to avoid one early failure vs. conventional drilling").

## Key Contributions
- Distinguishes "significant" (p<0.05) from "important" (clinically meaningful effect size).
- Explains the 95% CI as the more informative lens — it shows both direction and precision.
- Operationalizes NNT as the bridge from research statistics to bedside decision: 1/ARR.

## Methodology
- Single-case narrative. No data of its own.
- Walks through how the same trial yields p-value, 95% CI, and NNT, and what each adds.

## Results
No original results.

## Clinical Applicability
- When reading a dental RCT, look for the 95% CI on the effect estimate, not just the p-value. A 95% CI that barely crosses null is much weaker evidence than the same point estimate with a tight CI.
- NNT is underused in dentistry. Useful for patient communication: "to prevent one peri-implantitis case at 5 years, we'd need to apply this intervention to N patients" is more concrete than "RR 0.7, p=0.03".

## Caveats
- Frequentist-only. No Bayesian framing.
- Does not address multiplicity, family-wise error, or false discovery rate — the issues D'Arrigo 2024 covers.
