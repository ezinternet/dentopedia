## One-line Summary

Methodological quality assessment (CONSORT + trial-registration audit) of 32 RCTs on SRP adjuncts published 2010–2017 by a single research group (GDCRI, Bangalore); all RCTs scored low on reporting quality, most CONSORT items <50% (including randomization, registration, blinding), only 38% had follow-up periods consistent with declared trial start/end dates — so the group's unusually large effect sizes should be viewed with extreme caution.

## Summary

This is Part 2 of a two-part meta-research project examining why a single research group (Government Dental College and Research Institute, GDCRI, Bangalore, India) repeatedly reported unusually large effect sizes for adjuncts to scaling and root planing (SRP) in periodontitis — effect sizes never reproduced by other groups (and previously flagged in the 2015 ADA systematic review by Smiley et al.). Part 1 (a meta-analysis) could not explain the findings statistically; Part 2 instead audits **reporting quality** and **trial-registration integrity**. Across 32 included RCTs (2010–2017), the authors found uniformly low CONSORT adherence, registration discrepancies, and implausible study timelines. The clinical takeaway is a caution: clinicians should not uncritically apply the large benefits reported by this group, and the work is a teaching case in critical appraisal of a suspiciously productive, suspiciously favorable evidence base.

## Key Contributions

- Preregistered (OSF) structured audit pairing CONSORT reporting-quality scoring with trial-registration cross-checking on a single prolific group's RCT output.
- A simple, transferable **timeline-consistency test**: compare actual RCT duration (X, from published start/end dates) against per-protocol declared follow-up (Y). X>Y is realistic; X=Y is unrealistic (implies day-1 enrollment/randomization of all participants with all follow-up done within the interval); X<Y is problematic/erratic.
- Supplies the integrity/reporting explanation that Part 1's statistical meta-analysis could not — bridging "the numbers look too good" to "the reports cannot be verified or replicated."

## Methodology

- **Identification**: Scopus bibliometric search (+ PubMed hand search), 2010–2017, on SRP + adjunct keywords, to confirm GDCRI as the highest-frequency publishing group; duplicates excluded.
- **Inclusion**: RCTs (cluster, crossover, noninferiority, superiority) comparing SRP vs SRP+adjunct in periodontitis; English; baseline reported; ≥6-month follow-up.
- **Exclusion**: nonrandomized/observational designs; gingivitis, tooth-sensitivity, peri-implantitis; furcation-defect trials; laser/photodynamic/platelet-fibrin adjuncts; non-SRP comparators.
- **Extraction**: two independent reviewers, third-reviewer adjudication; Cochrane Handbook–based sheet (registration, population, intervention, outcomes, sample-size calculation, baseline + 6-mo results, journal/impact, trial dates).
- **Registration cross-check**: registry data matched to article text; presence of prestudy power analysis and pre-specified primary/secondary outcomes assessed.
- **Quality scoring**: CONSORT items scored categorically (fulfilled/not), expressed as proportional percentages; the abstract item and item 7b were excluded.

## Results

- 92 studies retrieved → 32 RCTs included for assessment.
- All included RCTs had low reporting quality; the majority of CONSORT items scored <50%, including critical items (randomization, registration, blinding).
- Registration records vs publications showed several systematic inconsistencies.
- Only 38% of RCTs had a follow-up period consistent with the declared initiation/termination dates; the rest were unrealistic or erratic.
- The group's RCT publication frequency was remarkably high relative to the ~2-year norm from trial to publication.
- Conclusion: poor reporting adherence + registration inconsistencies mean the unusually large effects should be viewed with extreme caution.
