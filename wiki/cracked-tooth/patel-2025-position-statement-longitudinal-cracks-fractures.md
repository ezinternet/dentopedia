## One-line Summary

European Society of Endodontology (ESE) consensus position statement standardising the terminology of longitudinal cracks/fractures (craze line, cracked tooth, fractured cusp, split tooth, VRF, split root) and recommending early diagnosis (bite test, transillumination, magnification, selective CBCT), cuspal coverage restoration, occlusal/parafunction control, and abandonment of the term "cracked tooth syndrome."

## Summary

This is a consensus position statement from an expert committee convened by the European Society of Endodontology (ESE), published in the International Endodontic Journal (2025). According to PubMed/PMC (PMC11812625), it provides evidence-based guidance on the aetiology, presentation, and management of cracks and fractures along the long axis of the crown and/or root. It is the authoritative society document framing the full longitudinal crack/fracture spectrum and explicitly recommends abandoning the term "cracked tooth syndrome" because of the broad variability in clinical presentation.

The incidence of cracked teeth (CT) and vertical root fractures (VRF) is rising, including a reported increase since the COVID-19 pandemic. Untreated CT may progress to irreversible pulpal disease and tooth loss; VRF progresses subtly and usually ends in extraction. Timely diagnosis and management are the central theme.

## Key Contributions

- **Standardised terminology (Table 1)** spanning the spectrum: craze line → cracked tooth (CT) → fractured cusp → split tooth (ST) → vertical root fracture (VRF) → split root.
- **Recommends abandoning "cracked tooth syndrome"** owing to heterogeneous presentation.
- Treats **VRF in non-root-filled teeth (NRFT)** and **VRF in root-filled teeth (RFT)** as distinct entities with different epidemiology and management.
- Management framework keyed to **pulpal/periapical status** and **restorability/root number**.
- Elevates **occlusal force and parafunction management** to a universal consideration across CT, ST, and VRF.

## Methodology

Consensus view of an ESE expert committee, grounded in current clinical/scientific evidence plus the committee's collective reflective practice — not a systematic review or meta-analysis (hence `confidence: consensus`). Organised entity-by-entity (CT, ST, VRF-NRFT, VRF-RFT) with Prevalence / Aetiology / Clinical / Radiographic / Management sub-sections, then universal occlusal considerations.

Endorsed diagnostic aids: bite test (e.g., Tooth Sleuth) in vital teeth, fibre-optic transillumination, methylene-blue staining, magnification (operating microscope/loupes), removal of existing restorations, and CBCT only when clinical/periapical-radiograph (PR) assessment is inconclusive.

## Results

Key figures (as reported in the statement, citing its referenced literature):

- **Cracked teeth**: visible coronal CT prevalence up to 70%; most prevalent >40 y; posterior teeth predominate; increasing incidence in unrestored teeth. Symptoms (2858-tooth US practice-based study): 45% symptomatic — cold pain 37%, biting pain 16%, spontaneous pain 11%. Only 2% of vital-pulp CTs show a crack radiographically. Narrow probing <6 mm associated with CT.
- **CT management**: cuspal coverage restoration favoured over direct composite (lower likelihood of subsequent RCT / restoration failure). Endodontic intervention after restorative management: 7.7–20%. Root-filled CT survival 84–88% (1–5 y), success 76–82% (1–2 y); 5+ mm probing reduces survival. Intracanal barrier apical to a crack + cuspal coverage + occlusal management gave 4-year survival 96.6% (Davis & Shariff). Negative prognostic factors: multiple cracks, radicular crack extension, terminal-tooth location, preoperative apical periodontitis, intracanal post.
- **Split tooth**: unfavourable prognosis → timely extraction.
- **VRF in NRFT**: incidence 6–37%; male, >50 y; first molars (MB root maxillary, mesial root mandibular); most research in Chinese populations; spontaneous occurrence linked to certain Asian dietary patterns. Single-rooted → extraction; multirooted → root resection/amputation possible.
- **VRF in RFT**: extraction prevalence 4–32%; causes 13–21% of RFT extractions; cyclical-fatigue pathogenesis (initiation → propagation → separation). Pathognomonic advanced sign = isolated narrow deep pocket on both sides of a root + multiple sinus tracts. CBCT cannot reliably detect intraradicular VRFs (50–100 μm vs insufficient resolution) but confirms periradicular bone loss. Single-rooted → extraction; multirooted → resection/amputation (no VRF-specific survival data; extrapolated root-resection survival 90.6% at 10 y, 96.8% at 15 y).
- **Prevention of VRF**: conservative endodontics preserving pericervical dentine, adhesive/passive post technique, avoid over-preparation, immediate composite access restoration, prompt cuspal coverage, maintain proximal contacts, bioactive/adhesive intracanal material beyond a pre-existing crack.
- **Universal**: occlusal stabilisation splints, manage interferences on all teeth, composite canine ramps to re-establish guidance, interdisciplinary care, patient education on occlusal load.
