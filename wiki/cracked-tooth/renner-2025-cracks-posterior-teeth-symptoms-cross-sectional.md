## One-line Summary

Retrospective practice-based cross-sectional study (147 patient records, single Australian general practice, 2014–2017) of posterior cracked teeth: 55.1% were asymptomatic and 55.1% of cracks were invisible until the restoration was removed; cold sensitivity was associated with unrestored marginal ridges (OR 2.89), pre-removal-visible cracks (OR 3.04) and combined body+cusp cracks (OR 3.11), and inversely with staining (OR 0.30); 51.7% gave a negative bite test, with positive bite test driven by pain-on-biting (OR 36.96), molars (OR 3.71) and visible cracks (OR 3.44); intersecting cracks were tied to molars (OR 8.79) and amalgam (OR 4.81).

## 한줄요약

## Summary

Cracked teeth are the third most common cause of tooth loss, but the published literature is skewed toward complex, symptomatic cases referred to specialist endodontic practices. This study characterises the *general-practice* reality: a single Australian general dentist's records (2014–2017, 147 posterior teeth after exclusions) examined with loupes and staged macro photography, with crack categorisation done after restoration removal and explicit inspection of both the pulp floor and the cusp bases.

The headline finding mirrors the US NDPBRN registry: **most cracked teeth (55.1%) are asymptomatic.** The key advance over NDPBRN is that this study did NOT require an externally visible crack for inclusion — and **55.1% of cracks only became visible after the restoration was removed**, a population the NDPBRN design systematically missed. Cracks that were invisible before removal were usually asymptomatic. Practically, the paper argues that after any routine restoration replacement (especially in asymptomatic teeth) the clinician should comprehensively re-examine the remaining tooth — cavity floor *and* cusp bases — to rule out cracks.

Logistic-regression analysis identified which features track with symptoms. Cold sensitivity (the predominant symptom) was associated with unrestored-but-cracked marginal ridges, pre-removal-visible cracks, and combined body+cusp cracks, and was *negatively* associated with staining (stained cracks are inferred to be stable/long-standing with reactionary dentine and tubule sclerosis). Notably, the bite test was negative in roughly half of all cracked teeth, so a negative bite test does not rule out a crack; a positive bite test was overwhelmingly driven by a presenting complaint of pain-on-biting.

## Key Contributions

- **General-practice ≠ specialist-practice presentation.** Asymptomatic dominance (55.1%) and cold sensitivity as the leading symptom contrast with the symptomatic, complex picture from referral centres.
- **Cracks hidden under restorations.** 55.1% only visible post-removal; these are usually asymptomatic and were excluded by prior network studies — supporting the view that cracked teeth are underreported.
- **Bite test is not a rule-out test.** 51.7% of confirmed cracked teeth gave a negative bite test.
- **Actionable inspection rule.** Where a crack is visible before removal, look specifically at the cusp base — visible cracking is more likely to carry combined body+cusp cracks.
- **Restoration-association as the explanatory axis.** With only 6.8% unrestored teeth, the authors frame the restored-vs-unrestored distinction as the reason symptom reports diverge across the literature.

## Methodology

- **Design**: retrospective cross-sectional, convenience sample, single practitioner/practice (2014–2017).
- **n**: 152 records → 147 analysed (5 excluded for incompleteness).
- **Crack types**: body (along the midline, BL/MD, on pulp floor), cuspal (under a cusp, needs cusp-base inspection), intersecting (joining). Categorised after restoration removal.
- **Tests**: Frost Bite refrigerant pulp sensibility (no CO2 ice); Tooth Slooth bite test, pain-on-release = positive. No transillumination.
- **Variables**: patient (age, gender, symptoms, periodontal), tooth (occlusion, excursive guidance, wear, tooth type/jaw, restoration type, crack visibility, opposing tooth), crack (direction, number, location, staining).
- **Stats**: Jamovi — descriptive, chi-square (symptom status), stepwise model building, binomial + multinomial logistic regression; p<0.05.

## Results

**Descriptive** — Mean age 62.3±10 y (51.7% >60); 57.1% female. Asymptomatic 55.1%. Among symptomatic (n=66): thermal sensitivity 40%, pain on biting 33%, combined 25%. Mandibular 2nd molar most affected (23.8%); molars 80.3%. Amalgam restorations 69.4%, composite 23.8%. Cracks not visible before removal 55.1%; bite test negative 51.7%. Crack direction both MD+BL 50.3%; single 44.2%, intersecting 27.9%; body 72.8%, body+cusp 23.8%; stained 46.3%.

**Symptom status (chi-square, significant)** — age <60 y (52.4% vs 35.7% symptomatic, p=0.04); crack visible before removal (57.6% vs 30.9%, p<0.001); unstained (50.6% vs 33.8%, p=0.04); body & cusp location (60.0% vs 37.5%, p=0.01).

**Cold sensitivity (binomial LR)** — unrestored marginal ridge OR 2.89 (1.06–7.82); crack visible before removal OR 3.04 (1.14–8.12); body & cusp crack OR 3.11 (1.13–8.54); staining OR 0.30 (0.11–0.74, inverse). Antagonist status NS.

**Positive bite test (binomial LR)** — molar OR 3.71 (1.16–11.78); pain on biting OR 36.96 (7.79–175.34); crack visible before removal OR 3.44 (1.49–7.92). Number of cracks NS.

**Crack number/type (multinomial LR)** — intersecting vs single: molar OR 8.79 (1.83–42.10), amalgam OR 4.81 (1.68–13.78); multiple vs single: mandibular jaw OR 0.40 (0.16–0.95, inverse).

**Location, body & cusp (binomial LR)** — restored marginal ridge OR 3.22 (1.18–8.77); crack visible before removal OR 3.27 (1.40–7.63).
