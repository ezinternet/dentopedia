## One-line Summary

Prospective RCT (n=106, post-RCT cracked teeth): porcelain inlay outperformed full crown on success rate, day-1/3/7 VAS pain, 6-month gingival indices, masticatory efficiency, and bite force (all P<0.05). (Abstract-only; Chinese-language study.)

## Summary

According to PubMed (PMID 40275667), this prospective randomized trial compared full crown (control) vs. porcelain inlay (experimental) for the definitive restoration of cracked teeth after root canal treatment. 106 patients (December 2020–December 2023) were allocated 53/53 by random-number table. The porcelain inlay group showed a higher clinical "good rate" and lower adverse-reaction incidence, lower post-treatment VAS pain at days 1/3/7, better gingival/periodontal indices (GI, PLI, BI, PD) at 6 months, and higher masticatory efficiency and bite force at 1/3/6 months — all differences favoring the inlay at P<0.05. The authors conclude porcelain inlay is clinically superior to full crown for these teeth.

This page is **abstract-only** (full text not retrieved) and the source is a **Chinese-language study** with no DOI assigned; methods detail (crack severity classification, blinding/allocation concealment, material specifics, long-term survival) cannot be verified from the abstract, and follow-up is short (≤6 months). The direction of effect — a more conservative intracoronal restoration outperforming full cuspal coverage — runs against the broader cracked-tooth emphasis on cuspal protection, so it should be read as a single short-term trial rather than a practice-changing result.

**Clinically:** for a cracked tooth that has had root canal treatment and retains adequate sound structure for an intracoronal restoration, a well-bonded ceramic inlay may preserve more tooth and yield comparable or better short-term function and gingival health than a full crown — but case selection (crack extent, remaining wall thickness, occlusal load) and the lack of multi-year survival data mean cuspal-coverage protection remains the safer default for high-risk cracks until corroborated by longer, higher-quality trials.

## Key Contributions

- Direct randomized head-to-head of **full crown vs. porcelain inlay** for post-endodontic cracked teeth — a comparison underrepresented in the wiki's cracked-tooth evidence.
- Reports the conservative porcelain inlay as superior across success, pain, gingival, and masticatory endpoints, contrasting with the cuspal-coverage default.
- Tracks quantitative masticatory function (chewing efficiency %, bite force lbs) to 6 months.

## Methodology

- **Design**: Prospective study, randomized by random-number table.
- **Population**: 106 patients with cracked teeth after root canal treatment, Dec 2020–Dec 2023.
- **Groups**: Experimental = porcelain inlay (n=53); Control = full crown (n=53).
- **Endpoints**: good rate & adverse-reaction incidence; VAS pain (baseline, day 1/3/7); gingival condition GI/PLI/BI/PD (baseline, 6 mo); masticatory efficiency & bite force (baseline, 1/3/6 mo).
- **Analysis**: SPSS 25.0; P<0.05 threshold.

## Results

- **Success / adverse reactions** — porcelain inlay: higher good rate, lower total adverse-reaction incidence vs. full crown (P<0.05).
- **VAS pain (day 1 / 3 / 7)** — inlay 4.43±0.68 / 3.15±0.63 / 2.04±0.51 vs. crown 4.86±0.57 / 3.81±0.67 / 2.86±0.52; inlay significantly lower at every timepoint (P<0.05).
- **Gingival indices at 6 months** — GI, PLI, BI, PD all significantly lower with inlay than crown (P<0.05).
- **Masticatory efficiency (1 / 3 / 6 mo)** — inlay 70.25±5.57% / 85.36±6.74% / 92.57±6.35% vs. crown 62.35±5.44% / 73.57±6.32% / 84.26±6.22% (P<0.05).
- **Bite force (1 / 3 / 6 mo, lbs)** — inlay 112.52±9.67 / 130.52±11.58 / 143.54±13.40 vs. crown 98.53±9.47 / 113.25±10.54 / 125.36±12.46 (P<0.05).
- **Conclusion** — porcelain inlay clinically superior to full crown for cracked teeth after RCT: better chewing ability, better gingival condition, fewer adverse reactions.
