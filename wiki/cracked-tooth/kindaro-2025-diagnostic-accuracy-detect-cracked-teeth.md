## One-line Summary

Ex vivo diagnostic-accuracy study (n=30 extracted teeth, micro-CT gold standard, STARD/QUADAS-2, 4 examiners) comparing macrophotography, surgical microscope (5.1/12.5/21.25x), fiber-optic transillumination, and DIAGNOcam: transillumination was most accurate (65.3%) and most sensitive (68.8%) but had poor specificity (53.6%, many false positives), whereas high-magnification microscope and macrophotography gave the highest specificity (92.9%) and PPV (96.7%/96.4%); no single visual tool can definitively diagnose or locate cracks.

## Summary

Cracked tooth diagnosis is clinically difficult because symptoms overlap with pulpitis, apical periodontitis, and occlusal trauma, and the diagnostic accuracy of the visual tools dentists actually use had never been tested under STARD guidelines against a high-quality reference. This ex vivo study mounted 30 teeth (extracted on crack suspicion) in a phantom-head simulation and had four examiners (two senior >20 yr, two new graduates <5 yr) score images from four index tests — macrophotography, surgical microscope at three magnifications, fiber-optic transillumination (six angles), and DIAGNOcam — against a **micro-CT gold standard** (a crack counting only if it crossed the dentino-enamel junction).

The bottom line: **none of the four visual tools can reliably diagnose a crack on its own.** Transillumination caught the most cracks (highest sensitivity/accuracy) but at the cost of many false positives (specificity 53.6%) — it lights up insignificant craze lines, risking overtreatment. High-magnification microscopy and macrophotography were the opposite: very high specificity and PPV (a positive finding is almost always a real crack) but they miss many true cracks. Location accuracy was poor (~50%) for every tool. Experience helped modestly but significantly. Combined with registry evidence that cracked teeth rarely fracture or progress, the authors argue for minimally invasive management and multi-tool diagnostic strategies rather than reliance on any single visual aid.

## Key Contributions

- First **STARD/QUADAS-2-guided** head-to-head crack-detection accuracy comparison against a **micro-CT** reference (vs the less-accurate ground sections of prior DIAGNOcam work).
- Quantifies the **transillumination overdiagnosis trap**: ~47/100 crack-free teeth would be falsely flagged.
- Identifies **high-mag microscopy (21.25x) and macrophotography** as the high-specificity/high-PPV "rule-in" tools that hold up across prevalence scenarios.
- Confirms **clinical experience improves accuracy** (64.6% vs 56.8%, p=0.011).
- Shows **no tool localizes cracks** adequately (~50%; only 11.1% spread best-to-worst).

## Methodology

- **Design**: ex vivo quantitative diagnostic-accuracy study; STARD reporting + QUADAS-2 bias assessment.
- **Sample**: 30 teeth extracted due to suspected cracks; kept hydrated (formalin → saline); 3-9 mo retention.
- **Reference standard**: micro-CT (Skyscan 1272, Bruker; 0.007 mm pixel, 100 kV, 100 uA, 0.11 mm Cu filter), read by a blinded endodontist. True positive = crack extending past the DEJ; enamel-only cracks ignored.
- **Index tests** (13 images/tooth): macrophotography (Nikon D850 + 105 macro + 20 mm extension), surgical microscope (CJ-Optik Flexion) at 5.1x/12.5x/21.25x, fiber-optic transillumination (Microlux 2, AdDent) from 6 angles, DIAGNOcam (plain + near-infrared 850 nm transillumination; fluorescence hidden). Tooth mounted in silicone putty → endodontic sextant → phantom head.
- **Examiners**: 4 of varying experience; randomized PowerPoint images over two Zoom sessions 2 wk apart, 10% overlap for intra-rater reliability.
- **Analysis** (Jamovi): accuracy/sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV; Cohen's & Fleiss' kappa; chi-square (experience); PPV/NPV modeled across 0-100% prevalence. "Unsure" and enamel-crack responses re-coded as "no crack."

## Results

**Prevalence**: 80% (24/30) of teeth had DEJ-crossing cracks on micro-CT; 20% (6/30) did not despite being extracted on crack suspicion.

| Index test | Accuracy | Sensitivity | Specificity | PPV | NPV | Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Macrophotography | 60.6 | 51.9 | **92.9** | 96.4 | 34.2 | 51.0 |
| Microscope 5.1x (low) | 52.2 | 48.0 | 64.7 | 80.3 | 29.3 | 43.4 |
| Microscope 12.5x (med) | 55.1 | 50.0 | 79.2 | 91.8 | 25.3 | 44.4 |
| Microscope 21.25x (high) | 62.5 | 54.6 | **92.9** | **96.7** | 34.7 | 49.9 |
| DIAGNOcam | 64.4 | 65.4 | 60.7 | 86.1 | 32.1 | 51.5 |
| Transillumination | **65.3** | **68.8** | 53.6 | 83.5 | 33.3 | **54.6** |

(All % with 95% CI in source; location accuracy as %.)

- **Rule-in vs rule-out split**: transillumination & DIAGNOcam = higher sensitivity, lower specificity (good for catching, bad for false positives); macrophotography & high-mag microscope = higher specificity/PPV (a positive is trustworthy) but lower sensitivity.
- **Location**: poor across all tools (~43-55%); only 11.1% difference between best (transillumination 54.6%) and worst (low-mag microscope 43.4%).
- **Reliability**: intra-rater kappa 0.481-0.650 (moderate-to-substantial; 74-84% agreement); inter-rater fair (Fleiss' kappa 0.275 across all four).
- **Experience**: experienced mean 64.6% vs inexperienced 56.8% (chi-square 6.54, **p=0.011**); but a new graduate was both the most accurate (Examiner 4, 70.4%) and least accurate (Examiner 3, 43.2%) individual.
- **Prevalence modeling**: high-mag microscope and macrophotography perform well at both low and high crack prevalence; low-mag microscope (5.1x) worst — relevant since only ~25% of dentists use loupes >3.5x.
