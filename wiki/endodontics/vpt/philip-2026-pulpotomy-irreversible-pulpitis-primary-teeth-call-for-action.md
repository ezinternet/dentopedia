## Summary

This narrative review by Philip, Duggal, and Nazzal challenges the long-standing clinical doctrine that a primary tooth clinically diagnosed with irreversible pulpitis requires pulpectomy or extraction. Drawing on structured database searches (MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane — up to January 2026) and 7 clinical studies plus 2 meta-analyses, the review builds the case that **pulpotomy is a viable, minimally invasive alternative** to pulpectomy for carefully selected primary molars with irreversible pulpitis.

**Histological premise**: Contemporary immunocytochemical and vascular studies show that primary and permanent tooth pulps have similar vascularity, immune-cell distribution, and defense mechanisms. Dhillon et al. (2023) found that the AAE clinical diagnosis of irreversible pulpitis matched histological irreversible inflammation in **less than half** of primary molars — suggesting the coronal inflammatory boundary is often more limited than the clinical label implies, leaving the radicular pulp potentially viable.

**Clinical evidence**: Across the 7 included studies (3 RCTs, 2 prospective non-randomized clinical trials, 1 retrospective cohort, 1 prospective single-arm), calcium silicate cements (MTA, Biodentine/BD, iRoot BP Plus, CEM, BCP) with preformed metal crown (PMC) restoration consistently achieved clinical success of **95–100%** and radiographic success of **90–100%** at 6-month to 18-month follow-up. Formocresol was the notable outlier at 75% clinical success. Two meta-analyses (Lin et al. 2024; Chawla et al. 2026) independently concluded pulpotomy is a viable alternative to conventional pulpectomy for irreversible pulpitis in primary teeth.

**New diagnostic framework**: The review proposes four pulpitis stages for primary teeth (adapted from Wolters et al. 2017 for permanent teeth): **Initial pulpitis** (heightened non-lingering response, limited coronal inflammation → indirect pulp capping/Hall crown), **Mild pulpitis** (lingering ≤20 s, limited coronal inflammation → indirect pulp capping/Hall crown), **Moderate pulpitis** (spontaneous dull pain controlled by analgesics, extensive coronal inflammation → full coronal pulpotomy + PMC), **Severe pulpitis** (severe spontaneous pain, limited analgesic relief, possible radicular extension → pulpotomy if haemostasis within 10 min; else pulpectomy/extraction). This replaces the binary reversible/irreversible terminology.

**Case selection for pulpotomy**: Vital pulp confirmed intra-operatively (uniformly reddish-pink vascular tissue); haemostasis achieved with NaOCl (0.5–5%) within 10 minutes; no clinical swelling or sinus tract; no periapical radiolucency; no pathological root resorption; early-stage (not extensive) furcal radiolucency acceptable. Permanent successor not due to erupt within 6 months.

**Procedural protocol key points**: Rubber dam mandatory; NaOCl preferred over saline (disinfection) and ferric sulphate (which masks true pulp status); 2–3 mm of calcium silicate cement over radicular stumps; immediate PMC or preformed zirconia crown. Follow-up at 6 and 12 months, then annually up to 4 years (mirroring ESE protocol for permanent teeth VPT).

**Call for action**: The authors explicitly call for revision of AAPD and EAPD guidelines to incorporate this diagnostic framework and recognize minimally invasive pulpotomy as a primary treatment option alongside pulpectomy for irreversible pulpitis in primary teeth.

## Key Contributions

- Histological reappraisal: primary pulp defense mechanisms comparable to permanent pulp; clinical diagnosis of irreversible pulpitis overestimates radicular inflammation extent.
- First comprehensive synthesis proposing a four-stage diagnostic nomenclature for primary tooth pulpitis with treatment recommendations per stage.
- Detailed procedural protocol with case selection criteria (including revised haemostasis threshold: ≤10 min vs traditional ≤5 min).
- Evidence summary: 7 studies, 95–100% clinical and 90–100% radiographic success with calcium silicate cements.
- Formal call for international guideline revision (AAPD/EAPD).

## Methodology

Narrative review; structured literature search (MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library) to January 2026. PICO: primary teeth with clinical irreversible pulpitis; pulpotomy vs none/pulpectomy/extraction; clinical + radiographic success at ≥6 months. Inclusion: RCTs, NRCTs, prospective/retrospective cohort/case-control studies, English language. No formal risk-of-bias tool applied (not a systematic review). Manual reference screening performed. Extracted data: study design, sample size, age, haemostasis details, medicament, restoration, follow-up, outcomes.

## Results

**Seven clinical studies** (aggregate n across arms: ~390+ pulpotomized teeth), patient ages 3–9 years, primary molars:

| Study | n | Medicament | Clinical success | Radiographic success |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Philip et al. 2025 | 50 | MTA | 100% (6 mo) | 93.9% (6 mo) |
| Sabbagh et al. 2024 | 74 | CEM | 98.6% (6 mo), 97.1% (≥12 mo) | 98.6% (6 mo), 96.4% (≥12 mo) |
| Al-Nassar et al. 2023 | 40 | MTA/BCP | 95–100% (6 & ≥12 mo) | 95–100% (6 & ≥12 mo) |
| Hu et al. 2023 | 88 | iRoot BP Plus | 98.9% (18 mo) | 95.5% (18 mo) |
| Eshghi et al. 2022 | 52 | MTA/BD | 96–100% | 96–100% |
| Al-Awwad et al. 2021 | 36 | MTA/FC/PRF | MTA 100%, FC 75%, PRF 91.7% (6 mo) | — |
| Memarpour et al. 2016 | 50 | CEM | 97.8% (6 mo), 92.8% (≥12 mo) | 97.8% (6 mo), 90.4% (≥12 mo) |

**Two meta-analyses**: Lin et al. 2024 (5 studies, 266 teeth, 3–9 years): pulpotomy a prospective alternative to pulpectomy. Chawla et al. 2026 (J Dent, DOI 10.1016/j.jdent.2026.106329): pulpotomy effective, minimally invasive alternative (clinical and radiographic success comparable to pulpectomy).

**Haemostasis**: NaOCl (0.5–5%) superior to saline and ferric sulphate; 10-minute cut-off proposed (vs traditional 5 min); NaOCl concentration not a decisive prognostic factor.

**Medicament comparison**: Calcium silicate cements as a class outperform formocresol; individual CSC types (MTA, BD, CEM, BCP, iRoot) show comparable high success rates.
