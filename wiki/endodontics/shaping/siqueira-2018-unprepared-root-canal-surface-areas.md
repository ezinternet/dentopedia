## Summary

Successful endodontic treatment depends on infection control, and chemomechanical preparation is its most important phase. But instruments physically cannot reach the whole canal wall. Micro-CT studies show **~10–50% of round/small canals** and **10–80% of oval/flattened canals** remain unprepared after instrumentation, with the **apical third worst affected**. The reasons are geometric: a final instrument smaller than the initial canal diameter, curvature concentrating cutting on a subset of walls, and irregular oval/C-shaped/flattened anatomies that round rotary preparation cannot conform to. Histology confirms this directly — instrumented oval incisors retain **predentin and pulp remnants** on walls the file never touched.

These untouched recesses, isthmuses, and ramifications shelter **biofilm** that survives both mechanical debridement and irrigation. Even with 2.5% NaOCl (a 10²–10⁵-fold bacterial reduction, ~95–99%), bacteria remain detectable in **30–60% of apical-periodontitis canals** after preparation. Because instruments act mainly on the main canal, disinfection of recesses depends chiefly on the **chemical** action of irrigants and medicaments. The review therefore frames the clinical response as a **supplementary-disinfection** problem: enlarge the apical preparation adequately, prepare to full working length, irrigate with large/renewed volumes at sufficient needle depth, add **irrigant activation**, and adopt anatomy-adapted techniques for oval/C-shaped canals.

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies **unprepared canal surface** as a primary, anatomy-driven cause of persistent infection: round/small ~10–50%, oval/flattened ~10–80%, apical third worst.
- Explains the geometric causes (sub-diameter final file, curvature, non-round morphology) — actionable for instrument/sequence selection.
- Connects untouched surface to the **biofilm-persistence → post-treatment apical periodontitis** chain, explaining why ~30–60% of canals remain bacteria-positive after preparation.
- Establishes that, since files mostly shape the main canal, **chemical disinfection (irrigation/activation)** must do the work in recesses — motivating supplementary disinfection protocols.

## Methodology

Critical narrative review integrating micro-CT surface-area measurements, histology/histobacteriology of instrumented extracted teeth, and antimicrobial-efficacy studies (culture + molecular). Organized as: antimicrobial effect of preparation → mechanisms by which bacteria evade instruments/irrigants → correlative evaluation methods → therapeutic strategies for untouched areas.

## Results

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Unprepared surface, round/small canals | ~10–50% |
| Unprepared surface, oval/flattened canals | ~10–80% |
| Apical third | higher untouched fraction than overall |
| Bacteria-positive canals after preparation | 30–60% |
| 2.5% NaOCl bacterial reduction | 10²–10⁵-fold (~95–99%) |
| CHX antimicrobial effect | similar to NaOCl |

**Therapeutic levers**: adequate apical preparation size; prepare to full working length; large-volume, frequently exchanged irrigant at sufficient needle depth; supplementary irrigant activation; anatomy-adapted (reciprocating/adaptive/supplementary) techniques for oval/C-shaped canals.
