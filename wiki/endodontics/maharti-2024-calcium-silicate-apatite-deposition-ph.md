In-vitro SEM/EDX + pH study (15 premolars, 14-day PBS immersion) showing premixed Ceraseal deposits more interfacial apatite (23.65 vs 18.49 µm) and reaches higher pH (11.48 vs 9.19 at day 14) than AH Plus Bioceramic, indicating superior biomineralization.

## Summary

This in-vitro laboratory study compared the biomineralization potential of two premixed calcium silicate-based root canal sealers — **Ceraseal** (Meta Biomed Co., Cheongju, Korea) and **AH Plus Bioceramic** (Dentsply Sirona, USA) — by measuring apatite deposition on root canal dentin (SEM/EDX) and pH change over 14 days in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Both sealers formed an apatite-rich interfacial layer ("mineral infiltration zone") on the dentin wall and alkalinized the medium, but Ceraseal outperformed AH Plus Bioceramic on both measures. The authors attribute this to Ceraseal's higher calcium silicate proportion, which yields more silanol nucleation sites and greater calcium/hydroxyl ion release; AH Plus Bioceramic's reformulation reduced calcium silicate and added zirconium dioxide. The null hypothesis (no difference) was rejected.

According to PubMed, this work is reported in the *European Journal of Dentistry* ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1055/s-0044-1788685)).

## Key Contributions

- First wiki anchor for the **bioactivity mechanism** of premixed bioceramic sealers (apatite deposition + alkalinization), complementing existing clinical/cytotoxicity Ceraseal pages.
- Direct in-vitro head-to-head: Ceraseal vs AH Plus Bioceramic on interfacial apatite thickness and pH trajectory.
- Mechanistic link: higher calcium silicate proportion → more apatite + sustained higher pH; zirconium dioxide substitution lowers alkalizing activity.

## Methodology

- **Apatite test (n = 15 premolars, 5/group)**: ProTaper Gold crown-down to F3; 2.5% NaOCl + 17% EDTA (sonic activation); F3 gutta-percha + sealer. Groups: AH Plus Bioceramic, Ceraseal, dentin-only PBS control. Set 24 h (37°C, 100% humidity), then 14-day PBS immersion (changed every 5 days). Roots sectioned 5 mm from apex, platinum-coated, SEM/EDX (Jeol). Interfacial apatite thickness = mean of 50 perpendicular lines via ImageJ; inter-observer ICC.
- **pH test (n = 3/group)**: sealers in molds (2 mm × 5 mm), set 24 h, PBS immersion; digital pH meter (Hanna) at days 0, 7, 14.
- **Stats**: Mann–Whitney (apatite), one-way ANOVA + post hoc LSD (pH); p < 0.05.

## Results

- **Apatite deposition (median, µm)**: Ceraseal **23.65** (21.93–25.78) > AH Plus Bioceramic **18.49** (18.02–19.43); significant (p < 0.05). ICC = 0.998.
- **Interfacial Ca/P ratio**: AH Plus Bioceramic 2.01, Ceraseal 4.01 — both above stoichiometric hydroxyapatite (1.67); dentin Ca/P 1.61 / 1.71.
- **pH (mean)**: baseline ~7.4 (0 h). Day 7 — Ceraseal 11.29, AH Plus Bioceramic 9.32, control 7.39. Day 14 — Ceraseal **11.48**, AH Plus Bioceramic **9.19**, control 7.39. Significant group differences at days 7 and 14 (p < 0.05).
- **Trajectory**: Ceraseal pH rose continuously through day 14; AH Plus Bioceramic peaked at day 7 then declined (within-group day-7→14 change not significant for either).
- **Bottom line**: Ceraseal demonstrated superior biomineralization potential — more interfacial apatite and consistently higher alkalinity.
