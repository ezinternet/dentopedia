## One-line Summary

Case report (n=1, 11-year-old boy): an odontogenic cutaneous sinus tract on the left cheek, secondary to chronic periradicular periodontitis of mandibular first molar (tooth 36), was misdiagnosed/treated dermatologically for 6 months but resolved fully after root canal therapy plus granuloma debridement, with complete healing at 6 months.

## Summary

Odontogenic cutaneous sinus tracts (OCSTs) are uncommon extraoral draining lesions that arise from chronic periapical infection but typically present without dental symptoms, so they are frequently misdiagnosed and mistreated by dermatologists. This case report describes an 11-year-old boy with a 6-month non-healing, periodically draining abscess on the left cheek who had already undergone dermatological surgery and repeated antibiotic courses without resolution. The offending tooth was the left mandibular first molar (tooth 36), which had a glass-ionomer filling, was non-responsive to pulp testing (necrotic), and showed a periapical radiolucency around the distal root apex. After excluding tuberculosis and confirming an odontogenic source, root canal therapy combined with chronic granuloma debridement led to complete healing of the cutaneous lesion and resolution of the periapical lesion at 6 months. The report reinforces that an endodontic origin must be the first consideration in any draining facial or cervical lesion.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates a pediatric OCST resolved with tooth-preserving root canal therapy rather than extraction.
- Reinforces endodontic origin as the priority in the differential diagnosis of facial/cervical draining lesions (vs. pyogenic granuloma, foreign body reaction, deep fungal infection, squamous cell carcinoma, osteomyelitis).
- Illustrates the diagnostic workup that confirms the odontogenic source: pulp/percussion testing, periapical radiographs, TB exclusion, and pus culture.
- Highlights the diagnostic-delay problem — only ~50% of OCST patients report dental symptoms, leading many to seek dermatological care first and undergo unnecessary surgery/antibiotics.

## Methodology

- **Patient**: 11-year-old boy, single case (n=1), referred after 6 months of a non-healing draining cheek lesion previously managed dermatologically.
- **Diagnosis**: ~2.5 cm fluctuant reddish abscess 2 cm above the inferior mandibular border with an unobvious sinus tract; tooth 36 necrotic (no response to electric pulp or heat test), tender to percussion; periapical radiolucency at the distal root apex. TB nucleic-acid and antibody tests negative; pus culture showed 0% specific aerobic/anaerobic infection.
- **Treatment (multi-visit RCT)**: trapezoidal access and incision/drainage; cleaning of 4 canals (MB, ML, DB, DL) with K-files and ProTaper Ni-Ti rotary (crown-down to S2, working length 19.5 mm via Raypex5 apex locator); irrigation with 3% sodium hypochlorite and 3% hydrogen peroxide; Vitapex (Ca(OH)₂/iodoform) intracanal dressing; obturation with gutta-percha and Cortisomol sealer by cold lateral condensation.
- **Adjunct**: residual red cheek granulation tissue treated by chronic granuloma debridement under general + local (Scandonest) anesthesia.

## Results

- Abscess markedly reduced after first-visit drainage.
- Cutaneous sinus tract closed by the third visit (~2 weeks).
- Granulation tissue resolved ~2 weeks after debridement.
- At 6 months: cheek skin indistinguishable from surrounding tissue (slight residual hyperpigmentation only); periapical radiolucency resolved — satisfactory healing.
