## One-line Summary

SR+MA (4 retrospective studies, 2,009 CBCTs, 179 cases) — pooled CBCT-assessed prevalence of dens invaginatus is 9.0% (95% CI 7.2–10.8%, p<0.001), roughly double conventional-radiograph estimates (0.25–7.7%); maxillary lateral incisor most affected, Oehlers Type I most common, no consistent sex difference.

## Summary

Dens invaginatus (dens in dente) is a developmental anomaly in which dental tissues fold from the outer surface toward the pulp before calcification, complicating the canal anatomy and the endodontic prognosis. This is the first SR+MA of its CBCT-assessed prevalence. Pooling four retrospective studies (2,009 CBCT images; 179 cases) gave a random-effects prevalence of **9.0% (95% CI 7.2–10.8%, p<0.001)** with slight heterogeneity (I² = 43.7%). This is roughly **double** the 0.25–7.7% range historically reported from 2D radiographs — because CBCT's 3D imaging avoids the anatomical noise, geometric distortion, and structural overlap that hide invaginations on periapical/panoramic films. The single head-to-head study (Capar) showed the gap directly: **10.7% on CBCT vs 3% on panoramic** in the same patients. The **maxillary lateral incisor** is the most affected tooth (then the maxillary central incisor) and **Oehlers Type I** (crown-confined) is the most common form. The authors conclude any suspected dens invaginatus should be imaged with CBCT for accurate diagnosis and treatment planning, balanced against ALARA.

## Key Contributions

- **First SR+MA** quantifying CBCT-assessed dens invaginatus prevalence.
- **Pooled 9.0%** (95% CI 7.2–10.8%) — establishes a CBCT reference figure ~2× the conventional-radiograph range.
- Quantifies the **CBCT-vs-2D detection gap** (Capar: 10.7% vs 3% in-sample).
- Reaffirms tooth predilection (**maxillary lateral incisor**) and **Oehlers Type I** predominance.

## Methodology

- PRISMA SR; CoCoPop question on adult dens invaginatus prevalence by CBCT. PubMed + SCOPUS to 21 Oct 2021, no language/year limit.
- Inclusion: adults, **CBCT** diagnosis. Exclusion: 2D-only diagnosis, no prevalence, case reports.
- 7 screened → **4 included** (all retrospective; from Capar only the CBCT arm pooled). 2,009 CBCTs total.
- Random-effects meta-analysis (OpenMeta Analyst); Tau² and Higgins I². Quality: Oxford scale (all 3b), Hoy prevalence Risk-of-Bias tool (80% parameters reported = low overall risk).

## Results

| Study | Population | n patients | Cases | Prevalence | Sex difference |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chen | Chinese (Beijing) | 1,004 | 85 | **8.47%** | Higher in males (10.75% vs 6.68%) |
| Alkadi | Saudi | 505 | 37 | **7.3%** | None significant |
| Mabrouk | Tunisian | 200 | 25 | **12.5%** | Men higher (NS) |
| Capar | Turkish | 300 | 32 (CBCT) / 9 (pano) | **10.7% CBCT vs 3% panoramic** | None significant |
| **Pooled** | — | **2,009 CBCTs** | **179** | **9.0% (95% CI 7.2–10.8%)** | — |

- **Heterogeneity**: I² = 43.73% (slight); Tau² = 0.00.
- **Most-affected tooth**: maxillary lateral incisor > maxillary central incisor (all studies).
- **Oehlers type**: Type I (crown-confined, enamel-lined) most frequent.
- **Oehlers classification reference**: I = confined within crown; II = extends to amelocemental line (blind, no PDL communication); III = extends through root (IIIa = pseudo-foramen lateral PDL communication; IIIb = apical-foramen PDL communication).

**Clinical relevance**: Because invaginations create a second, often non-instrumentable lumen that channels bacteria toward the pulp/periapex, early CBCT identification (especially of maxillary lateral incisors, and Type II/III) is decisive for prognosis — Type I may need only sealing/monitoring, whereas Type III often requires complex endodontic or surgical management. A ~9% CBCT prevalence means the anomaly is far more common than 2D screening suggests.
