## One-line Summary

SR+MA of 72 in-vivo CBCT studies (31 countries, 26,302 patients / 37,994 mandibular first molars): a third root (Radix Entomolaris) occurs in 8.85% of teeth and 10.3% of patients globally, with a steep East-Asia/Mongoloid → West gradient (East Asia 24% → Europe 2% → Africa 0.9%), ~46% bilateral symmetry, right-side dominance, and no sex difference.

## Summary

This is the largest prevalence meta-analysis of the three-rooted permanent mandibular first molar (PMFM) — i.e. the **Radix Entomolaris (RE)**, an accessory root that usually arises distolingually. Restricting inclusion to in-vivo CBCT (the only method that avoids the underestimation of micro-CT/extraction and the root-overlap blind spot of plain periapical X-ray), it pools 72 studies covering 26,302 patients and 37,994 teeth across 31 countries.

The headline numbers: **8.85% of teeth (95% CI 6.60–11.39)** and **10.3% of patients (95% CI 6.9–14.4)** carry a third root, and among affected patients **~46% show it bilaterally**. The most clinically actionable finding is the **ethnic/geographic gradient**: prevalence falls steeply from East Asia (~24% of teeth, ~29% of patients) westward to Europe (~2%) and Africa (~0.9%). The authors attribute the distribution to the Mongoloid lineage, with globalization/admixture blurring it in migrant populations (e.g. Americas). Right side > left side; no sex difference.

For an endodontist, this is a **missed-canal map**: in an East-Asian / Mongoloid patient, roughly one in four mandibular first molars (and ~one in three by side) has an extra distolingual root and its own canal — so the radix root must be actively searched for, the access cavity modified, and the contralateral tooth checked.

## Key Contributions

- Pooled **global tooth-level (8.85%), patient-level (10.3%), and bilateral-symmetry (46.22%)** estimates — first MA to add the latter two.
- Quantified the **East→West / Mongoloid prevalence gradient** by geographic subgroup, collapsing I² from 99% to as low as 0% (Africa).
- Separated **Radix Entomolaris (12.3%)** from **Radix Paramolaris (0.1%)** by sensitivity analysis — RP is rare and mostly anthropological.
- Documented **right-side predominance** (16% vs 12%) and **no sexual dimorphism** (male 13.3% vs female 13%).

## Methodology

- In-vivo CBCT studies only; 5 databases to Oct 2021, no language limit (4 translated). JBI appraisal (37 moderate, 35 high RoB). Random-effects proportion MA in MetaXL v5.3. PROSPERO CRD42022302195.
- Subgroup (geography, side, gender), sensitivity (RE vs RP), publication-bias (funnel/Doi/LFK) analyses. Publication bias present for objectives I (LFK −4.02) and III (LFK −3.19); objective II symmetric.

| Tooth-level 3-root | 8.85% (6.60–11.39) | 99% | 68 |
| Patient-level 3-root | 10.3% (6.9–14.4) | 98% | 44 |
| Bilateral symmetry | 46.22% (39.13–53.39) | 90% | 40 |
| Right side | 16% (12.2–20.2) | 97% | 35 |
| Left side | 12.1% (9.2–15.4) | 96% | 35 |
| Male | 13.3% (9.8–17.3) | 97% | 35 |
| Female | 13% (9.7–16.7) | 97% | 35 |
| Radix Entomolaris | 12.3% (9.3–15.7) | 99% | 43 |
| Radix Paramolaris | 0.1% (0.0–0.1) | 30% | 43 |

**Geographic gradient (tooth-level):** East Asia 24.1% · SE Asia 13% · South Asia 4.7% · West Asia 4.4% · Europe 2% · America 1.8% · Africa 0.9%. **Patient-level East Asia 28.8%; bilateral symmetry East Asia 60.3% vs Europe 18.7%.** Clinical implication: in East-Asian/Mongoloid patients, treat the distolingual radix root and its canal as expected, not exceptional — modify access (trapezoidal pulp-floor, DL orifice ~3 mm lingual to the distobuccal orifice), anticipate ~30° buccolingual root curvature (file-separation risk), and screen the contralateral first molar.
