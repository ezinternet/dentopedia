## One-line Summary
RCT (n=220 teeth, 18–45 mo follow-up): single-visit and multiple-visit non-surgical root canal treatment had statistically equivalent success rates (88.9% vs 87.4%, p=0.729) and postoperative pain, but single-visit took ~31 min less chairside time.

## Summary
This single-centre, two-operator randomised clinical trial at the University of Hong Kong compared single-visit versus multiple-visit primary non-surgical root canal treatment performed by general dentists. The multiple-visit arm used an inter-appointment intracanal medicament (calcium hydroxide) before obturation; otherwise both arms used identical materials and procedures (rubber dam, ProTaper Ni-Ti rotary instrumentation, 5.25% NaOCl irrigation, core-carrier obturation). Of 256 teeth (228 patients) randomised, 220 teeth (194 patients) were analysed with a mean follow-up of 29.4 ± 9.3 months. The two protocols produced statistically indistinguishable success rates and long-term pain outcomes; the principal practical difference was that single-visit treatment saved roughly half an hour of chairside time per tooth.

## Key Contributions
- Head-to-head RCT showing clinical equivalence of single- vs multiple-visit endodontics on success rate and postoperative pain.
- Quantified chairside-time advantage of single-visit treatment (62.0 vs 92.9 min; −30.9 min, p<0.001).
- Logistic-regression prognostic factors: maxillary location (OR 3.16) and absence of preoperative apical periodontitis (OR 4.35) predicted higher success.

## Methodology
- **Design**: Parallel two-arm RCT; per-tooth randomisation via calculator random-number function; blinded independent assessor.
- **Population**: Generally healthy adults (mean age 46.4 ± 14.1) needing primary non-surgical endodontics; periodontally healthy teeth with ≥50% coronal structure. Severe acute pulpitis with swelling, systemic infection/disease, and prior pulpotomy excluded.
- **Intervention**: Identical instrumentation (ProTaper Ni-Ti), 5.25% NaOCl irrigation and core-carrier obturation in both arms; multiple-visit arm received a non-setting (calcium hydroxide) inter-appointment dressing before obturation.
- **Outcomes**: Primary = success (no clinical signs/symptoms and no radiographic periapical radiolucency); also chairside time; secondary = postoperative pain at 7 days and at final evaluation (18–45 months).
- **Power**: Designed to detect a 10% success difference (80% power, α=0.05); 256 teeth enrolled.

## Results
| Outcome | Single-visit | Multiple-visit | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Success rate | 88.9% (104/117) | 87.4% (90/103) | p=0.729, OR 1.156 (NS) |
| Chairside time | 62.0 min | 92.9 min | −30.9 min (95% CI −39.4 to −22.4), p<0.001 |
| Postop pain ≤7 days | 21% | 12% | p=0.055 (NS) |
| Postop pain ≥18 mo | 0.9% | 1.0% | p>0.999 (NS) |

- Most failures were incomplete apical healing (single 8.5%, multiple 9.7%).
- Prognostic factors for success: maxillary teeth (OR 3.16, 95% CI 1.33–7.46, p=0.009); absence of preoperative apical periodontitis (OR 4.35, 95% CI 1.43–13.24, p=0.010).
- The near-significant trend toward more early pain after single-visit treatment (p=0.055) is worth noting clinically despite not reaching significance.
