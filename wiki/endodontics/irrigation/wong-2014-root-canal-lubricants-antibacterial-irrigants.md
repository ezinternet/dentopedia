In vitro study comparing the antibacterial activity of 10 root-canal lubricants with 7 irrigants against Enterococcus faecalis, finding several lubricants matched some irrigants and that activity did not correlate with pH (range 2.9–10.3).

## Summary
This bench study assessed whether root-canal lubricants (mostly EDTA-based gels/pastes such as Glyde, RC-Prep, File-Eze) have meaningful antibacterial activity compared with standard irrigants, using serial broth dilutions inoculated with Enterococcus faecalis and other organisms. Several lubricants exhibited antimicrobial activity comparable to some irrigants, while three irrigants (including chlorhexidine-containing Consepsis) were strongest. Notably, antibacterial activity did not correlate with pH (2.9–10.3), indicating composition rather than acidity drives the effect. Lubricants thus contribute a modest antibacterial role during instrumentation.

## Key Contributions
- Antibacterial benchmarking of lubricants against irrigants.
- pH-independence of antibacterial activity.

## Methodology
Serial dilutions in tryptic soy broth with E. faecalis (and others); 10 lubricants vs 7 irrigants; MIC-style comparison.

## Results
Several lubricants comparable to some irrigants; three irrigants strongest; activity uncorrelated with pH (2.9–10.3).
