## One-line Summary
Commissioned IEJ narrative review critically appraising irrigants and activation methods, concluding NaOCl + EDTA remain the evidenced standard and no adjunct activation technique has proven improved long-term root canal treatment outcomes.

## Summary
Boutsioukis and Arias-Moliz reframe the endodontic paradigm: instrumentation serves primarily to provide access for irrigants, which bear responsibility for cleaning and disinfection. The review catalogues the challenges — biofilm EPS barriers, anatomical intricacies (isthmuses, fins, dentinal tubules), vapor lock, and rapid chlorine depletion in the ≤30 μL canal volume — and critically appraises each irrigant class and delivery/activation method. Sodium hypochlorite (0.5–8.25%) with supplemental EDTA (15–17%) remains the standard protocol. Despite extensive laboratory evidence for adjunct activation (PUI, sonic, laser, negative-pressure), no RCT-level evidence supports superior long-term clinical outcomes over syringe irrigation alone. The authors call for a fundamental redesign of research priorities.

## Key Contributions
- Paradigm statement: instrumentation = canal access for irrigants; irrigants = primary disinfection agents.
- Identifies the three-tier challenge: biofilm recalcitrance (EPS, dormant/persister cells), anatomical inaccessibility (fins, isthmuses, tubules), and physicochemical limits (vapor lock, concentration gradients).
- NaOCl canal volume ≤30 μL; rapid chlorine depletion → frequent replenishment critical.
- EDTA 15–17% (pH ~7–8) essential for smear layer removal; CHX 2% offers substantivity but no tissue dissolution and precipitates with NaOCl.
- No adjunct activation method demonstrates improved long-term outcome: landmark negative conclusion from critical appraisal.
- Multidisciplinary research agenda: chemistry + microbiology + fluid dynamics needed.

## Methodology
Narrative (non-systematic) review commissioned for the IEJ 2022 supplement. Evaluates irrigants and delivery/activation systems. Source studies flagged for in vitro bias, non-standardised protocols, and inadequate sample sizes. No formal meta-analysis.

## Results
- NaOCl concentration range in use: 0.5–8.25%; lab studies favour higher concentration; clinical RCTs show no significant difference (methodological confounds).
- Preheating NaOCl to 50–60°C: drops to 37°C rapidly intracanally — limited in vivo benefit and amplifies undesired dentin effects.
- CHX 2% vs 2.5% NaOCl (CLSM imaging, 1 min exposure): both reduce biofilm viability but NaOCl more completely disrupts structure.
- PUI, sonic, negative-pressure, laser: laboratory improvements in debridement/penetration demonstrated; long-term clinical outcome superiority not established.
