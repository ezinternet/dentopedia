## One-line Summary

Integrative review of 17 in vitro/ex vivo studies (2007–2022): non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma (NTPP) shows strong antimicrobial activity against E. faecalis and C. albicans with ≥8 min direct exposure, synergistic when combined with NaOCl/CHX, but lacks clinical standardization.

## Summary

Non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma (NTPP) generates reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (ROS/RNS) at near-room temperature, enabling microbial deactivation without thermal tissue damage. This integrative review screened 279 records and included 17 studies evaluating NTPP in root canal disinfection. The body of evidence is predominantly in vitro and ex vivo.

The two main device categories are **dielectric barrier discharge (DBD)** — generating large-area, diffuse plasma — and **plasma jets** (including OAUGDP/kINPen types) that produce a focused, flowing plasma plume suitable for canal geometry. Helium and argon are the most common working gases.

**Exposure modes**:
- *Direct*: plasma plume applied to root canal or dentinal surface (15/17 studies).
- *Indirect (PAL)*: liquid pre-activated by plasma then used as irrigant (2/17 studies).

## Key Contributions

**Antimicrobial efficacy against endodontic pathogens**
- E. faecalis (responsible for ~80% of persistent endodontic infections) was the primary target; significant log reductions reported across all direct-plasma studies.
- C. albicans also susceptible; antifungal effect comparable to antibacterial response.
- Biofilm E. faecalis is more resistant than planktonic form; older/thicker biofilms require longer exposure.

**Exposure time threshold**
- Maximum antimicrobial effect achieved at ≥8 minutes of direct exposure.
- Below this threshold, dose-response is progressive — shorter times yield partial reduction.

**Combination protocols (NTPP + irrigant)**
- NTPP combined with NaOCl or CHX is superior to either agent alone.
- Synergy allows reduced plasma exposure time — clinically attractive because it shortens procedure length.
- ROS/RNS from plasma potentiate chemical irrigant penetration or directly complement their mechanisms.

**Mechanism: ROS and RNS**
- Key ROS: H₂O₂, hydroxyl radical (·OH), ozone (O₃), singlet oxygen (¹O₂).
- Key RNS: nitric oxide (NO), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), peroxynitrite (ONOO⁻).
- These species damage microbial membranes, nucleic acids, and proteins without inducing antimicrobial resistance — a critical advantage over antibiotics.

**Plasma-activated liquid (PAL)**
- Only 2 included studies; promising (allows indirect delivery without direct plasma contact with tissue), but insufficient data for protocol recommendations.

## Results

**Safety / cytotoxicity**
- Short exposure durations generally biocompatible with periodontal and periapical cells.
- Prolonged exposure (>10–15 min depending on device) may induce cytotoxic effects.
- Safe exposure window not yet precisely defined for root canal application.

**Dentinal tubule penetration**
- Direct plasma limited at canal surface; penetration into tubule depth (where E. faecalis persists) remains inadequately studied.

**PAL**
- Both PAL studies showed antimicrobial activity; delivery via syringe is technically simpler than direct plasma application in a narrow canal.

**Unresolved variables**
- No consensus on optimal working gas, flow rate, electrode-to-tissue distance, power density, or total energy delivered.
- Cross-study comparison is severely limited by device heterogeneity.
