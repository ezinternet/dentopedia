## One-line Summary

In vitro study (n=63 teeth, 21-day E. faecalis biofilm): TAP (10 mg/mL, 12 days) achieved complete bacterial elimination; helium/oxygen cold atmospheric plasma (10 min) produced significant CFU reduction; calcium hydroxide (12 days) showed no significant antibacterial effect — efficacy hierarchy: TAP > CAP > Ca(OH)₂.

## Summary

This in vitro study from Shahid Beheshti University (Tehran, 2022) used 63 single-rooted extracted human teeth inoculated with Enterococcus faecalis ATCC 29212 and incubated for **21 days** to form a mature biofilm penetrating dentinal tubules before treatment. It is the first direct three-arm comparison of cold atmospheric plasma, calcium hydroxide, and triple antibiotic paste against established E. faecalis root canal biofilm.

After a standardized biofilm challenge, the three active treatments were applied: Ca(OH)₂ paste (12 days), TAP 10 mg/mL (12 days), and helium/oxygen CAP jet (10 minutes). Outcome was percentage CFU reduction from respective positive controls; SEM imaging provided structural biofilm evidence.

The principal finding was a clear efficacy hierarchy: **TAP completely eliminated E. faecalis** (statistically significant vs control); **CAP significantly reduced biofilm** (p < 0.05 vs control) but less so than TAP; **Ca(OH)₂ failed to show significant reduction** — consistent with systematic review evidence that Ca(OH)₂ is insufficient against established E. faecalis biofilm, particularly in deep dentinal tubules. SEM corroborated these results, showing disrupted biofilm architecture only in CAP and TAP groups.

## Key Contributions

1. **CAP is biologically active against E. faecalis biofilm** — 10 minutes of He/O₂ plasma jet produces significant antimicrobial effect via reactive oxygen/nitrogen species (RONS) interacting with bacterial membranes.
2. **TAP remains the superior intracanal medicament** against E. faecalis at the concentrations and timeframes tested; its cytotoxicity on stem cells remains the limiting factor for regenerative use.
3. **Ca(OH)₂'s E. faecalis limitation reconfirmed** — the alkaline pH mechanism is insufficient against the acid-stress-resistant E. faecalis, whose survival strategies include proton pump activity and biofilm protection.
4. **SEM structural evidence** supports CFU-based quantitative data — an important methodological strength over CFU-only studies.

## Methodology

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Teeth | 63 single-rooted extracted human teeth, standardized to 12–14 mm |
| Canal prep | ProTaper F3; 5.25% NaOCl + 17% EDTA + saline rinse |
| Biofilm | E. faecalis ATCC 29212; 0.5 McFarland; 37°C × 21 days |
| Groups | 6 groups, n=10 each |
| Active arms | Ca(OH)₂ (12d) / TAP 10 mg/mL (12d) / He/O₂ CAP (10 min) |
| Controls | Positive (methylcellulose gel, 12d or saline) + Negative (no bacteria) |
| Outcome | % CFU reduction + SEM imaging |

## Results

| Treatment | CFU Outcome | vs Control |
|---|---|---|
| TAP 10 mg/mL, 12 days | Complete elimination | Significant (p < 0.05) |
| Cold atmospheric plasma (He/O₂, 10 min) | Significant reduction | Significant (p < 0.05) |
| Calcium hydroxide, 12 days | Negligible reduction | Not significant |

- Efficacy hierarchy confirmed: **TAP > CAP > Ca(OH)₂**
- CAP mechanism: RONS generation at near-room temperature; no thermal tissue damage
- SEM: disrupted biofilm matrix in CAP and TAP groups; intact biofilm in Ca(OH)₂ group

## Clinical Implications

- CAP shows genuine promise as an endodontic disinfectant, particularly for cases where antibiotic use is restricted or where minimal-medication approaches are preferred
- Key practical barrier: delivering CAP into the full length of root canals requires specialized needle/catheter designs not yet standardized
- Ca(OH)₂ alone should not be relied upon in cases with suspected persistent E. faecalis infection — TAP or alternative antibacterial protocols warrant consideration
- For regenerative endodontics, TAP's cytotoxicity vs CAP's non-chemical mechanism warrants further head-to-head exploration with stem cell viability endpoints
