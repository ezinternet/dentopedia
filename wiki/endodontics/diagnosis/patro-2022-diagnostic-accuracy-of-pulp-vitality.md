## Summary
Patro and colleagues meta-analyzed diagnostic-accuracy studies comparing pulp vitality tests (which measure blood flow) against pulp sensibility tests (which measure nerve response) in permanent teeth. Ranked by pooled diagnostic odds ratio, the pulse oximeter (628.5) vastly outperformed the cold test (17.24), electric pulp tester (10.75), and heat test (3.47), with consistently higher pooled sensitivity and specificity on pairwise comparison and SROC analysis. The clinical signal is that a true vitality (blood-flow) test discriminates pulpal health better than the sensibility tests in routine use — but every included study was low/moderate quality on QUADAS-2, so the magnitude must be read cautiously. This is the quantitative complement to Donnermeyer's qualitative "diagnosis is hard" conclusion.

## Key Contributions
- First DOR-based accuracy ranking of the common pulp tests.
- Demonstrates blood-flow (pulse oximetry) superiority over neural-response sensibility tests.
- Provides QUADAS-2-appraised evidence with an explicit quality caveat.

## Methodology
SR+MA across PubMed/MEDLINE, Cochrane CENTRAL, Web of Science, Google Scholar, OpenGrey. TP/FP/TN/FN, sensitivity, specificity extracted/calculated; QUADAS-2 quality appraisal. Meta-analysis in MetaDTA v2.0 and RevMan 5.3. 10 studies qualitative synthesis, 5 in meta-analysis. Permanent teeth.

## Results
Pooled DOR: pulse oximeter 628.5; cold test 17.24; electric pulp tester 10.75; heat test 3.47. Pulse oximeter showed higher pooled mean sensitivity and specificity than EPT, CT, HT; SROC confirmed best negative-screening. No study rated "good" quality.

## Clinical Application
- Where equipment permits, a pulse oximeter is the most accurate single pulp test; among everyday sensibility tests, cold outperforms EPT and heat.
- Heat test (DOR 3.47) is the weakest — least useful as a standalone diagnostic.
- Quality caveat: treat the ranking as directional, not a precise probability.
