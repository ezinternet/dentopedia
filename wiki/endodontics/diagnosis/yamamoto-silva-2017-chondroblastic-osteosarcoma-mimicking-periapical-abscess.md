## One-line Summary

Case report (J Appl Oral Sci 2017): an 18-year-old male with a periapical radiolucency, PDL widening, and lamina dura loss at vital teeth #29/#30/#31 — initially suspected periapical abscess — was diagnosed by incisional biopsy as chondroblastic osteosarcoma, showing non-endodontic malignancies must be in the periapical differential.

## Summary

A periapical radiolucency at a tooth apex is usually inflammatory (apical periodontitis / periapical abscess from pulp necrosis), but it can also be a non-endodontic lesion. This report describes an 18-year-old male referred for "discomfort on the right posterior gingiva" lasting over a month. Examination showed a small expansion and redness of the buccal and lingual mandibular cortical plates at teeth #29, #30, and #31, with intact overlying mucosa and no pain or inflammation. Periapical and panoramic radiographs showed a 4–5 mm periapical radiolucency at the mesial and distal roots of #29 and #30, periodontal ligament (PDL) space widening, and loss of lamina dura — features compatible with a periapical abscess.

The decisive finding was that **all teeth responded positively to pulp sensibility testing** (Endo-Ice tetrafluoroethane), indicating vital pulp and arguing against an endodontic infection — so root canals were not treated. CBCT showed an ill-defined periapical osteolytic lesion (hypodense with internal hyperdense areas), thinning of the lingual/buccal cortical plates, and altered trabecular bone. Because the radiolucency was associated with a vital pulp, intraosseous malignancy was suspected, and an incisional biopsy was performed. Histopathology showed round-to-spindle-shaped cells with pleomorphism and variable osteoid production plus a focal area of atypical chondroblastic cells; Ki-67 confirmed proliferative activity. Final diagnosis: **chondroblastic osteosarcoma**. The patient was treated by hemimandibulectomy with wide margins plus adjuvant chemotherapy, then mandibular reconstruction with a fibular graft; one-year bone scintigraphy showed no metastases.

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates a malignant intraosseous tumor (chondroblastic osteosarcoma) clinically and radiographically mimicking a periapical abscess.
- Identifies **positive pulp vitality with a periapical radiolucency** as the red flag that should redirect the workup away from endodontic treatment toward CBCT and biopsy.
- Reinforces that non-endodontic lesions (including malignancies) must be in the differential diagnosis of periapical radiolucencies before root canal treatment.
- Illustrates the radiographic warning signs of jaw osteosarcoma: ill-defined margins, PDL widening from tumor infiltration, and lamina dura loss.

## Methodology

Single-patient case report.
- **Clinical**: cortical plate expansion/redness at #29–#31, intact mucosa, no pain.
- **Pulp testing**: Endo-Ice (tetrafluoroethane) — positive (vital) in all involved teeth.
- **Imaging**: periapical + panoramic radiographs (periapical radiolucency, PDL widening, lamina dura loss); CBCT (ill-defined osteolytic lesion, hypodense with hyperdense foci, cortical thinning, trabecular changes).
- **Histopathology**: incisional biopsy — pleomorphic round/spindle cells, osteoid production, focal atypical chondroblasts; Ki-67 IHC positive.
- No dental trauma, no crown cracks, no prior restorations.

## Results

- **Diagnosis**: chondroblastic osteosarcoma (microscopy + Ki-67).
- **Course**: lesion enlarged markedly within 10 days of biopsy.
- **Treatment**: hemimandibulectomy with wide margins + adjuvant chemotherapy; fibular-graft reconstruction.
- **Outcome**: one-year follow-up bone scintigraphy showed no bone metastases (remission).
- **Clinical lesson**: positive pulp vitality + atypical (ill-defined, PDL-widening, lamina-dura-loss) radiolucency should trigger suspicion of non-endodontic malignancy rather than empiric endodontic therapy.
