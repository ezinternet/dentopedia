## One-line Summary
Narrative review guiding pulp-test selection in pediatric dentistry, arguing sensibility tests are no longer recommended by pediatric specialists (subjectivity/cooperation) and vitality tests are preferred for primary and immature permanent teeth.

## Summary
Igna and colleagues review how to test pulp status in children, where standard sensibility tests perform poorly: young patients struggle with the subjective response, and primary and immature permanent teeth have different pulpal physiology (open apices, evolving innervation) that confounds thermal and electric tests. The review reports that pediatric specialists increasingly do not recommend sensibility tests and that vitality (blood-flow) methods — pulse oximetry, laser Doppler flowmetry — are the more promising direction, though not yet routine. Test choice should be matched to tooth maturity. This carries the adult vitality-over-sensibility finding into the pediatric setting.

## Key Contributions
- Pediatric-specific synthesis of pulp-testing limitations and options.
- Documents the shift away from sensibility tests toward vitality tests in children.
- Frames test selection by tooth maturity (primary vs immature permanent).

## Methodology
Narrative literature review of pulp-testing methods, focused on primary and immature permanent teeth, summarizing current pediatric recommendations.

## Results
Sensibility tests (EPT, thermal) are unreliable in children (subjectivity, cooperation, immature innervation). Vitality tests (pulse oximetry, LDF) are preferred in principle for primary/immature teeth but lack routine adoption and a large trial base.

## Clinical Application
- In children and immature permanent teeth, do not over-trust a negative cold/EPT response — these teeth can read "non-responsive" while vital.
- Prefer blood-flow assessment where available; integrate with history and radiographs before committing to pulp therapy.
