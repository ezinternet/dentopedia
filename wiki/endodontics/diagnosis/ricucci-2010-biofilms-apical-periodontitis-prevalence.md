## One-line Summary

Cross-sectional histobacteriologic study (Ricucci & Siqueira 2010, J Endod) of 106 roots with apical periodontitis: intraradicular biofilms in 77% of canals (untreated 80%, treated 74%), increasing with lesion size and significantly associated with epithelialized lesions (cysts 95%, abscesses 83%, granulomas 69.5%; p<0.001); extraradicular biofilms only 6%.

## Summary

This histobacteriologic study put numbers on the long-standing claim that apical periodontitis is a biofilm-driven disease. Across 106 roots (64 untreated, 42 treated) sampled by apical surgery or extraction, bacteria were present in all but one specimen, and **intraradicular biofilms were observed in 77% of canals** — strikingly similar between untreated (80%) and treated (74%) teeth, showing that conventional treatment does not reliably remove the biofilm. Biofilms also lined ramifications and isthmuses.

Two associations are clinically important. First, biofilm prevalence rose with **radiographic lesion size** (small 62% → large 82% → very large 100%), consistent with biofilm sustaining longstanding pathology. Second, biofilms were **significantly more frequent in epithelialized lesions** (p<0.001): cysts 95%, abscesses 83%, granulomas 69.5%. By contrast, biofilm presence did **not** correlate with clinical symptoms or sinus tracts, and **extraradicular biofilms occurred in only 6%** of cases — reaffirming that endodontic infection is overwhelmingly an intraradicular, biofilm problem. The authors conclude apical periodontitis meets accepted criteria for a biofilm-induced disease, while noting no single biofilm morphology characterizes endodontic infection.

## Key Contributions

- **Quantifies** intraradicular biofilm prevalence (77% overall) — empirical backbone for the "biofilm disease" classification of apical periodontitis.
- **Treated ≈ untreated** biofilm prevalence (74% vs 80%): standard treatment frequently fails to eradicate the apical biofilm — the mechanistic root of persistent apical periodontitis.
- **Lesion-size dose-response**: larger/very-large lesions almost always harbor biofilm.
- **Epithelialized-lesion association** (cysts > abscesses > granulomas) — links microbiology to histopathologic lesion type.
- **Symptoms ≠ biofilm**: biofilm-positive canals are not predicted by pain or sinus tract.
- **Extraradicular biofilm rare (6%)** — infection is predominantly inside the canal.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional histopathologic/histobacteriologic study. 106 roots with apical periodontitis (64 untreated, 42 treated) obtained by apical surgery or extraction, serially sectioned and stained (Brown & Brenn / Taylor-modified Gram). Biofilm presence scored in the apical canal segment, ramifications/isthmuses, and extraradicularly, then tested against clinical condition (symptoms, sinus tract), radiographic lesion size, and histopathologic lesion type (granuloma / abscess / cyst, with/without epithelium).

## Results

| Stratum | Biofilm prevalence |
|---|---|
| Overall (all canals) | **77%** |
| Untreated canals | 80% |
| Treated canals | 74% |
| Small radiographic lesion | 62% |
| Large radiographic lesion | 82% |
| Very large lesion | 100% |
| Cysts | 95% |
| Abscesses | 83% |

- Bacteria in all but one of 106 specimens.
- Epithelialized-lesion association significant at **p<0.001**.
- No significant association with symptoms or sinus tract (p>0.05).
