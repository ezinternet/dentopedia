## Summary

Apical periodontitis is a chronic inflammatory disease of the periradicular tissues driven by microbial infection of the root canal system. When root canal treatment fails to eliminate that infection, the lesion persists — usually as an asymptomatic radiolucency — and is termed **persistent (post-treatment) apical periodontitis**. Nair argues this is etiologically and therapeutically more complex than primary apical periodontitis in untreated teeth.

Most failures stem from technical shortcomings (inadequate aseptic control, poor access design, missed canals, insufficient instrumentation, leaking restorations). But even technically excellent treatment can leave disease because the canal anatomy — accessory and apical ramifications, isthmuses, and uninstrumented recesses — shelters **biofilms** that are >1000× more biocide-resistant than planktonic cells and are not removed by instrumentation plus NaOCl. Block-biopsy histobacteriology (which, unlike random paraffin sections, reliably detects infection) shows residual intraradicular infection in the majority of root-filled teeth with asymptomatic apical periodontitis.

The review's enduring contribution is the systematization of **six biological causes** of a non-healing radiolucency.

## Key Contributions

The **"Nair six"** causes of persistent apical periodontitis:

| # | Cause | Nature | Typical management implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Intraradicular infection** | Microbial (biofilm) in uninstrumented apical canal/isthmus/ramification | Most common; address with orthograde **retreatment** first |
| 2 | **Extraradicular infection** | Microbial — periapical **actinomycosis** (cohesive colonies surviving in tissue) | Often requires **apical surgery** |
| 3 | **Foreign-body reaction** | Non-microbial — extruded gutta-percha/sealer, cellulose (paper points/cotton) | Surgical removal of material |
| 4 | **Cholesterol crystals** | Non-microbial — endogenous lipid crystals → giant-cell response | Surgical curettage if non-healing |
| 5 | **True (apical) cyst** | Epithelium-enclosed self-sustaining lumen | Unlikely to heal non-surgically → surgery |
| 6 | **Scar tissue (fibrous) healing** | Not disease — benign collagen healing | No treatment; recognize to avoid over-treatment |

- **Intraradicular infection dominates**: it is the first cause to address, justifying nonsurgical retreatment before surgery in most persistent cases.
- Causes 2–5 are genuinely **extraradicular** or self-sustaining and are the rationale for **apical (endodontic) surgery** when orthograde retreatment cannot succeed.
- Distinguishing a **true cyst** (enclosed lumen, won't heal) from a **pocket/bay cyst** (lumen communicates with canal, may heal after orthograde treatment) requires serial histology, not radiographs.

## Methodology

Narrative review synthesizing the author's correlative light- and transmission-electron-microscopy studies of anatomically intact apical block-biopsies (serial/step-serial sectioning) with the wider literature. A key methodological point: conventional random paraffin sectioning **underestimates** intraradicular infection, which is why older studies reported only 10–15% bacterial detection while rigorous block-biopsy work finds the majority of treated lesions infected. Cites the one-visit molar study (Nair et al. 2005) showing residual biofilm in 14/16 mesial roots within uninstrumented recesses, isthmuses, and accessory canals.

## Results

- Biofilm biocide resistance >1000× planktonic → biological basis of failure.
- Intraradicular infection is the most frequent and important cause of persistent apical periodontitis.
- The minority of cases resisting orthograde retreatment is attributable to extraradicular infection (actinomycosis), foreign-body reaction, cholesterol crystals, or true cysts; apical scar is a benign mimic, not disease.
- Clinical takeaway: persistent radiolucency ≠ automatic surgery — retreatment (intraradicular cause) is the default first step; surgery targets confirmed/likely extraradicular causes.
