## One-line Summary

Cochrane SR+MA pub4 (47 RCTs, n=5805): single-visit and multiple-visit root canal treatment achieve equivalent radiological success; single-visit carries modestly higher pain within 1 week (RR 1.55, 95% CI 1.14–2.09, moderate certainty), especially in vital teeth.

## Summary

This Cochrane Intervention Review (4th edition) evaluated whether root canal treatment (RoCT) completed in a single visit produces better, equivalent, or worse outcomes compared to RoCT spread over two or more visits. Forty-seven RCTs (5805 participants, 5693 teeth) were included after excluding five studies from prior versions that did not use rubber dam isolation or sodium hypochlorite irrigation.

The review found **no clinically meaningful difference** in the primary outcomes: tooth extraction (very low certainty; only 2 studies) and radiological failure at ≥1 year (RR 0.93; moderate certainty). For secondary outcomes, single-visit RoCT was associated with a **higher proportion of patients reporting pain within the first week** (RR 1.55; moderate certainty), but no difference in pain at 72 hours post-obturation, pain intensity, analgesic use, flare-up, or sinus tract presence.

Subgroup analysis showed the pain disadvantage of single-visit was concentrated in vital teeth (RR 2.16) and when mechanical instrumentation was used (RR 1.80). Authors conclude that neither regimen is superior in efficacy, and the choice may be guided by patient preference, tooth condition, and clinician judgment.

## Key Contributions

- Largest and most current Cochrane synthesis on this question (pub4, search to April 2022)
- Applied GRADE framework to all outcomes — first version to systematically rate certainty
- Reclassified analgesic use finding: unlike pub3, found no difference in analgesic use despite higher pain reporting rate in single-visit group
- Identified vital teeth and mechanical instrumentation as subgroups where single-visit pain disadvantage is detectable
- Excluded outdated studies (no rubber dam, no NaOCl) to align evidence base with current standard of care

## Methodology

- **Design**: Cochrane systematic review + meta-analysis
- **Inclusion**: RCTs and quasi-RCTs; participants aged >10 years; comparing single-visit to multiple-visit RoCT in permanent teeth
- **Primary outcomes**: (1) tooth extraction due to endodontic problems; (2) radiological failure (periapical radiolucency) at ≥1 year
- **Secondary outcomes**: postoperative pain (dichotomous and continuous), swelling/flare-up, analgesic use, sinus tract/fistula at ≥1 month
- **Analysis**: Risk ratio (RR) with 95% CI; mean difference (MD) for continuous pain; I² for heterogeneity; GRADE certainty
- **Subgroups**: pulp vitality, instrumentation type (manual vs mechanical), interappointment medication use, periapical status
- Risk of bias: 10 low / 17 high / 20 unclear

## Results

| Outcome | N studies / teeth | RR or MD (95% CI) | I² | Certainty |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tooth extraction | 2 / 402 | RR 0.46 (0.09–2.50) | 0% | Very low |
| Radiological failure ≥1 yr | 13 / 1505 | RR 0.93 (0.81–1.07) | 0% | Moderate |
| Pain within 1 week (proportion) | 5 / 638 | RR 1.55 (1.14–2.09) | 18% | Moderate |
| Pain ≤72 h post-obturation (proportion) | 12 / 1329 | RR 0.97 (0.81–1.16) | 70% | Low |
| Pain intensity ≤72 h (VAS/NRS) | 12 / 1258 | MD 0.26 (−4.76–5.29) | 98% | Low |
| Pain at 1 week post-obturation | 9 / 1139 | RR 1.05 (0.67–1.67) | 61% | Very low |
| Swelling / flare-up | 6 / 605 | RR 0.56 (0.16–1.92) | 0% | Very low |
| Analgesic use | 6 / 540 | RR 1.25 (0.75–2.09) | 36% | Very low |
| Sinus tract / fistula | 5 / 650 | RR 1.00 (0.24–4.28) | 0% | Very low |

**Pain within 1 week — subgroups:**
- Vital teeth: RR 2.16 (1.39–3.36); 2 studies, 316 teeth
- Mechanical instrumentation: RR 1.80 (1.10–2.92); 2 studies, 278 teeth
- Non-vital teeth: no significant difference detected

**Authors' conclusion**: "There is currently no evidence to suggest that one treatment regimen (single-visit or multiple-visit RoCT) is more effective than the other." Moderate-certainty evidence indicates more patients in single-visit groups report pain within one week.
