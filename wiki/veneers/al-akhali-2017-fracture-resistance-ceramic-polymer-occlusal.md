## One-line Summary

In-vitro study (n=64 premolars, 4 CAD/CAM materials) showed lithium disilicate and zirconia-reinforced lithium silicate occlusal veneers (0.5/0.8 mm) had significantly higher fracture resistance than polymer-infiltrated ceramic and PMMA; all materials survived 1.2 million chewing cycles at 98 N and exceeded average posterior occlusal forces.

## Summary

This in-vitro study compared fracture resistance of four major CAD/CAM occlusal veneer materials on 64 extracted maxillary first premolars prepared to a standardized enamel-only depth (fissure 0.5 mm / cusp 0.8 mm). Half of each group underwent thermodynamic chewing simulation (1.2 million cycles, 98 N) before quasi-static fracture testing. All specimens survived aging. Ceramic materials (LD and LS) showed superior fracture resistance compared to polymer-based materials (PI and PM). Counterintuitively, thermodynamic loading significantly increased fracture resistance in LS, PI, and PM groups. All four materials far exceeded average in-vivo posterior occlusal forces, supporting their clinical viability as ultra-thin posterior occlusal veneers.

## Key Contributions

1. **100% survival of all four materials** through 1.2 million thermodynamic chewing cycles (98 N) — establishes minimum safety threshold for all tested CAD/CAM occlusal veneer candidates.
2. **Ceramic superiority in fracture resistance**: LD > PI (P ≤ 0.015), LD > PM (P ≤ 0.015); LD vs LS borderline NS (P = 0.051) — but both ceramics cluster together, above both polymers.
3. **Aging paradox**: Thermodynamic loading paradoxically raised fracture resistance in LS, PI, and PM — likely via interfacial bonding maturation or residual stress redistribution. LD was unaffected (already optimally crystallized at factory).
4. All four materials exceeded average posterior occlusal forces — supporting minimally invasive occlusal veneer as a viable long-term option across material classes, not only for premium ceramics.

## Methodology

- **n**: 64 maxillary first premolars extracted for orthodontic reasons; selected for dimensional uniformity.
- **Groups** (n=16 each): LD (e.max CAD), LS (Vita Suprinity), PI (Vita Enamic), PM (Telio CAD PMMA).
- **Preparation**: Enamel-only; fissure depth 0.5 mm, cusp 0.8 mm.
- **Luting**: Phosphoric acid etching + Multilink Primer A/B + Multilink Automix luting resin.
- **Aging subgroups**: n=8 thermodynamic loading (1.2 M cycles, 98 N) + n=8 non-aged control.
- **Fracture testing**: Quasi-static loading to fracture.
- **Statistics**: t-test + one-way ANOVA + Tukey HSD (α = 0.05).

## Results

| Group | Material | Fracture Resistance (non-aged) | Effect of aging |
|---|---|---|---|
| LD | Lithium disilicate (e.max CAD) | Highest | NS change |
| LS | ZLS (Vita Suprinity) | Similar to LD (P=0.051) | Significantly increased (P ≤ 0.031) |
| PI | Polymer-infiltrated ceramic (Vita Enamic) | Lower than LD (P ≤ 0.015) | Significantly increased (P ≤ 0.031) |
| PM | PMMA (Telio CAD) | Lower than LD (P ≤ 0.015) | Significantly increased (P ≤ 0.031) |

- All aged specimens in all groups survived 1.2 million chewing cycles without fracture, chipping, or cracks.
- All materials showed fracture resistance exceeding average posterior occlusal forces in the dentition.
