## One-line Summary

Systematic review (6 in-vitro studies, PRISMA) finding that CAD/CAM composite resin and lithium disilicate ceramic occlusal veneers have comparable fracture resistance (both exceeding maximum bite force), while two of three fatigue studies show higher survival for composite resin.

## 한줄요약

체계적 문헌고찰 (in vitro 6편): CAD/CAM 교합면 비니어에서 레진 복합체와 리튬 디실리케이트 세라믹의 파절저항성(Fracture Resistance)은 동등(최대 교합력 424–630 N 초과)하며, 피로저항성(Fatigue Resistance)은 2편에서 레진이 더 우수함.

## Summary

This PRISMA-compliant systematic review analyzed the mechanical performance (fatigue and fracture resistance) of CAD/CAM composite resin vs. CAD/CAM lithium disilicate ceramic occlusal veneers for severely worn posterior teeth. A search across 9 databases through January 2022 initially identified 400 studies; 6 in-vitro studies (3 fracture, 3 fatigue) met inclusion criteria. Meta-analysis was not possible due to high heterogeneity and risk of bias.

Key finding: both material classes are mechanically viable for posterior occlusal veneers. Composite resin veneers achieve fracture values of 1,018–3,584 N even at 0.5–1.5 mm thickness, well above the non-parafunctional maximum bite force (424–630 N). In fatigue testing, 2 of 3 studies found composite resin had significantly higher survival rates than lithium disilicate. Thickness recommendations derived from the literature: composite resin < 1 mm; lithium disilicate 0.7–1.5 mm.

## Key Contributions

- First SR synthesizing both fatigue AND fracture resistance data for CAD/CAM occlusal veneers in worn teeth
- Composite resin (VITA ENAMIC, Lava Ultimate, Paradigm MZ100) fracture values all exceed maximum bite force, even at 0.5 mm
- Thermomechanical loading eliminates the initial fracture resistance advantage of IPS e.max CAD over VITA ENAMIC at 0.5 mm
- Fatigue evidence favors composite resin in 2 of 3 studies; the remaining study (ultrathin 0.3–0.5 mm) shows no significant difference
- All 6 studies had high or medium risk of bias; no low-risk studies

## Methodology

- **Design**: Systematic review; PRISMA-compliant; INPLASY registered (2021.10.0036)
- **Search**: PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus, Cochrane, OpenGrey, Redalyc, DSpace, Grey Literature Report; through January 2022; no language/time limits
- **Inclusion**: In vitro studies, RCTs, non-randomized trials comparing CAD/CAM composite resin vs. ceramic occlusal veneers on worn posterior teeth; fatigue or fracture resistance endpoints
- **Exclusion**: Endodontically treated teeth, crowns/inlays/implants, FEA, case reports, existing reviews
- **Quality tool**: Modified CONSORT for in vitro studies (7 parameters; high = 1–3 items, medium = 4–5, low = 6–7)
- **Studies included**: 6 total (all in vitro); 3 fracture resistance, 3 fatigue resistance
- **Materials**: IPS e.max CAD (lithium disilicate), VITA ENAMIC (hybrid ceramic), Lava Ultimate (resin nanoceramic), Paradigm MZ100 (resin composite), XR experimental blocks

## Results

### Fracture Resistance

| Study | Material | Thickness (mm) | Fracture Load (N, M ±SD) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Andrade 2018 | IPS e.max CAD | 0.6 | 3,067 ±933 |
| Andrade 2018 | IPS e.max CAD | 1.5 | 4,995 ±855* |
| Andrade 2018 | VITA ENAMIC | 0.6 | 2,973 ±635 |
| Andrade 2018 | Lava Ultimate | 0.6 | 3,384 ±922 |
| Al-Akhali 2017 | IPS e.max CAD | 0.5 | 1,408.8 ±215.8 (pre-thermo) |
| Al-Akhali 2017 | VITA ENAMIC | 0.5 | 1,018.5 ±155.5 (pre-thermo); NS after thermo |

*Significantly higher than other groups (p = 0.027); no significant difference among 0.6 mm groups.

- All fracture values exceed maximum bite force (424–630 N) for non-parafunctional patients
- VITA ENAMIC 0.5 mm: requires >800 N above max bite force to crack
- After thermomechanical loading, no significant difference between IPS e.max CAD and VITA ENAMIC (p ≤ 0.291)

### Fatigue Resistance

- **Magne 2010**: Composite resin (Paradigm MZ100) had significantly higher fatigue resistance than IPS e.max CAD
- **Schlichting 2011**: Composite resin ultrathin veneers had significantly higher fatigue resistance than ceramic
- **Heck 2019**: No statistically significant difference in survival rate at ultrathin 0.3–0.5 mm (Lava Ultimate vs IPS e.max CAD)

### Risk of Bias

- High risk: 4/6 studies (Magne, Schlichting, Al-Akhali 2019, Heck)
- Medium risk: 2/6 studies (Andrade, Al-Akhali 2017)
- Most common weaknesses: lack of operator blinding, absent sample size calculations, single-operator tooth preparation

### Clinical Implications

- Both material classes are viable for posterior occlusal veneer restorations; decision may depend on clinical scenario (brittleness risk, preparation depth, parafunctional status)
- CAD/CAM composite resin may offer a fatigue advantage, particularly for patients at higher risk of cyclic loading failure
- Recommended minimum thickness: composite resin < 1 mm; lithium disilicate 0.7–1.5 mm
