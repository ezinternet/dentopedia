## One-line Summary

Narrative review (1999–2024) bridging laboratory failure mechanisms and clinical survival of dental veneers: overall >90% survival at 10+ years; minimal/no-prep glass-ceramic veneers show highest survival; fracture > debonding > color change as failure hierarchy.

## Summary

This narrative review synthesizes laboratory (in vitro) failure studies and clinical (in vivo) survival/success data for dental veneers published between 1999 and 2024. The author identifies the principal failure modes — fracture, debonding, and color change — and explains how laboratory findings translate (or fail to translate) into clinical outcomes. The review concludes that dental veneers carry a high overall survival rate (>90% for 10+ years) when properly selected and cemented, and that the preserved enamel layer is the single most important determinant of survival.

Key actionable findings:
- **Fracture** is the most prevalent clinical failure; driven by unfavorable occlusion, parafunctional activity, inadequate preparation design, and low-modulus cement with thick layers.
- **Debonding** is the second most common; driven by dentin exposure, contamination, and inadequate surface treatment.
- **Color change** is linked to cement type, ceramic opacity/translucency mismatch, and thermal aging.
- Minimal/no-prep veneers have the highest survival rates because bonding remains in enamel.
- Glass-ceramic (lithium disilicate, 450 MPa) outperforms feldspathic porcelain (60–70 MPa) in survival.
- No survival difference between maxillary and mandibular teeth, nor between males and females.

## Key Contributions

1. **Failure hierarchy**: fracture > debonding > color change — clinically ranked with in vitro mechanisms mapped to each.
2. **Substrate stiffness principle**: dentin (18 GPa) vs enamel (70–80 GPa) means dentin exposure dramatically reduces fracture resistance; resin cements with high modulus of elasticity partially compensate.
3. **Adhesive luting transforms thin veneers**: 0.6 mm adhesively luted lithium disilicate equals conventionally luted 1.5 mm in fracture load; 0.3 mm lithium disilicate veneers (2002 ± 427 N) match 0.5 mm veneers.
4. **Die spacer rule**: ceramic/composite ratio must be ≥3; die spacer thickness must not exceed 1/3 of veneer thickness to prevent fracture/debonding.
5. **Surface treatment protocol**: HF etching 20 s (glass-ceramic) or 60–90 s (feldspathic) + silane 60 s; UDMA-based resin cement for color stability.

## Methodology

- **Type**: Narrative literature review (not SR/MA; no PRISMA)
- **Search period**: January 1999–January 2024
- **Databases**: PubMed/Medline, Scopus, Google Scholar, Science Direct
- **Keywords**: "dental veneer," "complication," "survival rate," "failure," "success rate"
- **Inclusion**: tooth preparation, aging processes, veneer type, translucency, thickness, fabrication technique, shade, cement thickness
- **Exclusion**: marginal/internal fit, microhardness, water sorption, solubility, polishability, occlusal veneers, surface treatments, wear

### Failure Mode Mechanisms

**Fracture prevention**:
- Avoid unfavorable occlusion; perform occlusal assessment before and immediately after cementation
- Use high-flexural-strength materials (glass-ceramic 450 MPa, zirconia 1200 MPa) for bruxers, diastema closure, lingually tilted teeth
- Select low-modulus resin cement in thin layers; never leave unclosed cavities (keeps cement thin)
- Night guard mandatory for parafunctional patients

**Debonding prevention**:
- Preserve enamel (minimal/no prep); IDS when dentin exposure >50%
- Contamination control: avoid blood, saliva, handpiece oil, fluoride-containing polishing paste
- HF etching: 20 s for glass-ceramic; 60–90 s for feldspathic; silane 60 s after
- Light-cured polymerization for thin (<1 mm) veneers; dual-cure for thick/opaque veneers
- Die spacer ≤1/3 of veneer thickness

**Color stability**:
- UDMA-based resin cement (smaller filler, lower filler content) for long-term stability
- Glass-ceramic preferred over feldspathic (more crystalline = less color change with thermal aging)
- Internal bleaching prior to endodontic treatment when shade masking is required
