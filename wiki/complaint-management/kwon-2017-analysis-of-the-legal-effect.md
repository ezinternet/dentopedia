## One-line Summary
Retrospective analysis of 287 Korean civil court precedents (2000–2015) on aesthetic-surgery disputes, identifying four factors that make a doctor–patient settlement agreement legally binding and showing fee-free reoperations are not credited by courts.

## 한줄요약

## Summary
Kwon and colleagues reviewed 287 Korean civil precedents on aesthetic-surgery disputes (2000–2015) to determine when a settlement agreement between doctor and patient actually holds up in court. Of 68 cases containing a covenant not to sue, only 18 (26.5%) were dismissed on the strength of the agreement; the remaining 50 proceeded to judgment because the agreement was held ineffective. Effectiveness was unrelated to surgery type, complication type, geography, timing, or economic magnitude — what mattered was the *content* of the agreement, for which the authors derive four requirements. A practically important finding for clinics: courts did not credit fee-free reoperations offered by the surgeon when calculating compensation.

## Key Contributions
- First Korean court-database analysis of settlement-agreement legal effectiveness (n=287 civil cases).
- Derives four content requirements that make a settlement legally binding.
- Demonstrates the *act* of signing a settlement carries little weight absent adequate content.
- Shows fee-free reoperations are not offset against awarded compensation.
- Establishes that effectiveness is independent of surgery/complication type, geography, and timing.

## Methodology
Retrospective review of 287 aesthetic-surgery civil precedents (2000–2015) from a Korean court database. 68 cases featured a covenant not to sue. Each was classified by surgery type, complication type, geographic distribution, settlement timing, effectiveness, and economic impact, with statistical testing for associations.

## Results
Covenant-not-to-sue cases: 68/287. Recognized as effective (case dismissed): 18 (26.5%); not recognized (judged on merits): 50. No statistically significant association between effectiveness and surgery type, complication type, geography, settlement timing, or economic magnitude. Four content factors identified as determinants of legal effectiveness. Fee-free reoperations were not considered by courts in compensation determination.

## Clinical Application (Dentistry)
- A signed 합의서/부제소 합의 does not by itself bar a later claim — its enforceability turns on content, so dental settlement agreements should specify scope, waived claims, and consideration explicitly.
- Offering free re-treatment as goodwill does not reduce legal exposure; document it separately from any compensation term.
- Korean civil-law/precedent structure is shared across specialties, so the four-factor logic transfers from plastic surgery to dentistry.
