## Summary
Park and colleagues analyzed 40 Korean court precedents (2010–2015) in which surgical site infection (SSI) after plastic surgery led to a malpractice claim. Plaintiffs prevailed in 34 of 40 cases (85%), with a mean award of 2,832,654 KRW. Courts assigned liability on four recurrent grounds: failure to perform bacterial culture and use appropriate antibiotics, failure to refer to a higher-level hospital or infection-control service, inadequate SSI control measures, and — critically — absence of a preoperative explanation about infection risk. The study frames infection *handling* and preoperative *consent* as the two levers that most influence whether a surgeon is found liable.

## Key Contributions
- Plaintiff win rate of 85% (34/40) in Korean SSI litigation quantified.
- Four recurrent judgment grounds against surgeons made explicit.
- Preoperative explanation of infection risk established as a liability threshold (informed-consent axis).
- Claim demographics documented (predominantly female claimants; breast surgery most common).

## Methodology
Retrospective precedent review. Cases sourced from Supreme Court Legal Information Service, LAWnB, and Google Scholar; full judgment texts obtained from the Supreme Court of Korea and lower courts. 40 cases (2010–2015) classified into facial vs body surgery and analyzed for accused parties, institution, complications, monetary amounts, judgment reasons, and breach of care standards.

## Results
n=40 (35 female, 5 male; mean age 36). Plaintiff success 34/40 (85%); mean award 2,832,654 KRW (~USD 2,636.6). Most claims involved breast surgery; common complications were deformity, scar, asymmetry. Liability grounds: (1) no immediate culture / inappropriate antibiotics; (2) no transfer or infection-control consult; (3) inadequate infection-control measures; (4) no preoperative SSI explanation.

## Clinical Application (Dentistry)
- For surgical dentistry (extractions, implants, bone grafts), the same four grounds map directly: culture/treat postoperative infection promptly, escalate/refer when indicated, document infection-control measures, and obtain explicit preoperative consent covering infection risk.
- Missing preoperative infection consent is independently actionable — not merely the infection itself.
