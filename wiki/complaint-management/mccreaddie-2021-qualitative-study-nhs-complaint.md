## One-line Summary
Discourse analysis of 59 NHS written complaint-responses showing they routinely use non-apologies ('fauxpology') and subjectivise grievances to evade responsibility.

## Summary
McCreaddie, Benwell and Gritti applied discourse analysis to 59 written NHS complaint-responses in Scotland. Rather than acknowledging the grievance, responses systematically minimised it: they ignored the labour of complaining, recast complainants' accounts as merely subjective, attributed failings to one-off exceptional circumstances, and relied on the 'fauxpology' that apologises for feelings rather than actions. These moves evade responsibility and explain why local complaints so often remain unresolved and are later upheld by the ombudsman. The paper functions as a catalogue of response patterns to avoid.

## Key Contributions
- Identifies the 'fauxpology' -- a false/non-apology ('I am sorry you feel...') that blames the complainant's perception.
- Shows responses construct complainants' accounts as merely subjective.
- Documents use of unintentionality/exceptionality to mitigate sub-standard care.
- Explains discursively why many local complaints stay unresolved and escalate to the ombudsman.

## Methodology
Qualitative discourse analysis. Corpus of 60 anonymised written complaints and their responses from an NHS area in Scotland, collated over one month; this paper analyses the 59 complaint-responses. Discourse-analytic coding of language choices examined how far responses addressed complainants' grievances.

## Results
Responses rarely acknowledged the effort involved in complaining. They subjectivised complainants' accounts via specific discourse strategies, used unintentionality or exceptionality to mitigate poor care, and deployed the 'fauxpology' (e.g., 'I am sorry you feel...') that imputes distress to the complainant's possibly misguided impressions, thereby evading blame for the complainable action.
